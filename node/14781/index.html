August 23, 2005 • dberlow

Someone asked for this:

  1. Extend your arm horizontally to its full length in front of you with your thumb pointed up to the sky. 2. with both eyes open and head still, site on an object in the nearby visual field, 2-4 meters is best, longer is more difficult, shorter less telling. 3. as you line up the thumb and object, focus on the thumb, leaving the object in the background to defocus. 4. When this is done, you’ll find by closing one eye at a time, that only one of your eyes is still sited on the target, and the other is not. 5. Now knowing your favored eye, and to paraphrase the use instructions on some chopstick packaging, “You can see anything!”
My favored eye is the right one and I had that lasered to perfect vision (it came out at about -0.25). Driving a car I can read road signs up to a hundred metres away ; ). My left eye has been lasered to about -1.75 and works quite well for things closer up, eg the speedometer. My brain is supposed to work out which image to use. It mostly does (took some time to get used to it). I sometimes wonder whether I oughta had the two done the other way round. But then I may not be a graphic designer for the rest of my life…

I discussed it with the guy who did the lasering and it’s perfectly feasible. My intention was to postpone the need for reading glasses as long as possible (since I an working with stuff at arms length mostly, like books. monitors etc.).

He told me a ‘funny’ anecdote about the capabilties of the brain to adjust: seems that when older people that have cataracts have their natural lenses removed and exchanged for artificial ones are sometimes unable to discern distances at all, upto a year. Their brains can’t adjust as easyly anmore. Since I don’t consider myself old yet, I was not to worried about this. ; )

Adapting to the ‘dual vision’ was quite easy and the sensation of being able to see sharply when going for a snacking spree in the middle of the night is still a thrill… (I was L -7.0 and R -3.5, thereabouts).
