Hi there, I’m a new member to these forums as it’s the first time I’ve needed to elicit the help of you guys, previously I was content to just peruse through the archives and find information there. So currently, I’m researching for my third year dissertation, and surprise surprise, it’s on typography.

One of my case studies is based around typography acting as an identity for a city/region. There is already quite a bit that I’ve managed to gather on it, for example Metro Letters: a typeface for the twin cities - but all this is fairly recent. One of my themes of investigation is how type and cultural identity have changed over time. So my questions really are…

Is it a recent phenomenon that cities feel like they have to have an almost corporate brand feel? If so/if not, then when did it begin?

N.B. I say “history”, but even going back as far as the 20th century is enough to compare.

Thanks very much in advance if anyone has any nuggets of information to part!

The concept of “branding” per se is a fairly recent phenomenon, although “trademarks” have been around since at least 1777, when the Bass Brewery registered Great Britain’s first ®, and longer than that in the form of the “hallmarks” used by craftsmen from medieval times.

City—or, perhaps more properly, Empire—“branding” is much older: in witness whereof, consider the iconic statue of Romulus and Remus and the SPQR banners carried by Roman legionaries.

JamesM: There is quite a nice book which exhibits the whole process of the project. It’s called Metro Letters: A typeface for the twin Cities. Basically back in 2003, in conjunction with the Twin Cities Design Celebration (TCDC), a type project was started inviting a handful of well established type designers/studios to create a unique face for the Twin Cities. The winning entry was by LettError, and I must admit it is a beaut, so do check it out. To this day, I don’t think it has been implemented which is a shame, but there is mention in the book that it was mainly to brand any publications/literature derived for the TCDC and to get people interested in type and design, more so than to brand the two cities. Kind of like the stuff Erik Spiekermann did for Glasgow quite a few years ago, I guess.

Thanks for the additional info on Metro Letters.

Offhand I’d think that cities have far less ability to brand themselves than private companies do. Branding a city’s official website and literature is certainly feasible, although in many cities (especially smaller ones) some of the city’s publications are put together by departmental staffers who clearly aren’t designers and who may not understand or care about typographic guidelines.

Regarding signage, city council members may be hesitant to vote the money to redo all the city’s signage just to update the typography. And some signage may be impractical to change or may need to be retained for historic reasons.

I’m not saying branding doesn’t happen, obviously some cities do try to brand themselves. I’m just saying it’s more difficult, for a variety of reasons, than for a corporation to do so.
