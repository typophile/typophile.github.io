FF Info is a rounded (or roundish) sans designed for wayfinding.

According to Erik Spiekermann, the rounded corners can save a lot of time in plotting and weeding the vinyl when manufacturing a large number of signs.

I just imagined with all the testing and research that goes into way finding typefaces there surely would be some commentary on rounded typefaces in signage.

I don’t know studies which researched such a specific design feature. In my opinion: It simply isn’t a relevant factor. Letter skeleton, stroke width, letter-spacing and of course type size are so much more important. So I would say it’s more a matter of stylistic freedom to pick a rounded font or not.

I am also not clear about how much roundness you are thinking about. Something like DIN Rounded? Or just rounded corners as in FF Info or Wayfinding Sans? If it’s extreme as in DIN Rounded, I would say it slightly decreases legibility, because you remove information from the stroke ends. They are more “fuzzy” and not as articulated as they could be. But like I said: that’s a minor effect and can easily be compensated.

2015-02-25 02:09:22 • Thomas Phinney

There are situations where research does go into the selection of typefaces for signage. Thus, we have the special typefaces used for highway signage in the United States - because legibility, even under the poorest conditions, will save lives.

In other circumstances, though, the choice of typeface is left up to individuals who make their choices based on aesthetics and the image they wish to project.

Thus, Helvetica Medium has been treated as the ideal typeface for certain kinds of signage - subway stations, airline terminals, and the like - modern, efficient, inoffensive.

Unless there are critical legibility concerns, therefore, if you happen to like a particular rounded sans-serif typeface, go for it.
