2009-11-22 18:31:54 • hrant

All typefaces, except the most unimaginative rip-offs and revivals, are experimental, because the type design process is one of “let’s see if this glyph shape works with the other ones”. So the designer experiments with applying variations of thematic qualities like stress, contrast, weight, spacing, serifs, and curve shapes to individual glyphs, and seeing how they look–first in the metrics window, then in sample documents in various media. Next step is to go back and refine things to work better en masse. It’s the feedback loop that’s a part of so much creative production.

But I think what is loosely meant by experimental design is that which is unusual enough to draw attention to itself, rather than being part of a seamless whole where only the message is apparent, not the means by which it is delivered.

Recently “experimental” has been updated (rebranded? obselesced?) in the concept of Critical Design, so perhaps you could approach your topic by looking to see how a typeface could be an example of critical design, which carries more intellectual clout.

2009-11-22 19:05:29 • hrant

But I think what is loosely meant by experimental design is that which is unusual enough to draw attention to itself…

I think it’s also become a term with pedantic connotations. I see the same ideas rehashed over and over and labeled experimental. They’re usually images from workshops or school projects. Sculptural letters, found letters, Sagmeisteresque letters made of string, counterless letters, modular letters, and code-generated letters are all commonly labeled experimental. But there’s a great deal of sameness to them; most of the variety comes from the raw materials or camera angles. It seems to me that experimental is often used in the same sense as school science lab experiments: the outcomes are predetermined and the purpose is hands-on education rather than innovation.
