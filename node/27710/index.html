The hand in motion and type design, one more once

August 16, 2006 • William Berkson

How do letters written by a moving hand influence type design for the better and worse? The discussion over ‘[[chirography]]’ in type has raged several times on Typophile.

I recently encountered two interesting discussions relevant to this issue. First, ‘[[Printers Type in the Twentieth Century]]’ by [[Richard Southall]] discusses the ‘[[Metafonts]]’ of [[Donald Knuth]]. If I understand the account of the Metafonts correctly, a spine of the letter is defined as if a stylus were drawing a thin line. Then variations of width out from that thin line are used to define a font, giving varied weights and contrasts–something like multiple masters, only different. [Knuth’s concept is somewhat different than the ideas of G. Noordzij, which appeared at about the same time.]

According to the account this effort at ‘chirographic’ type didn’t really work. The modifications in weights at joins and other optical corrections couldn’t be programmed in any easy or natural way, and so the effort was never developed further. Instead, people went over to outline fonts.

So score one against chirography.

My other encounter was with the presentations of [[Mark Jamra]] at TypeCon. He gave a fascinating account of the teaching methods of the late [[Martin Andersch]], under whom he studied and whose methods he has adapted in his own type design and teaching. Andersch, inspired by [[Rudolph Koch]]’s late work, has the students start with ‘expressionistic-interpretative writing’. For Jamra, this involves taking an unconventional tool, dipping it in ink, and drawing a word of 3-5 letters over and over for hours on reams of paper. He says that this induces a meditative, relaxed state in which the mind and body are able to get amazing, innovative results. These are then refined on the computer to produce a digital word.

Jamra has posted examples on his extensive web site. His own typefaces, including [[Kinesis]] and [[Alphatier]] were inspired by this process.

Jamra’s work seems to give opposite indications from Knuth’s: the moving hand is indeed a valuable and creative “underlying force” in type design, as [[Jan van Krimpen]] put it.

I don’t think you have to use quite so much paper to reach an intuitive state. Any hand skill benefits from a warm-up period (and that would include ball sports). Then the muscle memory kicks in and works fluidly. Muscle memory is pliant, too, affected by other things that happen to one physically. I haven’t found it becomes forgetful, though, in writing–more so in music. Altered mental states will produce different results, and there are many ways to achieve that. Basically, just get out of your rut.
