Strokes are generally thinned at oblique intersections. Otherwise, the joint becomes enlarged, and ends up looking bigger than it really is, due to "bleed" Bleed is of several kinds. Firstly, it is a traditional artefact of print typography, because these sharp crevasses are apt to fill in with ink. Hence the preemptive move of thinning the stroke(s), and even taking it further and enlarging the pit as an "ink trap". Secondly, there is a visual bleed in the perceptual process, because the apex of the joint is beyond the threshold of acuity. Thirdly there is categorical or conceptual bleed, which is further up the perceptual chain; this occurs because at a joint there is a wider distance of solid than either of the stroke widths that comprise it, and this does not appear to be aligned with either stroke-- it "reads" as a blot or hot spot, visual noise which serves no purpose. That third kind is more what happens at the bottom of the "a". Letter forms also suffer shrinkage as well as bleed, at the outside of corners. Or perhaps that could be described as a kind of "negative" bleed, of the surrounding ground into the figure. The serif is the most obvious method of dealing with shrinkage, but in phototypography, "ticks" were sometimes added to corners to prevent the arris from becoming rounded. Even in a sans, the end of a stroke is often widened and sharpened, as at the top left of the "a" above, to counteract shrinkage.
