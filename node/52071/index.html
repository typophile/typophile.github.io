The notion that you use lining figures with caps and old-style with lower case is just a quick guideline. I’ve seen many advertisements – print and web – that were about two lines long, used lowercase letters and old-style numbers, and just looked odd. Lining figures would have looked much more natural.

Contrast this to a book where the practice is to put citations run into the text instead of as footnotes: e.g.

“The facticity of the self is what it is not, and on the other hand, is not what it is” (BAN 113) [The quote is from Being and Nothingness but the page number is invented.]

Now imagine a book where there are about 5 such citations per page – 10 on a spread. With the book open, if the citations are set full caps with lining figures, what jumps out and clamors for attention are those citations. Probably not what the author would consider the most important thing in his/her text. If they are set small caps w/ old-style figures, they assume their proper importance visually.

So context matters. So too does audience expectations. You can set mathematics with os figs – it was done for years when they were the only set of figures available. But os figures look odd to most modern audiences reading math, and should probably not be used, even though the text will be use mainly lowercase letters.
