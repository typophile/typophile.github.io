Best Clarendon?

April 20, 2005 • Forrest L Norvell

Fortune, Volta, Consort, Farao, PNM Caecilia… I've been on an Egyptian bender recently, but I keep coming back to Clarendon and its wonderful curves and contrasty stems. After spending all night looking at the diifferent versions of Clarendon available digitally, I'm wondering if there's any meaningful difference between them in terms of quality or elegance of the typefaces themselves. URW has a billion different weights, AGFA/Monotype has both Monotype Clarendon and Monotype New Clarendon, and Elsner+Flake offers their version in OpenType, albeit with no OpenType goodies as far as I can tell.

Fred Showker says egyptians should have slightly concave feet; do any of the digital cuts actually do this? Which, if any, of the metal types do? I looked in my Encyclopedia Of Typefaces and Rookledge's, and I think I see what he means, but maybe I'm just suggestible.

Why can't I find Fortune or the expanded weights of Consort available online / in digital form? They sure are pretty… instead I've grabbed Volta and Farao. It generally doesn't take much persuading to get me to buy Storm fonts, and Farao manages to be warm and feminine and understated all at once.
Take a look at Sutro by Jim Parkinson at www.typedesign.com

Sutro is nice, but it loses much of the quirkiness of Clarendon and feels too much like what it is, which is a Jim Parkinson face for use in magazines. It's clean and effective, but looking at it gives me bad flashbacks to boring profiles of alt.country bands in Rolling Stone and Magnet. The same goes for Humanist Slabserif 712 and Egyptian 505, although those seem like they'd be useful in a lot of the same situations as PNM Caecilia, with a slightly less reserved character.

Oxtail is brilliant. Thanks for bringing it to my attention, Yves! It's as playful as Farao but goes in a different direction. Since I just licensed Farao, though, I think I'll hold off on Oxtail for now.
