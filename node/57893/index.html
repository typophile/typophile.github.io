Proper Usage of Okina Character?

May 11, 2009 • sirmon

I’m working on some lettering Hawaiian words using the [[Okina]] character. I’ve read a few things online about it and the consensus is that it’s the same character as the opening single quote. I can’t help but think that it’s just the way people are using it now and that there’s a more traditional unique character out there. This article on Wikipedia doesn’t make it any easier for me: http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Okina (probably because I never learned to read).

So to simplify my question: Is there a more traditional and correct character that is not the opening single quote to represent the Okina character? Any help would be appreciated.

Thanks in advance.

I quickly looked up a couple of old books on Google.

A Hawaiian dictionary from 1865 doesn’t seem to mention the Hawaiian glottal stop altogether.

A Hawaiian grammar from 1864 uses a simple apostrophe (a closing single quote as opposed to the opening single quote) to indicate the ‘guttural break’.

If you look closely at the table in the Wikipedia article, it says that the okina for Hawaiian is ‘transitionally formalised’, while for Tongan it is ‘officially formalised’. That means presumably that for Hawaiian at least, the glottal stop was indicated in a number of ways (apostrophe as in the grammar, or completely omitted as in the dictionary), but it has transitioned to using the present form of the okina. I doubt there was a traditional unique character; they probably used the apostrophe or single quote forms from the start.

Perhaps you could try searching Google Books for examples of literature for other Polynesian languages that use the okina such as Tongan and Tahitian. It would be interesting to learn when the okina and its form was standardized for these languages.
