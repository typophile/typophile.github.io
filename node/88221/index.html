2011-12-14 16:19:11 • dezcom

mimeographed … some chemicals to transfer what was typed onto a metal sheet

Perhaps there were different types of mimeographs. In my first job I had to mimeograph a weekly newsletter and no metal sheets or chemicals (except ink) were involved.

I removed the typewriter ribbon and typed on a stencil, which felt like a sheet of waxy paper. The typewriter punched through the stencil’s waxy layer, but a bottom layer which was permeable stayed intact. Then I wrapped the stencil around an inked drum. The ink would ooze through the areas cut out by the typewriter.

The waxy stencil would deteriorate after printing a few hundred copies. Perhaps the metal sheet you mentioned was used in jobs where more durable materials were needed to print larger quantities.

2011-12-14 16:52:43 • riccard0

I just remembered that there was an optional method in which a small machine scanned the layout and created a stencil or printing plate from that scan.

If I remember correctly, that method reduced the overall quality slightly, but it made it easier to produce more complicated layouts and/or include illustrations. (If you produced the stencil directly on your typewriter without a scanner, illustrations needed to be drawed directly on the stencil, or else you needed to purchase pre-made stencil illustrations that were then cut and pasted into the stencil. I did that occasionally and it was a pain.)

Since the scanning method required the purchase of a special scanner, it was probably used mostly by larger organizations that could afford the extra expense.
