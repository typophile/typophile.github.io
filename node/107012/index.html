You're welcome. As for your question "What typeface would be a common use by English printers in the beginning of the 1600's in a usable revived font today?" the answer is that there were a variety of dissimilar typefaces, some Garamondesques, others Dutch, some Venetians, etc. In the absence of an image from your book approaching the detail of the mid 17th century specimen I posted, it is not possible to recommend a specific digitized font. About all I can say is that it would be pre-Caslon with low contrast. If you don't have a more detailed image you could check the facsimiles of Shakespeare first folio 1623 http://internetshakespeare.uvic.ca/Library/facsimile/overview/book/F1.html and pick something digital from the references I posted earlier. There is a real gap in digitized typefaces for anyone wanting to recreate books from this era. Perhaps at some future date a publisher might commission a fontmaker to produce a font that would be used for a re-issue of the Shakespeare first folio 1623, complete with it's ligatures, contractions and other printer's quirks. At least that would be something. don

I checked the Shakespeare 1623 against modern digital typefaces and in my opinion it is printed in a Garomondesque type style. I suggest that would be best rendered by using "Garamond Premier" designed by Robert Slimbach. After he finished Adobe Garamond, Slimbach created a more "authentic" Garamond revival that was closer to its sources than other digital Garamonds. The Pro versions include Italic swash variations and Roman alternates. It is not perfect for all of the letter variations, contractions etc. in Shakespeare 1623, but is as close as I can find. If your examination of Chapman shows that it seems to resemble the Shakespeare facsimiles in the link I posted, then Garamond Premier is as close, in my opinion, as you are going to get for a match to Chapman. don
