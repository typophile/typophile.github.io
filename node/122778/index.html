A lot of people who are not from Britain, usually say ‘England’ to refer to Britain. Dutch people do this all the time. I usually respond with “Only in England, or also in Scotland and Wales (and Northern Ireland)?” Most people react confused. It is very easy to understand the different terms.

Great Britain (island) is England, Wales and Scotland. The United Kingdom (sovereign state) is Great Britain plus Northern Ireland. The abbreviation ‘Britain’ is used to refer to both the island and the state. Not complicated at all.

I wonder what people from Britain think when they hear foreigners say ‘England’ instead of Britain.

I might be overreacting here

Yup. You know perfectly well what someone is telling you if she says (in Dutch or German or French or …): ‘I’ve never been to England’. This means that the linguistic convention—‘England’ by default refers to the UK—works in those languages. Everybody, including you, gets the intended meaning. So, why would you want to impose a different convention and try to ‘educate’ others? Linguistic symbols (e.g. words) are more or less arbitrary anyway: It doesn’t really matter whether we call the United Kingdom ‘the United Kingdom’, ‘England’, ‘Albion’ or ‘Gnushty’—as long as everybody gets the intended meaning. That’s the case. It might be unfortunate that the colloquial naming convention in some languages (UK = England) conflicts with a naming convention that’s used in other contexts (UK ≠ England), but these contexts seldom overlap and misunderstandings rarely arise (and if they do, they can usually be sorted out quickly). This means that the convention you suggest is actually worse than the one in place: Instead of being able to say ‘Engeland’, people would have to say ‘Groot-Brittannië’, which is longer and more difficult to spell, without much added benefit in everyday language (excluding precision for precision’s sake and the satisfaction of having avoided confusion that wasn’t even there in the first place). So, what I’m trying to say is: Understand that in different linguistic contexts, different things matter (most)—for instance brevity and simplicity in colloquial language or precision in scientific language. No one asks you to ever say ‘Engeland’ if you find this all too brief and simple, but I don’t see a valid reason to foist your discomfort with a perfectly normal linguistic convention on others.

Since England and Britain are proper nouns, even if one disdains prescriptivism in general, the terms right and wrong do apply, because those words are names, like the names of people, or like trademarks.

Of course, there are exceptions even to this generalization.

So I’m going to open up the really big can of worms associated with this topic.

Thus, in English, “the Americas” is a body of land that includes North America, Central America, and South America. In French, the term for that same body of land is “Amèrique”, and something similar is true for other Romance languages, including Spanish.

At one time, decades ago now, the official name of Brazil was “Los Estados Unidos do Brasil”.

The official names of a few countries are, or were:

The Dominion of Canada The Commonwealth of Australia République Française (The Republic of France, lit. French Republic) The United States of America The Union of South Africa Los Estados Unidos do Brasil (The United States of Brazil)

The pattern is clear; it is <form of government> of <name of country>.

So these names referred to Canada, Australia, France, America, South Africa, and Brazil respectively.

At least, this suits English speakers quite well, and makes perfect sense to them. America is one country, and the Americas are two continents linked by an isthmus.

But in the Romance languages, the word for the Americas is their cognate to “America”. This causes a great deal of confusion, and has led some people to claim that the conventional English usage of “America” for the United States of America is in some way bigoted or exclusionary or ignorant and so on.

One way to reply is to note that neither Jamaica nor Ontario nor Guyana are part of “British Columbia”. And what about the country that dares to call itself Colombia?

Because Columbia is also a name for the lands discovered by Columbus - the Americas - as witness its use by Americans - the song “Hail Columbia”, the name of CBS, the Columbia Broadcasting System.
