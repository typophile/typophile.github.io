2007-10-04 16:13:42 • William Berkson

According to Robert Bringhurst’s fascinating book ‘The Elements of Typographic Style’ you’ll be needing British Neoclassical type. You might want to consider Monotype Baskerville (c. 1754), drawn by John Baskerville, Birmingham or Monotype Bell (1788), designed by Richard Austin, London.

this is true, if you’re being period specific.

my experience when doing prop research, however, is that the things people use are actually a little older than the period in which they exist. for example: a household existing in the american fifties, if brand new and owned by a fairly affluent character, would have all atomic-age artifacts.

but for most folks, the reality would be a mixture of atomic age, streamline, and maybe some late deco and nouveau – not to mention classic revival, which seemed to be everywhere in america at almost any point during the twentieth century. there would also be a tatse of whateevr the local vernacular happened to be.

i don’t know if that would hold true to a period as far back as the 18th century since economics moved at such a different pace.

To quote http://www.graphic-design.com/Type/typography.html:

1725 - William Caslon the first British type standard William spent his youth as an apprentice, journeyman and master in metal tooling. His specialty was gun-barrel engraving. During the early 1700s his concern grew to notariety and he became quite well known. He went on to found his own type foundry by developing his own variation of Garamond’s classic type styles. His “Caslon” became so popular that in 1730 it was adopted by British newspapers to become a standard. It was used for the printing of the Declaration of Independence and the Constitution of the United States. Today Caslon is considered one of the most beautiful of the Old Style families for its openness and its delicate yet uniform serifs.
