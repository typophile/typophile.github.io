The problem with these terms is that to some people they have interchangeable meanings today. In the past, there were more meanings and differentiation. The term “Logo” is the biggest contender. Logo is short for logotype. Logo means “word” in Greek. To some degree, people use the term “wordmark” like the term logotype used to be used. It meant the name of the company or organization depicted typographically, like Ford. Later, the initials or acronym was also considered a logotype, like IBM. Monogram, like the TDC, is a style of configuration where initials may be superimposed on each-other or placed within a shape. This was a common practice years ago for stationary and even clothing (monogrammed shirts). A symbol was initially a graphic which represented some entity but letterforms were not required. The Nike swoosh is a symbol. A trademark was either a symbol or logotype which was legally endowed with the identification of a company or corporation. Trademarking involved government registry and search for availability. Brand used to mean a product brand like “Rice Crispies” but later came to mean the parent company which made the product as in the “Kellogg’s Brand”. The brandmark might be either the “Snap-Cracle-Pop” characters or the script “Kellogg’s” name. Identity was all the things involved with identifying a company including symbol or logo, colors layout of print matter, etc.

Today though, everything is called “Branding” even if no product is involved. Corporate identity is even called branding. Everything used to identify a company is acalled a logo even if no letterforms are involved. The whole Branding buzz seems-to have been kindled from the marketing angle and has come to mean positioning a given organization to appeal to a certain set of customers—trying to make them look like a duck.

I am sorry for rambling so much (between phone calls here). I hope this helps clarify the very muddy waters that we now have in this business.

If I recall correctly, Peirce classifies signs according to these categories

Symbol. A symbol is a sign related to the actual object by an artificial or very abstract convention. Words (printed or spoken) and abstract symbols such as the cross fall under this category. They have not actual relation to the thing.

Indices. These are signs that refer to the actual object by indicating it’s existance such as smoke to the fire or a pictogram.

Icon. Is a sign used in substitution of the actual thing by resembling it’s qualities such as speed, color, texture, character, etc… most of brands fall under this category.

Trademark and brandmark I suspect are more related to the US commercial and trading laws.

Monogram is a logotype formed by the initials of a name.

Logotype is type whose semantic quality exceeds that of a simple type labelling. Originally logotypes were words casted in a single slug for type composition.

Brand is the semantic construction of a corporation’s identity by means of it’s communication and way of being.

Idetity is the perception built in the mind of a public of a corporation’s being.

Mark is the core element of a Brand. It’s visual device composed of an icon or a logotype or both, a color scheme and a graphic system.

I knew using Chris’s picture would lead to trouble, but someone else started it. Since it looks like the animal handler at Bembo’s Zoo, though, its a most interesting example. I would call it a strong avatar.

My proposed definition is based on the classical definition, adding in my suspicions of how it came to be defined like we use today, inspired by cyberpunk literature.

I think the original vision was that by now we were supposed to have throbbing surreal 3D virtual-raves for an internet, that we waltzed about like supermen, while data and applications labored to serve our virtual identities. However, in reality, we never got hover cars by 2000, we don’t all “see” the matrix code as redheads (without decoding software), and we can’t even get one standard for instant messaging.

But if the internet is indeed cyberspace, than the icons that represent “actual” people, would be avatars. When I describe them as such people always have distinguished them from other icons in a UI immediately with no pre-discussed scenario.

In massively-multiplayer online RPG’s, the worlds are generally populated with hordes of 3D characters, each individually directed, but these similar things are rarely called avatars. Funny, since most of the activity bypasses the role-playing factor, and the characters are as distinct from their owners as the doggy in Monopoly. I think this is because the character is intended to be “not you” in this case, so it cannot be your avatar.

I think there are various strengths of avatars, as well. Chris’s shows what he looks like and what he is ‘about’ and some of his mirth, so it is strong.
