Obsolescence Insurance and the Next Big Thing

August 24, 2010 • oldnick

In a previous thread I began in the “Release” forum, I mentioned receiving an inquiry about licensing some my fonts from a large online marketer of printed goods. What should have been a simple task of determining pricing for server-based use became more complicated when the agreement they offered included rights for any media or application which might be developed in the future—in essence, obsolescence insurance. After considering input from participants in the thread, I decided that this provision was a no-go. However, I am fairly confident that, if this entity sought this particular concession, other potential licensees may also do so at some time in the future. Despite some resistance to considering the community of fontmakers an “Industry” (see the “That if?” thread), wouldn’t it serve our best interests to have some kind of common stance on this issue?

What is the next Big Thing for fonts? Maybe this has already been discussed at various conferences but, at first glance, it appears to me that we might get a clue from Cloud Computing. The idea of subscribing to software served up on the web is, when you think about it, the functional equivalent of obsolescence insurance. The idea is, instead of paying moderate to princely sums for periodic updates, cloud users have their software updated on a continuing basis, and pay a monthly fee for the convenience. Is it possible that font licensing for the cloud is the Next Big Thing in fonts? Your thoughts?

Hey, folks, this ain’t no rope-a-dope. These are serious questions: do we prepare for the future, or get taken by surprise by it?

James,

This happens all the time, but most of the time you’d never notice it, since the song that was replaced wasn’t an integral part of the scene. I’ve played bass on a couple songs that ended up on either the DVD (Felicity) or the download (The Black Donnellys), and the songs are so much in the background that no one except crazy hardcore fans would notice the difference.

My favorite example of this is “Wayne’s World”. There’s a scene in a music store where Wayne (I think) is playing “Stairway to Heaven” on a guitar, and then an employee walks over and points to a sign on the wall that says “No Stairway to Heaven”. If you ever see it on TV (they still play it on Comedy Central every now and then), the scene makes no sense, because they replace the music with some random guitar noodling (it actually inadvertently makes the scene funnier).

I think the same thing happened in the syndicated reruns of “WKRP in Cincinnati” (at least with some of the songs).

Music licensing is really expensive. These days, the production companies are being smart about it and licensing maybe one well known (i.e. expensive) song for a show, and then licensing a bunch of songs from independent artists from much, much less money (a well known song might cost $30,000, while people like my friends who have songs placed on shows get $1000-$2000), and more importantly, they’re setting up the deal up front so that it covers the DVD or download. In the old days (i.e. ten or more years ago), they’d just license a song for a show, and then they’d have to deal with any repurposing of the show with new licenses. I just read recently that the show “The Wonder Years” probably won’t ever be released on DVD because licensing of all the late 60s/early 70s music, which was so integral to the show, would be too expensive.

So to bring this back on topic, if the same thing were ever to happen with fonts, I’d imagine the same thing to take place. People might license Helvetica Neue for a big fancy book, but for the web, or PDFs, they’d settle with Arial, rather than paying additional licensing fees.

As I understand it, the eventual full implementation of Cloud Computing will consist of applications which reside “in the Cloud,” that is, on remote web servers. Users will access the apps through their web browsers–or a new generation of web-interface apps–and word-process, design or whatever it is they do now with apps residing on their local machine drives.

The advantage this arrangement affords users is, because the apps would be subscription-based, the end user has effectively purchased obsolescence insurance, because any updates to the apps would be made on the server. The next time a user logs on, that user is working with the most recent version of the app, and he or she doesn’t have to pay for an upgrade. The advantages for the software distributors, because the apps would be subscription-based, are numerous: no costs associated with promoting and rolling out upgrades, and a steady revenue stream from subscriptions.

In many ways, the public has been conditioned to expect continuing costs as a prerequisite for life in the twenty-first century: people routinely pay cellphone bills which are larger than some car payments I have had, and don’t blink. The subscription model is here to stay.

When (not if) this happens, will the type community be prepared? If someone wants to subscribe to Nick Shinn’s Elegant Text Fonts package or Nick Curtis' Wacky Headline Fonts package, what are they going to pay on a recurring basis?

FWIW, the subscription trend may have already begun. Quite recently, I received a licensing inquiry with a proviso I am seeing more and more of lately> My redacted reply was:

The condition “media whether now known or hereafter devised anywhere in the world at any time” appears to be popular among would-be licensees these days. It is effectively obsolescence insurance and, as I am sure you are aware, insurance premiums are generally recurring, rather than one-time, payments. Without “or hereafter devised” et. seq., the price for a 500-user license would be a one-time charge of $xx.xx. With the proviso, the subscription fee would be $xx.xx per year, which would include any updates or changes in format necessitated by media hereafter devised.

I will keep you posted on whether or not this little experiment flies…

In fight for 3Par, HP tops Dell’s bid with a $1.88 billion offer By Jessica Mintz, Associated Press Saturday, August 28, 2010

SEATTLE - Hewlett-Packard boosted its bid for 3Par to $1.88 billion Friday, topping Dell’s offer by 11 percent and again raising the stakes in the bidding contest for the data-storage company.

The $30-per-share offer from HP came just hours after Dell matched HP’s Thursday bid of $27 per share. The latest price is three times what 3Par was trading at before Dell made its first bid last week, for $18 per share, or $1.13 billion.

Dell and 3Par said earlier Friday that 3Par’s board had accepted the latest bid from Dell, which only has to match the terms of other offers under its initial agreement with 3Par, based in Fremont, Calif.

On Friday, 3Par shares surged $6.43, or 25 percent, to close at $32.46, which is above HP’s latest offer price, signaling that investors expect Dell to respond. In a statement, Dell spokesman David Frink said the company will review HP’s latest offer and “act in the best interests of our customers, shareholders and long-term value creation.”

HP and Dell, among the world’s largest personal computer makers, are looking at 3Par as a way to build up their “cloud computing” businesses, delivering software, data storage and other services to customers over the Internet.

They’re also both trying to expand beyond the PC business, where profits are shrinking as computer makers compete with lower prices and where the cost of parts has increased.

Cloud computing holds the promise of rich profits for technology providers. Many companies aren’t buying their own computer servers for certain tasks anymore. Instead, they’re paying to have software they would have stored on those machines delivered to them over the Internet.

Dell, HP and others are taking advantage of the trend by offering cloud-computing services on a subscription basis and selling equipment and software to help customers build their own cloud systems.

Cloud computing can help reduce data-center expenses because the systems are designed to be shared by multiple customers, which spreads out the cost of operating pricey equipment. The servers and storage computers need to ramp up or scale down quickly based on demand to give customers the same high level of service; 3Par’s storage machines are made for that kind of system.

Both HP and Dell can afford to continue bidding. Even though Dell is half HP’s size, Dell has almost as much cash as HP. Dell reported $12.4 billion in cash and short-term investments at the end of last quarter. HP had $14.7 billion.

Shares of Dell, which is based in Round Rock, Tex., gained 14 cents, or 1.8 percent, to close at $11.89. Shares of HP, which is based in Palo Alto, Calif., declined 22 cents, or 0.6 percent, to $38.
