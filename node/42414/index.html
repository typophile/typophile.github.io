Inter-Pond Typography

February 24, 2008 • Ehague

Some publishers take it upon themselves, when publishing texts in America previously published in the UK, to convert UK typography and style to American. This usually means swapping out single quotes for double quotes, “favourites” for “favorites,” and a host of other, more subtle substitutions (such as “Philosopher’s” for “Sorcerer’s”).

I’m wondering if anyone here is privy to how this conversion is done. I imagine it’s done these days via a long, possibly scripted, find-and-replace protocol, but I’m curious to know the real answer.

Shouldn’t this be trans-pond?

2008-02-24 10:27:41 • Jens Kutilek

It’s called the philosopher’s stone everywhere in the English-speaking world, including the US, so the changed title for the first book in the Harry Potter series has nothing to do with US-UK differences. It’s just a marketing thing. Supposedly, the US publisher worried that a book with the word ‘philosopher’ in the title would not sell well. It’s actually quite common for the same book to be published under different titles in the US and UK markets.

The UK-US conversion process could be automated to some degree, but someone versed in the stylistic differences really should go over the work manually. If you want the vocabulary differences to be addressed (opposite meanings of the word ‘to table’, for instance), there’s no way to automate that. The changes made in the US version of the early Harry Potter books, such as replacing ‘jumpers’ with ‘sweaters’, were probably done by human editors just going through the whole text.

There are also slightly different conventions in mathematical notation, abbreviations, and the like. Grep searches will be helpful in those cases, I imagine.
