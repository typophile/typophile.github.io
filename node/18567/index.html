From Scangraphic’s blurb at FontShop.com:

“Scangraphic offers an extensive range of fonts, all available in two special Scangraphic design formats called “Bodytext” and “Headline”.

Scangraphic Bodytext (“SB”) faces are ideal for text in small point sizes. The hairlines and serifs of the Bodytext faces are thicker than normal hairlines and serifs to make them more legible in fine print. In addition to this, they have a special ink-traps incorporated in the font design to prevent blurring and loss of print quality.

The Scangraphic Headline (“SH”) faces are made for text in large point sizes. Scangraphic optimized the Headline fonts specifically for this purpose, for example, by narrowing the spacing of the fonts and re-designing the umlauts in order to make them work better aesthetically with the overall design.”

2006-03-15 22:07:23 • Miss Tiffany

The body/headline versions differ only in spacing but then there’s two different digital releases: from E+F and from Scangraphic. Both versions come from the same Ikarus data but were converted into PostScript using different tools, and have received different spacing and kerning. I have recently checked both versions – IMO, the kerning of the E+F versions is better than that from Scangraphic.

2006-03-17 01:00:49 • twardoch
Today Sans and Cronos have very similar proportions and basic lettershapes, such that a page of Today Sans and a page of Cronos will have the same overall colour and typographic texture. But the treatment of the detailing is very different, and the larger the type size the more obvious the difference in flavour this produces. Cronos is softer, Today Sans is more formal. Together, they present an interesting example of the degree to which detail treatment contributes to the individual character of two typefaces whose basic structure is almost identical.
