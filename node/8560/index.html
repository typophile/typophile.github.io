> if typography has been done well, the reader won't notice it

That's not 100% true (but what is?…):
1. There are different layers of transparency, and those who take the Morison doctrine too seriously only see one layer, and as a result they think that something is either readable, or worthless. For one thing, there is a difference between the conscious appreciation versus the subconscious effects of type.
2. It's not inconceivable that a reader can consciously appreciate a letterform, and this could add to the experience*. Yes, this ruins immersion during reading, but immersion isn't everything.

* And reading -like anything else humans do- is an experience, not just some data-collection task. Tasks are for computers.

> *Why bother?*

Good question. Increasingly, type design is about personal satisfaction, not some glorious role in society.

> would the standard reader tell the difference?

Not consciously (and in part, they shouldn't anyway). But the intelligent graphic designer knows the value of new fonts, or fonts with particular "moods".

2002-01-15 17:11:23 • Miss Tiffany
> Does that mean [Times] is now a 'poor' design?

I'm fond of saying that a design is only truly good for an instant in time.

Of course that's an over-simplification, but it's just as true as claiming that some designs are eternal.

What makes the evaluation of a typeface over time very tricky is the unavoidable role of the technology/medium. For example, Times was great for its original target reproduction conditions, but when its digital form is printed today, it might look OK on a laser printer (with the associated "ink" gain), but in high-quality offset it's too light, and too contrasty. So, which one do we judge?

In the end, we end up having to ask: what *is* Times? Is it the collection of all the versions of it (ignoring for the moment the naming issues), or is it a necessarily nebulous *concept* that -to some degree- has to be evaluated irrespective of any instance(s)?

By the same truth, ie readers can "feel" the difference, we know that they can also tell the difference between good type and bad, as well as good setting and bad, and good printing an bad.

It's not a "consumer's" position to articulate "why" they may find some settings easier or more difficult to read any more so than it's up to them to ascertain why the ride in one car is smoother/surer than another - that's the professional's job.

Your insistance on linguistic study is also a non-starter. I don't know any type designer that isn't fluent in their own language and that of typography - they couldn't do that job otherwise. Considering the colision of likely letter pairs isn't really an issue, because type designers consider *all* letter pairs (ie you make up test sheets of all posible combinations, which in the age of the intercap would include cap-lower-cap sets).

The only linguistics that may come into this process are whether a certain pair may be "useful" or whether a ligature is worth making (ie fffl does occur in German, but because of the grammar/structure used wouldn't be set as a four character ligature).

That's for Western European Roman anyway. Where designers are unfamiliar with accented characters in Central/Estern European they would seek help and reference from people with more knowledge in those areas. The same is true of those I have known who have completed Greek or Cyrillic versions of their fonts.

However, the questions here are not of linguistics, but of established style and practice.

As to Michael's original question, yes of course you should look at any typeface that you are developing in a composed form. It's so easy to do today that I'm amazed that one would even consider not doing so.

The point of a typeface in the end is not to define individually perfect looking letters - because you must end up compromising on that anyway - but to develop a set of characters that work well together in text (or display).

Building a typeface character by character is as flawed as stealing parts out of the GM plant and trying to build a Cadillac bit by bit over time (ie the trunk of a '57 with the fender of a '63…)
