I have been approached to layout a book in French and I was wondering if there is anything special I should think about before I accept or decline the project. My wife speaks french and was the editor of the english version of the book in question. She will be helping me if I accept the project. What should I know about layout in French that differs from what I know about laying out a book in English?

Thanks.

Traditionally, le sommaire (the brief list of contents) is part of the prelims, whereas la table des matières (the detailed list of contents) can be found at the end of a book. There are exceptions, though.

2006-01-29 15:05:36 • JoshW

Josh, with all due respect, you may not be designing the book from scratch, but you should still be concerned with the content, IMHO.

In addition to Maxim’s and William’s suggestions, I would add that, according to Robert Bringhurst’s The Elements of Typographic Style, French hyphenation is different (at least sometimes) from English hyphenation. Perhaps there are French hyphenation dictionaries or plug-ins for the program you will be using to lay out the text.

Also, if I am not mistaken, the text on the spine of French books traditionally runs from the bottom up rather than from the top down.

2006-01-30 05:40:24 • Ricardo Cordoba

The biggest thing you will need to address is the hyphenation but I’m not sure how to deal with that - I have traditionally been concerned with the other way around (translations into English).

I agree with Ricardo that you do have to pay attention to the content but your wife should be able to help with that.

The french also use different symbols for dialogue but I assume that will be in the manuscript and not something you will need to format. Also, I find a lot of french publishers don’t indent paragraphs, but I think that’s weird. I have also learned that European book design to a large extent is far plainer and less decorative than American. I have no idea why this is or why it should be.
