Adding glyphs to Times New Roman

December 5, 2005 • Shai

Hello all,

I’m a student of Semitic languages, and when I’m writig papers to my teachers, I need to use, every now and then, characters which are not found in the normal set of characters; e.g. “p” with a macron, or “a” with a macron and a ring.

I’ve added these to my Times New Roman font using a font editor. Now my teacher(s) were very enthousiastic, and asked me to put the font on the internet.

My question is this: suppose I delete all original glyphs of TNR, and include in the font only my changed glyphs (“p” with a macron etc.) - will that still be unethical to put the font on the net? Should I ask someone’s permission to do this?

I want to stress,

  1. that certain glyphs we use at the university are very unique, they don’t have even a unicode number, so they can not be purchased.
  2. that all added glyphs are based on TNR original glyphs: I just added the macron above the “p”, etc.

Thanks, Shai

Shai,

you should not use Times New Roman since it’s copyrighted. However, there are several different opensource font projects that “look like Times”.

The most ambitious seems to be the Savannah Free UCS Outline fonts: http://www.nongnu.org/freefont/

Their FreeSerif font is based on the GPL version of Nimbus Roman, which is a Times clone donated to the opensource community by URW++. Nimbus Roman is also included in GPL GhostScript: http://www.ghostscript.com/doc/GPL/index.htm

But since the FreeSerif project is more advanced, you may want to contribute your changes there.

Also, owners of Fontlab Ltd. products can use the FreeSerif font (unrelated to the previous one) that we offer on our site (http://www.fontlab.com/ ). There are no licensing limitations for use of the font, you can modify it as you wish and distribute it under any terms.

Regards, Adam
