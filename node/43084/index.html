I am flattered by all this attention, FDRC is a collective of foundries, individuals and others concerned that the DCMA laws that apply to commercial software are also being applied to fonts. If you have any questions, would like to use our services or report a shared commercial font, please do not hestiate to contact me directly either by email janet@fdrc.org or clicking on the link on our site: piracy@fdrc.org.

All my best, Janet Moore FDRC

2008-03-12 21:33:54 • Si_Daniels

If you follow any of the warez sites you will know that anyone who comes out against software sharing is seen as the enemy.

Right. If you are victimized, it’s OK to say “please don’t hit me again”, but anything stronger is asking for more beats–because you can’t rely on the authorities to protect you. Hence the softly-softly tactic. It’s sad, but what else can you do? It would be unrealistic to imagine that a well-publicized anti-piracy campaign with industry-wide support would have any effect, seeing what has happened with music downloads.

Another approach is to ignore the swarm of gnats, and make a better product that people will want to buy rather than rip off. And write easier-to-understand EULAS. And provide consumer education. But all these are happening too, and no doubt pirates find the better products to their liking, and the attempts at reforming their mind-set very amusing.

The problem is intractable, as DTP is predicated on easy-to-copy font formats.

“Is the massive sharing of commercial film, television and music files predicated on ignorance of their value?”

Let’s not presume that the general public has anywhere near the awareness of type that they have of film and music media. The general idea in the states is that those media do have value, but that some people enjoy somehow getting free access to them. By contrast, fonts are just there, they grow on trees or are made by machines, and have no value because you can get them for free. Note: I’m not saying this difference exists, I’m saying the attitude, and the awareness it comes from, is very different. Do you not see that?

2008-03-17 07:00:51 • Miss Tiffany

Is the massive sharing of commercial film, television and music files predicated on ignorance of their value?

Yes. People (present company excluded, of course) have no concept of what anything is worth. Blame the Wal-Mart gallon-of-pickles, executive compensation packages, and font bundling.

But really, what does it matter what anything’s worth? Smart people are the ones who get as much as possible for as little as possible, without getting caught, so why pay for something if you don’t have to? Why bother to even think about what it’s worth?

I was talking about fonts with a freelance graphic designer I bumped into at a party, and asked him where he got his from. He said a buddy of his gave him a CD full. I’m pretty sure he had no idea how many fonts there were on it, or what it would cost to licence them, and didn’t care. He knew I was a type designer but it didn’t phase him–probably didn’t make the connection that he may have been using my fonts for free.
