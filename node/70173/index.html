Use-based licensing. Is it more work? Or good business sense?

May 6, 2010 • Miss Tiffany

This morning I received an email from Stone Type Foundry announcing their new licensing model to be rolled out on June 1st. My first thought was, “Sounds like a lot of more work.” But then I thought, “well I have been telling designers to work directly with foundries for years.” So I emailed Sumner and asked him for a little more information. He replied saying:

“The license will be tailored to each project, and the fee will depend on the intended use of the fonts. Each use or category of use will be licensed separately. The licensing fee is intended to be realistic in terms of the value it the font adds to the product. A logotype is one thing, a book, or book title another."

In my opinion this makes a lot of sense. And is much more user-friendly. I can see how it might be a lot more work for the larger foundry, but for a boutique/smaller it makes good sense.

The license will be tailored to each project, and the fee will depend on the intended use of the fonts

First reaction: that’s how I license fonts all the time :-) and it’s often a lot of work for me, the customer - like buying a car from a second hand car dealer vs going to Starbucks.

Second reaction: this model really only works if the customer has easy access to evaluation versions of the fonts.

2010-05-08 21:56:26 • barkeep

As a designer who works with a variety of clients (including some large corporations), I would not license a font with use-based licensing. Sometimes one project will be used in the future as the basis for another project, and I might not know up front that that will be the case. The second project may not come for months or years. I simply don’t want to have to worry about accidentally using a font against the EULA, and I don’t want my client to have the perception that I am nickel-and-diming them by raising my price to cover extra licensing when they already licensed a font once.

I usually license fonts for myself without charging the client (especially if it is a font I can use again). However, use-based licensing would mean I would have to charge the client since the font could only be used on that one project. My projects are not usually big enough to absorb an extra licensing fee.

If the client made a special request for a font I wouldn’t turn them down, but I’d make sure they understood the licensing ramifications. Same with Rights Managed images. These days, I rarely recommend a rights managed image to a client, and if I do (or they request one) I make sure they understand exactly what that means. I’ve had some unpleasant conversations when a client asks to use an image in a second piece, and I have to remind them that they will need to pay to license the image again.

I do feel the same way about EULAs that forbid pdf embedding. I never know when a client will say, “Oh, yes, and please also make a pdf we can post on the web.” or “We decided that we’ll be putting that print ad in our email newsletter.”

2010-05-09 19:48:17 • Gary Long

If a designer really finds dealing with case-by-case font licensing terribly challenging he probably needs to find a line of work that is not so intellectually trying.

If they don’t like case-by-case licensing, they just won’t buy your fonts. No need for them to go find another line of work, unless and until case-by-case licensing becomes the norm.

Designers who can handle today’s existing EULAs well may very well be able to deal with case-by-case licensing well, too. The already-existing and larger problem, IMO, is that most designers do not in fact deal with the differences in today’s licenses very well. Most just pay the license fee and then do with the fonts whatever they think is covered by the license, or ought to be.

Therefore, I don’t think this majority of today’s users/buyers are going to both license and pay on a case-by-case basis. To me the question is, will they just license other fonts in the first place, or license and neglect to pay for future use?

Of course, if the licensors of your particular fonts are a specialized elite who “get” font licensing and have no problems with this stuff, then maybe it will work fine as a business model. But I don’t expect this model to take over the font world in a hurry.
