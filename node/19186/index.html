Hi all I'm currently cutting some characters for a translation of Frege's Grundgesetze and one of them, a variant infinity, has me completely baffled. Has anyone seen anything like it before? http://miles.shef.ac.uk/~jjg/infinity.jpg [I tried attaching the image but got "File copy failed: no directory configured, or it could not be accessed"] I'm tempted to think that this was something designed by Frege himself and cut by his printer (Hermann Pohl, Jenna, 1893); note the asymmetry in the overbar, the uniform width of the bowl strokes. But Frege took great care in the typography of the Grundgesetze (and paid for its publication himself) so surely this cannot be the case. Any pointers would be most gratefully received!

I know enough logic to be dangerous, but I am not familiar with Frege's notations, especially as originally published. My undereducated guess is that this is a stylized lowercase \omega with a wide inverted breve above. If I were drawing it, I'd use the same ductus (or lack thereof) as the \union sign. The part below might indeed by a double\union, but if it does mean some kind of infinity, \omega makes more sense as it is often used as an infinity sign. I wouldn't worry about the asymmetry - if it had real meaning, I doubt it would be done that subtly. What methodology are you going to use to draw your fonts? If it's not too many glyphs and you don't have a strong attachment to doing it all yourself, I might be tempted to pitch in.
