2004-10-11 21:07:29 • hrant
hrant: that's a complex question. The short answer is that you can go from Cornu spiral to quadratic or cubic Bezier with insignificant loss, as long as you have enough precision in the coordinates [in fact, my interactive editor converts to Beziers before rendering to the screen]. So there's no problem outputting PostScript code that has high-precision float coordinates followed by "curveto" operators, but an unsophisticated transform to TrueType or Type1 can introduce significant errors.

So, in order for this stuff to be useful for type, the output module has to be smart enough to avoid those errors (briefly, very short arcs are the enemy). Of course, the exact same thing is true of stroke offset algorithms (the constant-distance offset curve from a quad bezier is a complex fifth-order polynomial that can get quite numerically snarly where the offset radius gets close to the curvature of the Bezier).

2004-10-14 19:17:14 • aaron_carambula
Why the Cornu spiral as opposed to other spirals? Good luck with the dissertation!

Thanks! That's a good question. I'll try a brief answer.

1. It's one of the few spirals that has an inflection point in it, or in other words contains an "S" curve. Most other spirals only wind one way.

2. In fact, it's got a number of useful shapes hiding in there. At the inflection point (t=0), it closely fits a basic cubic polynomial (essentially the same curve you get when trying to make an "S" in a cubic Bezier). At roughly t=0.6, it closely fits a parabola, which is the geometric primitive of a quadratic Bezier (TrueType). And as t increases, it closely fits a circle.

3. It's the smoothest way to interpolate between curvatures. In fact, designers of roller coasters and highway on/off ramps use the curve to smooth out the lateral g-forces. If you were to ride a motorcycle along, say, the 's' from Auto Italic 3, you'd be in for some rather violent shifts of g-force.

4. It has deeply beautiful mathematical qualities. The more I study the curve, the stronger my intuitive feeling of rightness. For the details, you'll have to wait for my thesis (how's that for a cliffhanger?).

I should have linked a PDF of the Cornu spiral, so that you can see the curve I'm talking about.

Type design does expose materials and methods. To me, cubic Beziers are like carving letterforms into rough pine wood with knots (pun intended, of course), while Cornu spirals are like carving into pear wood. If you're going for an effect, the former makes sense, but given the choice, most of the time a seasoned craftsman will go for the latter.

2004-10-14 19:53:12 • William Berkson
It sounds like the Cornu spiral is more complex than a cubic equation.

It depends on what you mean by "complex". Beziers can be computed using only high school algebra, while the mathematical basis of the Cornu spiral is the Fresnel integral, a "special function" that requires a bit more mathematical skill to work with.

But if you're talking about complexity from the user's point of view, Cornu splines win hands down. Once you nail down the endpoints, a Cornu spiral segment has only two degrees of freedom, the same as a quadratic Bezier (and two less than a cubic). In short, it's as simple to use as a quadratic, but every bit as powerful as a cubic.

Of course, the real test is how natural it all feels in the context of an interactive editor. I've built one (for Linux only, so far), and so far am very encouraged. I'm eager to get it in the hands of typophiles (as opposed to math nerds) so I can get good feedback from the real world.

2004-10-14 23:26:06 • hrant
And with slope continuity (with adjacent segments) at those endpoints?

Right. Enforcing the tangent continuity across a knot point eats up one degree of freedom. The best representation, then, for a manually drawn Cornu spline is a "spinner" for the tangent angle at each knot point. Using an off-curve point instead of the tangent angle has the drawback of not handling "S" curves well; the two tangents could well be parallel to each other, so they would never intersect.

The curve editor I'm working on assigns the tangent angles automatically, as well, so all the user needs to put in are the on-curve points. I'm going to make the tangent spinner optional at each knot; it seems to me especially useful at corner joins and joins with straight line segments.

At knots where the tangent is chosen automatically, there is curvature continuity as well as tangent continuity. My sense is that when typophiles talk about the "bezier-y" feel of an outline, it corresponds fairly closely to the mathematical concept of disconuities (or wide variations) in curvature. Cornu splines are extremely good with respect to that criterion. Perhaps even optimum; it's a question I want to study.

Maybe the Cornu spiral will seem so natural that one will get a good result with only the one point in addition to the end points, but I wonder.

That is more or less my claim. Since the concepts of "seeming natural" and "good result" are so subjective, I intend to test that claim by putting the curve editor into people's hands.

Can you elaborate how you can transform spiral segments? Do you use 3D perspective transformations, for example?

No, it's simpler than that. I just cut a piece of the original spiral (the two degrees of freedom mentioned above correspond exactly to the points along the original curve where I cut), then scale, rotate and translate so that the endpoints of the spiral section coincide with the desired endpoints.
