2014-05-21 21:12:59 • stinkyfoot
Mike, not really a Clarendon, more like an emaciated version of the slab serif that we now label as Egyptian but was labelled as Antique in the 19th century. Stinkyfoot, Marder developed this font but others would have copied it by a process known as electrotyping and sold it as their own. A physical process similar to the electronic ones now used for stealing digital fonts and selling the clones. Font forgery was thriving then as now. As for the variation in the g, as I mentioned in my post this is most likely due to the fact that Marder almost certainly still cut the different font sizes by hand in 1887. Identity between different font sizes became possible with invention of the font pantograph by Linn Boyd Benton http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linn_Boyd_Benton which gave his company, which went on to be part of the core of ATF an edge over competitors like Marder which did not adopt the machine until a few years later. BTW this is not to say that Marder felt at some stage that the g was sufficiently off that it should be recut. Or someone tinkered with their stolen version. Don

Popular metal types were often recut by woodtype foundries. Marder’s Skeleton Antique No. 2 received this treatment. The result is included in Flat-it’s digital Dharma and Rama Slab families http://www.myfonts.com/fonts/flat-it/dharma-slab/ http://www.myfonts.com/fonts/flat-it/rama-slab/ Stroke widths, serif thicknesses and distances are more regularized than the MVB interpretation, but the condensed versions of Dharma and Rama Slab are very similar to Skeleton Antique No. 2. Dharma Slab has seven weights. Rama Slab has six weights. Could be tinkered with to make different weights of Skeleton Antique No. 2. Don
