Number Sign vs. Sharp sign

Does anyone know what the difference is between a number sign (# - Unicode 0023) and a sharp sign (Unicode 266F)? I've found references to the two symbols being similar but slightly different, but I haven't found a font that actually has a sharp sign.
The sharp sign is the musical sharp that one sees on sheet music. You’ll probably only see it in fonts made to use with music software.

2008-10-01 16:50:35 • blank
As with any other character, you can't absolutely define how a sharp should look, but it will always have these characteristics: the two horizontal lines are slanted upwards (they would straddle the stave lines if preceding a note which is in a stave space, otherwise they would fall between two stave lines); the two vertical lines are not slanted, and the rightmost of the two is slightly higher (matching the slant of the vertical lines); also the vertical lines are thin whereas the horizontal lines are thickened. The space between the vertical lines should also be less than that between the horizontal lines, somewhat. If designing a sharp for a font especially for use in music (and most such fonts are really awful) then there would be other things to take into account, such as how long the verticals should be. (I realise, of course, that this doesn't really answer your primary question of how it differs from a #, but you have that answer already) :)

2008-10-01 18:24:15 • cerulean
The sharp and flat can be useful in texts that make reference to works of classical music. Most often the key signature is spelled out completely. Some styles will prefer to employ the symbolic abbreviation. In casual use, the number sign can stand in for the sharp. Less convincingly, the lowercase b has been pressed into service in place of the flat -- e.g., Concerto for Horn and Orchestra No. 3 in Eb major, K. 447. I've been considering including the sharp and flat in the OT version of Whitman, as well as other designs in progress. -- K.
