2007-12-17 01:06:03 • John Nolan

While it is from a slightly earlier time, Monticello would be appropriate, and better, I think, than Walbaum or Bodoni for the time, place, & content.

Matthew Carter made the PostScript version of Monticello for Princeton University Press, as it had been used in their Thoreau series. This was done in partnership with Linotype as they had done some preliminary digitization.

The fonts are available from Carter & Cone, or I believe Linotype. The C&C has all the goodies, but as it is a Type 1 font, they’re spread out across multiple fonts. Monticello is essentially the same as Oxford, and could be thought of as a Scotch.

2007-12-17 23:25:07 • jupiterboy

Also take a look at Oldstyle #7.

& don’t get too bothered if a face hasn’t got small caps. They were not that common at the time, and I bet the guys down in Lamoni did not have them. Few Midwest country printing shops had them even by the 1960s, I can tell you that from experience.

& Monticello for sure; look at that.

But also make sure you have asked yourself how important it is that the job be set in a “historically-tenable font.” I don’t mean you should set it in just anything, but think about the purpose of your work. And the audience for your work.
