What makes expensive books smell so good?

November 29, 2007 • blank

What gives expensive, well-printed book that incredible luscious smell? You know what I mean—that soft odor of luxury and petroleum that comes out of every single book printed in Belgium, most of the books from France, Italy, and Japan, and not a damned thing printed in the USA. Is it the inks, the paper, the glue, or just some tangible residue of the dedication of insanely dedicated pressmen?

not a damned thing printed in the USA.

What is printed in the USA in this day and age? Maybe tax forms and loto tickets if you’re lucky.

2007-11-29 02:06:50 • Si_Daniels
Expensive Printing Smell. This is a spray on product manufactured in Japan and, under license, in Germany, by the same people who invented New Car Smell. The US imposes heavy tariffs on products whose only purpose is to produce an odour, which is why a lot of European perfumes are also not available in the USA or are very expensive. I believe this is due to the Hollywood lobby, which wants Americans only to purchase perfumes ‘designed’ by American celebrities. Unfortunately, Expensive Printing Smell got caught in the same classification. American used car dealers use a knock off of New Car Smell, but no one in the USA has succeeded in replicating the scent of the highly secret ingredients in Expensive Printing Smell, although some vegetable based inks have been found to have powerful if often unpleasant odours.

2007-11-29 08:26:09 • David Rault

Paper quality is also quite important. Cheap paper stinks. But in most cases publishers are not willing to charge 5-10% more for a book that is printed on good (or at least decent) quality paper.

Klaus Wagenbach, a publisher who has founded the Wagenbach Verlag in Germany, was famous for tasting the paper before printing his books on it. Wagenbach books are arguably the nicest books produced in Germany - and yes, these books are a bit more expensive than the average books, but it’s a pleasure to read them.
