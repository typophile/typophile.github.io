2008-05-31 23:23:26 • elsuertudo

Anatomy of a typeface is a very interesting book, but obviously out of date - though Lawson (who died a couple of years ago, RIP) has indeed some interesting things to say about sans serif as well. The book unfortunately is a no-design: mainly text, no layout, few images printed in a low quality… you might hold on if you already have some knowledge in type, otherwise you’ll sleep after a dozen pages.

The two best books i’ve ever read about history of type are unfortunately (for you) in french, but they are worth learning the language because they don’t have any equivalent in english, both written by yves perrousseaux: “manuel de typographie elementaire” and especially “histoire de la typographie”, a huge encyclopedia about the history of typography, large format, nicely laid out, with an unbelievable amount of historical facts and references and filled with very high quality scans of terrific documents. volume one is out since last year, volume two is due to be released in september 2009. A must have, really. If you know any serious publisher in the US or UK, please try to convince him to translate this book. You won’t regret it.

Lawson’s book may not be the flashiest book on type ever made but it really helps in understanding the history of type and how and why it evolved, if you are really interested on that and not only on identifying type styles and names give it a second tought. On the other hand it really is a text heavy book and you have to read it on episodes. Also there is no information of modern type styles on the book.

By the way, Univers might be misplaced on that genealogy (AG->H->U). If I my memory doesn’t fail both faces were released almost the same year or so. Univers was inovative on the concept and execution of a type family as a system and performed better as text face, Helvetica on the other hand was a restyling of AG and performs better as a display face.

2008-06-02 20:46:24 • Hector Muñoz

Héctor: “there is no information of modern type styles on the book”.

well, it depends what you call “modern”, if it is contemporary of course not, for the book was published a long time ago and the author died in 2002; but there is indeed some info about sans serif types from the 20th century, and even if not a lot, it is quite interesting though.

Helvetica and Univers were indeed released the same year. The actual genealogy would be something like:

William Caslon IV egyptian > Breite Grotesk > Royal Grotesk > Accidenz / Akzidenz Grotesk > Neue Haas Grotesk / Helvetica + Univers + Folio

not to mention all the revivals, inspirations, redesigns… including Standard / Basic Commercial, Arial, FF Schulbuch, Paralucent, Monotype Grotesque, FF Bau, Preface… and countless others.
