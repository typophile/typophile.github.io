2010-03-26 09:37:32 • Vladimir Tamari

@ altaira: Calibration refers to how accurately it can follow your eyes. None of the equipment I have used, or seen used, is capable of this accuracy and they involve elaborate headgear, head mounted cameras for motion detection, chin rests &c. I have seen a few that do not use head-gear and while easier to set up, are even less accurate. And in order to maintain accuracy over extended periods of time, they need to be re-adjusted and re-calibrated.

Additionally, some won’t work with glasses, some won’t work with contact lenses, certain eye shapes which cover more of the pupil make calibration harder and some will only work under certain lighting conditions. I once ran a participant who had been “smoking” before he came in, and wasn’t able to calibrate him at all.

Our lab will be testing a new system, EyeLink 1000, so perhaps this will be more accurate. I’ll need to spend a bit more time investigating the technology behind this story and cross-reference it’s technical specs, but for the price, I’d be very surprised to see if it can accurately measure eye movements on type set at 12/14.

But I must agree. I am very intrigued by this.

2010-04-01 17:11:34 • nadine_chahine

Our lab now has access to Eyelink 1000. An impressive piece of hardware. I hope to be taking out for a test drive in the next few weeks. It has two options: with or without a chin-rest. With the chin-rest it is significantly more accurate than our older Eyelink II. Without the chin-rest it’s about the same.

But here’s the best part: No headgear! That means easier and faster calibration as well as the ability to run longer experiments. With head-mounted gear you have to make it fairly tight on the participants head in order to avoid drift and after 30 minutes it can become quite uncomfortable to wear.

I’m still waiting for an opportunity to use our tachistoscope.
