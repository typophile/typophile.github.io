Why are some papers measured in weight and some in the ppi thickness? If a paper supplier doesn’t say what the paper is to be used for but only lists the gsm, how do you know if it is for text/cover/etc.? How do you match a bond, text, and cover grade for suitable collateral publications?

At least gsm (grams per square meter) is an objective measure that doesn’t vary based on it’s purported use. Beyond that you have to get to know each paper for its material content, surface, folding, cutting, tearing, and ink carrying properties. The name on a paper only gives a vague clue as to it’s usefulness for a particular purpose. You can have a hundred different “Bristols” and have the only thing they have in common be that they are semi-rigid paperboards. If you find a paper sample that you think will work for the task you intend to put it to, don’t worry about what it’s called.

Hey Jesse I notice on the other post that you have found M-real, who are very cool. Talk to the paper suppliers – it’s in their interest to educate you, and they oftentimes have truckloads of good informative (and, natch, promotional) reference material. Mohawk recently sponsored an exhibition all the way out here in NZ!

The design books often have background (it’s out of print I think, but The Designers' Handbook by Alistair Campbell, pub. McDonald Orbis, was always good on this) but nothing beats talking to a paper suppliers sales rep., preferably an old one. I never liked caliper measurements for paper anyway.

Watson Guptill, 1990. Has an entire chapter on paper and some useful definitions.

Look also for <a/href=“http://www.amazon.com/Production-Graphic-Designers-Alan-Pipes/dp/013194049X/sr=1-1/qid=1159329421/ref=pd_bbs_1/104-1948337-6139108?ie=UTF8&s=books"> Alan Pipes' Production for Graphic Designers (fourth edition) which is much more recent. Adobe also used to publish a Guide to Professional PrePress, which I haven’t seen in a while.

I say this because the titles by Craig and Campbell date from the late 80s/early 90s. The big deal then was about whether or not the paper surface could hold a conventional halftone dot; now that we have first-generation digital platemaking, stochastic screening and computer-controlled on-press densitometers, this is maybe less of a consideration, and people just expect to get better reproduction on even ordinary stocks.
