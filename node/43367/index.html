If you can avoid the bar and set it off different it's better. If there is no dominant language, if you can use two opposing emphasis techniques to distinguish but not give one importance it's better. Say for instance, making one bold and one normal, but then making the bold one gray and the normal one black so they end up standing out about the same. «El futuro es una línea tan fina que apenas nos damos cuenta de pintarla nosotros mismos». Canada is a bad choice. In Quebec, the most bilingual of the bilingual provinces, English is not treated equally. French must be larger and (I think) to the right or above. In the other bilingual provinces (Ontario, New Brunswick, Manitoba, Saskatchewan, I think) English is the dominant language although it gets fair treatment with French. Our federal government tends to try to make the department names similar in both languages, and if not, shows them side by side (order can be exchanged, depending on the predominant language where the sign will be). See http://www.tc.gc.ca/en/menu.htm and http://www.tc.gc.ca/fr/menu.htm
