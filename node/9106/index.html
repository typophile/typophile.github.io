2005-02-07 15:21:08 • jupiterboy
Nick is right. We did everything by hand in those days. It wasn't so much that we were "groovy and doin' our thing." There wasn't much choice. There were no computers and the normal way of working was to draw everything. Type was indicated by drawing it. You only had type set after the job was approved and ready for production. There were nowhere-near the type choices we have currently. Brush and paint, pen and ink, exacto knife and rubylith were the skills we practiced. Today, tools of the trade are software programs and type choices are broad and very cheap. You can digitally manipulate anything in a snap now. In the 60's what you may perceive as type manipulation was actually drawn, Photostatted, cut&pasted, retouched film-neg'd, opaqued and stripped with colors hand registered on a light box. We sent a man to the Moon then but designers were strictly low-tech.

2005-02-07 18:31:47 • hrant
>Would a bluegrass band from the 60's have a psychedelic album cover?<

No, not very likely. The 60's had a rather broad range of design. It was much more eclectic than some people assume. You had everything from Ben Shahn, Herb Luballin, Seymoor Schwast more illustative style in contrast to the modernist AG Helvetica International style. There was also, in advertising, what I used to call "The Typeface of the Month Club" style" where a typeface would become "in" and be overused for everything (Cooper Black had a longer stay than most.) and then die a quick death in disdain. Psychedelic was actually not all that popular beyond the acid rock youth. Peter Max posters had a fling in the late 60's but most truly psychadelic stuff was done by hand by college students.
A bluegrass band would have had more of a straightforward homegrown look. Maybe with hand written title or something more country looking–anything from Clarendon caps to a wooden type look.
