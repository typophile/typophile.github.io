2006-05-22 13:40:38 • Geoff Riding

Except for the modern links above, you might have found a slim topic. I grew up around Norwegian-Americans but none of them designed fonts. Even a Norwegian type designer is going to be challenged to single out (cultural?) “values” that can be translated into a unique typeface. Besides this, most typefaces are designed to express something in a particular kind of document, or something slightly less broad than a national “something”. Even if one were trying to compose an exclusively “Norwegian-looking” document of some kind, one would need to travel back to a time before the Latin alphabet was use in order to be clear of Sweden, Denmark, or Spiekermann.

Now in Boston, we have a single letter, a “B”, in a specific font, that sums the whole culture up. NY, being twice as mouthy and not nearly as discerning, needs two letters and the font doesn’t matter. Most other places, Seattle, the Bahamas or Milwaukee, e.g. have nothing much to show for themselves typographically.

2006-05-24 19:12:27 • Conor

Henrik,

Perhaps you could focus some of your research on how Norway has used design to project a national image to an international audience.

This would include the Norwegian tourist authority and, obviously, the design work done for the Lillehammer Olympics.

It might be interesting to speak with some of the people involved in these campaigns, because they had to deal with the very issue in which you are interested, although embracing issues beyond typography.

As a balance, it might be usefull to look at trends in Norwegian art, writing, music and film, as they relate to popular culture.

This idea that a particular culture can be captured in type is interesting. I believe that there is some truth to it, but that it is an elusive idea among countries that share a common cultural heritage.

I assume that you have seen Kitchen Stories, which would be an interesting, and amusing, point of embarkation for a project like this if you want to be whimsical.

Good luck.
