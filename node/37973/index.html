Making stem width appear consistent

October 11, 2007 • Capnhud

I have been reading the Logo, Font and Lettering Bible and was wondering how do font designers make stem widths appear consistent. For example a vertical stem that is 4px wide 20px high may look okay but if you rotate it so that it is horizontal the stem appears optically thicker, but in reality it is not. How do some of you correct for this?
The simple answer is, make the horizontal version thinner. There's probably a formula for this, but I just eyeball it. Typefaces are full of optical corrections like this, and a good lettering artist will make them almost unconciously when drawing letters manually.

I think type designers can sometimes exaggerate the amount of difference needed to counteract the optical illusion. This is because we spend so much time looking at perpendicular strokes that are unequal, and imagining them as equal, that when we look at perpendicualr strokes that ARE equal, we imagine them as being unequal in the opposite manner to our habit.

It varies with the typeface: Here's how much wider the vertical in the plus sign is than the horizontal,in a sample of typefaces: Gill 1.1%, Georgia 1.6 %, Californian FB 1.6%, ACaslon 6%, Franklin Gothic Book 8.6%, AGaramond Semi-Bold 19%. As you can see there is no hard and fast rule. Because roman faces generally have heavy verticals and lighter horizontals, this also affects what looks 'right', as well as the optical illusion. And then the style of the typeface also affects it. And different glyphs will be different. So as James says it's your design and your eye in the end. A way to get a feel for it is to look at other designs in a font editor, and compare them, as well as drawing your own.
