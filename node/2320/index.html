First of all, I found your g William! Read on ... forgive the rant.

I've got to speak up here. If somebody who knew no better were to
read this thread as it stands, they'd leave thinking that URW is a
scumbag font pirate company. I'm really not trying to be contentious
here, but I'd like to clarify some things.

Yves, you lumped URW into the same paragraph (next sentence,
in fact) with "those quick-steal-jobbies" and then "all they did was
change the most distinctive characters into something else, slant
the roman instead of providing true italics, digitally extrapolate some
extra weights, maybe add an outline or rounded version, and bingo:
1000 fonts for $24.95 whattadeal!". This effectively equates them
with Southern Software or Bay Animation or any of those other crooks
who renamed fonts and sold CD's full of them at a pittance.

Now surely you weren't speaking of URW here. If so, I'd honestly
like to learn what it's based upon. Specifically, URW Agenda has no
italic, much less a false one or outline or rounded variants. I'm not
aware of them doing this with other typefaces either.

Secondly, Clearface Gothic is attributed to Morris Fuller Benton. URW
also offers Clearface Gothic and gives proper attribution to Benton,
1906. So why offer Agenda as well, if it's meant as a thinly veiled clone?
Agenda is attributed to Phil Martin, who, prior reading has led me to
believe, was no slouch when it came to designing at least a few well
known and well used fonts in an earlier era. See here especially. First
hit the Font History button and be ready to hold your cursor over the
Stop button in this awkward presentation (at least Agenda is the 2nd
font shown). Mr. Martin makes a statement much like Mike's about the
letter 'g'. Now hit the Font Graphic button and behold the very 'g' thought
to be "homemade".

So, Martin takes a 1906 typeface and, in 1976, makes several significant
modifications to it. Absolutely he ought to have credited Benton. Two years
later, Victor Caruso takes that same old typeface and digitizes it.

My beef here is that, while I detest the folks like Southern Software who
steal, rename and sell, there are so many gray areas to this topic. What
occurred with Agenda has occurred to varying degrees with many, many
font foundries, some of them large and well respected. An existing typeface
was modified, sometimes precious little and called something new. Even
worse, in my opinion, has been perpetrated: 1 & 2 (Thanks for these articles,
Mark. I'm sorry if you didn't intend them to be used this way).

My intent is definitely not to paint Monotype black, but to show lurkers who
don't know better that ethical gray appears in lots of type foundries and to
call, say, ITC and Monotype the good guys and, say, URW and Bitstream
the bad doesn't do justice to any of them. I've seen, recently, Bitstream,
Corel and, now, URW labelled flatly as fiends, as if it's a known fact

My understanding of many of the URW fonts was that when they had one of the few (along with Bitstream) places where foundries could get typefaces digitized (with their tool Ikarus), they made a deal that they could sell every font they digitized as long as they changes the name (hence all the Latin-sounding names for familiar-looking fonts).

As for the other foundries imitating the fonts of others, this is a practice that goes back to early type history. I would love it if a type historian could unravel some of the 'genealogy' of many of these fonts. Linotype's "Memphis" is an example of a metal type they created in the 30's to compete with Monotype's "Rockwell", so that they could offer the same kind of typefaces to their customers (I think I named the right fonts with their foundries, but apologies if I got it mixed up). The point being, just as in Marks tale of the Arial's of Monotype, that foundries are just as determined to keep abreast of their competition as any other business is. They just copy the work of others and market it at prices that devalue all the other fonts, and give an excuse to folks who think if font companies can be corrupt, why is it wrong to steal fonts.
