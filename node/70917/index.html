2010-06-01 03:06:54 • Frode Bo Helland

does hinting effect fonts displayed in a PDF document?

Yes. Acrobat uses Adobe’s CoolType renderer, rather than system rasterisers, so what you get in PDF is outlines and hints as interpreted by CoolType, which will differ from how the same fonts are rendered by the OS in other applications.

2010-06-01 09:55:18 • Frode Bo Helland

Frode: What happens then?

You get whatever rendering that application is using. It might be a system renderer, or it might be FreeType. It it unlikely to be something custom, particular to the application, but that is also a possibility. The only renderer I am aware of that deliberately ignores all hints is Apple’s Quartz renderer.

I saw this thread and decided to add my two cents to this cauldron of suggestions (even though Lewisham appear to have made his mind up).

I am not adverse to Palatino particuarly, but something feels not quite right about it, and I can’t put my finger on it. The thesis needs to look great on screen and in print (perhaps more so on screen, as I expect that’s how most people will read it), and Palatino seems to do that.

How about using [[http://www.linotype.com/255806/aldusnova-valuepack.html#info|Aldus Nova]]? The com version of Aldus is specifically designed for display work (what Linotype call XSF). Aldus (for text) is to my eyes the better Palatino, and Aldus nova is its definitive digital incarnation. If you use Aldus you could use Palatino as an auxiliary font for the math work and for the display sizes.

I think that both Palatino and Aldus work very well for CS work. I have read a lot of published and unpublished CS related work set in various forms of Palatino, and it appears to me that this seemingly unlikely marriage between typeface and subject matter works quite well…

If on the other hand, you want to go for a more neutral (yet highly readable) look, I would consider using [[http://www.dutchtypelibrary.nl/DTLDocumenta.html|DTL Documenta]]. Although admittedly, it may literally dissolve your wallet…
