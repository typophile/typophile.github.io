2009-07-21 03:39:37 • Miss Tiffany

Meredith –

Lucian Bernhard was an important and influential German poster designer of the period. The Lucian typeface, designed for Bauersche Geisserei (as was the similar Bernhard Roman), was based on one of his styles of lettering. Bernhard subsequently designed a few other typefaces in this same style for other foundries, including Bernhard Booklet and Bernhard Modern Roman (ATF, 1937).

I can’t say what the “intended use” of the design was.

The 1920s & 30s saw a bit of a trend in these type styles with small x-heights, tall ascenders, short descenders, and wide capitals. Compare Cochin (Deberny & Peignot, 1915; Monotype, 1917; ATF, 1925) and Egmont (Amsterdam Typefoundry and Intertype, ca. 1930s).

I believe the first use of Lucian in an American book (maybe the only) was the first title issued under the Random House imprint – Voltaire’s Candide, illustrated by Rockwell Kent, composed and printed by the Pynson Printers, and issued in April 1928.

This information was gleaned from Mac McGrew’s American Metal Typefaces of the Twentieth Century [New Castle, DE. Oak Knoll Books, 1993] – seek out a copy through your university’s library – and my own copy of the 1975 facsimile of the Random House Candide.
