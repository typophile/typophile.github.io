Fonts for Education

January 7, 2015 • grubstreet

This is really a sad story.

So I just started my design school’s “fundamentals for typography” class with other 59 students who have no idea what a typeface is other than Times New Roman or Helvetica. Professor demanded we all buy Adobe Font Folio for $149, and students are immediately grunting over the price.

And then somebody found out that we can install endless copies of the same font files on different computers…! And everyone in the Facebook discussion group starts feeling the ecstasy of avoiding yet another hefty fee. (Wait that wasn’t hefty – you get 50 families for $149 from Adobe, while purchasing one family already costs normally $100-200!)

There are two sad points. For one, my classmates take font pirating as something to be celebrated, and openly discusses about it (“I’ll buy you meals if you send me that font folio!”); and second, I feel like typefaces are luxuries – the amount of sweat and blood they require are no less than other kinds of luxury products like crafting wooden furniture. That’s why they are so expensive (aside from the corporates’ licensing barriers or some Monopoly bad-ass behavior). Students can’t afford good typefaces, and there are no good way around other than “educational discounts.”

Well, there used to be Corel products with the Bitstream portfolio; older versions were sometimes available quite cheap in some stores. But I suspect that’s worse, not better, from your point of view - and getting a pile of fonts for $10 instead of $149 won’t stop people from pirating them afterwards anyways.

2015-01-07 17:44:10 • Thomas Phinney

Yes, I’m in Seattle. Students here can spend hundreds of bucks on new iPhones or new computers, but do not want to spend that $149 on a font bundle. They can definitely afford it; it’s just they haven’t realized the value of good type in comparison to the apparent utility of iPhones and computers. To start things off, typography education (at my program at least) shouldn’t teach “ascenders” or “x-height” before telling the students why these things matter and why we should pay for it.

But when I say students can’t afford good typefaces, my point still stands. Yes, spending $149 on Font Folio is totally affordable, but there’s only one Adobe, only one AIGA decision, and only one font bundle; you can’t simply demand every foundry to do the same. What if you want a family of, say, FF Kievit, to set your final project in a class? And you seriously go give $1000 to Fontfont, just to buy a family that you’ll probably not use again? (And your instructor may also say, “Kievit is so well designed, let’s make the purchase of Kievit mandatory”…)

P.S. Tuition is not free of course (even much higher for int’l students like me), but people do pirate (or try to pirate) Adobe CC for sure.

Obviously, tuition has to cost something, because professors have to eat. Actually, this isn’t obvious, since schoolteachers also have to eat, and professors could be paid the same way (of course, they partly are; tuition or the post-secondary institutions themselves are subsidized by the government in most places).

But a society in which everyone who isn’t labelled as mentally retarded is expected to stay in school until he or she gets a Ph.D. (or an M.D. or an LL.D.) is not likely to come about - one where all those with the ability to go to university can do so is already a radical and Utopian dream; assuming that nearly everyone has that ability is probably wrong, although given economic demand in different labor categories, our society has really not made the effort to develop everyone’s potential so as to find out.

Still, this is an unanticipated extra expenditure. It’s bad enough that students have to pay high prices for textbooks, which are in some cases revised periodically when changes in the subject matter do not warrant doing so, in order to limit access to books on the used market. Who has heard of a university using books from Dover for suitable subjects like first-year physics or first-year calculus?

After they finish paying their tuition and for their textbooks, after all, students usually aren’t rich. Unless they’re among the few fortunate enough to have wealthy parents.
