February 8, 2008 • ebensorkin

Anybody know a Bremer Presse font with the alternative "r" and "e"? I was reading about Blumenthal's font Spiral (Later Emerson with monotype) which, before Monotype sold it, had an r with a short arm and another with a long arm. But in the book it mentions that Blumenthal was very influenced by ideas about paper and type that were in vogue at the Bremer Presse. And that the Bremer Presse had a font with the alternative "r" and "e". The book is called "American Proprietary Typefaces", and the section was written by Jerry Kelly. Actually if anybody has info on how to contact him that would be great too.
As far as I know, no one has made a digital version of the Bremer Presse type designed by the printer publisher Will Wiegand. If someone has, I think I might prefer not to know about it, because I quite like the idea of private press typefaces remaining what they were -- the exclusive working tools of the people who made them or commissioned them to be used in very particular ways in specific books. I have a couple of Bremer Presse books, though, Eben, and have scanned the 1922 12pt roman, which includes the variants to which you refer. I had not noticed this feature of the types before, so thank you for drawing my attenion to it. Interestingly, only the 12pt has them: the 1924 11pt does not. The differences between the variants are quite subtle. I'm afraid I would need longer to track down examples of the individual variants in use, so we could determine in what contexts they were used. [img:sites/default/files/old-images/Bremer12_3906.jpg] In the earlier, 1913 16pt there are variants for a f i and t, but not for r. The variant i is particularly interesting: the difference is in the size of the dot, and I'm guessing that one is for use after the non-ligating f. [img:sites/default/files/old-images/Bremer16_6743.jpg] There are also similar variants in the eccentric Bremer Presse Greek type.

William Dana Orcutt, discussing the design of his Humanistic type in In quest of the perfect book, writes 'I decided to introduce the innovation of having several characters for certain letters that repeated most frequently, in order to preserve the individuality of the hand lettering, and still keep my design within the rigid limitations of type'. I'm not sure when the Humanistic type was first used, but Pankow's Proprietary American Typefaces shows a setting from 1906. 2008-02-09 13:47:28 • eliason
The Gutenberg 42-line Bible also shows evidence, as explained by Hermann Zapf, of variant letters as an aid to justification: letters of different width are used to maintain consistent letter and word spacing. Zapf had the idea to do the same thing dynamically, by intelligently scaling glyphs horizontally without distorting them or changing stem weights. This was implemented by URW as part of the Hz text layout engine, but not currently active in any shipping software, so far as I am aware.
