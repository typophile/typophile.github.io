May 3, 2009 • Griffin

Are the characteristics of a typeface completely subjective, or are there certain visual elements that can be agreed upon, more or less? Please take my quick survey to help me answer this question for my research project. Here is the link: [[http://bearsinflight.com/type]] I am surveying both designers and non-designers, to see if there is any correlation in opinion. When a designer chooses a typeface for an advertisement, is the effect of the typeface wasted on a different audience, or do they see the same thing? It is an important question and so far, the opinions have varied greatly. Please take the survey– all information will be put to good use. I am also interested in hearing your extended opinions here.
This is wrong. It's like asking the musician and the listener what they think of the sound of a particular musical instrument playing a random melody, and assuming that has some bearing on the effect of a musical performance. Or asking the chef and the diner what they think of the taste of an herb, and assuming this has some bearing on the success of a dish. For design it's useless, and is detrimental to students developing their own discriminating taste. As statistical research it is hopelessly amateurish.

It is axiomatic that lay readers and typographers see type differently, and that content, layout and specification affect the viewer's perceptions. Even for the typographer who is familiar with a face, and for whom it has a wealth of meaning of which the lay reader is ignorant, there are other considerations. For instance, in the many ways of categorizing types, consider this index of familiarity: - Types I have worked with but not used in finished pieces - Types I have used in finished pieces - Types I have seen occasionally used by others - Types I have seen often used by others - Types I have only seen in print or online specimens - Types I have seen discussed
