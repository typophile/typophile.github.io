Type decisions for 'Alice in Wonderland'

October 6, 2010 • Zach

Q: What faces would you use to set an edition of Lewis Carroll’s Alice’s Adventures in Wonderland?

It’s a really fun challenge – setting a text that gives you a license to get fanciful, but trying to do so tastefully. I’d love some feedback and additional ideas for the choice of faces.

I feel like you need at least a second face for display, and possibly a third for drop caps – I feel like you need drop caps in Alice, but I’m reluctant to use the same face as the text (too dull) or chapter names (too repetitive).

Beyond that, the iconic words “EAT ME” and “DRINK ME” in the text seem to cry out for a special, decorative treatment, but four faces is getting pretty ridiculous. Perhaps one should just make do with letterspaced caps/smallcaps. Also: I’m inclined to use the text face for the running headers and folios, though that’s not beyond question either.

Text My current top candidate is, to my surprise, some kind of Bodoni for the text. It never would’ve occurred to me, but I’ve seen a couple editions set in Bodoni, and thought it worked surprisingly well. (I tried Bulmer for awhile, to at least get the country of origin to match, but it wasn’t nearly as good.)

I originally wanted to use something 21st century, and my very first thought was [[http://www.underware.nl/site2/index.php?id1=dolly&id2=info|Dolly]], but I don’t think it captures enough of the mystery of the story – it sort of domesticates it. I feel the same way about [[http://www.fontshop.com/fonts/downloads/fontfont/ff_clifford_pro/|Clifford]] to a lesser extent, though I’m still considering it. In the Bodoni vein, I also considered Nick’s [[http://new.myfonts.com/fonts/shinn/bodoni-egyptian/|Bodoni Egyptian]] and Emigre’s [[http://www.emigre.com/EF.php?fid=97|Filosofia]], but the former doesn’t seem suited for book-length text, and the latter seemed too fancy, for lack of a better word.

Display & drop caps This largely depends on the text decision, but taken in isolation I’m thinking about Dear Sarah for display. For the drop caps, if I stick with a Bodoni, I’m inclining towards [[http://www.fontshop.com/fonts/singles/wiescher/bodoni_classic_deco_roman/|Bodoni Classic Deco]], to avoid juxtaposing modern and non-modern letterforms so close together. Also because it looks nice :)

I await your thoughts eagerly…

I would consider setting The Mouse’s Tale as being the acid test for the text face. That would require some serious metafonting.

The other poems are in italic, so that’s important too.

Also the relationship between the type and illustrations is crucial. Tenniel’s went very well with a Scotch.

I have a Puffin edition of the book, first published in the 1940s, then reissued in the 1960s, which is nicely set in Monotype Scotch Roman, a type that, altought cut in the early 1800s, seems to fit well with Carrol’s Victorian mood. If you want a contemporary usable Scotch, you can’t go wrong with Miller; olther choices would be Nick Shinn’s Scotch Modern and James Mosley’s New Scotch (which I don’t think is available for licensing, anyway). Also Hercules, by František Štorm, comes to mind.

2010-10-10 23:19:21 • Zach
Zach - not a difference in terms of quality per se but in terms of result, meaning how typefaces look on paper when printed digitally and offset. In my experience, digital print can achieve great results (considering its low cost), but, generally speaking, tyepaces look “thinner” and “colder” (I don’t know a better espression, sorry) than in offest printing. So, a sturdier, less contrasted typeface could work well in digital print too.
