Your questions are thoughtful and intelligent for someone with no background in type. Unfortunately, though, your request is elephantine. It’s almost 1:00 a.m. where I am, and I have to work in the morning, so I’m not going to get too into this, but here’s a little to get you started…

Just like in any craft, there are rules of varying importance and weight in typography .

[Quick sidebar clarification: typography refers to the functional/aesthetic setting of type; type design is not necessarily typography. Rather, it is the design of the letterforms and alphabets used by typographers. Not many typographers can masterfully design type, and vice versa (but before the sensitive crowd gets too upset, I fully acknowledge there are some serious exceptions to that statement). I’m replying to your post more from the typographer angle, as my knowledge of type design is limited.]

Depending on the abilities of two, say, typographers with the same skill set put to the same task with the same set of tools (e.g., software and fonts), they may construct pages that are markedly different from one another in style and feel, yet both function exactly as they need to (or, more frequently, not at all).

Why? Admittedly general, and in no particular order:

Personality (That is, the personality of the typographer and of the typeface(s) used on the project) A typographer may personally be more comfortable with a certain face for a kind of project. Anyone who has an opinion about “fonts” can relate to some more than others, and may thus lay out a page using something they are comfortable with. Which leads us to:

The right tool for the job There’s a reason United States IRS documents are composed in Helvetica and not in the “Chiller” font. So, if Helvetica is the perfect banal and transparent face for government documents, why is it so elegant and contemporary for branding such as Crate&Barrel or edgy and cool for American Apparel? See the movie for the answer.

Family History Various faces fall into different categories: there are genres and sub-genres and Return of the Son ofs (Reference? Anyone?) for serifs, sans, displays, texts, scripts, and on and on. Each has its developmental place historically, and thus has a “preferred” application, for various reasons (see “The right tool for the job,” above).

To make matters worse (better), a masterful typographer might be able to justify (no pun intended) using an historically inappropriate face for a job, and make it work beautifully (see “Personality,” above).

Variable Interdependence As you can see by my few and scattered examples, these factors can interdepend on one another, and there are way too many more variables for me to discuss here or now.

Believe it or not, I understand what you’re looking for, and I know that I didn’t give it to you, but I hope you might have a better understanding as to why yours is a difficult request.

Resources There’s more than a handful of fantastic books on typography, and I’m sure others will provide you with links galore, but here are some resources for completely backgroundless newbies: Thinking with Type, by Ellen Lupton Type Desk Reference, brand new publication, by Theo Rosendorf Thinking in Type, by Alex White

I love these books. Lastly, I hesitate to mention (and to not mention) a profound contribution to the contemporary type community. Despite it’s ironically appalling cover, Robert Bringhurst’s Elements of Typographic Style is considered by most to be the typographer’s bible. My hesitation is only because you’re a newbie. If you catch the bug, and I think you might, then get this book. It’s the best.

Hello, Lisle. Welcome to Typophile.

The first step into type is realizing how little you know, so that’s out of the way. As a graphic design student, I was struck by this realization around my junior year. Lurking here is good, but it can be a bit dense. There are some general guidelines, but the devil is in the details (along with god, I’m told), so reading up is the best bet.

Books will give you a good foundation, particularly for use and an overview. Ricardo’s recommendations match my own. When you’re ready for the next level, we’ll still be here. Also, start learning type history: what typefaces were popular during which eras and how they fit into the philosophy that drove their designs. The era contributes the “voice” of a typeface, because our popular culture often has a visual understanding of something before an intellectual one. You can evoke a time period or emotional response simply by choosing an archetypal typeface. The philosophies that drove their designs are often based in function or emotion, which is a good way to match type to subject matter (though not always). This is also a great way to find a typeface that defies convention and breaks expectations (see Avant Garde’s recent revival).

I find the most informative sources are the font makers/sellers themselves, once you’ve read the basics. Hoefler & Frere-Jones do a good job of giving context and usage info on their releases. FontShop (by way of the ubiquitous Stephen Coles) has a great newsletter they send out that often contains concise and helpful descriptions of their releases.

The arguably best way to figure out what works and what doesn’t is to start typesetting everything you make. Try a different typeface. Combine two for heads and body then take a look. Listen to others' reactions. Tighten the margins. Work with the line spacing. Then change the typeface again and see what it does in the same setting. Type is tricky because there are different realms with different goals. Identifying your overall goal will help. If you’re typesetting lengthy running text in a novel, your goals are very different from an advertisement or a business proposal.

Also, find examples of things you like and really scrutinize the type being used. Try to describe it. How does it differ from what you have available in Word? Use resources like What the Font to start identifying type you enjoy. Look for the colophons in books and learn what typefaces are used by the pros.

In the end, short of going to school, exploration (by doing and reading) is the best route. If you’ve lurked Typophile, you know that we rarely agree on things, so while there are general guidelines, it all comes down to judgement and experience.
