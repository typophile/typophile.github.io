2006-11-20 23:09:50 • Choz Cunningham

“Just how big was it, Johnny?”

Actually, if you look at some of the hot-metal fonts, they were offered with optional long descenders. Traditionally, “short” descenders were used by newspapers, so they could pack in more copy. Linotype wasn’t dumb, since more machines were bought by newspaper publishers than book printers, the short descenders became standard, and “long” descenders became an extra-cost option.

And I can’t resist, what are usually termed “modern” figures came about to support advertising, which used a lot of full caps. “Old style” numbers are better termed “lower case” figures, as that would indicate their use rather than date of origin. There is always a problem with date-of-origin: “modern” figures, for example, are just as up-to-date as corsets. Not really a “build” issue, though . . .

That said, aesthetics can of course be allowed to alter the course of efficiency, when it makes sense.

You are of course right that shorter extenders are not a requirement. But the practical constraint here is that if you have longer descenders at small size then you are more or less required to do more leading, and so the publisher has to pay more for paper.

It seems like whenever economy is a prime consideration (eg newspapers), then publications both historically and now generally go for relatively small type sizes with large x-heights. That way you still get decent readability with economy.

So as a designer, if you are going to do optical sizes–which I think are a good idea for readability–you have the question of what demands to serve.

I really wonder how much demand there is for 8 point type with a small x-height with 14 point leading. I must admit it sounds weird to me. I would think that it is going to be more readable with a larger x-height than is ideal for an 11 or 12 point font. And then if you want to make the 8 point type still more readable you can give it some more space between the lines.

Now you could design with three different extenders, like Trinite, and combine that with optical sizes, like Minion. But the question becomes whether you are making a huge amount of pointless work for yourself.

I guess what I am saying is that I can see that larger x-height at 8 point is an advantage for many uses, and I am having a hard time figuring out when a small x-height at 8 point would be an advantage.

Please remember that you’re talking about the use of quotation marks in English, but practically every language uses a different set of quotation marks. For Mrs. Eaves, it was someone else who pointed out how the serifs are so long and thick, and the contrast so low, they look geared to tiny sizes, but the x-height is tiny with long extenders, implying display, or at least larger-size use. It can’t decide what size it’s for. With ITC Garamond, the conventions that led to it developed in advertising; hand-lettered headlines tended to be based on the text type used in small sizes, but adapted for display, with tighter fit, more delicate serifs, more contrast. As the decades ticked by and standards dropped, all that was left of those conventions was the urge to space everything tightly and fuss over serif shapes, so ITC Garamond was essentially a display variant of Garamond 3, except that other inescapable trend in the 70s was to make x-heights gargantuan, so the result was a typeface with detailing appropriate for about 120 points and up, with x-height and other proportions suitable for about 5 point. And now we get to look at miles of it in 9 point on glossy paper in grey ink. Puke.
