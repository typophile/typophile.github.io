Typically the gradient and the colors are secondary to the actual geometry of the mark. There are some notable exceptions of course, but your case is not one of them. So I'd gather the feedback regarding the B/W version of the mark first, and fine-tune the color treatment and such second. In this context I think the original version is not bad. I would consider using uniform thickness for the circling line. Additionally, one way to avoid a loss of a thumb detail at small scale is to do something like this: [img:sites/default/files/old-images/hand_5891.png] . However in general the mark is a little to bland. As someone else said regarding some of my own work - "it doesn't have an AHA moment". At very least, the mark is reminiscent of WWII memorials you can find in (Eastern) Europe - two hands guarding fragile eternal flame representing a memory of fallen solders. As a suggestion - try and use the negative space you have within the circle. For example, have a look at [[http://logopond.com/gallery/detail/48073|this logo]] and see how it has three related concepts seamlessly blended into a single mark.

2009-01-26 17:43:04 • typerror
lateblues, the logo has an interesting idea that's not being executed as well as it should be. It smacks of too much computer thinking and not enough print and sketch thinking. The details on the thumbs and the type are just too small. Print it out at the size you would use it for a business card and letterhead to see what I mean. The details and the type will disappear or turn into 'ant tracks'. I suggest more curves in the hands to make them more hand-like. Think about the leaf/flame thing. Is this what you want? Also, the relative size of the leaf to the hands: Are the hands protecting the huge leaf? Are they supporting it? Are they avoiding touching it? In a logo, every line communicates, so be aware of it. Try shifting the relative sizes and see how it changes the message. Also, as others have said, choose type with more warmth or more personality. GESAC looks authoritative, but it also looks too bureaucratic next to the mark. The sans serif type is just too small and is not well paired with the serif type. If you're a student, I know your options are limited, but these are not strong type choices. They should have a harmony or a purposeful contrast with the mark. Right now, they look like they just happened to be in the same place. All this being said, there's a strong mark at the core. You can develop this and make it work. It does look like some other logos I've seen, but for student work it's more important to explore and understand the craft and concepts than it is to get worried about similarities (short of looking like a rip-off, of course). Keep trying and keep exploring. Go to a site like FontShop and poke around their hand-picked fonts for inspiration. Also, try looking at images like this one and figure out what they have that your mark does and doesn't. Use these explorations to start fresh on the same approach.

While the hands may well be the whole point of the logo, in none of the examples do they read as hands. Look at hands. Study some anatomy. Draw it without a computer. Find a good source image, trace it realistically and slowly abstract and simplify the forms. Then see if it reads at 1/4" at 72 dpi, the most unfortunate restriction that designers have ever been handed.
