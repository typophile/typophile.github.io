The Super Heavy Display trend

December 7, 2009 • kosal

What is everyone’s stance on the super-heavy, modular, geometric trend that’s coming from various design boutiques these days? I’m talking stuff that you’d see featured every now and then on Behance, and in the work of Non-Format.

Usually letters are deconstructed into basic shapes or pure line, then modularly designed, edging the border of legibility. I’ve seen it referred as type and typography countless times, but in my opinion it’s more along the lines of display lettering. I guess that sorta counst as type right? Is this artistic style a welcome departure from traditional typography, or is it in danger of hijacking the definition of what “type” is?

I don’t have any plans to design a type in this genre.

Type genre has nothing to do with whether something is type or lettering.

The lines between type and lettering have become blurred by OpenType alternates. When it’s possible to set a wordmark or headline with a font that has lots of alternate glyphs to chose from, including swashes, and do so either by selecting from a glyph palette, or going with the contextual substitutions built-in to the font, then the process of typographic design is more like that of lettering.

This isn’t really new. The famous Photo-Lettering Inc. was a type house (mid 20th century). They’d work from film-strip fonts, and then modify details of a setting by hand, e.g. making ligatures. So was that type or lettering?

Duchamp would have called it an assisted readymade.

Real display lettering is distinguished by the possibility that you might get your hands dirty–but so is real typesetting.

2009-12-09 03:16:35 • typerror

I could write a short book on this; but for now, a short paragraph: To me display faces are too superficial, too predictable. Because display faces pander to our conscious -and transient- tastes, making a successful display face involves only moderate skill, and only one notable talent: discerning what will be fashionable in the immediate future. Success in display face design is also largely a matter of luck - which to me is a turn-off. In contrast, making a good text face requires grasping and balancing so many subtleties, and having such a command of spacing and texture, that it takes my breath away. A text face reflects the personalities of billions of people, over centuries.
