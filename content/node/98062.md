  I\u2019ve recently written about the plugin for a magazine:\r\n\r\n\u2014\u2014\r\n\r\nSpeed
  Punk is a learning tool. It teaches you to better understand the nature of B\xE9zier
  curves and their curvature, the technical basis of digital type design. \r\n\r\nThe
  B\xE9zier curve was developed almost simultaneously by two Frenchmen working in
  computer aided design (CAD) in the French automobile industry in the early 1960s,
  Paul de Casteljau working for Citro\xEBn and Pierre B\xE9zier working for Renault.
  De Casteljau is today attributed with the invention, but Citro\xEBn decided to keep
  his research secret until the late \u201960s, hence they carry B\xE9zier\u2019s
  name.\r\nThe curves play an important role in many areas of industrial design, namely
  aqua- and aerodynamics, as well as computer animation (smoothly controlling the
  velocity of an object over time). And of course computer graphics in general and
  type design in particular, as they provide a memory efficient means of storing illustrations
  and can easily be scaled and rasterized on the fly for sharp printing on output
  devices with varying resolutions.\r\n\r\nWithout B\xE9zier curves many things we
  take for granted wouldn\u2019t be possible. Taking off in a roller coaster from
  the flat into a perfect circle loop would brake your spine, as the curvature would
  infinitely increase from the straight line to a fixed amount of curve speed in the
  circle. Or imagine the impossibility of your car\u2019s steering wheel needing to
  be turned instantly by a fixed angle from the straight road into a curve. Instead,
  any moving object turns into a curve progressively, starting with no curvature and
  slowly increasing it over time.\r\n\r\nCurvature of cubic B\xE9zier curves (the
  most commonly used form of the B\xE9zier curves in computer graphics, also part
  of the PostScript language) is controlled by two off-curve points per curve segment
  commonly known as \u201Chandles\u201D or \u201Cvectors\u201D. Pulling one of them
  will influence the distribution of the curvature of the entire curve segment.\r\n\r\nAny
  design more complex than a quarter circle will require the use of several consecutive
  curve segments, and this is especially true for letter forms. One of the problems
  the designer is confronted with is to distribute the curvature equally across two
  joining curve segments, across their shared on-curve point. If the curvature across
  this point is not continuous, one might see a smaller or bigger dent in the curve
  on either side of the on-curve point.\r\nCurvature continuity is called G2 in mathematical
  terms as opposed to tangential continuity (G1) where two joining curves have a common
  tangent in the joining point but no continuous curvature, as opposed to positional
  continuity (G0) where two curves join but have no common tangent, as opposed to
  no continuity at all (the curves don\u2019t even join). \u201CWhile it may be obvious
  that a curve would require G1 continuity to appear smooth, for good aesthetics,
  such as those aspired to in architecture and sports car design, higher levels of
  geometric continuity are required. For example, reflections in a car body will not
  appear smooth unless the body has G2 continuity.\u201D (Wikipedia)\r\n\r\nA technical
  requirement of digital typefaces adds to the type designer\u2019s problem: the necessity
  of adding on-curve points at the curves\u2019 extremes in both X and Y direction.
  It is (or was, as some say) necessary to speed up the rasterizer\u2019s glyph arithmetics
  especially for making glyph bounding box calculation efficient and reliable. But
  those extremum points will unfortunately in many cases have to be positioned in
  shape-wise unnatural locations.\r\nTake the construction of the letter \u201Co\u201D
  mimicking a broad nib pen for instance. Wouldn\u2019t it feel natural to position
  the on-curve points along the contrast axis for the counter or along a mirrored
  contrast axis for the outline? Instead we have to position the nodes horizontally
  and vertically in the most awkward spots. This poses the risk of discontinuous curvature.\r\n\r\nSpeed
  Punk illustrates the curvature on top of the outlines with shapes that stand perpendicular
  on the outline. This is a technique commonly known from CAD software. The \u201Cbigger\u201D
  the illustration is (the further away from the outline), the higher the curvature
  is at this point. This way it is easy to judge curvature continuity at on-curve
  points: if the illustrations are of same distance from the on-curve point (they
  \u201Cmeet\u201D), the two curves are of continuous curvature. If you see a jump
  in the curvature illustration, curvature is discontinuous. Simple. Mathematically
  speaking the curvature is the first derivative of the curve\u2019s direction and
  it is being calculated using the general cubic B\xE9zier equation.\r\n\r\nAnother
  thing to judge other than continuous curvature across on-curve points is the distribution
  of curvature within curve segments: how loose or tight are the curves themselves?
  While it is very hard to assess equal curvature on two opposing corners of an \u201Co\u201D,
  visually comparing the sizes of the curvature illustrations is much easier. As another
  visual aid, the illustrations\u2019 colors are being interpolated between the highest
  and lowest measured curvature amounts for each glyph. When the two opposing corners
  of the \u201Co\u201D have a similar color, their curvature amount is also similar.\r\n\r\nAll
  of this design trouble, however, will go away with increasing experience of the
  type designer. Which is why I call it a learning tool.\r\n\r\nStill with Speed Punk
  we can observe some interesting anomalies of B\xE9zier curves. \r\nHave you ever
  wondered what the much talked about \u201Cinflections\u201D really are? We get taught
  that a curve\u2019s one handle is not supposed to cross over the other handle\u2019s
  imaginary continued direction, but why? What this actually means is that the curvature
  at some point in the curve segment inflects into the negative \u2014 the curve becomes
  an \u201Cs\u201D form. However small the amount is \u2014 if not intended it should
  be avoided \u2014 another dent is inevitable. In Speed Punk this will show as the
  curvature illustration crossing over the outline into the other side (in \u201COuter
  side of curve\u201D mode) or bounce back from the outline (in \u201COutside of glyph\u201D
  mode).\r\nThere are exceptions, of course. One letter that is supposed to carry
  two inflections is \u2014 not surprisingly \u2014 the \u201Cs\u201D.\r\nThe other
  interesting observation is the curvature of a circle. Theoretically, curvature would
  be identical all around. So Speed Punk\u2019s illustration would form another circle
  at a distance. It is known that with no amount of B\xE9zier curve segments can a
  perfect circle be constructed. The lower the amount of segments, the less perfect
  the result.\r\nA normal circle\u2019s radius constructed of four curve segments
  will deviate from the exact radius by a maximum of about 0,27 per mill according
  to my measurements. The curvature starts out at the on-curve point with its minimum,
  reaching a maximum (highest deviation from exact radius) at about 14 degrees angle,
  then going back to a medium curvature at 45 degrees, then repeating over in reverse
  until the end of the segment.\r\n\r\nSpeed Punk lends its name from the curve speed
  (another term for curvature) and the Mohawk-like shapes that illustrate the curvature
  on the letter outlines. And it suggests that it\u2019s cool to draw curvature continuous
  B\xE9zier curves.\r\n\r\n\r\n[img:sites/default/files/old-images/01_4963.png]\r\nWhile
  the top curve may appear to the designer\u2019s eye as continuous at the on-curve
  point, it is not, indicated by the jump in curvature illustration. Also visible:
  the general quality of the two top curves differ from the two bottom curves. The
  top curves show a curvature emphasis in the center of the segment, the bottom curves
  show emphasis towards the sides.\r\n\r\n[img:sites/default/files/old-images/02_4738.png]\r\nThe
  curvature is continuous throughout this o\u2019s on-curve points. However, the counter\u2019s
  top right curve segment shows a significantly higher curvature than the lower left
  segment, indicating an uneven curvature distribution.\r\n\r\n[img:sites/default/files/old-images/03_3610.png]\r\nVisual
  proof of a geometrically unprecise circle constructed from four cubic B\xE9zier
  segments. The curvature illustration is far from circular and the interpolated colours
  clearly indicate the highest and lowest measured curvature.\r\n\r\n[img:sites/default/files/old-images/04_5653.png]\r\nAn
  inflection is where the curvature inflects into the negative \u2014 the illustration
  crosses over the outline. This example segment barely shows a dent. But however
  small, a dent is inevitable."
