Font which is slightly more "expressive" (or "forward") than Stevens Titling Sabre

October 20, 2014 • euryris

I’m currently designing a book with as its primary audience students from universities. The attached image will show some example text outlining my issues as described below.

In an attempt to move away from the mathematical perfection with which digital documents are now typeset, I’m using ITC F Caslon Pro 12 Roman for my body text. For my subsections I’m using Stevens Titling Pro Sabre.

I haven’t, however, settled for my section headings. Ideally I want it to stand out of the rest without obstructing the reader from reading the first page of the chapter in a convenient manner. My current section heading (and number) have two problems:

  1. The number is slightly too obtrusive, the first thing that I, as a reader, see, is the chapter number, which is (in my opinion) too thick. The incarnation that you see is from the ITC Modern Two 16 Light font. Suggestions welcome on how to improve this, so that the number has stature, but is not immediately obtrusive. I’ve tried a variety of fonts, including more “classic” fonts (Didot? Why not?), but none of the font features seem to deliver so far.

  2. The font of my chapter heading is currently the same as my subsection heading. Although I really like Stevens Titling Sabre, I think the titling should be only marginally more forward than the rest. This requirement is considerably hard to achieve, considering I couldn’t find an adaptation of font with numerical thickness (100-1200) rather than textual representations thereof (Heavy, Bold, SemiBold, etc.), which e.g. Lexicon does support.

Ideally I’m looking for font suggestions on font size and typeface choice. I’m also open to suggestions on other aspects of the design: margin size, usage of quotes, font size, line spacing and so on.

One thing I object to is the hyphenation, specifically: 1) hyphenation in the compound name Meissner-Ochsenfeld-Effekt, and 2) in the cutline beneath the photo. Both of these can be overcome by the use of judicious line breaks, keeping a watchful eye on the effects of letter and word spacing.
