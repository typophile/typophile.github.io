I find that letters must show some inclination. They must tell you, in some way, in what direction your eye should go. When we “revert the contrast”, as we say, thus going against convention, letters seem to tilt to the left, which “sounds” wrong; we know we read from left to right, but the letters are telling us otherwise. I’ve thought about things this way, too, and that’s where I got. I now use this piece of information whenever I look at or draw any letter.

2009-04-01 02:45:38 • russellm

I can’t comment on the convention, or what the ‘rules’ are, really.

But it feels wrong. It took some looking at it, and here’s what I’ve figured out:

  • The W starts with a thin stroke on the left, instead of a thick. I like the thick stroke; In the west we read left to right, so we need some kind of visual key to denote the start of a line, or a sentence. That’s why we capitalize. Capitalization of the first letter at the start of a sentence gives us the cue that it’s the beginning; Large to small. Likewise, starting with a thin stroke going to thick is a bit bizarre.

  • I’m also more used to W’s where the two outer strokes have more of an incline (or at the very least, the same incline) as the two inside strokes. But I’m not sure that this in itself is the issue, but rather that the two contrasting angles are not only different, but just very slightly different. It looks wrong. Most of the time when you create difference in proportion, angle, or form, you exaggerate to make it seem intentional, purposeful. If you don’t, it looks like a mistake or an oversight. For example, I don’t think the W would look as wrong if the outer strokes were completely vertical, and the inner strokes, by contrast more inclined.

It goes without saying there’s exceptions to every rule, but it helps to understand a letter’s essence, or its guiding principles. This shows a lack of it.
