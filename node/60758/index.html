I don’t think it’s a question of personal preference at all but a question of inherent legibility. Sans serif is good for short bits–a couple of lines of caption material, a few words on signs. It has 3 inherent problems that militate against legibility. 1, It has no serifs, therefore nothing to ‘sacrifice’ against the erosive effect of high contrast (as in black type against a white page). Hold your index finger up against a strong light source and you will see that the light erodes the outline of the finger, 2, Its lines have no ‘finish,’ no fixed terminal point, so that they bleed off into nothingness on the page while serifs provide a definite ending to each stroke, so that each letter is ‘closed’ and self-contained. 3, There is little or no variation in line weight (thickness), therefore none of the ‘rhythm’ that seems to somehow naturally lead from one letter to the next. All of these failings make sans serif less comfortable to read than Roman.

San serif is frequently found in museum and art-show catalogues, usually set at extremely wide meansures and always hard or even exhausting to read.

I have spent 40 years in magazine publishing and have found little use for sans serif in body type except when the type is very small and the layout cramped. Also, when paper-quality is low, like the rubbish we used for the listings of TV Guide magazine when I worked there too many years ago (the type was Bell Gothic, developed, I think for Bell Tel’s phone directories).

Sans serif has been around for a long time yet, for long passages, has found few admirers save for art directors. They like it because it is relatively unasserttive compared to Roman and therefore more easily integrated into the design of the page as a whole.

If you are setting long stretches of type and want people to read to the end of them, choose Roman and don’t look back. It will also help to provide compelling prose.
