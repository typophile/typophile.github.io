2004-12-22 23:26:11 • Bald Condensed
Doesn't that apply only to swashy ones?

Especially to swashy ones. Italic all-caps start showing up fairly commonly in title-pages in the 18th century, but seldom in any other context. I think typographers should generally ask themselves, when considering setting something in all-caps italics, whether there is any reason not to use roman all-caps instead. One can imagine, for instance, a title in all-caps that includes a non-naturalised foreign word; this might be a good reason to use all-cap italics. On the whole, though, I don't think there are many good reasons.

I'm reading Peter Burnhill's Type spaces: in-house norms in the typography of Aldus Manutius. It is an interesting study of the typographic work based on a few of the earliest of the Aldine books. The primary examples are those books showing the typographic treatment of a small roman cap with italic lowercase are predominant.

I'm finding this a difficult read but from what I can tell Burnhill is assuming the treatment is a single typeface design.

From my work on an extensive book detailing the UCLA collection, The Aldine Press, on which Nicolas Barker and Matthew Carter were collaborators, it is my understanding that the small x-height cap font and the italic font are two different typefaces, and were combined, partly because there was precedent (similar treatments can be found in earlier manuscript books), but mainly out of expediency, the matching italic caps had not been cast. I recall Barker correcting one of the bibliographers in this regard.

While I do not know of any Aldine treatments of italic small caps or all italic cap settings (just to stay on topic!), I was wondering if anyone had further information on this, as it seems some of the weight of Burnhill's argument may be based on an erroneous assumption.

Though I do like his speculation that size names for typefaces may have sprung from the Aldine attempt at standardization.
