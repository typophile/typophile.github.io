2007-03-26 07:58:10 • Dan Gayle

As Ken rightly says, Art Direction would be geared towards advertising, marketing and sales promotion. Culturally and conceptually there is a big difference between these activities and the rest of graphic design.

‘Art Direction’ is quite a misnomer because while ADs are typically paired with copywiters in ad agencies as creative two-person teams – no ad agency team I’ve spoken to has ever defined their work as an exact split between words and images; both parties are ‘creatives’ who are employed to come up with vast quantities of fresh ideas to shift product.

For this reason, the advertising/art direction educational programmes that I’ve seen firsthand tend to put students through large amounts of ideas generation – which doesn’t sound like your strongest suite. Although the visible effects of advertising art direction may appear disparate, the conceptual toolkit required is really quite small and very sharply honed.

Conversely, your abilities with recognizing problems and putting things together make you sound like a natural for general graphic design, including packaging.

2007-03-27 11:13:47 • ben_archer
As an art director, I can tell you that my job is more concept-oriented than that of a designer. As pointed out above, the same core competencies are needed for both jobs. Do you enjoy more developing concepts and picking the right elements (fonts, illustrations, photography) and the people to pull them off, or doing the hands-on design work yourself? It’s not that designers never come up with concepts or ADs never do design, but it comes down to how much time you invest in those two areas. Once you’ve been an AD for 3-5 years, you should be able to come up with headlines and copy points (not necessarily finished copy) and a CW should be able to come up with visuals (though not necessarily be able to art direct a photo shoot). If that is something you want to do, go the AD route. But really, school only begins your education – you’ll be learning for the rest of your life. So if the “designer” courses appeal to you more right now, take ‘em.
