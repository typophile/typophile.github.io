2009-11-17 17:39:50 • aluminum
There is always one thing one should consider (in my humble opinion): Am I going to create a logo, and is that going to be it? In other words, will my customer get the logo, and be happy with it, and simply use it? If so, I can use ANY font I deem suitable! however, should it be possible that my customer might one day use the font in the logo to do other stuff, like flyers, posters, or even use it as a textface.... then I would make sure I am using a font from a foundry that I can rely on to keep the font in good shape, and up-to-date. I would not use a free font. I ran across the following problem quite a few times: there was a logo made with a free font, and all of a sudden my customer wants to use it for some big time advertisement campaign in all kinds of languages, including Cyrillic. The free font was good enough to do the logo - but you could not do decent print jobs with it, there was no OT version, and of course no Cyrillic version, either. They were quite angry with me when I told them. Shucks. So if it is an artsy logo, which will end up being nothing but a cool logo, use everything you want! But if it is a business logo, and chances are your customer will get ideas, think twice before doing it. I, at least, learned the hard way :-)
2009-11-17 17:42:49 • Werfer
I'd argue that (most of the time) one would not want the logo typeface (or logotype) to be used as standard headline/body type in the rest of the branding. You'd want the logo to stand out on its own. Exceptions would be maybe something like the Walker, where the typeface *is* the overall branding element moreso than a specific logo. (But in those cases, you likely wouldn't be using a off-the-shelf commercial font--let alone a free font)
2009-11-17 21:42:37 • aluminum
Free fonts typically won't hold up well for a high-exposure, iconic, branded logo. But for lower profile, non-iconic logos that aren't seeking to develop a strong brand identity... yes. I think it really comes down to branding. Most free fonts just can't hang when it comes to competitive branding. But there are exceptions.

(Unless maybe if the font is custom and basically IS the brand.)
