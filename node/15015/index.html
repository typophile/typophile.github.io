Who's in charge here?

September 7, 2005 • Norbert Florendo

This question goes out to the governing bodies of Typophile. Eben Sorkin, Bruce Herbert and moi have been discussing the possibility of creating a Flash "game" (exercise is a better term) that would roughly simulate the ancient task of composing hand-set type, hence the name "Upside Down & Backwards." This is one of a few educational or entertaining projects under discussion that might be of interest to design educators and graphic design students alike. When the project is finally completed, we would like to make it openly available for download from the Typographic Edumacators section (a sadly under utilized topic area). The participants in the project look only for a credit line or such and want to know if Typophile is interested in holding the copyrights or intellectual property. I've noticed that the page footer declares "© 2000-2005 Typophile, All Rights Reserved. Typophile is a Punchcut gig." Questions: -- Is the Typophile organization interested in hosting such projects? -- Is the Typophile organization a legal entity that can hold a copyright? -- Can we start some dialog on these issues before we procede any further? We can follow up via email instead of through this topic if preferred. Thanks.

Before the relaunch, there was a clear statement saying the author of posts and of content retains the rights but that posting here grants Typophile permission to reproduce content for materials that would directly promote Typophile (and not any other entity). I think any forum or blog would have a very difficult time in a court trying to say that it held exclusive rights to intellectual property posted in forum discussions or as comments to articles. Likewise though, I have heard of cases of forums being sued for comments that users make there -- as if the forum controls and is responsible for user input. That notion is being heavily challenged in court though.

As there are a few threads related to the same interest and inquiry into developing interactive tools, aids, tutorials, etc. by several members of Typophile, let me recap some of the issues and threads: If Typography Was (Were) a Video Game The topic of type/typographic education by way of interactive media is being explored. Several members sited existing examples: Crimes Against Typography -- three games designed by Ellen Lupton (cited by Paul D Hunt) Letter Spacing -- University of Deleware, Visual Communications Program (cited by William Berkson) Tangrams -- Ancient Chinese Game A game of creating recognizable items using a limited set of shapes. (cited by Norbert Florendo) Sudoku as research A new topic started by Raph Levien citing a game of Japanese origin requiring very intense visual decoding. PROPOSED PROJECTS Exercise: Upside Down & Backwards A "game" proposed by several members of Typophile to help graphic art students to improve letterform recognition. The above topic link outlines issues in detail. (contributors -- Eben Sorkin, Bruce Herbert) Innovativations in Type Education Andi Emery et moi have made personal commitments to expand the Typographic Edumacators topic area by generating curriculum exercises and ideas, and by inviting other educators to participate, contribute and suggest useful and innovative approaches to type education. Who's In Charge Here? The current topic you are in which raises the possibility of the Typophile site being used for distribution of developed educational material as described above. The concern over rights of intellectual property has also been raised by members interested in developing content and projects for type education. I will endeavor to keep these topics current with other members who have expressed interest in participating.
