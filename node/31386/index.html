2007-02-13 09:57:33 • dan_reynolds

Of course I know that. In Germany we have the word “Maschinenstürmer”. Its out of a book “Die Weber”, from Gerhard Hauptmann anywhere in the beginning of the 20th century. After starting of Industrialization in Gemany (and other countries, like England, too, of course) lots of cloth was produced in small private homes. These webers went on strike, because the could not feed their families anymore. And they burned their looms (Hurray to Babelfish). “Maschinenstürmer” stands for people who are living in the past and who don’t see that times are changing. I am not a “Maschinenstürmer”, but I want to hold and protect my language for my german people. (In deep respect to other languages).

“Undeutsch” was “NS-Sprech” (Terminology of Nationalsozialismus). That’s not my way of thinking (and you know that of course).

To use german words for english ones in german language - if there are perfect words for it - is very senseful. I read that in Finland 95 percent of IT and computer terms are translated into finish - without any problem. The finish international specialists use both - english and finish - but for national requests they use finish terms only. Nobody thinks (as I know) that Finland is too nationalistic.

I’ld love to translate my complete website into english. But the problem is: My own english is too poor. And the translators I know are not familiar with all the terms out of typography and letterpress.

I just dislike the idea that something like the word “Internet” has to be translated :(

It isn’t like, before the introduction of the word “Internet” into German, there was a German word for the Internet. The Internet is a new concept. It was given a proper name (with a capital letter! By English speakers! What a concept!). Is it neccessary to translate that when both the sounds “inter” and “net” were already common in German-language anyway? (Z.B. Interaktivität oder vielleicht auch Networking?)

On the other hand, sometime German (marketers?) create new “German” words that are supposed to sound like English ones, but which are completely unintelligible in English. The best (or worst) example of this may be “Handy”, i.e., the German word for a mobile phone. A mobile phone may be a very handy thing to have, but no English-speaking country called that sort of telephone that name. This causes many young Germans minor troubles when they go abroad… they think that are communicating in a common language, when they are not necessarially doing this ;-)
