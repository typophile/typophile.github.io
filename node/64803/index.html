HD Hell

November 28, 2009 • Nick Shinn

Just bought a flat-screen TV, and can't watch the damn thing. In low definition, there are all kinds of artefacts. In high def, everything is over-sharpened, so all the type has a line around it, inside and out, and the talking heads have plastic skin with exaggerated blemishes. No, I'm not sitting too close. Why is cranked up unsharp masking so prevalent? It's a lot like the early days of stereo, or worse still, fake stereo. Is it just me ("Get used to it, Nick"), or is anyone else bothered by this aggressive display of artifice, this pseudo-hyperreal simulacrum, this voyeurism of exactitude?
Serious artifacts in low-def? Really? That shouldn't be a problem on an HD set, unless your TV isn't the greatest. For HD quality, if you're watching HD television rather than Blu-Ray discs, there are noticeable artifacts from compression that can be more or less awful. They bug me as well, although our particular cable provider seems to have improved noticeably over time. I remember when I first saw it a couple of years ago it was pretty awful. As I understand it, cable providers are up against some serious overall bandwidth limitations, so they have to make quality vs channel count tradeoffs, unless fiber optic becomes more widespread. Re-reading your note, I wonder if there is some sort of "sharpness" setting you need to turn off on the TV. It may indeed be doing some kind of unsharp masking.... Regards, T

2009-11-29 22:40:10 • david h
"Satellite doesn’t have the severe bandwidth limitations that (coaxial) cable has" Yes, it does. If you're getting your signal from Cable or Satellite, you're not really getting the best possible picture. Both will use various levels of compression on the channels. Some 'lesser' channels will be compressed to the point of looking like unusable JPGs. Sports and first-run movies tend to get the 'bigger pipes'. To fully test if it's your signal or your TV settings, hook up the rabbit ears and tune in your local HD PBS station. OTA HD isn't compressed so would be the 'purest' signal. Otherwise, it sounds like you maybe have a bad TV and/or bad settings and/or bad cables. My parents had a Samsung for a year before they discovered that the LCD panel had been 'sandwiched' wrong in manufacturing adding this odd distortion to the picture. (To samsung's credit, they fixed it). What bothers me the most is people that get the new big wide-screen TVs and then don't notice that they're still watching their old fuzzy standard def analog cable in stretch-o-vision. Watching Football with my FIL is agonizing. ;)

2009-12-02 01:24:47 • Tomi from Suomi
Nick, you should definitely check the "sharpness" setting on your new set. A few days ago I got a Blu Ray player and a copy of "2001: A Space Odyssey". I was horrified to see the same sort of over-sharpening effect. At first I thought it was the player, because I hadn't really noticed it with DVDs. But there didn't seem to be a setting for it on the Blu Ray player. Then I checked the setting on the HD set and it was cranked almost all the way up. I turned it all the way down and it looks great now. Having the sharpness cranked up also makes the compression artifacts look worse. They're less noticeable now, although not absent. (The type looks much better, too.)
