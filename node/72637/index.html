2010-07-29 17:23:05 • hoefler

A recent newspaper ad for Audi featured a font with holes in it, a metaphor for the “green” construction of their lightweight automobile.

This creative gem was full page, featuring acres (metaphorically) of white space. (Well, grey space anyway, it was after all newsprint.)

When I started Earthmark, an environmental marketing company, in 1989, one of our promises was to help reduce clients' environmental footprint, by means of expertise in producing powerful small advertisements and brochures. That was probably one of the reasons for Earthmark’s demise – there’s no glory in small ads for product managers, agencies, or creatives, regardless of their efficacy in shifting product.

As long as print marketing continues to rely on cheap size for impact, “saving the environment” by using less ink on a percentage basis is irrelevant.

In the world at large, pollution of all kinds has proceeded because industry standards are based on risk assessment tied to percentage concentrations, rather than capping toxic emissions to the finite nature of the physical environment.

In graphics, the high resolution of the digital workflow has arguably produced the most benefit to the environment, by enabling smaller documents. Newspaper rolls have been steadily decreasing in width, and newspapers in size, with the decline of that industry.

Increased resolution enables a downsizing of paper-size standards in general, such as has already happened in the newspaper business. (This process has been going on in garment industry dress sizes for many years).

It is time to shrink A4 etc., and 8 1/2" x 11".

Some time ago, after I saw that funny ink test made with pen, I did myself some comparison about ink coverage. Just for fun and curiosity.

My method:

  1. I set an article with 16 pages in InDesign with each of the fonts.
  2. Edited font size to match text extension.
  3. Exported each article to PDF.
  4. In Photoshop, imported these PDFs and rasterized them in 1200dpi.
  5. Did a histogram measure about back/white dots, considering the 127 level the mid-cut.
  6. Made arithmetical medias for all pages of each font usage to get an ink coverage.

Results: Font……………Size….Ink coverage Adobe Caslon…….10.25…10.51% Garamond BE………9.85…10.78% My project……….9.45…11.27% Minion Pro………10.25…11.53% Times New Roman….10.10…11.57% Baskerville BE…..10.15…11.91% Utopia…………..9.50…12.10% Guardian Text…….8.60…12.45% Meta Serif……….9.50…12.82%

There are several things to think about when discussing print savings. First, output frequently contains images, and no font in the world can help save toner or ink on that, and images are usually much denser than text. Second, ecofont does not support all text sizes, according to their website, they generally print only text up to 11 points in their font, switching to the standard font for larger text. Therefore, ecofont maybe excellent within its limitations, but there are alternatives for printing economically without having to tweak printer settings for every printer individually, use draft mode (about 10% savings in average and poor quality output) or forcing users to change their fonts. Our software, PretonSaver (see http://www.reduceprintcosts.com for information and explanation on how the magic happens) is certified to achieve savings of 25% to 40% of overall toner consumption in typical enterprise environments with no degradation in print quality.
