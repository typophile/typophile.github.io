I originally trained in the empirical sciences where line spacing tends to be quite narrow compared with the arts.

In that field, I observed that when arts professionals were confronted with papers written by scientists, they would squint and the page and then reject it without finishing a sentence. I thought artists were just stupid and unable to analyse either abstract or concrete subject matter.

However, when my career switched the arts a few years ago, and I experienced the inverse difficulty and its not the content. Rather, it is a fault of the type.

For example, [[http://blog.graydesign.com/wp-content/uploads/2006/05/semiotics_paper_rev2.pdf|this paper]] on graphic design has huge line spacing and its nearly impossible for me to read. Specifically, as my eyes scan across the words, my line of sight drops into the void below the text. This creates a pause at random intervals along the lines.

Furthermore, when I reach the end of one line and attempt the read the start of the next line - my eyes instinctively search the empty space between these wide separated lines!

I conjecture that all of the difficulties described above stem from learned convention rather than any disability of artists or scientists.

My career now spans the domains of art and science, and line spacing in my own work has become a serious concern because my audience may include both scientists and artists. Does typography offer a clever solution to a ridiculous situation?

Percy Snow, author of Two Cultures, would be laughing.

As to the sample paper, perhaps the goal is to make sure nobody will read it?

2009-09-27 21:00:59 • stormbind

This is interesting. One question, though: are you talking about manuscripts or any kind of publication, such as journal articles?

This, because I’m surprised that your Arts readers would be repelled by single spacing, since it’s what you find in all printed publications, including newspapers, magazines and almost all books, as well as on computers, whether in emails or on the web.

In my field, linguistics, anything in print will always be single spaced, whether it appears in a book or a journal article. However, there is some variation in manuscripts. It’s true that most recently, word-processed manuscripts tend to be single-spaced; however, in the days of typewriter manuscripts and the early days of computer-printed manuscripts up to the mid- and late-1980s, double spacing tended to improve legibility and for that reason was preferred. Many old typescripts and published theses I have from that era are double-spaced. However, this is no longer the case for those from the digital word processing era.

One more thing about manuscripts: most editors I have dealt with require manuscripts to be submitted with double spacing for practical reasons: the extra space simplifies the job of mark-up for reviewers and proof-readers and for those down the line who need to read the mark-up and make the specified changes.

We are the audience of that paper. The paper is provided on line. Consequently, his audience is the web user and not a particular instructor. However, that was not my concern.

Submission guidelines in the Arts are not dictated by an instructor. They are define by school regulations, and are typically double spaced or more. There are two reasons why this cannot be for the purposes of hand written corrections. First, similar rules require that instructors write on a separate sheet. Second, the submission is retained for published archives that must not be written on. The spacing requirement is equal to a border requirement.

In contrast, university submission guidelines in the Sciences typically require less spacing. However, I dug out my first degree dissertation and the total border is equal to the total line length. In that case, the layout was dictated by my university’s LaTeX template.
