I've been obsessed with tuscans and woodtype lately, and it was inevitable that I eventually create something in that area.

According to Martin Kotulla, SoftMaker is creating the following exclusive typefaces in woodtype design form:

• “Codex” (an exact all-uppercase only 19th-century woodtype font in the design of Memphis Ornamental, featuring solid upper half with an open triangular part, open lower half with a solid triangular part, and a deep shadow). • “Dixieland” (an exact all-uppercase only middleweight “rounded slab”-based woodtype font with western spurs) • “F690 Deco” (an exact all-uppercase only middleweight “square slab”-based woodtype font with western spurs, numbered from Festival) • “G832 Slab” (an exact heavyweight “art deco”-based woodtype slab-serif font, numbered from Gryphon) • “H780 Slab” (an exact “art deco”-based 19th century woodtype/Tuscan-hybrid font in the form of ornamental with western spurs, numbered from Howdy) • “Portland” (an exact heavyweight woodtype font in an actual mixture design based on Abilene and Rodeo No. 57, featuring two uppercase sets - “larger” for uppercase with western spurs, and “smaller” for lowercase without western spurs)

These font names are finally appearing on two of SoftMaker’s premium-quality typeface collections - “MegaFont XXL 3.0” (for home users) and “infiniType 4” (for designers) - both of which were being released around May 27, 2014.

2002-05-05 23:34:31 • j_hisekaldma
Nice work! But to put a spanner in the works… I think the discussion about the irregularities is putting the cart before the horse, not least because they only seem apparent at 120 point or above. Personally I think the weight is wrong. I think you have a great idea here for a font that looks like a genuine 19th century Tuscan, but I think if they were making this design in those days they would have either made the stroke weight bolder and kept the ornamentation the same size, or they would have perhaps made the stroke weight slightly thinner and emphasized the ornamentation. I have yet to see an easy to read Tuscan, but there are beautiful ones, for sure.

Without going all the way and turning all the serifs into these ornamental "claws" - and possibly turning this font into something that has already been done 120 years ago - you might experiment more with the serifs and terminals.

Sorry if I sound negative. Despite the criticism I think you are onto something very nice here.
