Here’s where I would start:

  1. Watch the film Helvetica again
  2. Take a look at all the newer typefaces that are meant to either improve helvetica, or to be somehow truer to the helvetica’s roots. ie: Bruno Maag’s “Helvetica Killer,” Christian Schwartz’s Neue Haas Grotesk, Michael Hernan’s Uniform.
  3. Read about Univers in Frutiger’s big book.

Then consider the claim to neutrality. And even if Schwartz isn’t after perfection or neutrality in his design of Neue Haas Grotesk – well, that doesn’t really matter. Surely we can strip out our intentional fallacies when viewing and critiquing type design. Which is to say that the intent of the designer isn’t as important as the design’s reception and use.

Last, and most important, read: Distinction: A Social Critique of Judgment and Taste, by Pierre Bourdieu

Your thesis really is one part typography and at least one part sociology.

2011-10-16 20:37:49 • tmac

I am of the scientific bent, and I do pursue the cult of readability, but I do also think that beauty is functional. Neither from the point of view of readability nor of aesthetics there can be a single ideal face. Different designs are more and less readable at different sizes, and in different media, such as print and screen. And what aesthetics is desirable will depend on your audience, the content of the document, and on cultural factors, which change with the times.

Even from the point of view of readability in terms of comfort and reading speed, aesthetics is also a major factor, because whether the reader is motivated to read is one of the biggest factors in readability. Both the content—how well written the material is—and the visual aesthetics of the experience are extremely important, as well as whatever burden typeface design puts on the decoding process. And that is assuming the content is of interest to the reader.

I do think that as far as design that puts the least burden on the decoding process, there are some fairly narrow windows for what is ideal at a given size. But this allows for a great deal of flexibility on aesthetics. And furthermore, in display type aesthetic considerations are usually more important than ideal reading comfort or speed.

David Berlow’s [[http://www.aiga.org/cyrus-highsmith-on-david-berlow/|shepherd story, as related here by Cyrus Highsmith,]] is pretty much on target.
