OK… I understand what you are asking for, which is any insight into what current “stylistic trends” are happening regarding line spacing (leading).

Over ten years ago, graphic designers were adding HUGE amounts of leading on copy blocks in ads and collateral print. Why? Not because of readability or text color, but because it was “da bomb.”

As far as I see (just looking through ads in Graphic Design USA) that affectation is still being inflicted today… body copy with line spacing at 30% – 50%+. That translates into 12 pt. type at 16 – 20+pts of lead.

Over seven years ago (to my recollection) there was a resurgence of minus leading in short or narrow copy blocks for a sort of “anarchist” appeal. Again, not for readability but as a statement of arrogance with an I don’t give a f#ck if you can read my message attitude of old puck rockers or even older dadaists.

Most of the people on this forum are more concerned about aspects of readability and even text color. I am also a graphic designer by nature and understand your temptation to follow the “in” thing.

My personal suggestions is to become more aware of what type is all about, and develop an “eye” for spotting how headlines and copy blocks are constructed. If you look at TDC Award Annuals you can see what was considered the best of the best uses of type and typography for that given year.

For the graphic designers' point of view, you can open any of the design award annuals of various organizations or countries, and view what was considered exceptional or hot. Remember, style is not a universal trend, but often segmented into genre, market segments, cultural, regional and country preferences as well.

You need to focus on what your piece is trying to say, and to whom you are directing the message in order to start making decisions on your approach to the design.
