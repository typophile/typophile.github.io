Oooh, a personal favorite designer. There are so many designs that get taken for granted that have fairly anonymous origins, but I’d have to say my favorite EXAMPLE of design (other than DVD or VHS box covers, whose designers are pretty well documented) is found in children’s toys.

Let’s face it. Someone designed this.

While it might not be so glamorous or typographic as the design we’re talking about here, I think 3d design is an important creative supplement to layout and cover designs. No, I’m not alluding to the sculptures of letterforms we’re doing for another class… I’m talking about the muppets.

Puppet design isn’t exactly what I’m talking about. I’m talking about the interaction between the puppet and set design and the type design. Ads and things.

Muppet Show album cover. I’ve had a cow of a time trying to dig up the actual names of graphic/type designers for the show, but Henson had a heavy hand in it at least, and it shows.

I like the Muppets. Puppets are fun :)

2009-03-01 15:38:45 • nab
To make a font more personalized (or personable), it really helps to have a model to go off of, or a physical item in front of you to model after. The old dusty saloon background in the picture would not fit so well with a sans serif, spidery, unremarkable gray font. Yknow? If the muppets had an absurdly rigid typeface representing them in print, it wouldn’t capture the show nearly so well.
