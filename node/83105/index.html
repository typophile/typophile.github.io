If it’s not about size, and it’s not about gain, then we do indeed need a new term. Rotis comes to mind: it uses “semi” to mean “not really”; in the serif pair it means semi-serif, while in the sans pair it means “sans with contrast” (since in oldschool terminology “sans” means no serifs and no contrast). But it sounds like you have a gradation going, so you need a scalable term - maybe “contrast grade”, or “CG” for short; you might suffix “CGn” where “n” is an integer. 2011-06-19 01:42:04 • hrant

It’s not an “optical” idea, not is it grading. Optical variants adjust for a variable such as size or letterspacing. Grading variants adjust for press gain.

But what you are doing doesn’t adjust for anything, it is purely stylistic.

As such, you are working with a stylistic axis.

Other stylistic axes are of course weight, horizontal scaling, and slant—as in the famous Univers family chart.

Since then, following the metafont/multiple master idea, other axes have been explored. For instance, Chalet proposes a fashion axis. Penumbra has a serific axis. Carl Crossgrove invented an axis of agitation with Reliq. I explored the axis of oldstyle/modern, with Sense and Sensibility at the extremes.

What you have is an axis of contrast.

Another term for an axis might be a scale, but scaling is used in typography to mean “making bigger or smaller”.

Admittedly, any Multiple Mastering/tweening — including optical sizing and grading — is done along a design axis. But I think that if you identify your fonts as variants along the stylistic axis of contrast, that is quite clear about what they are, and distinguishes them from opticals or grades.

Or just call them contrasts (as one would refer to weights), e.g. Jasper Low Contrast, Jasper Medium Contrast, and Jasper High Contrast.

2011-06-19 17:25:42 • hrant

The categorical distinction between “Contrasts” and “Opticals/Grades” is apparent if you consider that it would be possible to make Optical or Graded variants of your Contrasts.

I’m not sure how this would be achieved in practice, but theoretically:

Because press gain adds an even amount to both x and y dimension, it shifts contrast and changes horizontal/vertical proportions. Therefore if you wanted to maintain the same contrast in different press situations, you would indeed have to make graded versions of your different contrasts.

Also, you would have to shrink the heavier grades in size.

It would be a better practice to say, “when press gain is heavy, use Contrast 7, Grade 1”, rather than “…use Contrast 8 because it will look like Contrast 7”.

There doesn’t appear to be a standard terminology for grading: H&FJ (e.g. Chronicle), FB (e.g. Benton) and Typotheque (e.g. Greta) have different approaches.
