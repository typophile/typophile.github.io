Just use something that looks okay visually in your font. This is like asking if the acute accents should be more vertical or horizontal or if the dot of the umlaut should be in line with the i. The answer is Yes and No at the same time :) Spanish also uses -er and, much less commonly, -os and -as. There was a thread back a while ago asking if there was a way to automagically figure this out and the result was that for some languages like Russian it's impossible because of declensions. In my current text font I'm placing all ordinals the same, but with a slight underline. In Spanish this is quite common and (almost) expected. In English and other languages it's not a bother. There's another set of superiors ('script') to go with the math set. Nick, that's a good question. In all caps situations, roman numerals are generally used which avoids the question, and in dates ordinals aren't used at all. In older times in such situations, I see it put in all (small raised) caps or with a large tilde with small caps. I think if someone abbreviated María as M.A with a small cap A with tilde (which is hypothetical, I've never seen it that way), they'd be quite confused, ditto for HRMNOS with a (single long) tilde over the small capped (baseline) rmnos. In modern day applications I generally it's not uncommon to see the lowercase used if another avoidance technique isn't used. If perchance your small cap and numerators have a similar or same height / weight, that might be a good option. «El futuro es una línea tan fina que apenas nos damos cuenta de pintarla nosotros mismos». (La Luz Oscura, por Javier Guerrero)

Take your small caps and instead of aligning them to the baseline, pop them up to align their caps height with that of your normal caps.
