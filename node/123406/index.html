OK, here’s what I can find….

The font in the picture has not been identified. It does, however, have many quirky characters which the old metal face Grasset (Deberny & Peignot, 1898; ATF, 1904; and Monotype, 1912) has in common. The overall look is very similar.

I suspect the font in the picture is based on Grasset – or the other way around. Grasset is not available in digital form under that name that I could find, but there are two digital versions under other names.

Octavian, at http://www.myfonts.com/search/octavian/fonts/ (not a very good version – look at the lowercase /t – it is way over-size)

Grantham, free download at http://www.moorstation.org/typoasis/designers/lloyd/grantham.htm. This appears to be a pretty good version and should at least give you the appearance you are looking for.

Unless, of course, you are wishing to digitize the font in the picture. If that is the case, you still have to identify it and find a usable specimen of it.

I think Louvaine, designed in 1928 and issued in 1929, but did not last long enough to appear in the 1934 ATF catalog, is closer than Grasset. Consider Louvaine Medium: Comments by Jaspert: “…designed by M.F. Benton, based on a Bodoni model, but with the peculiarity found in a number of modernistic types of making the thicks and thins meet at an angle. This is especially noticeable in the O and the S. The g and y have cursive tails….” Comments by McGrew: “LOUVAINE series was designed by Morris F. Benton for ATF in 1928. It is an adaptation of Bodoni (the working title was Modern Bodoni), and many of the characters are identical. Only g and y are basically different; otherwise the distinction is in the more abrupt transition from thick to thin strokes in this series. In this respect, Ultra Bodoni has more affinity to Louvaine than to the other Bodoni weights. The three weights of Louvaine correspond to Bodoni Book, regular, and Bold. This series did not last long enough to appear in the 1934 ATF specimen book, the next complete one after its introduction. Might be a lead in this account of the Black Sun Press:

—start quote— Started in 1927 by Harry and Caresse Crosby … young expatriates living in Paris, the Black Sun Press was created to publish its founders’ … attempts at verse in beautifully bound, hand set books. … the Crosbys found Roger Lescaret, a “master printer” whose previous works had been primarily funeral notices, to print Harry’s poems in a fine edition. They were so delighted at the result, Red Skeletons, with illustrations by their friend Alastair [the professional name of Hans Henning von Voight], that they decided to found a press. In a typical combination of high ideals and deflating humor, it was first called Éditions Narcisse – after their black whippet, Narcisse Noir. —end quote— http://www.english.illinois.edu/maps/poets/a_f/crosby/blacksun.htm So perhaps a bespoke typeface for or by Roger Lescaret. Good point. No disputing that the typeface verges on Frankenfontism. The most striking feature is the pregnant S and s, but this is not reconciled with other features such as the Louvainesque feature of making the thicks and thins meet at an angle, the German single story g, the Didotesque y with the plumb drop vertical descender, and some of the quirky touches in the styles of Grasset, Bewick Roman and Graybar Book. However, perhaps it was appropriate for a press that dealt with topics that were far from conventional. Don

The production runs were very short, so I believe that this is a letterpress font rather than something that meets the technical specs for a typecasting machine like Linotype. So I wouldn’t rule out bespoke. Private presses in the early 20th century with aspirations to uniqueness sometimes made the effort to have a unique typeface. Recall for example the Doves Press. In these cases vanity trumped profitability. Might be worth a search of the books on private presses to see if there is anything on Black Sun Press. for example Hugh Ford’s Published in Paris: American and British writers, printers, and publishers in Paris, 1920-1939 [1975] http://www.amazon.com/Published-Paris-American-publishers-1920-1939/dp/0855112123 I trust there are others. For more leads Google “Paris private presses black sun” or visit a good university library and check their holdings for LOC classification Z321 and nearby. Don
