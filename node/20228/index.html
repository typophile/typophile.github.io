2006-05-25 20:52:37 • Conor

Paul Kohlers claimed that the right half of both upper and lowercase Latin type contained the most salient cues, and showed that text with the right half available was more legible than text with the left half available.

Kohlers also looked at removing the top and bottom half of text, and found that lowercase was more legible when the top half was available and that uppercase was more legible when the bottom half was available.

My guess is that each letter has it’s unique identification cues, and that these studies demonstrate the most frequent location for these cues, but don’t limit the possibility that the unique identification cue for a lowercase letter may be in the lower left quadrant.

Cheers, Kevin

Kohlers, P.A. (1969). Clues to a letter’s recognition: Implications for the design of characters, Journal of Typographic Research, 3 (2), 145-168.

This may have something to do with the cursive writing on which the lower case letter shapes are based. The letter forms evolved, to some degree, to facilitate efficient joining. The lower left quadrant is the part of the letter which is least involved in joining with its neighbours.
