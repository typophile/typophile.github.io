Bumper Sticker Font - Legability

July 7, 2009 • SunnyGosal

Hello all,

Recently I’ve been commissioned to design a basic bumper sticker/decal. You’ve seen them before “HOW’S MY DRIVING? 1-800-555-5555”. I’m looking for suggestions on typefaces to use. Legibility is essential, as the bumper sticker/decal has size limitations and will be read while in movement.

I’m reaching out to the typography community for suggestions - You input would be most appreciated.

Sunny.

Some of the most legible typefaces are the ones that designers hate, like Arial and Souvenir. Designers avoid them because they’re bland and boring, but there’s a reason folks keep using them: Their lack of subtle refinement makes them easy to read under less-than optimal conditions (e.g., driving past at 70 mph).

I think the “invisible/boring/familiar” approach should be strongly considered (Helvetica?). It is somewhat ironic making an eye-catching bumper sticker, taking drivers attention away from the road, in the spirit of driving safety.

Without getting into a scientific debate, I would recommend upper and lowercase over caps (Bhouma, 1971) and opening up the tracking a bit (Pelli, 2008).

It may be worth finding out the predominant road-sign typeface in your clients region and select the same so it blends in with the typographic landscape.

Additionally, you may want to take the “+ frequency of exposure = good” approach. Following that logic you could rationalize using basterdized Airal caps. An interesting challenge. To counterbalance attention to the road with attention to the sticker while communicating a message.

2009-07-12 04:11:17 • William Berkson

SunnyGosal -

Consider whether your bumper sticker will be appearing on a long-haul semi truck or a neighborhood delivery vehicle. The legibility of the DIN-Interstate-Clearview highway fonts is tested and scored in the context of headline display letters on stationary signs posted along state or federal highways, and I don’t know if it’s all that applicable in the context of a bumper sticker that’s to be seen at local stop lights and businesss parking lots. A non-highway sans font might serve your purposes better.

Both Standard Highway Alphabet and Clearview are used in both the USA and Canada.

In fact, Canada embraced Clearview much faster than the US did. I believe that Canada does not have the federal equivalent of the FHWA, so these decisions were able be made on the municipal and provincial level without approval from the federal government.

At last count, Clearview was being used in Quebec, Ontario, Alberta, Manitoba, Saskatchewan, British Columbia.
