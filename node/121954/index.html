Celtic body typeface face suggestions

December 12, 2014 • VickersCreative

Hi all, I’m looking for some advice on choosing a body copy typeface…

I’m designing a logo for a client based on the hebridean isles off the west coast of Scotland, currently in the concept stage and not far from presenting these back to her. The logo is part of an identity job relating to a bespoke tartan which has been commissioned, and will be sold through gift shops.

The hebrides and west coast of Scotland have strong ties to Ireland going back to the days of St Columba, who founded his famous abbey on the island of Iona: http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Columba http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iona http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hebrides

The client has expressed a wish for me to include a calligraphic, manuscript looking face like Ophelia (https://www.fontfont.com/fonts/ophelia), Omnia (https://store1.adobe.com/cfusion/store/html/index.cfm?store=OLS-US&event=displayFont&code=OMNQ10005000) or Carolus Magnus (https://www.myfonts.com/fonts/fontfont/ff-carolus-magnus/)

However, I’m finding it hard to pair these blackletter/uncial faces with a legible body face for extended pieces of copy whilst keeping the same feel. Can anyone suggest anything which might be suitable?

Thanks, Lee

An interesting project to be sure! I can see various possibilities, based on constrains and design choices. There are faces suitable for body text with more or less subtle references to celtic/uncial/insular styles. The questions I think one should answer in order to narrow the search could be:

  • There need to be just one typeface, or there could be two (one more calligraphic for headers and such, and a “tamed” one for texts)?
  • How long would texts be (longer text = tamer typeface)?
  • There will be need of emphasis inside the texts? And, if so, it should be expressed with italics or it could be other means (darker weight, colour, a different style)?
  • As always, expected language coverage should be taken into account.

A couple of typefaces that could fit the bill, devoid of any historical or stylistic accuracy (found looking for “uncial text”): http://www.myfonts.com/fonts/eurotypo/farfa/ http://www.myfonts.com/fonts/delve/rieven-uncial-pro/

For a more modern take, SIAS' Hibernica could be an interesting choice. Perhaps paired with its elder sister Lapidaria for a wider range of styles and permutations: http://www.myfonts.com/fonts/andreas-stotzner/hibernica/ http://www.myfonts.com/fonts/andreas-stotzner/lapidaria/

It’s OK to use a newer face for body. Back in the day, the principle was to use progressively newer lettering styles as one proceeded down the hierarchy of type size, on the manuscript page.

I recommend two of my neo-incunabula styles, Paradigm and Goodchild. Paradigm, loosely inspired by Ratdolt’s work, has a kind of built-in distress parsing the effect of letterpress, or looking like it was done with a calligraphic pen at the small size limit of its ability. Goodchild is a robust Venetian, a Jenson revival, with a large x-height. So both styles will hold up very small.

(For a fuller-featured Goodchild, please contact me directly.)
