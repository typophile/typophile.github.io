This sounds like a great opportunity to involve the students in the writing, editing and design process. You can be the art director and manage the student teams. First, you might have students bring in magazines they like (if HS students still read magazines), then you can dissect the typefaces, grid and so on. Which will inform your layout decisions for your own magazine.

Do HS-aged youth still read magazines? When I was in high school I read Adbusters, Thrasher, and towards the end I remember reading Speak! and then on to Emigre. I also read the Burton catalog every fall until it fell apart in my hands. So what I’m really saying is that HS-students might like typographically sophisticated publications. I would advise not to condescend with “youth-looking” typefaces like comic sans.

If I was you, I might take a look at youworkforthem.com for headline faces, as their work is nothing if not contemporary and youthful (plus inexpensive).

But again, HS-aged people are intelligent. So it really might depend on appropriately matching the typography with content.

Or hire a suitable designer/director to run the workshop and make the zine a collaboration with the students.

2010-06-11 01:58:07 • Ray Larabie

When it comes to body text, I would think that the youngsters would have the same preferences as old fogies; something readable.

Assuming you have a good general selection of typefaces at your disposal - such as is provided with WordPerfect - you could try Optima, as a readable sans-serif. Or Palatino, if Garamond, or Baskerville, or Century Expanded are too old, and Times Roman too overused.

You could even use Kabel (or Geometric 231) if you were so minded. That was intensely fashionable in the 1920s, it is true, but its popularity is perennial.

(I may be giving you bad advice, though, but not deliberately. Were I intent on doing that, I would commend Papyrus and Comic Sans to your attention; as it is, they are faces generally avoided by typographers of taste, despite their overuse.)
