2005-06-24 20:19:09 • jupiterboy
Marnie's right: Photoshop invisibly transforms the RGB file to L*a*b before reconverting it to CMYK, so converting to L*a*b yourself is redundant. The transform is also high-bit (I forget, but at least 16bpp. It might be 18 or 20). So when you convert RGB->CMYK or do a profile->profile conversion, it goes: RGB (8 or 16 bit) -> L*a*b (high-bit) -> CMYK (8 or 16 bit). L*a*b, being a device-independent space, is effectively the middleman for any type of colour transform. W/r/t to retaining out-of-gamut colours, there are various approaches. First of all there are the four built-in conversion methods (rendering intents): - Relative colorimetric moves out-of-gamut colours into the new gamut and leaves in-gamut colours alone. It works well for most transforms and is my default setting. RelCol, unlike AbsCol, also remaps the white and black points of the old gamut to the new one. - Perceptual compresses the entire colour gamut in order to pull in o-o-g colours, so there may be some overall loss of saturation. It works well for photographic-type images with an overall mid-grey colour average (ie lots of different colours). It also remaps white and black points. Try it if RelCol gives a poor result. - Absolute Colorimetric clips o-o-g colours and represents old colour numbers from the viewpoint of the new gamut. Doesn't remap white/black points. It's used for soft-proofing on screen, for example. Never to be used for straightforward RGB->CMYK conversions. - Saturation retains saturation information at the expense of hue and luminosity. Good for big areas of pure colour -- ie not photo-type images, but block-colour designs etc. The bulldozer approach is to switch on Photoshop's gamut warning and work with the Hue/Saturation sliders until you can manually bring all colours within gamut. I've never managed to get great results doing this -- the built-in colour engine is a lot smarter than me :). One other approach is to change your CMYK working space. There are various different flavours of SWOP and ISO working spaces that have slightly different colour gamuts and better or worse transforms. The Adobe SWOP v2 space is good, but I've always got the best results with the old Kodak SWOP Press profile and build all my custom profiles around that. The last approach is to convert and then, post-conversion, work with the CMYK file to bring out the best possible balance of saturation and detail. This is what I generally end up doing if I have a problematic image to convert. In the old days this wasn't a problem because you sent your trannies to a good drum scanner op and he sent you back ready-separated CMYK files. You never even saw the RGB images, so didn't latch on to the wider gamuts available in RGB spaces. These days a good 70% of images sent to me are originated digitally and arrive as RGB (often untagged -- ie, with no information about the colour space they're originated in). You can read a lot more about this stuff at the Yahoo Colour Theory group. Or Google 'Dan Margulis makeready' for some fascinating colour-oriented articles. R
2005-06-25 13:32:05 • rjohnston
You seem to confuse a device's gamut with the color-space that device use for calculations. RGB and CMYK can both span the same spectrum: the differenent monitor and printer gamut is due to the phosphors and inks used, which span different subsets of the visible spectrum, and thus a conversation is needed. L*a*b* is used as a middleman because it's perceptually uniform: that is, linear distances between colors agree with the visual perception of that distance (unlike in the XYZ/RGB color-spaces, e.g.), and thus simplify the math involved in the conversation, since when in L*a*b* we can match near colors using a linear metric.
