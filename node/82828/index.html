2011-06-08 13:25:11 • ncaleffi

Cycles is an really great typeface, I have to say I am very content with it. I obtained it, when it was still under the normal license and it is to me a really perfect balanced serif font for extended text. It has a classic, old style inspired, appearance, a reasonable proportioned x-height, and the letters seem to harmonize well on a page. It does not have any too fancy features, as some old-style revivals or interpretations have (Garamond Premiere, Arno for example – they are beautiful, but a different category). Yet it is not as cold and sterile as some other typefaces are, like Times New Roman or according to some maybe even Sabon. I have to say I find it hard to find a classic looking text-face that would compete.

Having said that, I can only say that I have no experience how any font would behave in a professional environment, as I am not working in such. Although I printed with Cycles in ink, laser and offset, I am not the person who could evaluate its performance in a way that would matter to you.

Personally I like as well: [[http://www.signographie.de/cms/front_content.php?idcat=35|Andron]] (which has similar qualities to the mentioned cycles, but an almost universal character-set, on the other hand no optical sizing variations, like cycles), [[http://www.commercialtype.com/typefaces/lyon/lyon_text|Lyon]], [[http://www.terminaldesign.com/Fonts/#/Rawlinson2|Rawlinson]] and [[http://www.type-together.com/Edita|Edita]]. All are a bit wider set than Minion, which I find a tad to condensed and tight.

If you publish as well in the German, Swedish, Brasilian or other language markets with diacritic marks, then have a look at this issue before you decide on a typeface: http://typophile.com/node/40439 The before mentioned typefaces in my post all avoid this problem.

2011-06-09 18:05:45 • flooce

I’m not sure what you are looking for, but as you mention the new Bembo, I assume that’s Bembo book, you might have a look at [[http://www.fontbureau.com/fonts/WilliamsCaslonText/|Williams Caslon Text]]. It is also intended to capture the look of a classic metal font, in this case the “dark but open” look that Caslon, well printed in metal, had. I just recently went through the exercise of comparing it and Minion as far as economy. I was pleasantly surprised that used somewhat smaller size, it could match Minion in economy. Because it is a little wider, lighter, and a little looser spaced, it is equally as readable at the smaller size—well, more readable, but I am prejudiced.

If you are looking for a clean and modern look, I am also a huge fan of Whitman.
