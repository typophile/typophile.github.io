Dear Mr. anony,

I've been particularly annoyed with this thread
and really didn't know how to react to it, which
is why it's taken me so long to post.

Excuse me if this may sound harsh, but this is a
community of type designers, type users and -first
and foremost- type lovers, and to me your request
betrays a lack of respect for type. To create a
succesful type family takes talent, skill, and
countless hours, days, months, sometimes even
years of hard work. If I understand it correctly,
you're just looking for a free ticket, thus depriving
Mark van Bronkhorst from his livelyhood.
This is not the first time this happens.

As Nicolai pointed out, do you really think that
someone who went through all that trouble would
make the fruit of his labour available for free,
just like that. How would you feel if you didn't
get paid for your work say the next three months
or so?
Any freeware or shareware font you're likely to
find on the internet will most probably have design
flaws, poor kerning, incomplete character sets,
maybe even technical defects.
I hope you'll consider purchasing fonts in the
future. The addage "you get what you pay for" is
very true of freeware and share fonts. If you want
quality type, you must be prepared to pay for it.

And if you want to use share/freeware type: I have
absolutely no problems with that. But then at least
be honest about it and don't try to masquerade it
as genuine quality type.

This is a very personal statement and does not in
any way represent an official viewpoint of Typophile.

2004-03-02 22:41:23 • cheshiredave
OK … some dissenting points.

1) I certainly agree with the desire to have posters be honest about their
intentions. However, requiring every poster to show a respect for type is
just not realistic or even, IMO, reasonable. The vast majority of people out
there just don't know enough about typography to have respect for it. As a
result, we see plenty of posts from people who ignorantly believe that one
can get a freeware/shareware font comparable to Verdigris. It seems to me
that education would be more appropriate (and effective) than scolding.

1a) This type of font request is, IMO, very different from those occasionally
seen on the MyFonts forum, where one poster actually stated, "I'm not going
to pay money for fonts!". That type of post goes well beyond ignorance and
reflects a relatively recent, largely generational attitude that it's not necessary
to pay for fonts, music, software, etc. Those people (and they're legion)
definitely need verbal spankings.

2) Mr. van Bronkhorst's creations are truly splendid and he does indeed
deserve whatever price he asks. However, he rigidly offers his fonts as a
package only. Certainly there are many situations in which paying $159
to obtain a single desired font is out of the question. I can think of two fairly
recent posts here by well respected mainstays of this forum asking for
alternatives in (I believe) similar situations. I can't find those posts just now,
but I remember them verbally "walking on eggshells" for fear of condemnation.

In the case of the home hobbyist, noncommercial user or even small firm,
posters desiring a font they cannot reasonably afford have two choices: They
can ask that someone e-mail them the font (in which case they need a verbal
whacking) or they can seek a cheaper alternative. I see no reason why we
cannot help out the latter poster, educating them along the way. Fonts bundled
with applications (wihch the poster may even already have) can be a great option.
And most of those fonts supplied with MS apps (mostly Monotype) are of very
high quality.

Daniel,
I strongly disagree with the "junk yard" analogy, as that would infer
crappy near-uselessness of the merchandise.

There are very many freeware/shareware fonts that are extremely
useful to a niche that I sometimes think the professional designers
and font makers here can not relate to at all (and sometimes, dare
I say, resent greatly).

This niche doesn't in any way NEED top quality. And once they are
shown what their zero dollars will buy, I find that many of these
people are perfectly willing to settle for "good enough". This occurs
regularly on the MyFonts forum.

The fellow wanting to make a flyer for his local club is quite willing to
use the closest free font for his project. I submit that he shouldn't have
to phrase his request just so and show a respect for type in order to
post here.

So, a better analogy, IMO, would be a car sales lot. The prospective
customer seeking a much cheaper alternative to that Mercedes
might be shown a Kia instead. If he accepts that the Kia is the best
he can afford and states that the Kia will meet his lowly needs just
fine, why should he denigrated?

2004-03-03 17:14:49 • mike_f
I might as well put in my two cents here too, since this is an issue I am pretty opinionated about, like many of you who have commented earlier.

I find myself most in agreement with Mike Freiman, although in the right circumstances I feel like Yves too. The difference is in the purpose or intent of the user, and unfortunately we are usually only able to take their word for it about those things.

If someone is willing to take a near-match because they can't afford the real thing for the purpose at hand, or if they are just looking for something with certain similar qualities, I think we can refer them to places where that can be found.

If they want to get the real fonts for free they can go somewhere else, and I think we ought to refer them to a special FAQ page about why that sort of question is improper, unethical and unfair (and whatever other terms Yves used above).

My 'gray area' happens when the font they want is only available as a 'clone' version, as far as we know. I will usually recommend those fonts. The FontBank fonts are a good example, because I think often the font the posters are trying to duplicate came from that collection in the first place.

I am all for education, and promotion of original, quality fonts. That's why I like to credit the designers – so people realize this was someone's creative work. However, I realize, as Mike said, that there are situations when that expense can't be justified. Those are times when we should suggest alternatives, but I don't mean rip-offs of the same font. We should explain that if you aren't willing to pay for it, then you must be willing to settle for something else, but here are some things that might be similar that come with applications you may have, etc. I think it's a challenge, because most of us don't spend much time looking for cheap approximations, but if we have ideas for them, that would be the place for it.

The last thing we need to try to be doing is guessing at their motives, because there is no assurance that they will tell us the truth. You either want the original,and will pay for it, or you are willing to take something similar that we might suggest.
