March 7, 2004 • Nick Shinn

How big should the "-mark" symbols be in a font?

Many fonts have the trademark and copyright mark at full cap height. Others have the Registered mark as a superior.

But surely this is a mistake, because while it makes sense in the main text, if a typographer then has to set the character in an accompanying footnote, it becomes the size of a fly dropping, and too small to decipher without a magnifying glass.

What's your experience?

I think the copyright mark should match the numerals in vertical span, while the registered and trademark should be floating to the height of the caps (but not too small).

hhp
2004-03-07 20:30:21 • hrant
The contrary argument is that at display sizes, the registered and trademark need to be even smaller than the main text.

In general, copyright is supposed to align with the body text, and registered and trademark symbols should be superiors; that is, about 1/3 of their size above the cap height, and 2/3 below, with the overall size being considerably smaller than the cap size. (How much smaller being dependent on both the weight of the font and the intended average point size of usage.)

2004-03-07 20:52:43 • Thomas Phinney
>the intended average point size of usage

But in practice that will vary:

In my experience designing ads, flyers, and marketing newsletters, the Registered symbol is used in body text (size 8 - 14 pt) as a superscript to a brand name.

Then it is repeated in a footnote (with the name of the owner of the brand) as small as possible, (4 - 6 pt).

I always prefer it when fonts have a cap-height Registered symbol, because it is easy to "superscript" the symbol in the body copy, but very difficult to increase the size of a pre-shrunk symbol in the footnote and not have it look too bold and misaligned vertically. If you just set it straight, it's but a speck.

That's why I make cap height Reg symbols in the fonts I design, unless they are outright display faces.

However, I seem to be going against the flow here, so I would like to confirm with typographers:
Do you prefer a pre-shrunk Registered symbol in your text fonts?

2004-03-09 19:23:38 • gerald_giampa
How far should the mark match the style of the typeface -- in particular, why do most italic fonts have roman marks?

The answer, as always, is: it depends.

I have strongly suspected that some sloppy font producers throw in a generic humanist "r-ball" or "tm" on any font they create. To me, it's an obvious error if the R in the registration mark doesn't bear a strong resemblance to the R in the typeface it "modifies."

The "depends" part deals with condensing and/or obliqueing the circle along with the R inside the circle. That's just wrong! Keep the circle round. Then condense or oblique (if you have to, but you really should re-draw) the R within the circle.

Of course, after I've gone through the process of doing all that, the effect can be so distracting that I throw the end product out and default to the roman registration mark. Italic Rs within a circle sometimes look silly. Italic TMs sometimes look silly. Avoid silly.

Thomas, I agree with Gerald -- providing alternate versions is a great solution -- I'd be tempted to provide a version set at x-height as well.


2004-03-09 20:57:51 • kristin
In the metal type days, fonts did not come with matching registered, trademark, or copyright symbols. These were purchased separately in a range of sizes and had generic designs. They came in only serif or sans serif if there was any choice--no condensed, bold or italics. Phototypesetting fonts were similarly limited. The idea of having these characters match the font is a recent development and one that seems not to have been completely worked out yet.
