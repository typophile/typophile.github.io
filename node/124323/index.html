When would thin and hair spaces need to be breakable

April 2, 2015 • inktrap

Are there any uses for thin or hair space where it wouldn’t make sense for them to be non-breaking? I ask because I can’t think of any, and the fact they aren’t hard spaces makes them pretty unusable in reflowable text such as web. Why did Unicode not at least go with two equal sets of spaces, one of which would be non-breaking, instead of just a single non-breaking word space?
The intent of those were originally to do fine-tuning by hand on metal justified type and should not be breakable. Even in today’s world of digital type they are still useful.

2015-04-03 13:15:46 • Anthony Barnett

According to the Unicode Standard Annex #14, Line Breaking Properties, the following characters are in the class BA, which means there is an opportunity to break after, but not before.

That being said, the breaking algorithm (cf Example Pair Table) specifies that you may not break after a character in BA if it is followed by a character in one of the classes CL, EX, SY, IS, ZW and WJ.

EX contains the exclamation mark and the question mark; IS contains the semicolon. It appears that French typography is saved and does not need the narrow no-break space (U+202F; cf Unicode Notes on French). A consequence of the algorithm is that those characters may not be “unusable in reflowable text such as web” as you say.

2015-04-03 19:51:26 • Michel Boyer

The intent of those were originally to do fine-tuning by hand on metal justified type and should not be breakable.

In the case of characters with a place in Unicode, a hairline space indeed should not be breakable, since it would be used for letterspacing - or for adjusting kerning (but that is not a “legitimate” use, because the font is responsible for getting that right).

But when a hair space was used in metal type, it might well have been used to adjust the size of a breakable space between words. Not that text gets to reflow after it’s set in metal, of course… but it means that the example given here seems to contradict, rather than reinforce, the point being made.

But this gets me to thinking of some possible pre-Unicode computer representations of text, in which a hairline space might have the same meaning as with metal type.

Let’s think of an IBM Executive typewriter as a first step to a concrete example. It used a coarse 5-unit proportional spacing system; it had typebars, being an ordinary electric typewriter otherwise, not a Selectric.

The space bar had a little “2” printed on it, with a segment on the left side having “3” on it. The 2 unit space was the normal word space, the 3 unit space was a wider space which took up the same amount of space as a digit, useful for tabular matter.

The backspace key went back by only one unit.

So it didn’t have a hairline space key that advanced by 1 unit; when justifying lines by a second retyping, one would have to remember the sequence 2, 3, 22, 23, 33, 223, 233, 333 to know what to type for the successive widths of spaces.

Thus, in a phototypesetter system where the unit system is hardcoded and fairly coarse, one could imagine a code that stands for a “one-unit space” which might be part of a group of several spaces, and the sequences of spaces as a whole might be either breaking or non-breaking. (Presumably this would be determined by the presence of a “justifying space” of variable width in the cluster, although a non-breaking justifying space is also useful - one doesn’t want a non-breaking space to have to be at risk of winding up conspicuously narrower than the other spaces on the same line.)
