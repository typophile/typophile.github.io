Stylising Japanese characters

November 1, 2006 • ill sans

Any tips on how to stylise Japanese characters for a logo? What I basically want to know is how much of the characters can be stylised? Eg. if you make the curves straight, is it still legible or do you run the risk of involuntarely producing other characters? I’m starting with Japanese Hiragana, but wouldn’t want to end up with something that would read a whole other thing in Katana.

My understanding is that there is very little room for play with Japanese characters. Someone once told me that there are only a few fonts used on Japanese signs since the subtlety and complexity of the characters greatly narrows the stylistic options for font designers.

For the greatest legibility you will probably have to consult with fluent reader or maybe even a group of them.

Another option could be limiting your scope to a smaller character set. If you are only designing Hiragana and Katakana then you would presumably have more room to play than if you are trying to design several thousand characters.

You may want to consider Japanese similar to the roman alphabet. If you are looking to make a highly readable, body-text typeface then your creative options become much more narrow. However, if style precludes crystal clear legibility then that is just a choice you have to make.

Hope that helps,

2006-11-01 19:09:03 • hrant

When I was learning to write Hiragana and Katakana it was explained to me that Hiragana should be written with more “sweeping” strokes while Katakana should have more even “rigid” strokes. It helped me to think of Hiragana strokes as brush strokes and Katakana strokes as pen strokes. I guess the point is that according to native speakers even though the characters do overlap there are stylistic diferences that signify the differences. For example か (Hiragana) & カ (Katakana) are both “ka” in the same font.

Another significant difference to note – especially if you are looking to focus on a certain word – is how Hiragana and Katakana are meant to be used for different types of words. For example, Katakana is used to transcribe foreign words. Wikipedia has some pretty good information about Katakana usage and history.
