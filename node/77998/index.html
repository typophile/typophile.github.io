Price is what you can convince someone to pay, not what you think you are worth. The right price is the one where both you and your customer are happy with the transaction and want to do it again.

Most designers sell themselves short by a long shot — they’re great at creating value, horrible at capturing that value. The world is full if talented designers who are terrible business people, pricing themselves by what their expenses are (or worse, by what they ‘deserve’). Some designers even feel guilty charging for their work. This is the difference between designers and business people. Business people do not feel guilty when they close a great deal, they look for a bigger deal.

2011-01-14 12:31:48 • Frode Bo Helland

While a small item on your homepage, I notice you do books. In the States, rates vary according to the kind of book. Design & production supervision for a museum catalog for a prestigious museum can run $20,000. On the other hand, design, with no production supervision, for a scholarly monograph interior runs about $700, with a like amount for the jacket.

As far as hourly rates go, our shop charges $60/hr for things not done on a unit basis (most are unit prices). We will only work for a press that has an editorial dept, and handles their own production. My wife’s shop, which specializes in more hand-holding (i.e., they will arrange for editorial services, and handle all production), charges $75/hr.

In short, I think your question is too broad. As with anything, one charges what the market will bear, constrained by costs. If your prices are high, some folks will look elsewhere. No argument, or citation of rates others charge will change their mind. But if you have enough work, somebody is obviously willing to pay your prices.

“What the traffic will bear” as opposed to “how hungry are you” is a worldwide measure of what to charge. If you want to first look at a comparison of GNP and average earnings per capita, you can probably figure a sliding scale per country. Some places are tough to figure because of great disparity in wealth from one person to the next. In the USA, there is a great disparity so what you charge a neighborhood shop keeper is not what you charge a mega-corporation (if they will even talk to you). Truthfully, your reputation must precede you to get connected to those who can pay the higher rates. I think James is right when he says expenses vs livable income. A college professor in design who has a tenured position and freelances can afford to charge less because he knows his bills are paid. For the same reason, he can charge more for jobs or clients that don’t interest him. Either way, when someone is not hungry, they can pick and choose. I have seen well known design firms do pro bono work because it interests them.

IMO, serious web/graphic… any designer must have a couple “fixed”, stable and serious clients, clients that you’ll work with for long term relationships and, for example, if you have 5 clients, you can expect that you’ll have at least one job per month from then.

Under this way of working, there are a lot of subquestions that can be asked… but I found that one as the best way I personally work and so far it gets me more jobs and people made a trust in me as a part of their company even I’m freelancing. Even I get a nice bonuses from time to time. Both way trust and catchy communication is the key where I’ll put my energy to do my job the best, to be as much as original as it can be, to make a client feel like I’m really (and I am) always there for them, even they don’t have a money to pay me in that moment….

Look on clients as a girls, try to impress them, to take care of them and they’ll show you what they can do for you. But you have to build experience to find a good and right people to work with, otherwise you’ll end up naive.

I know this sounds like some guide or religious preaching… but in my case, this gets me, on monthly bases, more and more jobs and I can keep the run with tempo of new projects.
