2009-12-27 21:35:32 • quadibloc

Tom wrote: The fact that OpenType has still difficulty handling it, has nothing to do with calligraphers setting the bar high.

I agree. I think one can also be quite precise in just what aspect of nastaliq is most difficult for OpenType. The actual shaping of letter groups (what Tom calls fusions) is really quite easy, especially if one avoids using ligatures and instead uses a set of contextual letter shapes with GPOS cursive attachment positioning. This is not difficult, either from a design perspective nor from a layout programming perspective. The thing that is very difficult in OpenType – is, in fact, the greatest weakness of the OpenType Layout model – is the interaction of spacing and mark positioning. Shaping the letter groups is easy: positioning the letter groups relative to each other and then placing the dots and other marks relative to the letter groups is where the whole thing becomes very difficult indeed. The problem isn’t particular to OpenType, but to any font layout technology that has inherited concepts of sidebearings and kerning from metal type. Simply put, this paradigm doesn’t work for nastaliq (it doesn’t work well for any Arabic style, but is particularly bad for the strongly diagonal styles).

@John Hudson: Ligatures are a dumb way to implement Arabic, and especially nastaliq.

I hadn’t been thinking of ligatures as a method of implementation of the unique features of the Nastaliq script.

But I can see where the idea could have occurred to you that this was what I was talking about.

Recently, I found this image

of the Arabic type due to Smith and Hallock in [[http://books.google.ca/books?id=8QwAAAAAMAAJ|a book]] on Google Books: on page 134 of a book titled “The Fourth Exhibition of the Massachusetts Charitable Mechanic Association, at Quincy Hall, in the City of Boston, September 16, 1844”.

This illustration of the type shows how successive letters that move the baseline up or down are still individual types. So words need to be built up with short pieces of leading. In the metal type era, this was a serious issue.

Hence, for naksh, if not nastal`iq, ligatures did become the preferred method of implementing the calligraphic niceties:

as I learned from The Traditional Arabic Typecase Extended to the Unicode Set of Glyphs by Yannis Haralambous.

And, yes, he may have chosen the “dumb” way to upgrade typesetting for Urdu and Farsi to what was available for Arabic, since fiddling around with leading is no longer an issue in the computer age. But I’m more inclined to praise his efforts as a start, to establish what the minimal things that need to be done are, as part of the progress towards doing them right.

Of course, by now, what with the efforts of people like Thomas Milo, the Arabic script is, or is close to being, a solved problem, I suspect: excuses to accept substandard typography on technical grounds are no longer valid, even if there is still room for progress.

2012-04-04 20:26:27 • hrant

Further on this topic:

The paper by Yannis Haralambous on the Cairo or ’Almatab‘ al’amarya typecase does note the purposes that the ligatures serve, dividing them into a limited number of classes - about twenty - and so it can serve as a guide to a non-ligature implementation.

What I really would have loved to have found somewhere would have been detailed explanations of the font designed by Mühendis-oğlu and the “American Arabic” of Smith and Hallock - so that a comparison would be possible.

Because, of course, a proper analysis of the problem requires one not only to make contact with the Arab calligraphic tradition, but also to systematically survey the previous typographic efforts to determine what portions of that tradition are considered most significant.

It might be that some calligraphic niceties actually inhibit readability instead of promoting it, and so effort might be being put into the equivalent of repeating Gutenberg’s mistake of preferring blackletter to Roman.

Also, while there has been discussion of “Simplified Arabic”, where only two versions of each letter, rather than four, are used as being the ultimate debasement of the script, it has seemed to me that this merely displaces the join between two letters to always be on the first letter of a joined pair - which works on a typewriter, but not on metal type - and thus does not result in anything being lost.

That is, it doesn’t result in anything additional being lost as against typical ordinary fonts which incorporate the drastic simplification of having only four forms for each letter, and having an unchanging baseline - that is, those which follow the lead of the “Yakout” font for Linotype machines.

2012-04-05 00:41:43 • quadibloc

Hrant: Unless one implements a (much) better application of ligation.

In explaining the implementation in the Brill types to the client, I coined the term ‘ligaturing’ to make it clear that what is involved is an action, not a thing. A ligature, in contrast, is a technical solution to joining behaviour involving the display of multiple characters as a single glyph. And that is what I consider dumb in the implementation of Arabic script, and increasingly for other scripts, including Latin: the only time ligatures make sense are when the representation of the multiple characters is not decomposable into linking contextual and positioned forms, e.g. in some of the Byzantine Greek combinations where the characters are represented by what amounts to a new form. In Arabic, ligatures are particularly dumb because a ligature system cannot capture the range of joining behaviours represented in the script without tens of thousands of ligature glyphs (the old Monotype nastaliq had c. 22,000 and still failed in some situations), whereas the natural efficiency of the writing system means that all those behaviours can be captured with just a couple of hundred well designed glyphs.

Tom Milo’s ACE engine definitely does it best. Recent OpenType fonts that have followed his lead – avoiding ligatures, breaking the script down into achigraphemes, shaping joining behaviour on the fly, and contextually adjusting the position of dots and marks – include some very nice design work, and some innovative solutions within the strictures of the OpenType Layout architecture, but they still don’t come close to the flexibility provided by the combination of the ACE engine, ACE fonts and the Tasmeem UI. They are, despite the beauty of their designs, hampered by a technology that wasn’t designed based on a thorough analysis of the Arabic script, and which involves serious bottlenecks.

I’ll be talking about one such project at ISType in Istanbul this summer: and will discuss the design achievements, as well as both what OpenType has been able to do well and where it runs into problems.

In this connection, the “American Arabic” of Smith and Hallock did implement Naksh by casting individual characters as “linking contextual and positioned forms”.
