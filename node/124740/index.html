First, I did a visual search for it on three major type databases. No hits. Second, the era. In 1961, something like this would be done by a lettering artist, not a typesetter. A digital typeface that evokes the same vibe would be Mr. Rafkin, by Sudtipos. It’s based on the sort of hand-lettering prevalent in advertising until the advent of the desktop computer: http://www.myfonts.com/fonts/sudtipos/mr-rafkin/ To replicate the nameplate, search on Google for the largest, clearest sample of the magazine cover you can find. Then import the image into Adobe Illustrator and autotrace the logo to create a vector version.

EDITED: A leading men's magazine of the era, but without the "skin" or soft pornographic content of _The Gent_ or _Playboy_, was _Esquire_. It's title was digitized by Harold Lohner as Esquivel [img:sites/default/files/old-images/Esquivel_4414.gif] [img:sites/default/files/old-images/EsquivelAsEsquire_4874.jpg] http://haroldsfonts.com/portfolio/esquivel/ _Gent_ was a minor competitor. I looks to me like the design of the _Gent_ title is intended at least in part -- note the double story _E_ -- to imitate the flavor of the _Esquire_ title. Another font with the "same vibe" would be the lowercase of Holly Script [img:sites/default/files/old-images/HollyscriptAs_gent_4111.gif] http://www.myfonts.com/fonts/urw/holly-script/ Don
