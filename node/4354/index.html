2004-04-22 04:44:08 • kakaze
> Just surf around places were "common" people go

Well, let he who is without sin cast the first stone (only joking Chris ).

I don't think it's necessarily about purism, or linguistic imperialism (I'm sure Polish speakers get equally wound up about greengrocers' ogoneks or whatever). To me it's about supposedly qualified people who are quite well paid to do one thing (copyeditors) not doing it properly and thereby ultimately making language less beautiful and useful.

I'm most certainly not a lingua-purist -- I'm quite content with neologisms and I'm quite content to let archaisms like 'an history' and the don't-end-a-sentence-with-a-preposition rule slide. But it annoys the hell out of me to be reading a quality newspaper and find redundant parentheses (commas all over the place where they're not, needed) and the like. It confuses meaning and annoys a close reader.

It is actually the job of the copyeditor to uphold certain values in language and punctuation. He/she should know not just the correct usage of colons and semicolons, but should be able to, e.g: distinguish em and en dashes; correct 'three period' ellipses; remove redundant punctuation; etc.

I think when these skills decline -- as they are declining -- we lose a certain amount of precision from our language, and are thus less able to be precise in our communication. That's important, and worth getting wound up about. Wack apostrophes in the local supermarket, not so much.

Are they declining?
In England there was a neglect of teaching English properly at primary schools throughout the 1980s & much of the 1990s, but under the new National Curriculum, standards seem to be improving, and going beyond the levels that were prevalent before the decline. My kids learn grammar in a much more formal way than I ever did.
I think we (in the 1970s) were expected to pick up the rules through osmosis, via exposure to correct English, rather than by actually being told what they were.

I am concerned about cultural imperialism (even within England, the language police have been, and continue to be, busy suppressing regional and class variations in speech and writing).
On the other hand, I think that where precision is important, it is necessary to be able to write exactly what you mean, and to expect others to be able to correctly read what you have written. The primary purpose is communication, and if the rich tapestry of cultural variation gets in the way, then maybe that's not a good thing.

2004-04-22 16:33:17 • hrant
>>Maybe we should rethink using words such as "damage" and "proper"

Yeah, agreed. That's sloppy on my part. Variation and innovation in language is of course inevitable. But variation/innovation can be beautiful and intelligent -- when it makes its own kind of sense and/or grows out of a love and deep understanding of communication and language -- or it can be just dumb, lazy mistakes that get to seem OK because they're repeated often enough by 'authoritative' sources. E.g. I really enjoy reading David Foster Wallace's stuff. He has an amazing ear for language. You find yourself eventually thinking the way he writes, which is very far from 'proper' usage a lot of the time (beginning sentences with 'And but' etc.). And regional vernaculars often have their own grammatical tics which make total sense within their contexts.

But there is usage, I think, which is just plain wrong and ugly -- generally usage which is trying to be transparent and authoritative but which falls over owing to lack of knowledge or skill on someone's part (either the writer, who can be excused, or the copyeditor, who can't).

2004-04-22 16:38:12 • rjohnston
This is the Post-modern era, and the authority of printed text is not what it was.

So the "sloppier" grammar of speech (and the "semi-speech" of email) finds its way back to the printed medium, dumbing it down, but also making it closer to the mainstream of reality.

Two landmarks: the Watergate Tapes, and the Roger Clemmons and the Boston Press Incident, really showed up the difference between corrected, edited, proper grammar, as it is supposed to be done in print, and the vagaries of language as it is spoke.

It was quite revealing to see, from transcriptions of the Watergate tapes, just how un-proper real speech is. (Of course, the plays of Beckett and Pinter made the point a lot earlier.)

Clemmons was always complaining how he was mis-quoted by the sports press in Boston, so they decided to report his utterances verbatim, without the benefit of normal "speech-to-text" copy-editing, thereby making him come across as a complete neanderthal.

The point is not that normal people are "morons", but that printed text has for a long time been mis-representing normal speech, and this is a correction.
