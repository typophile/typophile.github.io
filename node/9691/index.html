I'm attaching a jpeg image of a typical plaque.

I've actually been doing a fair amount of research on this over the past several days, and I'm learning that, as usual, you get what you pay for. I have a budget for the simplest casting process, which gives you crudely cut letters and ornamental borders.

Most commercial operations use a computer-driven process. They take my art, in the form of an eps file, and produce rubber a mold, which in turn replicates a negative image of my plaque in plaster. The molten bronze is poured into the plaster mold. Even if I do a great job of the typography, this process forces a two dimensional object to live in three dimensions, and the results are dicey.

To make it worse, in 3d, a letter needs "draw," that is, seen in section, it has a bit of a slope on each side. This allows the casting to be separated from the mold after the bronze has hardened. The foundry sands the raised elements to a "satin" finish–but as they remove material, the letter forms get fat & awkward. It's like applying a "bold" style in Word.

I've tried to minimize the bad effects by using a robust sans serif font (Syntax), which can stand up to some abuse. It's also possible to get better results by doing a chemical etch instead of a casting, but many of my clients expect a cast plaque.

For about 3 times my per-plaque budget, good foundries will create a mold by hand and use some real artistry in shaping letters that are sculptural and quite beautiful.

The question remains, though, are there any techniques out there that will let me improve the results without tripling my budget?
