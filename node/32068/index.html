I wanted to know how to add diacritic marks when typing arabic words using the english alphabet. For example a dot needs to be placed under various letters like H and T to indicate that they are guttural sounds. I've been trying to figure this out for months and have been unable to. Any help would be GREATLY APPRECIATED. Thanx "Non-pure abjads (such as Hebrew and Arabic script) and abugidas use diacritics for denoting vowels. Hebrew and Arabic also indicate consonant doubling and change with diacritics; Hebrew and Devanagari use them for foreign sounds. Devanagari and related abugidas also use a diacritical mark called a virama to mark the absence of a vowel." - WIKIPEDIA
In Word (2007 version on previous version with service packs) use a font that either has these characters pre-composed or supports combining diacritics, such as the latest version of Times New Roman, or one of the fonts from SIL. Use the insert symbol dialog to insert pre-composed characters or combine the "dot accents" as needed.

Best way is to make up the characters in a program like FontLab. Most of what you want already has a Unicode number -- see the Latin Extended Additional code sheet. For characters that are not precomposed in Unicode, you need to make up the character and give it a glyph NAME (it will not have a Unicode index) of its Unicode components. Take a look at Adam's writeup on this method at the MSN FontLab site, he also covers how to write the feature in the font that will allow them to be more easily entered in a program like InDesign. Most existing commercial fonts I know of do not have these characters. Probably your best bet is Gentium, available from the SIL site -- but Google "gentium" for the download info. Alas, Gentium does not have ligatures or kerning -- which takes you back into making your own characters in the fonts you want -- where the EULA allows, as with all Adobe fonts. I believe Adobe has some plans to revisit some of their fonts and add these characters, but that will be for the future. Maybe the people who frequent this forum know of fonts where they already exist, but a lot of our work involves setting texts where such characters are needed, but not available, so I know a good bit about what is not available in traditional fonts. Linotype Palatino (TT) may have the underdots for transliterated Arabic, but not, I think, the underlines (not used in Arabic are they?) -- but used in Sanskrit. As far as entering them in InDesign, you can use the glyph pallet (the worst way), or enter them directly by using the Unicode number. You can also have them in the text you import, and they will work fine. Good luck.

Actually, there is a way to typeset these marks. The same way we had to use in the old days of photocomp. Set your "d", then set a period, kern it back, lower it, make it smaller if you need to. Now, when you set your next letter, it will likely collide with the "d", so you will have to kern it forward. And so on. With the macron over an "r", same technique. And again the same for an "r" with both a macron & underdot. A lot of work, and one reason why the older editions of the Chicago Manual recommended not using the underdots in transliterated Arabic. So 7 gained opposite (8-3) in need ?! ______________________ Rules of Transliteration: 1. Sokôn — the a vowel-less sign — is transliterated into (-), making the consonant stationary as there is no vowel after it, as n is gun + d in bad 2. Shadda is transliterated by doubling the letter it hovers above. When Shadda appears over any consonant, that consonant is voiced twice, as n in run-ner. But in Arabic , the letter is voiced separately, run-ner, fod-der, the first is stationary. 3. [a and â], [e and ê], [o and ô] Fatha that sounds like u in cut or but is transliterated into (a). Fatha+Alif that sounds slightly longer as in far is transliterated into (â) Fatha+Alif+Mad that sounds like extra long aa is transliterated into (ââ) Kasra that sounds like i in pin or sin is transliterated into (e) . Kasra+ya that sounds slightly longer as ee in seen is transliterated into (ê) Kasra+ya+Mad that sounds like extra long ee is transliterated into (êê) Zamma that sounds like u in put is transliterated into (o) . Zamma+Waw that sounds slightly longer oo as in soon is transliterated into (ô) Zamma+Waw+Mad that extra long oo is transliterated into (ôô) In the beginning of a word or syllable (after a dash), a, e and o are consonants. But, if they appear after a consonant they are vowels. 4. 3ain — voiced from middle throat — is transliterated into (‘). Thus (‘a) and ‘â], [‘e and ‘ê], [o and ‘ô] are consonants wherever they appear. Just got into the office & checked. Palatino (TT) does not have the needed diacritics. Gentium does, including the ones for transliterated Indic Scripts (Devanagari). Gentium is fine for casual use, but as mentioned, not quite up to the task of typesetting books. In pasing, I have seen translitertated Arabic that also uses a dot below the "D" as well as H S T and Z. Lower case and small caps also needed. The "lines" over "a" "e" and "o" can be handled routinely with the macron over the Latin vowels, included in many OpenType fonts in upper & lower case, though often not with small capitals. I haven't seen any commercial fonts that include hamzas and ayns; the usual convention is to use an apostrophe (/quoteright) & single open quote (/quoteleft); special sorts can be made up, but of course, once you get into making up your own characters for a font, you can pretty much make anything you need.
