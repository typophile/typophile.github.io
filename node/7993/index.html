Squared Sans

December 3, 2004 • William Berkson

There seems to be a recent trend of partially squared Sans faces. There is Thirstype's Apex Sans, Christian Schwartz's Simian from House Industries, and the new Klavika by Eric Olson from his Process Type Foundry. Also here in the critique section, we have Tramway, being developed by Hanno Bennert.

There are probably a lot of others I haven't mentioned. What do you all think of this trend? How do you compare the different fonts in this category?

I think Simian, which is wide, is very stylishly done. Klavika is striking because it brings some warmth to this style, and seems exceptionally readable for a sans.
Personally I find them to fill a gap in the classification of sans serif typefaces. Somewhere in between Geometric and beyond Humanist.

There is also Font Bureau's Stainless and Amplitude.

"The origin of the "Super Ellipse" has an historical curiosity.
Piet Heine, was a Dutch architect, poet and acknowledged
creative genius. (Somna, Hex, Super-ellipse, Super-egg,
"Grooks", etc.)
Well, regarding the super-ellipse, here is the story.
In 1959 Piet Heine the architect was approached by the Stockholm
city planners to solve a difficult problem in urban planning.
This was the first time Heine ever focused on the subject of
ellipses. The city square, with all the streets leading into it,
needed a fresh approach, a pleasing architectural shape to create
a focal point for the town. None of the city planners were able
to solve the problem. The square didn't work, the ellipse didn't
work and the "segmented ellipse" didn't work. Indeed, it was a
devious puzzle.
Enter Piet Heine … Voila! … and the super-ellipse was born!
The curious thing about this story is that Hermann Zapf had used
a "super-ellipse" like shape in the design of his typeface called,
"Melior", which was designed in 1949 and commercially released in
1952. Now, that is 7-10 years earlier then the "super-elllipse"
was invented!"
