2006-01-09 06:37:36 • John Hudson
On the subject of accent angle in contemporary, non-culturally specific type design, the story of Matthew Carter’s Fenway is quite interesting. The original Fenway has grave and acute accents with angles close to 45 degrees. When Maxim Zhukov asked if Matthew would donate copies of the fonts to use in the ATypI Country Delegates report a few years ago, Matthew supplied a modified version to set the Polish report, so that Polish names would be displayed with steeper accents. The publication used both sets of fonts, tailored to different nationalities, much to Adam Twardoch’s delight. When we came to typeset the ATypI/Graphis publication Language Culture Type, Maxim wanted to use Fenway again, and I went about making an OpenType version to facilitate the typesetting and added a number of diacritics for other languages not supported by the Matthew’s fonts (e.g. Chinese and Arabic transliteration characters). Matthew at this point decided that he preferred the steeper accents he had made for the Polish fonts to the original forms, and so these became the default forms in the OpenType font used to set Language Culture Type.

Well, to answer your questions, firstly I meant horizontal as in “more horizontal…”. There are a few resons for this. Firstly, because of the space economy and the fact that nearly horizontal (or flatter, if you will) accents are easier to work with, especially for accented caps. You see, in French (from France), it is preferred to just leave the accents off caps. In Italian, the accent is replaced by an apostrophe after the letter (since the accents occur almost exclusively at the ends of words).

However, in Quebec usage, caps are supposed to be accented as well. Besides that, I just like the freedom of having as many options as possible.

The second reason for the horizontal accents is that it’s much closer to the way accents are handwritten. I don’t think I’ve ever seen handwriting or printing with accents like those of most typefaces, but rather they are usually quite horizontal and take up the full width of the letter. So, I personally find that this style of accent is more natural.

As far as your 1950s French Bodoni, Nick, I can understand the disctiction between the two (since they can be seen as two distinct symbols rather than mirror images of each other).

Another weird thing about accents is Italian. It makes frequent use of the grave accent (to signify stress), but the accent is often replaced by an apostrophe (a carryover from the days of typewriters I guess), or, the accent is sometimes written toward the right side of the letter:

Si può dire “un bambino”, quindi la forma maschile sarà tronca Si puo' […] sara' tronca Si puo [...] sara tronca

In printed and handwritten texts the first two are both common (the second one because some people are just too lazy to bother finding accents on their keyboards). The last one is just a variation on the placement of the accent (probably because people in the old days were too lazy to go backwards on their typewriter to type the accent over the letter) and is only seen in handwriting. For the record, only the actual accented letter is “correct”, not that anyone takes heed.

In an all-caps setting, both of these would be common:

SI PUO' […] SARA' TRONCA

SI PUÒ […] SARÀ TRONCA

Why? Probably just because Italians have to do everything in the most complicated way possible. Variety is the spice of life, after all…

Anyway, in my limited experience with accents (I only speak French, Italian, and Spanish, so I can only speak from those viewpoints), I have come to the conclusion that while, on the surface, it seems like all these languages use the exact same accents (because we give the same name to the same direction of accent), in fact the preference of shape varies from language to language and country to country. I guess in a perfect world we would have several choices of accents to use, so that Poles, Czechs, Quebecers, and Italians, and the many other users of diactrical marks could all be contented.

In the meantime I need to create some tighly lead all-caps French for consuption in Quebec so… any suggestions?

You see, in French (from France), it is preferred to just leave the accents off caps.

This is a fairly recent and bad habit adopted as a result of the limitations of typewriters. If you look at older French books, you will see the accents on caps. If you look at quality French typesetting such as practiced by the Imprimerie nationale, you will see the accents on caps.

The accents on caps are almost always flatter than on the lowercase letters (unless the font is specifically Polish), so I guess you are looking for very flat cap accents.
