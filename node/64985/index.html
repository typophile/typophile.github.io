How web fonts are different

December 3, 2009 • Garrick Van Buren

As I mentioned at TypeCon this summer, I think web fonts are different creatures than desktop fonts (just as desktop fonts are different creatures from woodcut fonts). The Web is a different medium than print - as such some fonts work better with the medium than others. I wrote up a first pass at the broad characteristics of a font that make it work well on the Web, what I'm calling: 'web native' - [[http://garrickvanburen.com/archive/web-fonts-identifying-a-new-species|Web Fonts – Identifying a New Species]]. I've boiled it down to openness and speed-of-communication in both letterforms (large x-heights, open counters, etc) and licensing (OFL, MIT, etc). What have I missed?
If you are only interested in @font-face-based options, you might specify that at the top of your page. Other models are going to be equally prevalent for various licensing and display purposes. @font-face is a part of the picture, so it seems inaccurate to describe this subset of web font technologies as "Web Fonts" or even "web-native" fonts. Many commercial and proprietary fonts will be engineered for native web use as well. Our previous model for web-readiness of fonts was the system fonts that come with PCs. Those, rather than the primitive early designs from Emigre, set a benchmark, and satisfy the most important criteria for robust web display. Openness and speed in the context of fonts should also refer to fonts' technical abilities to download and display quickly and well on any device. Fonts won't perform, and therefore can't be considered web-native, if they aren't also built intentionally for the low-res media of web/mobile devices. So consider adding a third trait: web-native engineering. Unhinted TTFs, however free, open, or distributable, aren't likely to gain wide acceptance on the web if they look like hell. There have already been missteps in this area. A permissive license doesn't preclude a font from failing in this new medium. I wonder if you can tighten up some of your language. "the thin and thick parts of the letterforms ... should be close." This implies they are close in proximity. You might say about letterforms that web fonts should be low-contrast or that their hairlines should be thicker than those of ordinary print fonts. I would also suggest calling the enhanced visual quality "readability" rather than "scannability", since the reality is that web fonts are going to be part of increased screen reading. Consider other possible licensing models for web-native fonts. Plenty of marketing occurs, and will increasingly occur online, that depends on specific, exclusive fonts as part of brands and campaigns. The Guardian, New York Times, GE, and other entities want to communicate broadly and well, without freely sharing design assets that can be re-used by others. Facility in delivering web pages or other digital content in design-specific fonts isn't necessarily linked to free distribution, reproduction or remixing. I might be misinterpreting some of what you have said, but I think you could be more specific about just what sorts of fonts you are discussing.

You should make a clear distinction between typefaces and fonts. And address the issue of the relationship between web fonts and screen fonts. A typeface is a general, fairly abstract design that may exist in different physical manifestations (fonts), for use in different output situations. Thus, a typeface may be nuanced as more than one font, for use at different sizes in the same document ("text" and "display"), or for different media (e.g. smooth or glossy paper) in the same printing process. See H&JF's "grading". Garamond, for instance, has been adapted from letterpress fonts, to phototype (ITC Garamond), to low-res LaserWriter output (Adobe Garamond). Which of those will the best web Garamond font be like? Typefaces may be adapted for radically different media: hinting is one thing that accomplishes this in the digital realm. One thing about typeface design is that the old styles have persisted across media changes, why should that change with the Web? Large x-heights and open counters are not a necessary characterisitic of screen typefaces. Why would a good screen typeface not vary x-height and counter size with pixel resolution, exactly in the manner of an optically-scaled print typeface?! Look at how clumsy Verdana and Georgia become on screen at larger sizes. Having said that, there are type design issues which are screen-specific. But that's getting into the relationship between sub-pixel hinting and curve shape. You can see this happening in the ClearType faces.
