FF DIN - defending its honor

November 7, 2004 • Chris Rugen

So, there's a videogame called Half-Life 2. Its interface uses what appears to be FF DIN Light. I'm a huge fan of both.

Well, as most fans of a game or movie are inclined to do, they want to get their hands on the font. Being the foolish typophile that I am, I chastised the people running the site for allowing font piracy on their site, while they berate game piracy.

Here's a response I got from a fan on the site:

"as for "the amount of effort it takes to make a font" please.. dont make me laugh. Making a font is EASY. There are fonts out there that cost thousands of dollars, just so you have the right to use it publicly, its a joke.

Fonts are not hard to make, stop kidding yourself. and FF DIN is a very basic looking font, clean lines, nothing fancy. I bet it didnt take long to make at all. It would be like me making signatures for people and charging them $1500 a go."

I've already blasted him for his ignorance and explained (briefly) that he's wrong, but what I'd like to know is if anyone here knows how long Albert Jan-Pool's FF DIN actually took to make?

This is why people pirate fonts. Why non-designers do. I'm just throwing fists against the castle walls in an attempt to change maybe one of their minds.
You'll have to get two metric tons of flack from people who believe whatever works for them for each head you convince. But I'll point Albert here and see if he thinks it's worth a bother.

BTW, Linotype Maral took about 800 hours for the Regular and Bold (no italics). But it was my second outline font - I can do it a bit faster now. But not in an hour, like those guys probably think.

I've never made a font, and I've only made small promotional games, but I'm guessing that a game like half-life 2 would have taken many many times more hours than FF Din. I haven't checked the prices of either, but I would guess that FF Din costs more than a copy of half-life 2.

But its not really about the hours, is it?
You would assume that game designers feel that their role is a creative one, and probably the programmers do too. Designing and creating a typeface is also a creative role. The cost of both products includes a premium for the creativity of their designers. The size of the potential market is also a factor, as well as the composition of that market in terms of individuals or businesses.
There are so many other factors that determine the price of a product that to reduce it to how many hours it took to create is irrelevant.

Now Mr. 'FF DIN is a very basic looking font' has to answer the question:
If it didn't take very long, and its easy, why did he want to steal someone else's work instead of just knocking up a clean, basic font himself?
