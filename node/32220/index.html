Hi James, For posters “inna Swiss Stylleeee” best is the archive of the Swiss national Library. Type in Emil Ruder, Carlo Vivarelli or whatever into the Search box. http://posters.nb.admin.ch/cgi-bin/gw/chameleon?lng=en&skin=posters

For book design ask the Arts Council of Switzerland if they can send you “Book Design in Switzerland” by Jost Hochuli http://www.prohelvetia.ch/

Order “Designing Books, Practice and theory” by Jost Hochuli and Robin Kinross from Hyphen books. http://www.hyphenpress.co.uk/titles/index.html

If you want to splash out a bit more for some eye-poppingly beautiful coffee table books on the subject go for “The Typographic Grid” by Hans Rudolf Bosshard and “Printed matter, mainly books” by Jost Hochuli (yup, him again) from the Arthur Niggli publishing company. http://www.niggli.ch/

Best wishes, Simon H.

Re: fonts from Swiss books, The interesting thing is that when you look closely at Swiss Typography/objective Typography/International Style you can actually distinguish between the graphic design coming out of Basel and Zurich. The differences seem to have origins in the surprisingly angry 1946 slagging match between Max Bill and Jan Tschichold in the pages of the Schweizer Graphische Mitteilungen, (more in the University of Reading Typography Papers 4, 2000) or a general long-term rivalry between the two cities. Using Univers in a book would be more associated with Basel - Emil Ruder used it a lot (and I believe he and Adrian Frutiger were good friends). For reproductions of Ruder’s book (and children’s book) designs in Univers (and one in Baskerville) check out the excellent article “Emil Ruder, Typography from the inside” by Helmut Schmidt in Baseline 36, 2002. In Zurich Max Bill and the people editing Neue Grafik magazine (Lohse, Müller-Brockmann, Neuburg, Vivarelli) seem to have been concerned with making graphic design that was actually modern (concrete) art for whome Univers was a bit too stylish and flashy and seem to only use AkzGrot and Helvetica in their attempt at ‘zero degrees’ typography that was pure communication with no style (which of course quickly became a style). This is mostly true for book design of the style and the period you are referring to but strangely enough my copy of “Graphic Design Manual” by Armin Hofmann is Univers 55 on the front cover and spine but the rest is Akzidenz which I’ve never really understood at all.

Un grand “tout d’bon” helvétique, Simon H.

P.S. There’s a heavyweight study on this subject by Zurich-based academic Christoph Bignens called “Swiss Style 1914-1964”, but I’m not sure if it has come out in English yet.

My family used to be good friends to the Ruders, and I litteraly grew up surrounded by Swiss style graphics and typography (I don’t think, however, that Emil Ruder—an excellent musician, by the way—was that close to Adrian Frutiger …). I’ve never thought about it, but I think what Simon wrote is perfectly true: There are considerable differences between a Basle version and a Zürich version of Swiss style. It undoubtedly has something to do with the respective protagonists—Ruder, Tschichold and Basel Gewerbeschule vs. Bill and Zürich Kunstgewerbeschule. Add to that a traditional rivalry between the two cities and the different styles of the respective publishers. Ironically, a lot of what is called “Swiss style” today (especially in American publications), Ruder and other Basel teachers would never have approved of. One easily forgets that many Swiss—or Basel—designers of the time had a rather different idea of “Swiss style”, including many not-so-sober styles like the ones of Leupin or GGK agency.

Tout d’ bon to all the Swiss typophiles Florian

Simon, I studied design and typography with Basle graduates. Ken Hiebert was my typography teacher and a Basle grad. He was a student of Ruder and Hofmann. We used Univers exclusively in those classes in the 60s. Two years later, I met Peter Megert while working at the Westinghouse design Center. He is from Bern and apprenticed with Müller-Brockmann. He ALWAYS used Helvetica. It was clear to him that the Basle people were from a different planet. He would qualify anything about a Basle designer with, “But he is from Basle.” said in such a way as if to excuse his failing do to an affliction :-) It would be like saying, “That man just walked into a wall, but then again he is blind.” Peter is still a friend and was always a big joker so you have to understand his dry sense of humor. He made many jokes about his Swiss homeland, complaining how boring it was to see nothing but cheese and watches all day–he then would smile and say, “But the chocolate is good.”

Simon, I don’t know any particulars, but it is very well possible that Ruder had some interest in Eastern graphics and/or philosophy (I was born after Ruder died, so I cannot speak from memory). However, this is a penchant—should I say a hobby—that many of the designers of the time shared. The view of Japanese and Chinese graphics being the ideal of printing and visual culture was extremely widespread. Not all designers had the insight and zeal of Tschichold, who did really some research on the topics. Others’ knowledge never got beyond Eugen Herrigel’s Zen-books.

What strikes me is another thing: When I browse to new publications on “Swiss design”, I sometimes find that one aspect is undervalued: the fact that almost all of the leading figures of Swiss design were teachers and saw themselves formost as teachers. This ›tradition‹ extends to living designers such as Hochuli and Bosshardt. It is also interesting to see that some of the designers of international fame were happy to live for thirty or forty years in one and the same Swiss city, almost without travelling further than their holiday home in the Swiss Alps once a year! If you compare that to hectic schedule of todays ›stars‹, you might find another reason for the quite and concentrated look of Swiss style of the time. And indeed, I sometimes wonder whether today’s extensive travelling and ›congressing‹ is that helpful for design itself.
