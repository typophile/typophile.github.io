2009-11-29 19:02:02 • Uli
Uli, signatory nations to Berne Convention on copyright agree to extend to works of foreign origin the same protection that they grant to domestic works. In other words, German copyright applies to Linotype fonts in Germany, US copyright applies to Linotype fonts in the USA, Canadian copyright applies to Linotype fonts in Canada, etc. What matters is not law of the country of origin of the work but that of country in which an infraction may occur (and, of course, the willingness and ability of a company to combat infractions in that local jurisdiction).

2009-12-06 07:00:48 • damascity
quadibloc: > "The fact that a font design has entered the public domain does not impair the ability of someone to copyright a True Type font, which is a sequence of bits, that draws that font." Digitising an old public domain work, i.e. converting it into "a sequence of bits", does not resuscitate the dead copyright. Here are a few examples: 1) Project Gutenberg (www.gutenberg.org) digitised, i.e. converted to ASCII files, innumerable public domain literary works, but this did not resuscitate the dead copyrights. 2) If you convert an old public domain shellac gramophone record or an old vinyl phonograph record into a digital compact disk, you do not resuscitate the dead copyright. 3) If you photograph an old public domain analogue Kodak photography using a digital camera, you do not resuscitate the dead copyright. etc. etc. etc. So, for example, if you digitize the public domain "Stempel Garamond" (aka "Original Garamond") by converting it to a TTF font and rename it to "Aldine 430" see www.sanskritweb.net/forgers/bitstream2.pdf, page 4 and page 12 you do not resuscitate a dead copyright nor a dead design right (which in the case of the 1925 "Original Garamond" or "Stempel Garamond" never existed). For the USA copyright law, also consider the "Sweat of the brow" doctrine: http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sweat_of_the_brow

2009-12-06 14:44:21 • dtapia
It requires the "sweat of the brow" to create a .ttf file for, say, my own imitation of Caslon. Fonts are created by drawing, not scanning. (Although some font construction programs may attempt to make a first-cut version of a font from a scan.) As it happens, font designs weren't protected at all by copyright or patent law (despite some foundries obtaining design patents for their fonts anyways) until, possibly, very recently. So the only thing type foundries had were their trademarks - and some had been aggressively suing people who listed which knockoff fonts looked like which original fonts. (This apparently has been happening recently - books on typography were published in the phototypesetting era that had such lists, but now I hear of websites being sued.) While the intellectual property of original type designers is certainly more important to protect than those of knock-off type designers, given that original type designs don't enjoy much protection, the claim that a .ttf file lacks normal copyright protection would threaten the intellectual property rights of the original designers of typefaces, not just those of knock-off font makers.

(And if anyone feels I've got it wrong, please, please correct me. I'm trying to present this neutrally and factually.) When it became possible to record the visual features of a typeface as numbers within a computer file, the fact that the typeface could not be copyrighted, did not mean that the computer files which digitally describe them could not be copyrighted. These files are protectable as literary works. As far as type designs that were clearly in the public domain or clearly the product of someone else, the same holds true. The original type design cannot be copyrighted but the digital file describing it can. So, even though a font re-creates a typeface design that is clearly in the public domain, that fact is inapplicable in determining whether it's digital representation is, or is not, copyrightable as a literary work. In regards to typefaces, the adoption of digital technology reset the clock so to speak - the previous 550 years of typeface design for print and the hand-done faces before that no longer count for anything in regard to whether a font can be copyrighted. Once a typeface is digitized, the resulting files are copyrightable and will be so for the life of the creator plus 70 years. (I think that's the current term in the USA. Works done for hire have a longer lifespan.) Anyway, that's the position as I understand it. Regards, rich
