Sloped roman vs. italic. The serifs on the sloped roman are bilateral, abrupt (emphasize the end of the stroke). The serifs on the italic are unilateral, adnate (lead into the next stroke).

2011-06-17 18:50:46 • Frode Bo Helland
Another relevant concept: folded. This term was used by Stanley Hess in The Modification of Letterforms to describe the way in which the curved stems of letters may be configured—for instance, the c and s in Frutiger are unfolded, but the grotesque c and s of Helvetica have folded forms. These cursive terminals/serifs or entry/exit strokes are folded back against the letter. Joshua, how can they be adnate if they're not connected? Adnate means "growing from", i.e. a bracketed serif or an old-style triangular terminal may be considered adnate, growing from the stem, as opposite to a slab-like didone serif or a ball terminal, which appear attached.

2011-06-18 05:53:26 • hrant
> Joshua, how can they be adnate if they're not connected? > Adnate means "growing from" That's how. They "grow from" the previous stroke, in the way that they would if the stroke and serif were made in one stroke of the pen, whereas to produce "roman" style serifs with a pen requires drawing the serif as a separate stroke or doubling back. Thus, "roman" serifs do not "grow from" the attached stroke in a natural way. They are adnate with respect to the letterform they are attached to, not the following one, though they do suggest a link to the next letter.
2011-06-19 05:09:12 • Joshua Langman
However since type is never handwritten, the better way to use "adnate" is based purely on how the outline segments relate. So Times (I mean the upright style) for example has fully adnate serifs; Calluna's serifs are adnate on the left, and abrupt on the right. hhp
