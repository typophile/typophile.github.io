2005-04-22 08:34:11 • Bald Condensed
I disagree with the philosophy that one should have a stable of fonts, ready to use on whatever job comes along.

As an art director, I was always looking for the new font for the new job. As a type designer, the one(s) I'm working on at the moment are always the most essential.

And bear in mind, the right font for the job in hand may not have been designed yet, and may require commissioning.
2005-04-22 11:16:11 • Nick Shinn
Please bear with me while I make an analogy.

When my wife's brother got married and bought a house, he and
his wife purchased all of their furniture and decorations in one
sitting, so they could have their new house entirely furnished by
the time they moved in.

Their interior looks like a cacophony.

My wife and I on the other hand started out with leftover stuff
from family and friends, and gradually acquired new items.
Every time we'd bought something, we waited till we got used
to it and evaluated how it interacted with the house and the
other objects in the room before buying the next item.

Decorating a house is not unlike building a type library.
2005-04-22 13:51:24 • Bald Condensed
There are no "10 essential fonts", just like there are no 10 essential
furniture items. Every time you find there's a typeface missing in your
library for a specific job, select a new one that will both do for that
job and make sure it stylistically fills a gap in your collection, so odds
are you'll be able to get some mileage out of it.

Instead of going for Garamond, Caslon, Perpetua, Gill Sans, DIN, Helvetica
and so on try FTF Rongel, Enigma, Delicato, Parisine, Klavika, Galaxy Polaris…
There's so many brilliant contemporary designs which are tomorrow's classics.
