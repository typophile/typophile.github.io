Bot Mono

December 8, 2014 • Core0

While I’m still working on the greater releases of the Hikari font family and Theory Serif, Sans and Mono, I was crazy enough to begin work on a fifth font project.

The idea was born out of a personal need. For months I have been working in Coda to write CSS and HTML code, and I got tired of the array of fonts in the monospaced category, some of which come with the system, some I collected over the past couple of years. From Menlo (Apple) to Droid Sans (Google), I couldn’t find a font that I found was well designed for an on-screen reading experience. Designers of monospaced fonts often claim they were thinking about computer displays first, but when you look at the results, especially in common sizes ranging from 10 to 18 points, the letter shapes often look washed up and have sometimes been just compressed or extended to fit a space (I’m looking at you, first version of Source Code Pro). Bad hinting and unclear shapes result in a nearly unidentifyable letter shape and the overall legibility is suffering. Of course, the very idea of a monospaced font is actually against improved legibility, because it takes away nearly all options to even out white space and create a harmonic experience.

What can be changed though is the individual shape. I think nowhere else it is more crucial to get this part right, than with a monospaced typeface designed for screen. We have various screen resolutions today, ranging from tiny devices (like wrist watches) to an iMac with a 5k Retina Display. For programmers looking at a display for hours, the reading experience becomes as much, if not more, relevant than other practical considerations when choosing a monospaced font.

So this is Bot Mono, a very young, fresh project of mine and I hope you will share your thoughts and critique with me. There will be a few more changes and a large set of glyphs need yet to be worked out, the numbers are missing yet, but it’s already showing a lot of its personality and I am curious to hear what you think.

To the open/closed counter discussion:

The fact some counters are closed and some are open is of course not by accident. The idea is to distinguish individual glyphs more, without sacrificing overall readability.

The thought model that readability of a sans serif (or any font) is always improved by open counters everywhere, is a bit outdated, if you ask me. Closed counters improve readability by forming invisible rails, just like the baseline and x-height, or caps height. Open counters are wanted, necessary even, if the overall shape becomes muddled (as it would be the case if I kept the lowercase “e” counters closed as I have done it with the “c”). In the case of the lowercase “c” and “s”, the shape’s distinctive characteristics are emphasized, they would become less distinctive if I kept the canon.

The current design tries to join the best of both worlds, the closed counters-philosophy with open counters where it helps distinction. Keep in mind I was designing the font to be used in 12 to 18 point sizes (on regular, modern displays). If you keep a distance of a foot or more to the display, anything below 12 point will look closer to 7 or 8 point on a printed business card. Bot Mono is not well suited for large sizes, unless imperfection is part of the message you want to express.

I am truly greatful for all your input. It’s great to be pointed to details and think them over. Work is still in progress, as this is a young project, but I’m dedicated to finish up by the end of the year and release it under an open license, perhaps on Google fonts or Font Squirrel. Beta testers are welcome, there will be updates every week now. Just send me an email, you’ll find it on http://core-ux.com/contact.
