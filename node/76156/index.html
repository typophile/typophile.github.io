Psychedelic typefaceses

November 9, 2010 • Coccoline

Hi, does anyone know how they made psychedelic typefaces in the 60s? Was it all handwritten? Why did they make it the way it was? Where did its origins come from? and how did they print the psychedelic art posters?
I am fairly confident that more than one old hippie (other than myself) has done a comprehensive study of the genre, and some dedicated Googling would probably turn something up. This old hippie's take is that the recognized masters of the art--such as Rick Griffin, Stan Mouse, Victor Moscoso and Wes Wilson--hand-lettered their posters, which were sometimes printed offset and sometimes silk-screened. At the tail end of the phase, commercial typesetters like Photo-Lettering Inc. got into the mix. As far as origins go, I would opine that Push Pin Studios was probably a strong influence. Their brash revivals of Art Deco probably prompted a look at other style of the past, notably Art Nouveau, which heavily influenced psychedelic poster work.

2010-11-24 17:28:49 • Paul Cutler
AFAIK, the specific effect Paul invented was new, whereby an input was re-recorded over itself, slightly out of sync, for a progressive, echoey degeneration. After a certain number of over-dubs, the effect became too muddy, and it became necessary to manually ease up the recording head. I'm not sure the Beatles' interest in sound effects can be traced directly to contemporary classical music -- they grew up listening to the Goon Show, with sound effects by the BBC Radiophonic Workshop, and George Martin had worked with the show's comedians. Prior to his involvement with the Beatles, he had produced all kinds of music at the dog's body Parlophone, including classical, novelty, and "regional music", and under the moniker Ray Cathode, even released an electronic dance tune, recorded at the Radiophonic Workshop. So it's reasonable to assume that Martin turned the Beatles on to such experimentations and special effects, which came from a commercial recording studio milieu, rather than an avant garde classical source.
2010-11-24 18:09:04 • Nick Shinn
The Beatles were certainly aware of the avant garde. This is from the wikipedia article about Tomorrow Never Knows: >McCartney supplied a bag of ¼ inch audio tape loops he had made at home after listening to Stockhausen's Gesang der Jünglinge. By disabling the erase head of a tape recorder and then spooling a continuous loop of tape through the machine while recording, the tape would constantly overdub itself, creating a saturation effect, a technique also used in musique concrète. That Stockhausen piece was realized in 1955-56. Terry Riley had also created what he called a Time Lag Accumulator (2 reel to reels with tape loops) in the 50s which achieved the same thing as disabling the erase head. It is also true that the BBC was using tape loops in the 1950s for sound effects so the phenomena was concurrent. Whatever the case - back then pop music was a very different animal, with the largest stars looking everywhere for information/inspiration. This was also true in early punk rock - as many musicians as came from the David Bowie/Iggy Pop school came from the avant garde (think Stockhausen, Mauricio Kagel, John Cage, Sun Ra, The Art Ensemble of Chicago). People that are serious about what they do tend to be aware of the history of their craft. Nick - you seem to be. pbc

Actually, since we old farts were not bound by what was on a pulldown menu and getting type set then was way too expensive compared to dirt cheap today--so we just took out brushes and paint and did it until it looked groovy. "Are there anyone here that used to design psychedelic art in the 60s ?" That would be a yes. You have surely heard about Peter Max an the others listed in previous posts. They were just the commercial successes and the tip of the iceberg. Every headshop, macrobiotic restaurant, and tie--die joint had friends who would just do one-off of posters and menus for food or weed. The REAL stuff got washed away by the rain decades ago--only the high end commercial stuff got printed and has survived until today.
