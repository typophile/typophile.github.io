2003-07-31 20:17:50 • keith_tam
I've only seen "lining numerals" to mean where the numbers are the same height. Usually this means the same height of the caps as well, but not always: there are short lining numerals (pioneered by Austin/Bell), and I think they generally work better. Lining numerals are often fixed-width.

The "opposite" of lining numerals are old-style numerals (which are called all kinds of funny things, like "up-and-down numerals"), and they generally behave like lc letters, but not always: there's what's called "hybrid" numerals, which have larger-than-x=height bodies, but also feature extenders. The foremost practitioner of this style is Luc[as] de Groot, although happily this style -which I consider the ideal for text- is getting quickly more popular. There are actually tentative traces of this in some ATF fonts (like Garamond), but Luc[as] was the first to really flourish the hybrid style, as far as I've found.

Old-style numerals are rarely fixed-width, even though structurally they actually fit that mold more easily!

2003-08-01 23:07:46 • karen
>I'm waiting to see if the list grows.

I have added Preface, with tabular, old-style figures as standard, in six weights, Roman and Italic.

It's far easier to design an even-colored set of tabular figures, if one does them old-style – there's more leeway to make a wide "1" that doesn't look anomalous, and a "0" that doesn't end up being too condensed (and with excessively narrow sidebearings) as in lining figures.

Tabular lining figures have terribly uneven color and fit in things like area codes, eg loose (416) and tight (905).
