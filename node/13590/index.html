2005-07-13 02:38:58 • John Hudson

In the Jewish classic Pirkei Avot, there is a passage where the sages try to reconcile the miracles in the Bible with the Greek concept of natural law.

The way they reconcile it is by saying that all the miraculous stuff that goes against nature, such as Moses' staff, was specially created by God in the last moments of creation. One of the things that was created was the Ten Commandments, ready for Moses to receive. But to do this God also had to create “the script, the stylus, the tablets” (Avot 5:9).

Thus according to the sages, God invented script, and so was the first designer of letters. As a typophile, I had to smile when I read this; it is somehow fitting that those who almost worshiped written language–and were often scribes–were comfortable with the idea that God invented writing.

(ps John, ‘Law’ is not an accurate translation of ‘Torah’. Some of the mystics thought that God first wrote the Torah, with the account of creation in Genesis, and then used it as a blueprint to create the world.)

David, I checked some of the very few religious references I have, and didn’t find anything specific to the script or the paper, but did note the following:

  1. The “terrestrial” Qur’aan is a copy of the original that resides with Allah.

  2. There is mention of Arabic being the divine language of the Qur’aan. But I guess it is possible to equate the script with the language in this context.

  3. There is a hadeeth (which I think is basically external -but “official”- commentary) by Al-Baaqillaani that mentions something seemingly related to composition, if not the script or paper per se. In explaining the miraculous nature of the Qur’aan, he writes that “it is wonderfully arranged, and marvelously composed, and so exalted in its literary elegance as to be beyond what any mere creature could attain.”

  4. Muhammad was essentially illiterate before having the Qur’aan abruptly revealed to him.

  5. The Qur’aan -although revealed as a book- is fundamentally phonetic. They way it’s written, euphonically, it is claimed causes it to only be understood when sounded out (or least voiced internally). And this is why many people think you have to know Arabic to understand Islam.
