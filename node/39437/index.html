A sign for what? I’ve thought about this at length, in terms of a letter being a sign for a sound.

In summary, [[http://www.heavydutypress.com/books/alchemy/|I wrote some improvisational narrative text while composing copy with a job case full of 14 Spartan]] (by hand, with lead sorts) with the objective to empty the case, using up every last sort. As the narrative progressed, I ran out of some letters and had to use other letters or figures or puncuation (upside-down) as the “signs” for the sounds in the English language. By the last page, every letter represents a different letter, but if you read through the book from the beginning, you can actually read it right to the very end. To prove it could be done, I asked an eighth grade student to translate the last page for extra credit. He brought in the translation the next moring.

And then there’s the Phoenecians to think about…

2007-11-28 21:39:52 • Alessandro Segalini

Arun: Well, that’s a German thing … It’s the sign all pharmacies (‘A’ as in Apotheke) use – though they’re not connected economically. And it’s one of the last vestiges of Fraktur in everyday life.

The red simplified blackletter ‘A’ was introduced nationwide in 1936 by ‘Reichsapothekerführer’ (Nazi pharmacists’ leader) Albert Schmierer. It then still featured the ‘Man’ rune (the original winning design had depicted a white cross) inside the ‘A’. After WWII – in 1951 – pharmacists agreed to stick to the red ‘A’, however the rune got replaced by a Rod of Asclepius, snaking around a drug bowl (?). [From de.wikipedia.org]
