Alternative Characters and "Logotypes" in Monotype Metal

January 25, 2008 • ebensorkin

Reading about Bembo just now I came across a reference to the long R & short R in Metal Bembo from Monotype. Which got me wondering what other alternative glyphs or alternative glyph combinations there might have been. I know that Linotype had quite a few "logotypes" which were a mix of Kering solutions and ligatures (mainly f related but sometimes including much more ) and sometimes there were even contextual alteration of non ligature kerned logotypes. I found the contextual stuff at the Printing History Museum in North Andover. Here is some more info on that: http://www.typophile.com/node/36157 My questions are, if I wanted to see if similar things had been done with Monotype Metal fonts where would I look? Did Monotype use the Term "logotype"? What alternative characters do you know and or love?
If this were a research project (ie you weren't planning on making a Bembo knock-off, but were, say, writing a dissertation on Bembo) you'd get in contact with Robin Nicholas, and request an appointment to visit the Monotype UK office in Salfords. Robin, might then let you examine the original engineering drawings which would include the special sorts.

2008-01-25 22:38:25 • Si_Daniels
There's no need to go all the way back to the engineering drawings to see what special characters were available. If you can track down any copies of the specimen sheets or books that Monotype prepared for their customers, they'll usually show the special characters along with the part numbers that were used to order the correct matrices for any given typeface. For instance, here's a note from a specimen for Bembo Bold 428 (unfortunately I don't have the corresponding sheet for Series 270, the regular weight): [img:sites/default/files/old-images/bembo_bold_alts_8pt_3673.gif] You can see the alternate versions of R available, as well as a few additional ligatures. Granted, the drawings would make it easier to see what's been adjusted to make those ligatures, but the printed specimens I've seen have always had really clear printing. (That's 8 pt. type shown at 300%, in case you're wondering.)

The later the better for any given form of the typeface (that is, the latest specimen of either the metal or the film version you can find), since any specials that were custom ordered by any customers would be added to the next round of updates. I'm not sure when the major sets of specimens were released, but I've mostly seen binders of specimens that allowed customers to insert the updated sheets as they were produced.

2008-01-26 10:00:04 • James Mosley
Eben -- Have I mentioned the Spiral/Emerson contextual r to you before? It was part of Blumenthal's original foundry type, but didn't make it into the Monotype cutting of the face -- probably because they couldn't really accommodate it in the matrix case and there wasn't likely to be much interest in such a specialized sort as an extra. If you can get your hands on a copy of the APHA's American Proprietary Typefaces, Jerry Kelly discusses this on page 115 in his article on Emerson. -- K.

Another angle might be to identify some popular composing houses that used Monotype and look for their specimens. Major composing houses would periodically produce publications outlining the faces that they had on hand, often with extensive showings. Kingsport Press is one that comes to mind -- a three volume set: one for Linotype, one for Monotype, and one for Display faces. I've seen a few sets before, but I don't recall if they give full character showings in addition to the copious settings. -- K.
