Licensing fonts for a design school

April 24, 2007 • Keith Tam

Hello everyone – long time no see! Glad to be back on Typophile. I miss all of you! Long story short, I got married last June, moved to Hong Kong and now teaching at the Hong Kong Polytechnic University (more updates later, I guess).

Now – I’m writing a proposal to seek funding to license a set of fonts for the school, mainly for the Visual Communication and Advertising disciplines. Right now we have an abysmal set of (mostly) default system fonts on our computers in the studios and labs, mostly PCs. I’m tired of seeing the same fonts over and over again in students’ projects. And we have next to nothing in terms of fonts with advanced features (multiple weights, ‘pro’ fonts, or fonts with ‘expert sets’, etc. we do have a few Adobe Pro fonts) for fine typographic work.

Does anyone here have experience in equipping a design school with fonts? Could you share your experience? I have a few font families that I have in mind. Ideally, I would like to have a set of 20-30 high quality, professional fonts. Some of which will be classic typefaces – several from each typeface category; some will be ‘contemporary’ typefaces (really difficult to choose here). We also need some good Chinese text and display faces too. So, we’ll have to license fonts from several different foundries.

I’ve talked to two Chinese foundries and Adobe Hong Kong about this. None of them seem to offer volume discounts or education site licences. Both Chinese foundries charge a premium on PDF embedability, which is unrealistic for a school, since the students mostly just create PDFs for assignment submission.

We have around 300 computers in the school in total. We could implement this in phases to minimize cost upfront. I’ve already written a ‘justification’ for our need for fonts. But I’m having a hard time working out the budget for this.

Any advice on this? Or recommendations for what I should include in the set of fonts?

I attend and work at a medium-sized privately-funded liberal arts college. Our Art and Design department has 38 macs in classrooms and a handful of laptops for faculty use. A little while ago, the department decided to update our aging typeface collection by purchasing 45 licenses for the Adobe Opentype folio, in addition to 45 licenses for a few extra typefaces that were deemed hip by the faculty (Meta2 OT, Mrs Eaves OT, Matrix, HFJ’s Requiem).

In my estimation, this was a gross miscalculation. I spend about an hour a week clearing sometimes problematic fonts from dafont.com and elsewhere (Eduardo Recife of Misprinted Type fame is apparently a local hero) out of the folders where we keep the Adobe collection (We use Linotype Font Explorer X). A cursory glance at the student work reveals that there is very little use of the display faces included with the Adobe folio, and sparing use of everything else. I think this can be blamed mostly on instructor leniency, which runs rampant here, but I still can’t help but wonder if it would have been more economical to purchase a handful of licenses for the Adobe Type for Learning folio and install them on only a few computers.

Personally, as a student, I think the majority of so-called grunge fonts are very dated. They are appearing on stuff in Walmart and besides, if you want a grungy look it’s way more fun to produce it yourself, digitally or otherwise. Also, I would have liked to see something like the Umbrella Selects collection from Veer thrown into the mix. There are some inclusions in the adobe collection that are downright questionable… Hobo Std? Come on.

Anyway, to get back to your question… I think it is really important to come to a consensus with the instructors. If you’re going to invest funds into purchasing professionally developed typefaces, then there should be some encouragement from the faculty to use the fonts.

I think I’m starting to ramble on here, but I just thought I’d share my experiences.

2007-04-25 03:36:06 • Village

In my Typography 2 class, each student was required to purchase one typeface. It could be an entire family or just a single, but they had to use it for one of their projects (rather than just buying the cheapest font they could find and then not actually using it). They were told this at the very beginning of the semester so they could budget for it, if need be.

By this point in their schooling, they were on the track to becoming graphic designers, so this experience was relevant to their future careers. The school had purchased the entire Adobe library, so they did have another ready and rich pool of legal typefaces to choose from. In other words, buying their typeface was a supplement and a learning experience rather than a desperate need.

We discussed in class both licensing issues and issues to consider when making a purchase. They really learned quite a bit by going through the process of choosing, buying and using a typeface. For example, they would find out that their typeface didn’t have the right character set they needed or they had chosen the wrong typeface, but already paid for it (this hit home the typeface-as-investment angle!). Many learned what a pleasure it is to work with a quality typeface of their choosing and what a difference it makes on a project. And so on.

It the right circumstances, students purchasing typefaces is a great learning experience and one they will engage in often as professionals. We ask them to buy all sorts of things… books, print outs, paint supplies, drawing pens, and so forth, why not fonts.
