Your sample predates the digital type era, so it was either phototypeset or was created using an electric typewriter.

The typeface may not have a digital equivalent.

A much larger sample would aid the search (letters need to be at least 100 px tall and scanned at a decent resolution to run through visual database searches).

In 1981 the record industry was a long established user of photo type products which were used to distort typefaces to compress or expand width, height and other features. My impression of the image – here enhanced to facilitate visual examination is that this is a font that has not been digitized, which was applied in an expanded width version. There is also a possibility that it was a typeface for a an electric typewriter of the era, but the record industry was not noted for typewritten album covers. Also, very few typewriters could produce proportional spacing. In any case, It is not possible to duplicate it exactly with a digital font, but there are some digital fonts that correspond to most of the letter forms. After looking at quite a few I settled on a font by Tobias Frere-Jones, which is being sold by Font Bureau as Poynter Gothic Text http://www.fontbureau.com/fonts/PoynterGothicText/styles/ Here it is, used to display some of the album text Here it is, stretched horizontally 120%, and used to display the same text Most people would not notice any difference. Perfectionists could edit A to have a broad flat top and G to have a closer fit on the crossbar and spur and tinker with other letters to suit their taste. Don
