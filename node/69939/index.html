The most obvious current development in type design, although it’s been going on for several years, is the creation of lots of new Humanist sans-serifs, many of them suitable for text.

The other big change is the development of text typefaces intended specifically for extended reading on-screen.

I think the zeitgeist is in fact the huge amount of brilliant, original, typographically complex, multi-script “text” typefaces recently published, in the second half of the previous decade.

The OpenType format has made this possible, as has the development of an international retail online font market, and the maturity of independent foundries (i.e. designer-owned, not legacy publishers or Big Silicon bundlers).

The Reading and KABK courses are cause/symptom, as is TypeCon and Typophile. All contemporary phenomena.

2010-05-03 22:30:37 • eiiviind

Script ≠ Language.

Examples of common scripts are Latin (including a smattering of accented glyphs), Greek (which may or may not include pre-katharevousa accents), and Cyrillic.

You can usually set a number of languages with each script (Cherokee has its own, and I’m sure there are quite a few others). The number of accented glyphs per script is the real touchstone for “supports this or that language”.

Nick refers to fonts like Minion Pro (supports every Western European Latin script, as well as pretty much Eastern Europe languages – but not pinyin Chinese transcription, because it lacks one of the accents; Cyrillic; and Modern and Old Greek), Myriad Pro (Latin, Cyrillic, Modern but not Old Greek), Adobe Garamond Pro (Latin, Modern Greek).
