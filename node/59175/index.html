2009-06-30 15:05:43 • Richard Fink

James, you have a point, but “hobby arm” is not a correct characterization. My impression is that the vast majority of those attending are either professionally involved with type, or are students planning to be. Most, it is true, are graphic designers, with an interest in type, maybe having drawn some type themselves, but not full time type designers. That reflects the name of the society: “Society of Typographic Aficionados”.

However, full time type designers are there in significant numbers, and a good chunk of talks are by and of interest to type designers. For example, I am looking forward to the Gerard Unger & Friends panel, and to Gerry Leonides on “What I have learned about Type”, as well as the panel on fonts for the Web, which seems to be the current hot issue for the type design industry.

So while it’s true that TypeCon doesn’t have the technical and business issues of professional type designers as its primary and exclusive focus, it does include those issues, and it brings type designers together with their customers. And that is a service to type designers as well as graphic designers.

I’m 99% sure that I won’t be at TypeCon this year, a decision I made before the speaker and content information was available and which I am now regretting for a number of reasons.

In regard to what James has written, in some respects I agree with him, particularly with regard to the amount of serious business that takes place at TypeCon, but this was a criticism I long made of ATypI, which used to purport to be a professional association. TypeCon, at least, has no such pretensions. But I disagree with James that the lack of business reduces the event to a solely or primarily social function. I consider a conference good if I learn some things and have some good conversations about type: the educational component is what I value. As the years go by, the opportunities to learn something new diminish, although the quality of those opportunities, when they do occur, tends to be high. Even when I don’t find much to interest me personally, it would be churlish to criticise an event if it offers opportunities to those younger and less experienced.

I have attended three TypeCon conferences: Minneapolis, Seattle and Buffalo. I had fun in Minneapolis but didn’t learn much of anything and wasn’t very impressed by what was on offer in general. It was pretty lame, which is why I didn’t bother with the next couple of years. Seattle and Buffalo were both very good conferences though, among the best type conferences I have attended in the past fifteen years. I learned lots and had some good conversations.
