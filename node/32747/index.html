Paul Renners 'Plak'

April 2, 2007 • eeliene

I just started doing a project about the ‘Plak’ typeface Paul Renner made, but I can hardly find anything. Does anyone has some more information on Plak other than the basic information available?

There is a very short mention in Christopher Burke’s book ‘Paul Renner — The Art of Typography’ on pp 110-1 (and ill’s of the Cover and pages of a type specimen dated 1928):

“Renner himself designed a grotesk bearing some similarity to Futura as a display typeface for Stempel. It was called Plak (1928), and it was promoted as an accompaniment to Stempel’s Neuzeit Grotesk family. This seems a strange state of affairs, since Neuzeit-Grotesk was a rival to Futura; moreover, Renner was stull very occupied at that time with the second wave of Futura, released by Bauer in 1930. Plak was produced as wooden letters from 72 point to 624 point in size. Being a poster type, it was available exclusively in bold weights, but in three width variations. The widest variant bears similarities to some of Futura’s geometric letterforms, but in the condensed variants, the letters necessarily become more grotesk-like ([reference to figures]). Grotesk typefaces were developed in the nineteenth century for use at large sizes on posters, and Renner realized that grotesk forms were more adaptable than Futura in maintaining maximum weight with narrow width.”

End of quote. Nothing else on Plak in this book.

Christian Schwartz cites Plak as an influence on Graphik, the sans he designed for Conde Nast Portfolio and expanded for Wallpaper:

“The heavy end of the family is based in part on Paul Renner’s Plak, a relatively obscure display typeface cut only in large sizes of woodtype, that is related to his heavier weights of Futura but has rounder, friendlier, fatter proportions.”

http://christianschwartz.com/graphik.shtml

Graphik is available from Commercial Type: http://commercialtype.com/typefaces/graphik
