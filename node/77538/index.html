Type choice for publisher of historical novels

December 27, 2010 • Frode Bo Helland

Hi guys. I was recently approached by a publisher of historical novels to help develope their identity and wanted to ask for your advice on the type choice. The books aren’t confined to any specific period which makes it a little harder. The identity wil have to work online, so I’m particulary interested in well hinted @font-face licensed fonts.

FF Clifford is the first thing that came to mind, as it is relatively silent all the while it’s ruggedness really fits my bill, and the optical sizes and hinting is spot on. I’ve also looked into antique map fonts, like for example Tierra Nueva, and consider trying a modern/didone of some kind. (Whatever I end up using should be relatively consistent in a historical context, so I probably won’t mix Clifford with any didones … Just saying.) Nick Shinn’s Figgins Sans is also on my list.

They use Trajan (the obivious choice) in their logo, but I hope to be able to tweak that one ever so slightly without abandoning the feel of it.

I’m not only looking for web ready text fonts, but also just plain display stuff. Any ideas and/or inspiration when it comes to graphic style or type treatment are also welcome.

I’m basically trying to create something generic ancient, while hopefully managing to avoid the clichés.

Thanks.

I’m also very interested in symbols used by early(ier) printers for retail/trade. Stuff like for example printers fists (something I could use instead of the typical shopping basket, and also symbols for price listings and such).

2010-12-27 19:38:30 • riccard0

Rather than choose a single text typeface for the entire series, I’d suggest working out a more flexible but easily recognizable visual identity, which could accommodate different typefaces for different books – especially books about different historical periods. (Not that the typeface has to be from the same period, just that it has to feel compatible.) That would also give you the flexibility to use a different typeface online than on paper, if need be.

That said, as you asked about “antique map fonts,” I’d suggest taking a look at Mark van Bronkhorst’s MVB Sirenne family, which was based on the engraved lettering used for captions and other map-like information in an 18th-century book from a map publisher in Amsterdam.

http://mvbfonts.com/mvb_sirenne/

I thought about choosing [[http://www.fontbureau.com/media/pdf/PoynterOSText.pdf|Poynter]] for a book project lately. It is elegant (cap P, G, Q etc), yet relatively generic in style. Though having it’s roots in low-country Renaissance it could fit Baroque and later texts too. Plus you can get different optically scaled variants for different sizes and [[http://www.webtype.com/font/poynterserifre-family/|a well hinted version for the web]] via webtype. Or maybe you find some others over there. http://www.webtype.com/catalog/style/?ts=692

But Clifford is a good pick, too, the FF-fonts usually work well on screen. I’d go for some late moderate Renaissance or Baroque face, something that sits a bit in between the historic periods and doesn’t resemble one particular style so much.
