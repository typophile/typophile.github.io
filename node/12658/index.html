Your friend is in trouble. ;D It sounds like there is no time to make a custom typeface. Choose a typeface with several weights, clear numerals, nice open spacing, with letters clear and different from each other. Maybe your state is not too conservative? Your friend may have success proposing another style of typeface. Typefaces I suggest comparing: Chianti from Bitstream Cronos Sans - (smallest size masters) from Adobe Stainless from FontBureau Felbridge or Zemestro from fonts.com Aroma from FontShop Ask the type foundry about the price of licensing a typeface to a state government office. To save time, your friend can learn a lot by reading about the development of Clearview, which was done for highway signage in the US. http://www.clearviewhwy.com/ http://mutcd.fhwa.dot.gov/HTM/clearfont/ This is enough to give your friend a lot of useful information. I doubt your friend will need to supply such a large number of weights and widths. Most important: Much more letterspacing than for print Clear, open letterforms (like Frutiger, not like Helvetica) Large x-height (but not like Avant Garde) Strong, even strokes and terminals (like Frutiger, not like Optima) Your English is very good. Good luck to your friend, and please report back how the project turned out! Carl

Crossgrove gave some great links. Another typeface suggestion, you might consider, is Parisine from Porchez Typofonderie. Originally designed for the wayfinding system of the Paris Metro. An added bonus to this would be also licensing Parisine Plus which has more flourish and could be used to add a softer alternative for certain settings. Makote, Faced with the same dilemma, I'd look to the masters (and all of them are still living). I would strongly suggest you look up the works of master of systems typographer [[Erik Spiekermann]]. He's overseen the type systems for Berlin's street signs, the public transportation signage and system for Berlin light rail and underground metro, the identity and type for Berlin's postal service. His latest project (still underway?) is for the German (Berlin?) railway. (Good thing I'm not a professional journalist, because I don't have to check my sources. I'm sure someone could correct some of these facts, but the net result is the same.) The overwhelming feeling I got in Berlin was that I'd been transported to Spiekertown. His commercially available fonts (for signage and wayfinding) include FF Transit, FF Info, FF Meta--view all the Spiekerfonts here. Carl already recommended ClearviewOne which is as comprehensive in weights and options as any of Spiekermann's. (Designed by [[James Montalbano]].) Then there's the ubiquitous Interstate from New York designer [[Tobias_Frerejones|Tobias Frere-Jones]].
