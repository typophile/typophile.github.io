Al Gore wasn't the first person to do this to non-ranging numerals! It certainly is true that this looks as though Al Gore is ignorant about how non-ranging numerals are supposed to appear traditionally. Perhaps he never read the Readers' Digest, which used to use a typeface with them. But I can think of one class of typeface which - consistently - used a ranging style 1 and yet the other digits went above and below the line following the pattern of non-ranging digits. Typewriter faces. Although the numerals in the typeface of a typical manual typewriter were cap-height and not x-height, like ranging numerals, 3, 4, 5, 7 and 9 descended slightly below the line, and 6 and 8 ascended slightly above the line. None the less, if a 1 was provided on the typewriter, it still had the conventional ranging appearance (and if it wasn't, lowercase l, not uppercase I, was used as a substitute conventionally). So these two styles of numerals have been hybridized before.

And the trend goes back much further than typewriter faces, of course. The kind of three-quarter hybrid that quadibloc describes descend basically from Richard Austin and trace back to the late 18th century. Customization of fonts for clients is also nothing new, of course. Everyone here knows that. Nonetheless, it's always amusing to see under-the-radar stuff like this achieve widespread media attention. Good PR for Typotheque and Nikola Djurek (I think Brioni is a wonderful design). However, I do think some of the reports exaggerate the effort required to refit an oldstyle 1. This particular customization is not that “hard.”
