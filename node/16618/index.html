Re. Gill. Yes, a badly broken man in many, mostly sexual, respects. My friend Alan Wall described Gill’s attitude as ‘priapic adventurism’, which I think is a fair assessment. To be fair to Gill, his diaries do indicate remorse over what he did to his daughters, and they for their part seem to have forgiven him and not ceased to love him. [One of Gill’s grandaughters, Petra’s daughter, lives in Vancouver, by the way, and sent me a very nice catalogue of the paintings, drawings and engravings that David Jones had done for her mother.]

I was disappointed in the article to which Chris linked, since the author seems intent on using Gill’s pedophilia and Penty’s socialism – which he clearly thinks on par morally – to dismiss the distributist movement. In fact, neither Gill nor Penty were ‘founders’ of the distributism, as the article claims, and the economic philosophy was formulated in England by Chesterton and Belloc and based on Leo XIII’s encyclical Rerum Novarum and other Catholic social justice teachings of the 19th and 20th century. What Gill and others tried to do was apply this philosophy in the particular context of rural communal life: a kind of Catholic back-to-the-land movement. The philosophy of distributism, particularly its dual critique of both capitalism and socialism, shouldn’t be dismissed because of the moral failings of one of its advocates. [Also, as someone who has recently moved to a rural community and is giving a lot of thought to the differences between life here and in Vancouver, I think the critique of industrial urbanisation in, for instance, Fr Vincent McNabb’s The Church and the Land has a lot of merit.]

2005-12-31 21:56:14 • John Hudson
Hrant, I think you might find very little to rebut in The Stroke. While some of what Noordzij wrote in the occasional pieces that comprise Letterletter may be taken as a kind of chirographic design advocacy – although I’m not convinced that it is intended as such –, in The Stroke he is explicit in stating that what he is outlining is a way of analysing and describing what one is looking at in writing. There is almost nothing in the book about type design or typography: it is really presenting a new methodology for palaeography and other studies of specifically written forms that focuses on deciphering how strokes were made during writing rather than on the classification of the resulting letterforms. There is an adaptive functionality available to type designers from this methodology, that can be utilised to aid judgements that affect, specifically, uniformity or harmony of structure as it contributes to the overall style of a typeface. That is, it is a methodology that assists in aesthetic decision making, and is not intended or promoted as something that overrides or undermines other methodologies, whether focused on the aesthetic or functional aspects of type. Noordzij doesn’t even go this far – this is my own formulation of what I find useful to my work in his analysis –; as I say, he barely mentions type at all.

2006-01-02 03:27:14 • hrant

You were talking about a rebuttal, specifically, to The Stroke, and this is what I think you will find, when you read it, a misplaced notion, because The Stroke is not a book about type design or typography. It is a much more narrow piece of work than Letterletter, which covers a broader application of Noordzij’s theory. I think there is stuff in the latter to which your critique applies, but I think you will not find it so in The Stroke. It is a much less polemical work than Letterletter – excepting when he briefly gets onto the subject of how reading and writing are taught in schools –, and basically consists of an analysis of a number of different stroke patterns in writing to determine how they are made and how to describe them. It is a book about looking at written forms.

Because Noordzij’s books have been published by typography and design oriented publishers (Hartley & Marks, Hyphen) and he has taught in a type design programme, I think we are tempted, as a community, to presume that we are Noordzij’s audience and that he is telling us something about how we should do what we do. But what strikes me about parts of Letterletter and basically all of The Stroke is that he directly addresses palaeographers and students of writing systems. If you drop the presumption that he is talking to type designers, and read the books as a critique of assumptions and methodologies in the study of the history of writing, I think you get a truer picture of Noordzij’s engagement.
