What I know of Greek fonts first comes from the TeX documentation and I understood that the word “apla” was used to refer to that type of Didot font with rolling stress. Here is a link http://www.tex.ac.uk/tex-archive/help/greek/greek.faq and what is said about it

Levy made his Greek fonts after the Didot design, a typeface which was originally created two centuries ago by the famous Didot printers in Paris and which is still used extensively by Greek printers today. Among Greek printers, this Didot typeface is known as "apla" (plain), but often outsiders call it incorrectly "Greek roman".
A version of Monotype Greek 90 is called Ἁπλᾶ by Haralambous' Atelier Fluxus Virus http://www.polytoniko.org/fonts.php?newlang=en#AFV where Ἁπλᾶ might just mean "regular". So maybe I misinterpreted the documentation though Haralambous in a paper entitled "From Unicode to Typography, a Case Study: the Greek Script", has a subsection 1.7.1 on the apla style where he includes the italic and the bold.

But I can add that absolutely the same one has been used by Adelphi publisher for some ‘older’ books, as e.g. La ragione errabonda by Giorgio Colli, up to about 1995. Afterwards, this publishing house used the more ordinary -and, I would add, more ugly- greek series of MS Times New Roman (!). I know that when wanting something, one wants EXACTLY that; but at least I may suggest something appearing to be inspired by similar approach : provided by the [[http://www.sbl-site.org/educational/BiblicalFonts_SBLGreek.aspx|Society of Biblical Literature]]. It has the same ‘broken’ ends, some ‘archaicity’ in the ductus, but it differs for the light slant to the right and more explicitly in some features, as the theta design. The hunting goes on
