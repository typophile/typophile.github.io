What is the best way to present your typeface to the world?

April 13, 2009 • dmarx

If you have created a new font and want to share it with the world how would you make its debut? Obviously, you need to show the font in different sizes with different word combinations. But do you share conceptually how and why it was created? If it is for print do you create a booklet or a poster? Or, would you just create a website with examples of how the font can be used? I know you can go to any font foundry's website and see the different typefaces and how they are used but there is something about seeing that typeface in a well designed piece. Emigre creates beautiful booklets that show the new typeface and how it can be used in a design. The font I created, Knot Linear, was inspired by the knots, twists, and turns in some three dimensional wire letters that I made. The concept I had behind this font was to capture the playfulness and interest of the wire letters, while still creating a cohesive, clean typeface. The font includes variety in thick and thin strokes, as well as rounded serifs. Overall, I think that it has grown into an effective display typeface. I think that a poster displays a font well because there is endless possibilities of how to combine point sizes as well as direction and balance. For the poster I made to "advertise" Knot Linear, I developed a story that tells how the typeface originated, or in this case, landed on Earth. This design is the front of my poster (though it is not completely finished) and the story is as follows: Somewhere, far away in our own galaxy, near Pluto we suspect, there was a war of the native Font Tribes of that cold moon. The Knot Linears were forced to become refugees, they were just not linear or symmetrical enough to compete, and they wondered the galaxy looking for a new home. Mars was hot enough to melt their metal bodies (or so they felt, Knot Linears are known for being melodramatic), calling Saturn home just didn’t have the right ring, and Jupiter was a bit too gassy. On Earth, however, they found many odd and nonsensical shaped beings. There was also a lot of metal, which they worshiped due to being made from it. After watching the native life, it seemed like a nice place to live, where they could fit in well, and where they could even make nests in the lush vegetation. And so, on a clear dark night in New Orleans, Louisiana, the tribe of Knot Linear happily settled on Earth. The back of my poster focuses on the actual make-up or anatomy of Knot Linear. I think that this aspect of any font is important to understand because it is in fact the essence of the font. The back of the poster breaks down one large letter R (a particularly visually interesting letter), as well as an entire word, "simplicity" (a word that describes this font well). Overall, I think that my poster design is effective and does an adequate job of showing off my font. [img:sites/default/files/old-images/OTCPOSTERB_6129.png] When we began this assignment, our professor had us create letters with everyday household objects. My object happened to be pills, hence the name OTC for my typeface. I really enjoyed this project, and in the beginning thought it would be easier than it was. I could definitely spend months perfecting OTC, and have gained a new respect for typeface designers that can spend day after day, week after week, ect., looking at the same typeface. I knew that the basis of my typeface would be the oval pill shape. Creating such a bold typeface would limit OTC's usage to display only. During the creating process I knew that the overall shapes would create a typeface that had a futuristic feel. That's why I chose a passage from 1984 to illustrate different sizes and ways that OTC could be used. I chose the cool color palette to illustrate a technology/futuristic feel within the poster. I stuck with mostly type to present my typeface, because I felt that Orwell's words carried a certain power behind them that needed little imagery to push my concept. I did however put a vector image that I created with my original pill shape and circles to give the poster a slight added depth. Our professor had us create three stories for our typeface. The story we chose for mine was that it was created in the future to brand people's identities, and it evolved into the Orwell theme. I think it's interesting to think of how people will be identified in the future and what form our identities will take. We already are labeled with numbers and letters, but what is our identity? That was the exciting part of creating the typeface was seeing the evolution of the letter's from their original form to the finished project. I am really happy with how my poster and typeface turned out, and would like to continue to work with it. (Maybe a summer project, eh?)

The font I created is named Lilienstijl ("lily style"). I chose the name for two reasons. First because it was a term used to describe the organic forms used by art nouveau artists and second because my name (Azucena) means lily in spanish. The delicate letter form is organic, resembling that of art nouveau, but also has a mechanical feel, giving it a contemporary twist- becoming new nouveau. The poster was designed to display this idea by incorporating art nouveau floral elements made from the letters and flourishing decorative borders mixed with pattern. [img:sites/default/files/old-images/Blinds_blog_4945.jpg] It would be ideal to have a booklet that enables the display of both the type used in a poster, as well as being used as copy or literature. This allows the typeface consumer to see all aspects of the font's capabilities and forms. In making my own poster for my typeface, Xylem, I combined the simple with the ornate, and in both, the concept of growth is demonstrated. The lines of text from the story grow in size up the page, while the letters in green organically show growth as they emerge in both size and levels of opaqueness. Growth is the concept behind the design and the typeface itself. The letter forms of Xylem are named after the xylem produced by plants in nature. Its quirks and simplicities are reminiscent of the organic growth seen in nature. That is why the two elements of growth in the poster weave in and out of each other like the vines amongst the trees. [img:sites/default/files/old-images/font_poster_5687.jpg] The typeface I created is named Mies, after architect Mies Van der Rohe. The font itself is very angular and geometric, which reminded my of modern architecture and furniture. My poster design was created using one of Van der Rohe's drawings. I substituted the walls, ceiling and floor with the typeface, added texture and depth to the poster. As you read a quote from Van der Rohe himself, your eye is drawn to a sketched man standing next to a 'chair' and 'table'-which reads the name "Mies." This poster was a struggle for me because I was having a difficult time identifying with what the font was suggesting. In the end, however, I think I created a compelling poster which suits the typeface's geometric form perfectly.
