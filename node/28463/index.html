2006-09-27 22:13:05 • jselig

Hi Maurice,

As several people have already said, to produce 250 copies, the quantity mitigates for digital printing. As a very general rule of thumb, somewhere between 500 and 1,000 copies, you should get a similar cost between digital and litho (which is the alternative, screen printing being another matter entirely).

It is also the case that design and origination costs would be much the same as for producing 250,000 copies.

Maurice, don’t hang your hat on this one. You don’t know enough about it and we can’t give any definitive answers. By all means get a phone quote from a local digital printer for an eight page newsletter this big - don’t worry about stock etc., as it won’t make a lot of difference on this quantity - get them to quote the best they have.

The design cost will largely be decided on who blinks first. If this is to be a pilot, design cost could be amortised. I expect that the designer (in a situation like this) is also producing the setting and artwork, the cost of which depend on loads of factors (photography, licenses etc), and client’s revisions could exceed the original price in any case.

I haven’t done a newsletter in years, never enjoyed the experience or been that good at it, but I would do it for $10,000 (50% up front, the rest now) for a bang-up job and my undivided attention. There are lots of good people who would do a great job, and then I could let you have a price for doing what they’ve done - but anybody can quote someone else’s work, in retrospect and without actually having to produce it.

By all means question making the communication in this way, or at all - question the tactic - but there are good reasons for using printed words and pictures - just look at all the magazines about the internet.

Lastly, could I please be considered for the post of director of this division - I’m great at instruction and I could probably figure out what the job entailed within a few weeks.
