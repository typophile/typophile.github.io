A simple oblique would be better than a true italic, which has different letterforms from the upright letters. Lots of italics designed for early sans typefaces were simple obliques, which used the same letterforms as the uprights. These of course have the optical corrections to adjust for the effects of skewing.

There are, of course, hand-lettering alphabets meant to be drawn in the way you describe, and no shortage of old hand-painted signs whose glyphs rise or fall.

Most of the letterpress shops I’ve been in have a few drawers full of electroplated letterpress cuts that run at an angle, mostly of company names and advertising terms. There are also probably a number of pieces of triangular type furniture, used for locking up these odd-shaped blocks. I would wager that someone, somewhere out there, has drawn a digital font that does this, and you can probably find printed reproductions of an angled alphabet in a book. Start with the Dover Press letterpress clip art series.

I recently came across a font of angled type in which the body of each character was rhomboid in plan, not rectangular, as it would be in a conventional face. It was part of a mixed-media piece, made up of found type and electroplated company logos, at the Smithsonian American Art Museum.
