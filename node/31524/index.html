Sans and Pi Recommendations for Travel Guide

February 16, 2007 • hellogreg

Long time listener, first time caller…

I’m currently designing a travel guide for a small American city. When picking fonts, I needed faces that set nicely at smaller sizes in narrow columns. (The book is to be a trade paperback with two columns per page.)

At first, I was using Minion Pro (for body text) and various weights and widths of Myriad Pro (for headings, place names, addresses, operating hours, etc.). Minion is Minion: it gets the job done. Myriad was the surprise to me. Though I’ve rarely used it in the past, its condensed faces looked just right for a travel guide with the feel I was going for.

A couple days in, I realized why it looked so “right”: Lonely Planet uses Myriad everywhere. Duh.

So now I’m looking for a good workhorse, OpenType sans family with condensed and regular widths. Right now, I don’t have a huge library that fits the bill. It’d be nice to expand my roster beyond the Myriads, Frutigers, Futuras, and Franklin Gothics.

My initial thoughts were to go for Verlag or Proxima Nova–probably because I’m looking for excuses to buy them, anyway–but I’d love to hear what you folks recommend. (And if, by chance, you have a favorite text serif with which to pair the sans, I’m all ears… er, eyes.)

Also, I’d welcome info on any Pi fonts useful in travel guides (e.g., map markings, symbols to signify that a hotel has a pool, etc.). Carta and Transit Pict seem like good places to start, but are there more out there?

Thanks so much in advance.

I’m always pleased with Officina in small sizes, but as far as I know it only has a single (semi-condensed equiv.) width. Bell Centennial is great if you have to go really very very small, as you might in marking minor streets and streams on a map.
