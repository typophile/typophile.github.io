How small (pts) can you print text if you have a powerful magnifying glass?

Hello,

The idea is to give out magnifying glasses to patrons so that people can read the tiny text. I want the text to be as small as possible, but still legible using a visual aid like a magnifying glass. It is fine to have the text not legible without a magnifying glass though.

I suspect that a printer’s DPI would be important in figuring out the smallest size since you need to fill a character’s stroke with sufficient number of dots (ie. ink). For sake of discussion, let’s assume the printer is a commercial printer who mass prints books.

How small in pts can I print the text?

Note that at sizes smaller than – guestimating here – around 4 pts some additional factors come into play: the roughness of the paper, and the thickness of the ink or toner. Even with super-smooth paper, ink may spread out (the same problem as when trying to accurately produce an exact level of gray, where it is called “dot gain”) and toner may not be delineated sharply.

Once, while retouching small faults in a bitmap image – which, by all rights, should have reproduced faithfully – I inserted my initials in it with 5 pixel high characters into the dot of an ‘i’. Unfortunately, after offset printing, the ink had run ever so slightly. The dot was visibly not ‘sharp’ anymore, but that was about it.

Typonaut wrote:

Nobody has mentioned DPI yet. Is everyone making an assumption on the DPI? I may be missing out on something significant, but is everyone assuming a DPI of 300? Aren't there commercial book printers who can print at higher DPI than 300?

There are very few printers that output at resolutions of 300 dpi. I think you’re confusing the 300 ppi that’s a common resolution of photos and scanned imagery intended for printing at about 150 lpi.

Type is typically vector-based, so resolution is irrelevant. Unless the typography is printed with a screen tint, the 150 lpi is also irrelevant.

What is relevant is the resolution of the output device. An inexpensive B&W laser printer typically prints at least at 600 dpi. The output resolution of a typical commercial-quality imagesetter or platesetter typically varies between 1200 and 4800 dpi. The resolution of microprinting used for anti-counterfeiting purposes on currency is even higher than that.
