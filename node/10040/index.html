Question concerning the lowercase "g" and "a"

March 31, 2005 • rezmason

Hello. I'm not a typographer, but the craft has always interested me. Something that I've wondered about for a long while is why there seem to be two distinct (but identifiable) versions of the lowercase "g". I'm unfamiliar with the lexicon for discussing type, so I'll do my best with a visual description. In some fonts, the "g" is composed of a bulb, a loop that drops below the baseline, and a slight vertical protrusion at the top right corner. In other fonts, the "g" is composed of two bulbs, one below the baseline and attached to the other with a short loop, and a horizontal or diagonal protrusion at the top right corner. Whether a font has one "g" or the other is seemingly totally independent of its style. Two examples of these "g"s are in the fonts Futura and Trebuchet MS. Why is there this duality? And how did the double-bulbed variety come about? I've been pondering this over with friends; we feel that they both must have developed from one variety in the past. We also realized that the same duality occurs with the letter "a", though not necessarily in the same font as the "g". Does anyone know how these dualities came about?
These dualities stem from the derivation of type from written forms, and represent two historical ways in which these letters developed prior to the invention of printing from moveable type. These are not, by the way, the only forms of g and a encountered in palaeography, there are other forms which did not influence the development of typographic forms, or whose influence was short-lived or localised. A good example of localised influence is the traditional Irish g. You can see some examples in Michael Everson's digitisations of historical Irish types.

Hi Paul, "Correct me if I'm wrong, but… The double-storied g and a are commonly features of serifed roman fonts, wheras the single-story forms are more characteristic of an italic or a cursive font (or writing style)." This is generally true, though there are certainly always exceptions. Double-story "a" and "g" are also fairly common in sans serif Roman fonts as well. But of course, in an effort to simplify the structure, many sans serif designers opt for a single-story character. "for your consideration: does the following glyph look more like the double-storied g? or a single-story g?" As always, this is one of those cases where one could argue it falls under either category. (Sort of like, is the movie a drama with comedy, or a comedy with drama.) Within the branch of Gothic characters, it was also quite common to find "a"s that appear to be in both categories at the same time. But if one wanted to be a historic stickler, the"g" example you show would more technically come under the heading of a single-story character. This is because it is a Textura character following the same principle as the Italic (and other manuscript hands of that era) lowercase "g." The lowercase "g" of that time was always closed (i.e., the descender hooked back up to the nadir of the body). This was done to prevent it from being confused with a "q," which back then was written like we make today's "hooked-g." This enclosed Italic "hooked-g" was a single-story "g" (even though it appears to fall into either category) because within the same Italic alphabet there was also a version (sometimes multiple versions) of "linked-g" (double-story g) as well. Both "g"s were freely used side by side.
