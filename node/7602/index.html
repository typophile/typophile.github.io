Photography was invented in the early 19th century, by means of simultaneous developments among a number of researchers (Daguerre, Herschel, Niepce, Talbot) who were all exploiting Schulze's 1727 discovery of silver nitrate darkening on exposure to light.

The French government considered the invention so important that they bought Niepce and Daguerre's patent(s) and put them in the public domain.

The first book containing photos was Talbot's "The Pencil of Nature" (1846).

Many further big technical breakthroughs in portability didn't come until the early 20th century. Portable flash photography (developed in large part by my great-grandfather, George R Lawrence); the 35mm camera in 1925 (Barnack) was another huge advance in portability; and then faster films made it possible to do away with the flash altogether. Then you got color, and the Polaroid, all before 1950.

Any museum/gallery using Arial for exhibit labels didn't have a decent typographer involved in their development. I'd agree with most of the above suggestions, except that stuff like Bulmer and Walbaum is probably not best for readability under sub-optimal conditions (which exhibits usually are, in my experience).

Personally, I'd go with a good humanist sans serif, if the client is hung up on sans serifs.

2004-11-09 19:01:21 • Thomas Phinney
Most everything I did for the American Museum of Natural
History (in Manhattan) was set in Thesis Sans (often called
TheSans because it shows up in your font menu that way). It's
timeless, readable, versatile and most importantly, it doesn't
upstage the exhibits.

Whitney is also an excellent choice (designed for
The Whitney Museum of course).

Both of these typefaces are easily more readable than Arial.

Good luck.

The more body text you have, the more sans is a bad idea.

But for the heading, simply figure out the flavor you want.
Sans and serif give different flavors, but functionally they're both fine for headings.

"Use only sans for heading" is naive Modernism.
