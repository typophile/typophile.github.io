2007-03-11 17:19:09 • William Berkson
The ink film in letterpress is physically thicker on the paper and impresses more into the paper fibers. Offset has a wash cleansing of extra residue from the plate and a second transfer from plate to blanket which further thins the ink that hits the paper. This allows for finer detail and less need for trapping but the thickness of ink is less. The effect is greater on noncoated paper which is more typical of books than slick mags. Web presses can get overly washed sometimes too so even coated stock impressions can be a bit anemic. As usual, a good pressman is required no matter what method is used. ChrisL
2007-03-11 17:36:37 • dezcom

I've specifieded jobs printed rich black but not ordinary text. It was always large solids, usually reverses and, most typically. where a photo was knocking out of solid black and the one-color black looked dead next to the 4-color build. 2007-03-12 17:48:11 • William Berkson
Hrant Generally across the board yeah, perhaps contemporary letterpress doesn't make the grade, but then, so doesn't the majority of offset work. Examples of exemplar? I'd think that high-end commercial letterpress prior to the Great Depression years was about as clean and crisp and black as it could get without the need for noticeable impression. A readily example would be the 1923 ATF specimen book and other type specimen books of the period. Those machines, processes, techniques, and men are, alas, long gone. Still you might find it hard to make your case in examining the work of post-war fine press printers such as Victor Hammer or Lewis Allen, et al (and even some contemporary fine printers), where controlled impression on dampened handmade paper results in letterforms that are crisp and darkest black and without ink spread. And a breath-taking marvel to behold. The deep black of letterpress isn't dependent upon the film thickness, but rather the ink coloration, the technique of application, and the materials of production. And, of course, the rationale of the pressperson. In this regard, it's good enough, isn't. Gerald

2007-03-13 03:31:14 • William Berkson
William Not to be contrary, but I think to compare letterpress and offset is sort of like comparing apples to oranges. Yes, the inks are different, as are the processes. On the other hand, there are no old formula letterpress inks still available. Most letterpress folks today do use offset inks. I use stone lithographic (printmaking) inks in printing my letterpress work, though they too would be quite different than commercial offset inks. That said. I'm not sure I can adequately explain in words how one gets the deep black in letterpress (without accompanying ink spread). At least I'm not up for it tonight. It's a combination of a number of material factors combined with a theoretical approach to printing. Bit pretentious I suppose, but I think accurate. But what you are seeing through the loop is inadequate film lay on the letterform. The fibers of the paper are showing through. Not uncommon. The white line is due to the wrong tack/flow of the ink or incorrect roller pressure or wrong printing height (this isn't necessarily an impression problem though it could be as well). Or a combination of one or the other or all three. The ink film is being pushed or squeezed off the letterform. You are seeing separation of pigment and carrier. Not so cool actually. To correct this, one would have to start all over. Not always an option to the commercial printer. Gerald
