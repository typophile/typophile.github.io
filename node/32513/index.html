The font is modified Monterey from Bitstream:

Get out brush and ink, load the brush, loosen up with a few dozen practices, let fly, try different size brushes and from different distances, standing or sitting, eventually you will produce a smooth logo that you can digitize, the lettering look you are after is nearly always best done on paper first rather than by altering a typeface.

I realise that isn’t what you were looking for, an alternative might be to print it out at varying sizes and sketch over them, the main problems I see is the form of the v which can easily be mistaken for u, the lack of variation of the the right side and the joining stroke from o to x creates a light space in the centre and the last stroke of the x (that travels up to the top right) could be more exuberant, the typeface is built to work in extended setting (well, as extended as you would want to see a brush script) and so is restricted by the requirements of typesetting methods, a brush script logo is rarely used in close conjunction with similar text so can/should be more in nearly every direction.

2007-03-24 13:26:52 • sandrosandro

I think the information at the ‘typies’ site is misleading. For example, he shows the elimination of intermediate points between extrema as a way to improve curves. Often this is true, but sometimes not. You can see intermediate points in the letters of masters such as Matthew Carter and Robert Slimbach and many others.

So the directive to eliminate intermediate points, with no qualification, is misleading. Personally, I find I need to try it both ways and see what gets me the best result: I try first with no intermediate point; if that works, fine. If not, try an intermediate point. Then when you get the curve pretty near what you like, copy the outline to mask and test eliminating the intermediate point. If that works, fine. If not, keep the intermediate point.

I find one of the most useful tools in eliminating bumps is to slide the node while keeping the handles fixed–then moving the handles later. [In FontLab, that’s shift-click to drag the node or handle only.] All this with your starting point copied to mask.

I would be interested in the comments of others on when they find intermediate points helpful, and when not.
