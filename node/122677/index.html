19th-century Swedish map

January 20, 2015 • Doctor Science

I’m trying to find a font (or pair of fonts) that look as much as possible like the text on [[http://idahlgen.scienceontheweb.net/Hermelinweb/ | S.G. Hermelin’s map of Värmland]] (1808).

Note the difference between the regular and italic font, especially with regard to “g”. In the original this type was very small – it is an extremely detailed map.

I’m working on a book of family history, and my plan is to use the magic of Photoshop to erase the names on the map, and put back only the names of the larger towns and the various hamlets where my ancestors lived, to make something that is more easily read and understood nowadays. I’m fully prepared to photoshop in the Swedish accent marks, if I can’t find a modern font that includes them.

It’s kind of Baskerville-y, but not quite. Where do I even go to look for such things? or to find out what the original font might have been?

– Doctor Science

This is engravers lettering not a font as such. Some fonts are based on the lettering in 19th century Germanic maps. They used to have rather generic names, like ‘Kartenschrift’ [map type]. Consider: Römisch http://www.myfonts.com/fonts/linotype/roemisch/ and Kursivschrift Pro Stehend http://www.myfonts.com/fonts/linotype/kursivschrift/ Don

John Baskerville designed his eponymous typeface with the distinctive g around 1752 and the engraver of the Hermelin map would almost certainly have seen it in use. He seems to have thought the ear was unnecessary. Nineteenth century map engravers and sign makers seemed to find a source of expression in making unusual versions of the letter g. Consider this remarkable Ausgang: https://www.flickr.com/photos/hardwig/2524903281/ Don

Nineteenth century maps were also the source of Surveyor by Hoefler & Frere-Jones http://www.typography.com/fonts/surveyor/overview/

Also consider Berthold’s Karten-Augustea, which unfortunately does not seem to be currently available from a legitimate source. Seems to have been cloned by Softmaker as “Map-Type.” Don
