2008-10-15 17:40:40 • hrant

Like much of the type market, information is mainly anecdotal. I’m sure my experience is very different from James'–although I agree with his theory of the brand-driven impetus.

If you are interested about this sort of thing Jon, you could commission a survey now, and follow up later to see if there has been a change. It would involve analyzing a list of foundries and seeing what they reveal about their custom work. However, they might not reveal all their custom work (I don’t get around to posting everything I do, whether or not I am happy with the results), and just scanning foundries might not pick up all active work. For instance, some lettering artists also do custom type design, such as Ian Brignell’s new font for Bell.

Custom type varies by country. I get the impression there is more corporate work in the UK, more periodical work in North America.

With regard to Hrant’s concern, I would say that declining news sales in the US, coupled with consolidation (both economic and technological), might lead to a reduced demand for custom news fonts. On the other hand, technology can open up new markets (on the internet perhaps?)

While it might seem that an economic downturn would reduce the budget for custom type, on the other hand, a dramatic change in the zeitgeist could rapidly obselesce exisiting identities, leading to a demand for rebranding. And then of course, with bankruptcies and takovers, new corporate identites emerge which will require expression. Recession as opportunity.

In any market there is going the be a moment when saturation occurs — eg most people own your product(s). After this the market is one of steady renewal of obsolete stuff. Example: iPods. You own one, you buy a new one when it breaks down.

Type is not a normal business, of course, but the basic principle of saturating a market still applies, imo. How many typefaces do you use? How many do you need? How much new stuff is exciting enough?

Something to think about: the implementation of OpenType is probably an impetus for continuing growth is type sales, but the time will come where this technology has matured with the evident consequence that sales of OT-faces will slow down.

Like a lot of other products/services there will be a high end part of the business where snobbish people/firms will want to display their uniqueness by commissioning custom typefaces (example: David Berlow did a fontfamily for some billionaire who wanted the digital interfaces for his multimillion dollar ship to reflect the excellence he aimed for in every aspect of its design and build — David presented that during Type 2008). [Fashion: haute couture like John Galliano).

A larger, but still small group of companies/individuals will buy the more exclusive and pricy fonts that might for instance trickle down to the market after their exclusive rights have ended. Some designers may cater to this group. (Fashion: like Dries van Noten).

A greater part of the market will just follow the trends that smart marketeers will initiate. As happens in a lot of other fashionlike industries (cars, clothes, shoes, etc.). Fast-and-furious typedesigners could make a bundle here (Kris is fast!), but they need marketing prowess and power. (Fashion: Donna Karan, Ralph Lauren).

The masses will just use what comes along, preferably free, maybe with some likeness to what is hip. But most of all: cheap. Think of thousands of name– and faceless type-engineers in sweatshops in China or Vietnam, drawing/building quick and dirty knockoffs of bestselling typefaces, marketing them through the internet, or by including 10 cent coupons in breakfast cereals.
