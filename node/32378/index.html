paragraph formatting when using drop caps?

March 19, 2007 • speedlace

I’ve seen the discussion thread concerning the use of quotation marks in drop caps, but I’m wondering if there is a preferred model for situations that involve short sentences, such as the below:

“I have a formatting question.”

“What’s the problem?”

“Drop caps.”

“You’ve come to the right place.”

“I hope so!”

If I drop cap the quotation marks and the I in the first sentence, the next few lines become indented to accomodate it. So in a situation such as the one above, do I need to indent them FURTHER to indicate each line represents a new paragraph (and speaker)?

I’ve thumbed through numerous books that employ drop caps, and I can’t seem to find anything on my shelf with chapters opening with short bursts of dialogue as I’ve outlined above.

Any help is much appreciated!

Of course, there are no rules when it comes to this. And BTW, I use quotes about halfway between the dropcap and text size, though rarely over 20 points. They are then hung in the margin. If the editor doesn’t object, I just skip the openquote – it is implied. Some editors won’t accept this, though.

As to subsequent lines: If the first paragraph is less than the depth of the dropped cap, I indent the subsequent paragraph, and it’s “paragraph indent” is further indented the additional amount of a normal paragraph indent.

In the case you show, with several lines all less that 1 line long, I think I’d forgo using a drop cap – unless there is a compelling reason, like this is the odd chapter in an otherwise straightforward book. But I’d take basically the same tack as outlined above, indent subsequent lines to clear the cap. As long as the reader isn’t confused about what begins a new paragraph, I think you can let the graphic look take over & do as you choose.
