I have a challenging book cover project for which I did a contemporary calligraphic brush title. I am co-designing or consulting on the overall layout of the cover and font choices. The title is now final. Early layouts tested Lithos and Optima for the subhead directly below the title, and now people are leaning towards a version of Copperplate Gothic (no serifs). It's not bad, but what to put WITH it is challenging. What is the vernacular with this font? Serif fonts like Bodoni or Times have been tried but seem to have no relationship at all and are jarring. I'd love thoughts on this.
Hmmm, ok so this question apparently doesn't light anyone's design fire. Maybe I'll re-post with a more general but equally burning question about vernacular in general. But focused on Copperplate and its 1400 variations. Surely there is someone out there who is a fan of this face and how to mix it into contemporary design?????

>I had thought there was a sans serif version out there but can’t find it, so I have no idea now what it actually is. [[http://new.myfonts.com/fonts/bitstream/engravers-gothic/|Engravers Gothic]] is like Copperplate Gothic, but without the tiny serifs. Also [[http://new.myfonts.com/fonts/agfa/sackers-gothic/|Sackers Gothic.]] I agree with you that Copperplate Gothic can look great--or tired--it just depends on how it's used... Kris Sowersby's [[http://vllg.com/KLIM/Feijoa/mudTyper+Weights/|Feijoa]] has nice soft serifs, but I really don't know if it will work with what you have. Also there are outright calligraphic romans like [[http://new.myfonts.com/fonts/adobe/brioso/|Brioso]] and [[http://new.myfonts.com/fonts/linotype/alcuin-lt/|Alcuin]].
