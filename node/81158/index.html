Been using http://www.fontsquirrel.com/ and http://www.google.com/webfonts, @font-face, no-Javascript. With the resources available to me, “free” is a mightily attractive option. My concerns with that are two-fold: (1) quality of available fonts, and (2) being able to sleep well at night, knowing that I haven’t taken anything from type designers that they weren’t already giving away (see [[http://www.google.com/webfonts/family?family=Cabin|Cabin]]).

Well, and there’s always (3) availability of the client-specified font(s) for embedding. When they insist on [[http://new.myfonts.com/fonts/linotype/itc-eras/|Eras]], or [[http://www.fontspring.com/fonts/fontsite/honeymoon|Honeymoon]]…

When embedding any-font-you-darn-well-please becomes too easy, we’re going to see some God-awful web designs using too many typefaces and horribad mismatches. But that’s a risk I’m willing to live with to put the tools for good typography into the hands of responsible web developers.

So, with WebINK, are we closer? Or is this just another more-of-the-same also-ran?

2011-04-13 17:04:31 • Frank ADEBIAYE

Speaking for myself and the WebINK team, I can say that we care about quality fonts and typography; personally, I’ve been a Typophile regular a lot longer than WebINK has existed.

The world of web fonts is kind of a wild west frontier, but we’re doing our part to help settle it. Some examples of things that are already public: engaging in W3C web font standards discussions, making showcase pages showing nice usage of web fonts, recording minimum pixel sizes for all our specific fonts, inventing the FontFuse system (where users can submit font combinations and vote on other people’s combinations). Of course there are some other things we are working on that I can’t yet talk about. :)

There are some issues still being wrestled with for web fonts, and of course any web font system can be misused. But we do draw a line on font quality, for example: there are foundries and fonts I have turned down, or deliberately chosen not to approach, because of concerns about spacing, outline quality, or the like. There’s one foundry who we signed a contract with some months ago, but are waiting to see some reworking on their fonts to deal with concerns about outline quality.

Of course, “free” is great, and we aren’t really trying to compete with that. Instead we offer a bigger selection, and better quality, at a reasonable price. (Don’t get me wrong, there are some fabulous free fonts out there, but one has to be pretty savvy to avoid the drek.)

Cheers,

Thomas
