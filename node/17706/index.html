I don’t really know why designers may have liked Apple systems traditionally, but there are currently some substantial differences between how say, Mac OS 10.4 (Tiger) and WindowsXP handles type, in terms of programming interfaces available to developers.

If you have a stomach for details, you may want to follow this link and examine (google!?) related documentation.

http://developer.apple.com/documentation/Cocoa/Typography-date.html

Apple has made some significant commitments to including advanced OTF layout support in their basic text layout APIs. This is very cool. I expect, Microsoft will include these features in the APIs used by Vista developers.

As always, “the devil is in the details.” It may be, and it may have been in the past, that Apple has made it easier for developers to add new typographical features in their applications. This doesn’t mean that one platform is inherently better than another…

2006-02-09 01:54:11 • dezcom

I have been a Mac user since the first model in 1984. As a designer, there were several things that attracted me personally, such as the high resolution black on white display (most computers back then had green or amber on black screens or chunky day-glow color), the easy mouse interface, the bitmap display (most other computers had character-based displays), and multiple proportional fonts (and the whole wysiwyg thing).

Then the LaserWriter and PostScript came along and the whole desktop publishing thing hit. MS-DOS had a bigger market share than the Mac even then, but the Mac was so much better suited to doing design work (and its simplicity also appealed to designers), that the Mac dominated the graphics and publishing market for years. This early lead is why Macs are still popular today with designers in spite of the fact that Macs and PCs are more or less equally suited to the work. Macs are not as dominant in publishing and design as they once were, but there is still the perception that they are best suited for the job and easier to deal with. They still dominate the high end, where they easily pay for themselves, while Windows dominates the low end, where overhead costs are more critical.

I’ve observed (and sometimes participated in) this stupid debate for over twenty years. I got tired of it a long, long time ago. Macs are still around and don’t appear to be going away anytime soon. Same for Windows PCs. I’ve come to the conclusion that behind this argument is a frustration on both sides with the fact that everyone isn’t running one common operating system. If only we were all running the same OS then all our problems would be solved. We could all use the same peripherals, the same programs, make the same kind of files and share them. But wait–we already can use the same peripherals, the same programs, make the same kind of files and share them. And we already have (at least) one universal platform: the internet. On the internet, who knows or even cares what kind of computer or what operating system you are using?

The Mac/PC debate has for most practical purposes been irrelevant for quite a while now, It’s long past time we stop beating this poor dead horse and get on with life.
