The firm of Laurent & Deberny derives from the foundry of Gillé. Alexandre de Berny bought out the foundry of his partner Laurent in 1840, and ran a business as Laurent & Deberny, or Deberny & Cie. In 1923 the foundry was merged with that of Georges Peignot and his son Charles, forming Deberny & Peignot. Specimen des Divers Types de la Fonderie Laurent et Deberny is a hard to get type specimen from that time. You could check out Fonthaus.com for fonts that might be similar to what you are looking for..

2006-10-12 05:54:01 • dan_reynolds
"Originally the leading nineteenth century typefoundry Peignot (of Gustave Peignot). On his death, his son Georges Peignot took it over. Georges’ son Charles took it over when Georges and his three brothers were all killed in the First World War. It became Deberny & Peignot in 1923 when the Laurent & Deberny foundry was purchased. From then until c1960 Charles Peignot directed the creation of a series of truly original designs. Starting in the late fifties, the company prepared the fonts for Lumitype, European Photon. In the sixties, Charles Peignot invested heavily in Lumitype, which used up some of the money to buy control of Deberny & Peignot, and let Charles go. It was closed in 1979, at which time the designs passed to Haas." Further Details Deberny & Peignot 18 rue Ferrus Paris XIV France Ceased to exist: This foundry is no longer producing fonts.
2006-10-12 06:15:40 • Palatine
In 1985, Linotype took over of the type division of D. Stempel AG. In connection with the liquidation of D. Stempel AG, Stempel's shares in the Haas’sche foundry were acquired by Linotype (see http://www.linotype.com/49-14026/1973-1989.html). There is an article about the history of Deberny et Peignot in the most recent version of the Linotype Matrix, which was written by John D. Berry (http://www.linotype.com/2720/linotypematrixvol4no2.html). Most of what typefaces from Deberny et Peignot there are to be found in digital format are in the Linotype Library (http://www.linotype.com). The biggest exception that comes to mind is BiFur, of which P22 has a version (http://www.p22.com/ihof/bifurset.html).

I once went to a very old printer's shop who for generations had done bank stationary, notes, bonds, certificates, etc. While in the back room, I notices stacks of old litho stones in the corner. On the stones were the most beautifully done scripts and ornaments. Each stone was a blank for a particular bank note. They used to be inked and pulled in the traditional art printmaker style for years! I was young and stupid and did not have a camera (or a huge truck to carry the hundred pound stones) or I would have asked to keep some of them. Someone must have labored hours by hand crafting each letter. They sure looked better than todays, "Grab some clip-art and throw some Arial at it" kind of stuff. :-( ChrisL
