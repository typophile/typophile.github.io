Is the paper uncoated, or coated? If uncoated (always my preference for anything with lots of text) the halftones will be a lot trickier to get right. Certainly a really high quality sheet would work – here in the States I would spec something like Dulcet or Superfine to behave best on press, but even a lesser sheet such as Cougar Opaque has good enough formation to give you decent ink hold-out. I’d definitely work with the printer on this one from beginning to end, to maximize the results you are after, and reduce chance for painful surprises.

If the photos are all old and have uneven dynamic range (and thus not a lot of detail in the three-quarter tones), I suppose you could try to have the pictures print a bit fuller down there in the dark end, risking plugging here and there in exchange for a bit more sheer weight and presence in the shadows. No doubt about it, though, a properly-done duotone is always going to carry things off better than a single-strike halftone, simply because you have two passes of ink, one sealing up that sponge-y paper surface and allowing the other one to sit up on top.

If it is going on coated paper, my instinct tells me that there is even less chance of halftones looking okay, as they’ll simply feel washed out. Even black halftones can look washed out. Weirdly enough, I’d venture that you stand a better chance of the pictures looking rich if printed on uncoated, by a really fine printer, than doing them with any printer on coated, just because the clay will let those tiny relief dots shine out in the shadow areas. If coated, and only one pass of ink, I’d plump for dull finish over silk or gloss (better for the reader reading the type anyway).

Unless you are going with a really top-flight printer (here in the states it would be somebody like Stinehour), I’d also tend to use a slightly coarser screen ruling (maybe 150 lines per inch instead of 175 or 200) simply to gain the tiny bit of extra contrast in the pictures. (That would be 60 lines per cm instead of 70 or 80, yes?)

And you say sepia: please look at the fanned sheets of your Pantone book and notice what happens to 497 as it pastels out (using the sheets is not a perfect means of predicting this, but it is a useful exercise). Basically you have 499 to which either K or transparent white has been added, and as you go toward pastel, you can see how very pinky-red the color goes. That is what your quarter tones are going to look like. I have often used 4625 for this kind of work, as it is almost as dark as an ink like 497, but its color feels more like what people think of as sepia toning across the spectrum of darks to lights.

Duotones would surely be safer if you could afford it, as they would give you more latitude on press, and more punch in your shadows and three-quarter tones.

My two cents anyway. With luck Patty and some of the other bookies will chime in with their instincts and experiences.

As for printed examples, visit your favorite paper merchant’s sample room and see what you can dig up. Here in the States, in the old days you could have asked S. D. Warren’s Idea Exchange for samples, but since they got bought out by Sappi I don’t think that is running anymore. But there should be plenty of printed propaganda lying about from the usual suspects of paper mills to give you a sense of how it will look. (Do you guys get sheets imported from U. S. Mills such as Mohawk, Monadnock, Simpson/Fox River, or Weyerhauser?)

Here’s a way to emulate what the pictures will look like:

In PhotoShop RGB-mode — fill a small square with the Sepia colour. Convert to Grayscale. Use the pipet-tool to determine the gray value (eg 82%). That’s what the darkest parts of your photo’s are going to be. Open a few sample pictures in Grayscale. Using Adjustments:Curves pull the 100% input handle to the value you just determined. Now convert the image to Duotone (using a single PMS-colour of course — the one you are going to use to print the job). This will give you a fair aproximation…

2007-01-04 18:54:54 • Nick Shinn

Here’s a thought, Nick. If you have already “painted yourself into a corner” with the pricing, and cannot afford duotones, what about making the pictures into line conversions? If the author is okay with it, and the content can bear up under the decidedly non-19th-century appearance, that might be a way to get a bit more dynamic range out of the images.

The trouble with halftones is that you can’t flood the deep shadows with ink – you have to leave a tiny relief dot in there or they look strange.

With a line conversion, you can let the deepest areas just go solid brown. Play around with coarseness, the fewer lines-per-cm giving you the most ink impact (with consequent loss of detail in the pictures) and the more lines-per-cm giving you more detail (but tending to look more washed out.)

The examples I’ve put in here are something I just wumped up in Photoshop using a quick-and-dirty filter routine. Usually I make line conversions in Quark, right in the document I’m designing. So the example here is very soft-looking compared to what it could look like. Judge it for the indication of “liney” personality, not for quality of finish. I’ll stick a line conversion of a product shot at the end, with sharply-defined lines, just so you see what I mean if this concept is new to you.

If one of the printers you’ve asked for pricing is one that feels really simpatico, and you’d really like to work with them, why not take the problem to them, voice your concerns, and see what ideas they have to contribute.
