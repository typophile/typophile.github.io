Here is a couple of references I have found in my library about the Italian “Etrusca” typefaces. They show how the same name was given, in different times, to very different typefaces.

The first is taken from “Questioni di carattere”, a 1997 book a book Italian 20th century Italian typography, written by Manuela Rattin and Matteo Ricci. An “Etrusca” typeface was quoted, among others, by designer Carlo Frassinelli in 1921, as a “new” typographic way, linked to the the Liberty style; in Frassinelli’s view, the Etrusca, crafted by Augusta foundry in 1910s, is a loose, dynamic typeface, able to set a certain “movement” to the printed page (a feature most seeked by the Futurism typography). Here is the typeface from the 1910 Augusta foundry catalogue – the Art Nouveau influence is noticeable:

[img:sites/default/files/old-images/augusta02_6329.jpg]

The other reference is from a 1969 printed specimen, by Fonderia Tipografica Cooperativa, which was founded in Milan in 1886. The specimen was designed by Umberto Fenocchio, who worked at Nebiolo with Aldo Novarese, and features severals typefaces, some of them designed by Fenocchio himself (like “Linea”), others most likely from the Monotype or Nebiolo catalogues. The specimen features two similar sans serif typeface families named “Etruria” and “Etrusco”, grounded in the Grotesk tradition. Etruria came in light, book, demi, bold, black, light italic, bold italic (approximate transalation of Italian terms); “Etrusco” was a different, condensed sans (for titling?), in light, book, bold, bold extracondensed, extrablack.

Zappa, Wilmant L., Baccigaluppi & C., Ferdinando Negroni, Rayper & C, Fratelli Alessandri, Cucco & Gorigli and Dell’Orto. It seems that common specimens were also printed. The image I posted was published in Rattin and Ricci’s book. Hello, since I’m more on the Nebiolo-side than Antonio (with whom we are doing this research in spare time) I can say the term “etruschi” used to be quite common in Italy to indicate generically all kinds of industrial-age sans-serif alphabets.

Most of the samples you provided are from the ubiquitous German typeface which is mostly known as “Aurora” (from the C.E. Weber foundry) in its original incarnation. I have still to track down which was the first version. In Italy, we have had our “customized” version, Cairoli, which was also copied and copied (“Etruria” here is an example of these “copies of a copy”).
