2015-01-24 14:43:11 • Michel Boyer

Brian, allow me to give you a straightforward explanation using a reference to metal type.

Point size is determined by standards: 12 point type is .166 inches high on a metal slug. In today’s world that could be referred to as the total of the Ascent and Descent as measured from the baseline. For example, using an EM of 1000, you might set your Ascent to 700 and your Descent to -300, totaling 1000 in height. Consideration must be given as to how the font will be used in determining these values.

That does not mean, nor should it mean, that your Ascenders should be 700 and your Descenders -300. One must build in vertical white space so when typeset with solid leading there is still a tiny amount of space between the lines, that amount being the decision of the designer. Dependent upon the design you can also have the design overshoot the Ascent and Descent but I would only do that for display or special-use faces such as ornate scripts.

The design itself merely sits in the point size space, oriented to a common baseline. There are no strict standards regarding capital size or x-height, or for ascender and descender length in relation to the other glyphs. For design comparisons, use Helvetica and Futura, and Garamond #3 with Adobe Garamond.

Hopefully this is helpful to you. If not, ask more questions…. :-)

@Michel Boyer: Your illustration makes an important point that had been missed in this discussion so far.

With foundry types, many manufacturers settled on three positions of the baseline - a standard position, for typefaces with fairly short descenders, a “script” or “art” line for faces with long descenders, and a titling line, just slightly above the bottom of the slug for titling faces.

This allowed different typefaces to be mixed easily - if two of the typefaces used different baselines, some leading above the one and below the other would be needed to bring the baselines into alignment, but that leading would be an integral multiple of the point, or at least the half point. Possibly, for the smallest sizes of type, of 1/4 point.

Whenever two typefaces with different baselines are mixed on the same line, that means the line requires more space than the point size, as your diagram shows.

2015-01-26 01:50:23 • Michel Boyer

It’s certainly true that Chinese, for example, doesn’t have x-height, ascenders, or descenders.

Some Indic scripts have a… vertical structure… as well, but it is different from that of the Latin alphabet, and Cyrillic, Greek, or even Armenian. Armenian is slightly different, in that the baseline of capital letters is below that of letters within the x-height for the traditional faces.

Hebrew is treated as an all-caps face - and Quoph, at least, has a descender, which somewhat justifies that, but even it is outside the Western paradigm.

Generally speaking, though, the Latin alphabet is usually thought of as the paradigm of simplicity among scripts. Here, it seems to be the most complex.

In any event, it makes sense that printers, trying to print texts in the Latin alphabet, would deal with how to fit its letters on type - and leave other scripts for later. Yes, it’s Western-centric, but what would you expect of Westerners? In the case of Chinese - one slaps the character on a type slug… they’re all aligned the same way. So there is no separate body of complexity that needs to be reconciled with Latin-alphabet practice with careful thought - Chinese faces can just be treated as titling faces.
