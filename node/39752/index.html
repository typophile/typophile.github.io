Claus: It’s not used in Danish as Hudson claimed in that other thread. This character is not normally used in writing Danish, but if syllable stress is marked in Danish, as may happen in e.g. dictionaries, then any standard Danish vowel, including the A with ring, may take an acute accent. This isn't unusual for any Latin script orthography: the acute accent is widely used to mark stress, and the only thing that makes the Danish situation a bit more complicated is that the A with ring is a standard vowel, not a diacriticised vowel, and hence you have the phenomenon of stacked marks that is otherwise not usual in European orthographies (but is very common in Africa for example). Now, most diacritic stacking in Unicode makes use of combining mark character sequences, so letters with two marks above are uncommon in the standard. If they exist in Unicode it is a very strong indication that they pre-date Unicode in some 8-bit character set, since Unicode has a policy of providing one-to-one round-trip mapping for earlier encoding standards. My guess is that the Aringacute character probably existed in something like a library cataloguing encoding, since these tend to be quite exhaustive in their coverage of possible accentuation. If you check the Danish information in the The Alphabets of Europe, you'll see that Aringacute is included there. This document originated as a CEN project, and included input from a wide range of national experts and standardisation bodies. If you were to check the documents listed in the bibliography, I'm sure you would find the justification for Aringacute in one or both. When Microsoft were spec'ing the WGL4 character set, they decided to take an inclusive approach to European diacritic letters, even if the characters in question were not found in everyday orthographic use. Hence, the Aringacute (along with Oslashacute and AEacute, and lowercase equivalents) were all included and, through this, became fairly standard inclusions in OpenType glyph sets for European language support.

2007-12-05 18:30:52 • John Hudson
In Danish typesetter terms the å is called 'Swedish å'. The character was, as it is written today with the ring on top of the a, invented in the 15th century in Sweden. In the 14th century the first ways to indicate the o-sounding a included ā ă â and ao written together like æ is. The origin of the aring was writing the a with the ring right just to it's right side, later in the 15th century it was moved up on top of the a. The first time the å is used in print is in 1526 for a Swedish bible. After a few centuries of mucking about with aa versus å, the å was implemented by law in Danish in 1948.

ISBN 9979-9004-3-1 Interestingly, the Wikipedia entry on the Danish alphabet has this to say: Standard Danish orthography has no compulsory diacritics, but allows the use of an acute accent for disambiguation. Most often, an accent on e marks a stressed syllable in one of a pair of homographs that have different stresses, for example en dreng (a boy) versus én dreng (one boy) or alle (every/everyone) versus allé (avenue). Less often, any vowel except 'å' may be accented to indicate stress on a word, either to clarify the meaning of the sentence, or to ease the reading otherwise. For example: jeg stód op ("I was standing"), versus jeg stod óp ("I got out of bed"). Most often, however, such distinctions are made using typographical emphasis (italics, underlining) or simply left to the reader to infer from the context, and the use of accents in such cases may appear dated. That explains the use of the acute accent (which is similar to the use of optional diacritics in Norwegian), but whoever wrote that entry maintains that å cannot take the accent mark, which is presumably contrary to whatever document resulted in the inclusion of Aringacute in ISO 10646 and Unicode. Annex G of the CEN document User requirements study and programming for standardization in the field of Character Set Technology provides the table of contents of the Nordic cultural requirements on information technology publication: http://www.open-std.org/CEN/TC304/docs/pt01-fin.htm Note that this includes 'Library application', and as I wrote above such use tends to be very inclusive of possible accentuation, since library catalogues need to be able to accurately represent information from books of different periods, when diacritic use may have varied.

2007-12-06 00:55:14 • ebensorkin
Ken Whistler at the Unicode Technical Committee confirms that the Aringacute entered Unicode as part of the merger of character sets with ISO 10646, i.e. Unicode 1.1. U+01FA..U+01FF came in as a set, and presumably from one of the Scandinavian national standards bodies, most likely the Danes. Ken also confirms that the characters with acute accents, including Aringacute, are listed on page 24 of the Nordic Cultural Requirements on Information Technology publication, and are annotated in the Danish column as 'Letters seldom used in writing the language'. Nick: Well, they are the folks that gave smileyface and a cup of coffee their own code points The smiley face may have come into Unicode via Apple, and I believe the 'hot beverage' symbol originated with the Japanese. There's this mistaken idea that the Unicode consists of a bunch of people coming up with characters to encode, but most of the characters are proposed by other bodies or by individual researchers, and the UTC's task is to review and determine whether the proposed characters should be encoded and how, and it does so in coordination with WG2, the ISO body responsible for 10646. It just happens that most font developers come to know the Universal Character Set via Unicode.

Michael If you are used to line commands, then here is a Python script that outputs all the unicode points in a font file. ---- #!/usr/bin/python import fontforge,sys fnt=fontforge.open(sys.argv[1],1) for g in fnt.glyphs():    if (g.unicode != -1):       print "%04X" % (g.unicode) ---- It works well on a mac (more generally on unix) and assumes FontForge is installed. Say you call the script lstucodes, you make it executable, and you put it in some folder in your path. Then, to see what unicode characters there are in GaramondPremrPro.otf you just type "lstucodes GaramondPremrPro.otf" where the font is. If you don't want the error messages, you type "lstucodes GaramondPremrPro.otf 2>/dev/null"; if you want the list to be sorted then lstucodes GaramondPremrPro.otf 2>/dev/null | sort does it. If for some reason you want both the unicode point and the glyph name, you replace the line       print "%04X" % (g.unicode) with       print "%04X;%s" % (g.unicode, g.glyphname) Here is then a copy paste of part of the output on GaramondPremrPro.otf (Version 1.014) 01FA;uni01FA 01FB;uni01FB 01FC;AEacute 01FD;aeacute 01FE;Oslashacute 01FF;oslashacute It is surprising to see all you can do with very short scripts like this.
