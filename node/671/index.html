Hrant: It's a problem common to many digital cameras, right up to the semi-pro devices. I get frustrated with my own Fuji S602 for the very same reason. (Why don't camera reviews make a bigger deal of this? Sigh…) Even when set to manual everything I feel I'm making a request for a picture to be taken, not taking the picture myself. It turns photography into something akin to video: you press the button with the hope that the desired shot is imminent, and shooting tons of footage you use your "creativity" in the editing process. The difference between film & digital stills may only be 0.2s or so (~0.1s vs. ~0.25s), but psychologically that's huge. To capture moments, stick with SLR.

2003-08-04 21:54:14 • Miss Tiffany
> In fact 0.1 seconds is the approximate fastest human finger reaction time.

The relevant physiological constraint is not reaction time, but with what precision can one operate a trusted piece of machinery. From experiments (aged 11) I know I can stop a stopwatch within 0.02s of the full second quite reliably. A photographer sees moments coming, and can adjust for a 0.1s delay in a mechanism to an accuracy better than 0.1s. But with longer delays, there will be a rapid fall-off of such accuracy. The probability that you can predict what's going to happen 0.1s from now in a complex environment, is much higher than for 0.3s hence. This is armchair musing, of course. We should both ask good photographers whether they agree… But I liked a story I heard from a friend who once found himself at a party with Henri Cartier-Bresson. The great man would stand in a corner on his own, chain-smoking Gauloises and looking depressed. Every few minutes though, he would whip out his camera, click!, then hide it away again. Hardly anyone had noticed a thing. This seems to me to be the human equivalent of a frog catching a fly: a neuronally complex, supremely precise action, the perfect result of those minutes of observation. Any error in its timing - just by a tenth - would mean the prey would escape both frog and Frog.

> Or full-motion (hi-res) video and then select a frame? :-/
