U.S. Government recommends Garamond, Century Gothic

September 14, 2014 • rayzb92

The U.S. Government has started a campaign to reduce print costs: PrintWise. Step 7 of their action plan states that ‘one of the three toner-efficient fonts’ should be used. Those fonts are Times New Roman, Garamond and Century Gothic.

You can read the ‘Seven Steps to Lowering Print Costs’ here: https://strategicsourcing.gov/sites/default/files/uploads/seven_steps_to_lowering_print_costs_within_90_days_guide_vjune2012_0.pdf

They even used Garamond and Century Gothic for the campaign logo: https://strategicsourcing.gov/sites/default/files/uploads/printwise_logo_full_color_1200px.jpg

Undoubtedly the amount of Word documents set in Garamond and Century Gothic have grown by the thousands or even millions due to this campaign.

What do you think of this campaign? Are these fonts actually ink saving?

2014-09-24 13:53:11 • Fournier

A serious analysis should consider all the relevant parameters. At fixed point size (since that value is to be 11 points), and without condensing the font, there are two obvious parameters left: x-height, and contrast.

Obviously, a sans with low contrast wastes more ink than a contrasted font where strokes are thinned here and there. Less obviously for the layman, but probably more significant, is the effect of the x-height. Here are Arial (large x-height, low contrast), Times New Roman (medium x-height, medium contrast) and Mona Lisa Solid ITC (small x height, high contrast) all at the same point size.

We are now left with an optimization problem provided we know the right objective function (the thing you want to optimize); that can’t just be the amount of ink.

And I did not even consider an additional parameter: hinting. Is it not possible to make a truetype font that is good to read on screen and that saves ink when printed (especially if all the reports end in drawers anyway).
