My EULA prohibits it. But my OpenType fonts don’t. That way, if a customer needs editable PDFs, it’s only a licensing issue, not a technical issue as well.

This approach is somewhat problematic. Say someone embeds such a font in a Word document, the recipient of that document would be able to edit the content. How would they know that there were prohibited from editing the doc? In general it’s best to avoid putting embedding permissions and EULAs in conflict.

So was I. I assume they don’t want to be bothered with replacing fonts they already have installed, if all that’s changed is licensing.

I think a font vendor would prefer to sell a site license.

Speaking as a font manufacturer/vendor, sure, but as I understand it most PDF documents using my fonts will not be in-house templates. The main reason to limit editing is so that documents that are sent to clients or suppliers of the licensee may not perform typesetting on the document, without having a license for the font.

If licensees use the documents as templates, on multiple terminals when they are only licensed for a few, there’s not much I can do about that.

However, if they attempt to do that, and find they can’t, because the embedding bits disallow it, they will be really pissed of at my foundry.

And they may well be accustomed to editable PDF functionality, because of the practice at Microsoft and Adobe, dishing out PDF-editable fonts willy-nilly, to help sell your big profit-centre products.
