2010-06-19 08:24:28 • Nick Shinn
The problem with the “ruler” is you have to know what you are measuring for. Almost every single letter in fonts regarded as excellent have what are sometimes called “optical corrections”–meaning that in order to look “right” or good geometric simplicity and uniformity has to be departed from. As Nick says, sometimes what you need is uniformity, and then using a ruler helps. For example overshoots and stems are usually pretty uniform. However, even there, there are exceptions where uniformity is not desirable.

2010-06-19 23:58:09 • Chris Dean

Note that the responses have veered toward geometry (ruler v. eye) in letterforms and away from Russell’s original comment about “working on some letter spacing & thinking, it is just geometry.”

Russell — W.A. Dwiggins and C.H. Griffith had some correspondence debating this topic 70 years ago. Dwiggins always felt that there must be some kind of mathematical formula that would allow the quick arrival at optimal spacing for a given weight of design and was frustrated at the iterative process. Griffith maintained that the eye must be the final arbiter. I spoke on this topic at the 2003 ATypI conference.

2010-06-20 14:34:54 • kentlew

2010-06-20 17:57:34 • Chris Dean

My source was copies of the correspondence which is held in the C.H Griffith Papers collection in the Margaret I. King library at the University of Kentucky, Lexington.

Fitting ultimately was done by the Linotype drawing office, under the watch and guidance of Griffith. Dwiggins would frequently indicate on his “thin paper” drawings some starting values for sidebearings, as he saw it. But these might or might not be the final values at the end of the fitting process. Final decisions were made from printed proofs: it either looked good or it did not. (And if it did not, then the degree of dissatisfaction would sometimes have to be weighed against the costs and efforts of recutting.)

The Linotype machine did not admit of any kerning, so there were never attempts to “kern by math.”

Use it or lose it. A good eye requires training, to develop taste; and exercise, to maintain the faculty. It is debilitating to rely overly on mechanical aids.

However, the human body is not built for routine (that would be an adaptive disaster, an inability to cope with the unexpected). So taste should not be allowed to ossify. At the simplest physiological level, this becomes apparent after one has been working for several hours on an italic, whereupon an inspection of roman letter forms reveals that they have mysteriously acquired a backward slant.

That’s why I check the glyphs I’m drawing by flipping them, and FontLab obliges with a little button in the Metrics panel which performs this task.

Christopher, Jos Buivenga has used IKern, and said he’s happy with it. This is both a program—and hence a mathematically based algorithm—and a service, as there are variables that are adjusted for the specific typeface, to suit the designer. Other automatic spacing programs include the original HZ program, and its development into the “optical” spacing program of Adobe. Designers generally think none of the automatic kerning programs capture what the eye can do, though they get close.

I don’t think IKern is so public, so it hasn’t been widely evaluated. Jos’s endorsement is impressive, though.
