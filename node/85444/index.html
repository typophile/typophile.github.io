2011-09-14 13:15:56 • Kristianm

How fast metal type wears down and has to be discarded varies — how much it’s used and how is of importance, and also the kind of quality control in place. But metal type is a kind of renewable resource, as long as the master matrices are still around, new type can be cast of those.

You should not be confused by the term ‘cut by’ — that refers to the master type. In short: a craftsman like Griffo creates a glyph by cutting and working a small piece of metal. During this process he checks the form by making smoke proofs on paper (using root from a candle or wick). Once the glyph is to satisfaction the piece metal is hardened (using proven techniques with chemicals and tempering). The resulting master is struck into another piece of softer metal. From this the type can be cast. As should be evident one could strike multiple exemplars of these matrices.

In English, a Prince is first in line for the throne; in Latin, princeps means pretty much the same thing. The book was written for those aspiring to power; thus, translating the title as “The Ruler” doesn’t take into account the aspirational aspect. If Machiavelli were writing today, his titled might be translated as “The Wannabe Head Honcho”…or not.

2011-09-15 15:35:13 • oldnick

(as I under stand) Machiavelli came from a fairly poor but once well-off and respected family. He managed to get a decent education but not a classical one. He never studied Greek, or commerce and banking. Florence was a centre of Greek scholarship and of trade and commerce at the time. People who wanted to get ahead studied those subjects in the same way that they go for MBAs today. He said of himself that he “studied to script rather than to thrive.”

Under the Florentine republic, he worked as a “Secretary of the Second Chancery of the Republic and Secretary of the Ten of Liberty and Peace”. These were a permanent, fairly high level jobs. He was involved in negotiations with Spain, France and the Papal States and was in a position to advise the Florentine Gonfalonieri (head of state in the republic) on matters of state.

After the Medici overthrew the republic and re-installed themselves as the ruling family, as often happens in the wake of an hostile takeover, former managers were liable be executed or exiled - err…. Made redundant. Niccolo was dismissed from his job and shortly afterwards jailed and tortured on suspicion of involvement in a plot against the Medici. on his release, he went home to the family farm and and wrote The Prince, but not as an application for employment with the Medici. If it were, it would have been full of the kind of fawning, flattering hyperbole of the style with which a duke would have expected to be addressed by a commoner. Far from that, the book is a realistic assessment of the politics of his time that advises pretty much the opposite to what a Medici would have wanted to hear and very little about Lorenzo’s magnificence.

Being a long time civil servant, he did want a job in the Medici government, but he wanted to be hired on the basis of his skill and talent in the “art of the state” and on his record as an honest and loyal public servant. Not his ability to flatter the nobility. (turns out he wasn’t much of a Machiavellian himself.)

(I keep a copy of the Prince at my desk. If you leave out the bits about killing your rivals, it’s still chock full-o-good advice for dealing with cubical mates. :o)
