James — I’m pretty sure the “No. 3” designation was introduced by Mergenthaler Linotype.

I would imagine the numerical designations were used to distinguish different versions of other foundries’ Garamonds that Linotype might have been called upon to produce for various markets.

I get the impression from McGrew’s account that Mergenthaler attempted their own version first (which presumably would have been No. 1), which did not meet with any commercial success.

According to McGrew, the “No. 2” designation was used for some other fonts of a Garamond design produced by German Linotype. (Maybe this is the version Dan is referring to?)

No. 3, then, was assigned to the version they adapted from the popular Benton revival in conjunction with ATF, which has been the most enduring.

All,

Linotype Garamond No. 3 was the cut licensed from American Type Founders (ATF) which was a refined redrawing of Garamond.

I’m not sure where Tetterode/Amsterdam would factor is, except there may be some confusion imparted by TFA / ATF confluence.

That is, Type Foundry Amsterdam or Amsterdam Type Foundry as it was sometimes referred in the States.

As far as I’m aware, Tetterode/Amsterdam didn’t engage in matrix manufacture. Megenthaler in Brooklyn produced mats for the US market, and Linotype AG, (well before being absorbed by Stempel) produced matrices for the European market.

And yes, Stempel has it’s own cut of Garamond for foundry type, as well as historic other designs, such as their take on Baskerville which differed from the “Fry’s” Baskerville ATF issued (but not called the Fry’s cut), and further, the Baskerville Mergenthaler released for Linotype in the US.

Hi James

I went through what you’re going through while researching to provide the design history commentary at http://www.100types.com/100types.com.23garamond.html

The answer to your original question is that Garamond 3 as a designation originates with Mergenthaler Linotype and their agents. As Dan and others have pointed out, this was done to distinguish it from ATF Garamond (Benton and Cleland’s original work), because the design was tweaked to make it fit the spec for Linotype machine matrices.

Bert is not mistaken; Tetterode/Amsterdam Type Foundry released their own cut named as Garamont, and I believe that Goudy/Monotype and D&P simply followed this trend for the name.

As regards the link to “Illuminating Letters”. Aside from the terrible design… I’d take the scholarship with a grain of salt.

That is, they refer to “… one produced by the Monotype foundry,…”. Monotype was not a foundry, and further, there were two Monotypes. Lanston Monotype in the US, and Monotype and Machinery Limited in Britain.

Lanston worked with Goudy (as did ATF) and produced their take, the “Garamont (Goudy) 248”. They also issued an “American Garamond 648” which, in comparing with a proof of ATF foundry Garamond laid in a case, appear to be the same cut. (Looking specifically at the g, b, and d. The bowl on the b in both is larger that the bowl on the d which is more diminutive, and the g is identical.)

English Monotype released a series No. 156 in roman and italic, as well as a refined Italic No. 174.

Pulling a late Deberny & Peignot specimen book, their “Garamont” bears relation to Lanston’s “Garamont (Goudy) 248”.
