Your specimen is from a 19th century specimen book of "Caratteri di legno" -- wood type characters -- published by Romano Scherer. "Cicero" was a big size of type, note how it fills the page. This type would have been used for display advertising. There was a great variety of wooden type but, as Ryuk already mentioned, this style was commonly called Roman. Note the ball terminal on _c_. This is a 19th century version of the high contrast "modern" style developed by Didot and Bodoni in the 18th century. Many 19th century foundries did not provide names for their typefaces, but merely used numbers. Sometimes, as in this case, they used different numbers for different sizes of the same typeface design. 19th century foundries frequently copied the designs of other foundries. A check of specimen books of William Page and James Hamilton might provide more letters with the same design as the Romano Scherer specimen. A good place to start is Luc Devroye's page on the Hamilton Holly Wood Type Co. http://luc.devroye.org/fonts-39705.html AFAIK this specific typeface has not been digitized. For substitutes with the flavor of the typeface I recommend Penny and Roman X, both already suggested by Ryuk. Dick Pape digitized many of Hamilton's typefaces in his American Wood Type collection. It might be worth going though them. They are hosted by Luc Devroye http://luc.devroye.org/pape Don
