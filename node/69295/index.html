2010-04-13 18:02:42 • Bloodtype

I think what we’re discussing in this thread are techno fonts. Techno fonts grew up alongside high tech electronics and industrial equipment. It’s a term that’s less likely to show its age than futuristic. 10,000 years from now, the technological revolution will still be traced back to the late 20th century. Whereas the term futuristic will always be vague.

Futureman: “What do you mean futuristic? Like 8060’s futuristic or more of a 9040’s look?”

When I classifying techno I think it’s important to define the distinction between industrial and techno. It’s similar to the difference between hard rock and metal. Many consider the first metal album to be Judas Priest’s British Steel. Judas Priest deliberately shed all blues influences from rock and the metal line was drawn. <-read aloud in a booming voice. Techno fonts deliberately shed art deco influences. I think the original techno font was Microgramma. It has mechanical, industrial forms but deliberately kicks off all Art Deco leanings: no crossbars that pass through the stem, varying crossbars. From that point in time, you can trace the lines of indostrial and techno. The industrial line continued through the 1950 and died in the early 1960s. Think of the joined line automotive scripts, gas pipe square fonts with aligned crossbars and Huxley. By the 1960s industrial was out of fashion. The techno line didn’t expand much until the 1970s. Between Microgramma, Stop, Data ‘70 and Handel Gothic, there wasn’t much happening. When I look at an early 1970s Photo Lettering catalog or there’s not much that I’d classify as techno. Many of the square fonts were too cute or expressive to be techno. In the 1970s you had Japanese companies using techno style for logo design, particularly for electronics. The industrial style was old fashioned and would have looked out of place with high tech electronics. That’s around the time of the Nasa worm logo (1975), Digital Sans, China etc. Around 1990s, industrial made a retro comeback with fonts like Industria and Bodega. At the same time the Designer Republic was bringing back early techno.

I hate to see techno thrown into the futuristic basket. Let’s just leave Futura, Bayer and a few others in that one.
