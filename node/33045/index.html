2007-04-15 16:50:20 • gthompson
I think outward-pointing guillemets are esthetically and communicatively the best kind of quotes: (1) As so many of you have said, guillemets beat the so-called inverted/raised commas because they leave no ugly gaps. Hrant’s point about avoiding confusion with the apostrophe may be communicatively even more important. (2) For maximum clarity, opening and closing quotes should point in a different direction. It may seem odd that I mention this, but in Finnish composition we have this unfortunate convention – it may come from Sweden – of all the quotes pointing right (i.e. either »this way» or ”this”). (3) The outward direction looks better, the quotes – especially when they are guillemets – enclosing the quoted matter in the same way as parentheses. I don’t think things like this should be considered part of the language per se. They have little to do with the grammatical system; they are arbitrary conventions (though certainly part of the literary tradition and culture of each country). Having said all this, would I have the guts to use guillemets in English text? Probably not. I considered it when doing my own thesis (in English), but ended up using the traditional forms.

I wonder how much more smoothly this conversation would go if we were all in the same room, sipping coffee or tea (or obstler) together . . . Even though I am a card-carrying adorer of guillemets, I have to back up Linda's latest observations. It seems to me that we must carry out our responsibilities to the content first, and then practice «art» as a secondary responsibility. Certainly a gold-plated curmudgeon and conservative person in many ways, Stanley Morison said something like, «It is the duty of the typographer to carry out the intentions of the author without distraction along the way.» (I paraphrase, obviously.) Communication -- in which we are all engaged as designers -- must get the ideas and feelings across without confusion. If this can be accomplished successfully, then everything else is up for grabs. I don't think Linda is saying Adhere to This Rule or We Will Tar and Feather You -- I think she's observing that conventions are necessary and expected, such as the placement and clarity of navigation on web pages, or where street signs ought to go, or where the horn button is on a steering wheel. There was a calligrapher in New England whose work I saw over the years, and she really wanted to be a painter. Her lettering became more and more abstract, until it was just pure gesture, and yet the words were still there (but illegible). Perhaps I, too, am a gold-plated curmudgeon and conservative, but I felt teased and frustrated by this. I wanted her to use pure color and line at this point, or to leave enough clarity so that if she intended me to read the words, I could still do that. Otherwise she is reading aloud a poem in the forest when no one else is present. Oder? Maybe what we are tossing back and forth cannot be fit into simple rules, and must be interpreted by each designer and client on a case-by-case basis. Certainly for Alex's catalog (not knowing the style of the furniture, the style of his design, the intended audience, the amount of material to be quoted, the nature of the material to be quoted, or the client's preferences!) my instinct would be to use »auf deutsch« and “in English” so that each language has its own differentiations. If the whole job were in one language, I would be happy to experiment with setting everything in «this style» in any western language, as long as the client felt it would be okay. In the case of a bilingual catalog, having »auf deutsch« and »in English« might be more stylistically unified, but I would think it less clear linguistically, especially if the readers of English were not Europeans. If they are Europeans, then it is much less of an issue. So many factors, and we all know so little about the job . . .
