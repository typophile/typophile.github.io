Bitmap kerning:

It's actually sort of two-sided: since bitmap fonts can only have kerning in whole pixels, kerns that are less than what would be half a pixel get dumped, which makes for fewer pairs. On the other hand, because bitmap fonts have chunky sidebearings* (like in the "v"), they require: certain pairs that an outline font wouldn't; and bigger kerning adjustments. And to make the overall spacing good, any single kern can cause a cascade of others that might not seem necessary on their own. This is a big reason Verdana's loose spacing helps it a lot.

* One great thing about grayscale design is that you can use grays not just to create higher fidelity of letterform, you can also use what I call "grayspacing": using grays to nudge the boundaries in/out to accomodate chunky spacing. Like my "v" doesn't have solid black pixels at its outermost edges, because its sidebearings are zero. Good notan, baby. ;-)

And the smaller you get the more this is true, which means that let's say a 13-pixel Mana (in the pipeline, actually) with 1-pixel stems would need fewer pairs but stronger ones. So I think if you added up all the kerning values in fonts at various "resolutions" (made to the same quality level) they might just come out about the same!

Why do most bitmap fonts not have kerning? I think the main reason is that they're seen as "lowly" - a lot of them are free. Tellingly, Bardram who takes bitmap fonts seriously does use kerning (although I don't think anything has many pairs as Mana). As for the MS fonts, their reason must be technical: they want to match linebreaks -not to mention browser rendering- with print. My stuff ignores the artificial WYSIWYG boundary.
