I have also seen this in German science books circa 1900 but, like you, I don’t know the name. AFAIK it has not been digitized. The two story g with the ear out of the top of the bowl is rare in serifed typefaces. Litho Roman – designed by Inland Type Foundry in 1907 onwards– has the g. Several weights and variations were produced by Inland before its merger with ATF in 1912. For a specimen see: http://dailytypespecimen.com/post/30458775686/litho-roman-specimen According to Mac McGrew’s book on American Metal Typefaces of the 20th Century, the Litho typefaces —start quote— are intended to imitate a style of lettering popular with lithographers in the days when lettering and designs were carefully drawn on lithographic stones. This process was especially in demand for high-quality stationery and announcements. The original Litho Roman is rather heavy, with fine hairlines. Serifs on heavy strokes are slightly filleted, while those on hairlines are heavy triangles. The ear of the g starts straight up from within the bowl, and in some series the tail of the cap R is not quite connected to the bowl. —end quote– I have lettering similar to your font on maps lithographed for Petermann in the 19th century. Don
