Given the amount of OpenType features and language support James put into the font, I don’t think he can be criticized for building the typeface on the premise of a verbatim interpretation of the Operina.

It is after all the archetypal model of complex calligraphy rendered in print. If one is going to address the issue of how to render complex calligraphy in a font, the Operina is a good benchmark.

James opted for rough rather than slick, on the premise that people find this “charming”, and because, I would imagine, it is readily comparable with the original, so that the way the alternate glyphs and swashes are implemented is highlighted.

Even so, despite this supposedly literal interpretation, he still had to decide on which particular instances of each character to base his digitization on–because the Operina is not made from type. So he also had to figure out how to make it fit as type.

There are many more considerations in making a calligraphic typeface than the degree of finish.

It’s true that most calligraphy-mimicking fonts are either too slick or too rough, but that is because, as you are well aware, taking the middle course requires the utmost skill and concentration, and is a shit-load of work.

Michael, which do you think are the most successful fonts that interpret calligraphy with creativity and finesse?

Or are you imagining something between Olicana Rough and Olicana Smooth?
