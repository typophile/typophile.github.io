The Pursuit of Invisibility

January 15, 2011 • quadibloc

This [[http://blog.modernmechanix.com/mags/ScienceAndMechanics/2-1936/murderous_automobile.jpg|image]] of a page from an old magazine on a [[http://blog.modernmechanix.com/|site]] devoted to the amusing contents of the popular science and mechanics magazines like Popular Science, Popular Mechanics, Science and Mechanics, and Mechanix Illustrated of long ago… impressed me as an example of just what I felt was truly invisible type.

Looking through Linotype’s One-Line Specimen book, I was able to determine that the little area on the lower right where we are told ‘How to subscribe for “Everyday Science and Mechanics."’… was set by means of two-line matrices which mixed Linotype’s modern face “No. 10” with Gothic No. 4.

The body of the editorial, which is what I am concerned with, is in Linotype’s version of Century Expanded - which seems every so slightly wider than Century Expanded as it came from ATF. Which explains why I was reluctant to think of Century Expanded as the perfect invisible typeface, and assumed that some admixture of Caslon, or the Scotch Roman from the Farmer foundry pictured in the DeVinne specimen, or Linotype’s Number 22 would be necessary. (Century Schoolbook, of course, is wider than Century Expanded, but it’s too bold and too much like a Clarendon to not be distracting.)

There is no such thing as an ‘invisible’ typeface, nor is invisibility something that typographers have sought after. Most famously, Beatrice Warde likened the ideal of typography to a crystal goblet, something that is transparent to be sure, but hardly invisible.
