2008-02-18 02:19:06 • HaleyFiege
It's not quite the same thing, but "Art Director" has a similar perception problem. Here is what Saul Bass had to say about it: "For years, my mother wondered what I do; that is, what i really do. She new I did art work--and actually she was very happy about it despite some vagueness that surrounded the matter. Her only concern was that I should make a living, and I seemed to be doing all right. But finally her curiosity overcame her timidity, and, I think with some prompting from the neighbors, she finally asked me what it was that I really did. "I pulled out a proof of an ad that I had designed and I showed it to her and said, ‘Mom, that's what I do.' She looked at this and said, ‘Oh, I see . . . ' and she pointed to a photograph in the ad and said, ‘That's what you do?' I said, ‘Oh, no, you see, there are photographers--and they are specialists--they know all about cameras and things of that kind, and they make the photographs.' “‘Oh,' she said, disappointed, ‘Then this is what you do?' I said, ‘No, that's typography. You see there are special organizations who do nothing but set type--you know, Garamond, Caslon, etc. . . . these people do that.' Again she was a little disappointed. She pointed to the lettering and we went through this again. Finally she looked at me somewhat concerned, and said, ‘Well, now, what do you do?' I said, ‘Well, you see, I conceived the whole thing, and then I get all these people together and get them to carry out the process.' She looked at me very coyly, and said, "Oh--you devil!'” (From "The Design of Advertising" by Roy Paul Nelson)

missgiggles -- the beauty of the design industry is it's possible to not only be creative in terms of the work you produce, but also create your own job on your own terms (if you're willing to hustle). People who receive graphic design training can go on to be any myriad of things, not just a graphic designer at a graphic design firm who does graphic design projects like brochures, logos, and websites. There are all sorts of niches and different worlds to live in within the umbrella of the design industry, and a lot live on the peripheral edges. I think of myself as a graphic designer, but I've had the opportunity to work on projects that overlapped with photography, fashion, industrial design, interior design, etc., and I'd say that's fairly typical for designers.
