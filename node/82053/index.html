Diluting the brand

May 10, 2011 • Nick Shinn

I’ve never liked the kind of comparative ad that shows the wrong product choice, without having the guts to go head to head with a competitor. It seems counterproductive to devote most of an advertisement to a screw-up, and then attach one’s brand to it.

Usually Stupid Dad is mocked, but here the object of scorn is rocking grandmas. Sexist and ageist.

Typographically, the error is compounded with what appears to be a selection from 1001 Free Fonts.

The only fonts that should appear in an advertisement are the corporate fonts, anything else corrupts the identity.

The only fonts that should appear in an advertisement are the corporate fonts, anything else corrupts the identity.

That’s a very old-fashioned approach to brand standards. Something I have been wondering lately is if using the same identity forever just leads people to take it for granted and not notice it. For example, I can’t even remember the last time I saw any promotions for American Airlines because the brand standards have been the same for almost fifty years. Everything AA does just blends into my environment. I don’t see the Coke logo much, either. Logos and type standards may have worked well in the twentieth century but I think we are moving into a world where brand standards are less important than meaningful customer interaction.

That said, a halfassed grannies-with-guitars gimmick is not even close to meaningful customer interaction!
