TrueType longevity?

February 22, 2006 • Palatine

Since [[TrueType]] is nearly the same as [[OpenType]], will TrueType (in the future) be supported by major software makers alongside OpenType? I do know that PostScript will be phased out eventually (unsupported by Avalon, for example.)

I’ve got quite a few quality TrueType fonts and I’m a bit worried.

OpenType is simply an extension of the TrueType format, adding some purely optional tables to the basic sfnt structure. To a system or an application that doesn’t understand the OpenType Layout tables, a TT flavour OT font is largely indistinguishable from any other TrueType font. The same rasterisers are used for TT and TT-OT fonts, so I don’t think you have to worry about TT longevity.

Also note that your comment about PostScript fonts is not accurate: it is specifically Type 1 fonts that are likely to be phased out and which are not supported in Avalon. PostScript CFF OT fonts are supported in Avalon.

2006-02-24 05:40:55 • canderson

Strange that MS is being so sympathetic to foundries’ existential grief all of a sudden.

I think it is myopic to try to understand the development of OpenType and shifts in font format support in terms of the economics or politics of the typical North American and European font foundry and its graphic design market. While Adobe obviously has some interest in this area, Microsoft has little or none.

Micrsoft invented OpenType because they needed to extend their existing TrueType format to support complex script rendering for their Middle Eastern and Asian markets. All the fancy typographic features of OpenType Layout are riding on the back of the complex script handling. OpenType is fundamentally an internationalisation technology. The impetuses for its creation are Microsoft’s international market, the character/glyph model and associated rendering demands of the Unicode Standard, and the massive increase in dynamic electronic text serving of the Internet (contrasted with the largely static print media of the desktop publishing revolution that gave rise today’s digital font business.)

Adobe’s reasons for signing on to OpenType are largely similar to Microsoft’s, although with a different set of priorities. The fancy typographic support that Microsoft saw as an added bonus of their complex script support is obviously of more immediate interest to Adobe. But the importance of electronic text serving to Adobe’s current strategy is obvious: PDF has become a viable web-publishing format, and this is only possible if the underlying text behind a typographically sophisticated document layout remains cleanly encoded, searchable, copyable, pasteable, etc. In short, when Microsoft were developing OpenType, Adobe were already well aware that the whole 8-bit ‘expert set’ encoding architecture that had served the static media of desktop publishing was inadequate for the Internet. On the internationalisation side, Adobe have long been involved in the Far East market, especially Japan. More recently, though, they have begun to show their hand in other areas: Acrobat 7.05 includes support for Arabic, Hebrew and Thai, taking advantage of the complex script support of OpenType.

Regarding Microsoft’s decision not to support Type 1 fonts in Avalon (and bear in mind that Avalon will co-exist with the old GDI layout engine, which continues to support Type 1 fonts, and it is far from clear how many applications will actually make the switch from GDI to Avalon), I think the reasons are pretty mundane. Although they are both PostScript formats, Type 1 and CFF format fonts require separate rasterisers. There appears to have long been resistance from within the Windows core engineering group to supporting multiple rasterisers. Dropping one from Avalon, for what is clearly an obsolete font technology from the internationalisation perspective that is of most concern to Microsoft, is a no brainer. They save resources, simplify their layout and rendering, and at the same time encourage greater adoption of the sfnt font file format.

Many foundries that do not offer upgrade-pricing on individual fonts do offer such a deal for their whole-library collections.

Free upgrades on OT conversion doesn’t make any business sense for a type designer or producer. The process of making good OpenType fonts is very labor intensive, much more difficult than generating old Type 1 fonts. If OT conversion fonts were just given away to customers, this work would go uncompensated.

Also, in most cases, OT fonts have larger character sets than the fonts they replace. If I had just purchased Times Roman from foundry X, with a standard 256 character set, an free upgrade really wouldn’t be all that fair for foundry x if they decide to include CE characters, small caps, and old style figures etc. in the new OT version. I’d be getting all of these glyphs for free if I’d never licensed the old expert versions! And most foundries do release OT fonts with bigger character sets, because that is what customers want. Especially in Europe where heavy degrees of language support is a must. Many Americans just don’t see this. In the US, we can print products with instructions in just one (or maybe two) languages. In Europe, everything is printed in a gazillion languages. Even the money has two separate writing systems (Latin and Greek).

As a separate issue, I do think that it would be interesting for a foundry to offer a “lifetime upgrading possibility” price to fonts. 2006-02-24 16:51:37 • canderson

Nick: The OpenType format certainly has enough space for typographic subtlety — was this incidental? I’d be inclined to think not, as typographic complexity (the old “expert” sets) seems inherent in the character/glyph model.

Insofar as any unencoded glyph variant of a character is an inherent presumption of the character/glyph model, yes. My take on this is that Microsoft were certainly aware of the typographic potential of OpenType when they were designing it, so it wasn’t incidental in the sense of a byproduct, but at the same time fancy typography wasn’t a driving consideration for Microsoft. The character/glyph distinction had been around for more than a decade, during which time it is notable that MS never once shipped anything like an expert set encoding or used PUA codepoints to access typographic glyph variants. Clean text encoding had always been more important to Microsoft than it was in the desktop publishing world, where any mechanism to get the desired glyphs next to each other was considered acceptable. Fine typography essentially requires the Latin script to behave as a complex script. This leads me to conclude that fine typography was not a driving consideration for Microsoft in the development of OpenType, because if it had been we could have expected OpenType, or something like it, much earlier. It is only in the context of actual complex scripts, i.e. scripts that require higher level protocols in order to display text in a readable way, that the impetus to develop the new font format and rendering engines emerges.

2006-02-25 22:54:12 • Mark Simonson
The type aspect of GX is still supported by Apple as AAT (Apple Advanced Typography), and Apple make free tools available for defining and adding AAT tables to fonts. The big drawbacks to AAT are a) the layout tables are difficult to develop compared to OT, and b) support is limited to a very small number of applications on a single platform with a small market share. I’m not sure which of these problems is the chicken and which is the egg, but the result is that few people are inclined to invest much in developing AAT fonts. The most substantial and impressive AAT development has been done for Arabic, for which there is currently no other option on the Mac OS outside of Middle East versions of Adobe apps.
