2010-12-15 02:43:09 • blank

Urm, I wouldn’t say it makes perfect sense. Basically there is a popular belief among children’s book publishers that ligatures are hard for kids to read. So they ask book designers to turn them off. But there is no evidence for it that anybody has ever presented, other than anecdotal. (And we’ve already seen that the anecdotal “evidence” for infant forms for reading is contradicted by actual research.)

I wrote a section on this ligatures-in-children’s-books issue in my (co-authored) article on typography in children’s books in the current issue of the American Library Association journal “Children & Libraries.” Our sample was the children’s books which received Caldecott honors in the past 20 years: 85 books once you eliminate wordless and hand-lettered books. Here’s what we wrote on ligatures (albeit with some loss of formatting):

In children’s books, especially those for young readers, ligatures may be a controversial option. While most typographers would prefer to use ligatures (as opposed to having colliding letters or excess spacing), it seems many of those involved in publishing for children are concerned that merging the letterforms is confusing to new readers, and ask that ligatures be turned off in the page layout software. Of our Caldecott sample, eighteen books use ligatures, forty-six do not, and the remainder are either unclear or don’t have enough text for the question to come up. In some cases one can see a typeface in one book with ligatures, and the same typeface in another book without: Tops & Bottoms uses Cochin with ligatures, while Duke Ellington has it without.35

Of the forty-six books that are obvious about their lack of ligatures, most of them are not beginner books. In eight of the forty-six, the lack of ligatures creates a serious problem with the letter spacing, including Bill Peet: An Autobiography, which is one of the most text-heavy books in our entire sample, and far from being a beginner book.36

Yet several of those eight books with ligature problems feature otherwise exceptional typesetting or typographic design. These include: Tibet Through the Red Box; Click, Clack, Moo; Duke Ellington; Olivia; and The Dinosaurs of Waterhouse Hawkins.37 Olivia is an especially interesting case, because the book designer was clearly aware of the problem created by the lack of ligatures. Instead of allowing the letters to collide as they do in most of the other “problem books,” the designer adjusted the spacing between the “f” and the “i” and the “f” and the “l,” to avoid the collisions created by turning off ligatures (increasing or decreasing space between specific latters is called “kerning”). How I Learned Geography is a similar case in that with ligatures turned off, extra kerning was required to separate the “f” from the “i” and “l.”38 Both cases leave an odd-looking gap between the letters with the fi and fl combination, but somehow it isn’t as obvious in How I Learned Geography using the Galliard typeface as it is in Olivia using Centaur.39 Perhaps this is because of the smaller type size in How I Learned Geography. A typographer might argue that if ligatures were considered unacceptable, a different typeface should have been used, such that the standard spacing would work well. Many common text faces have an “f” with less or no overhang to the right, and such typefaces do not fare as badly with default spacing and no ligatures.

2010-12-15 13:20:37 • kentlew

I have designed no-lig “f"s for all my news faces, because many newspaper pagination systems do not support f-ligs. Also, I don’t believe that f-ligs (and osf, while we’re at it) belong in headlines.

But I also include f-ligs in the fonts, for those who prefer them.

However, in this case, I think what you want is a font that has no ligated forms in the “Ligature” characters. In fact, that makes a lot of sense for justified text, the reason being that if the typographer applies a value other than 100% to character spacing (and the defaults in Quark and InD are not 100%), then in tight lines ligatures will appear, but not in loose lines. Such inconsistency is poor typography, IMO.
