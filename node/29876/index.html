Well, not all dictionaries. Also, many dictionaries use both sans and serif faces together, a common combination being a bold sans face for the entry headers and a serif face for the texts.

Typefaces for reference works have to be somewhat neutral and not draw too much attention to themselves. In addition, they have to be readable at the small sizes that they are used in because of size constraints. There are both serif and sans serif faces that are suitable for such purposes, and well-designed dictionaries will use such faces.

Don’t get too stuck on the serif vs sans serif distinction. There are both serif and sans faces that make for good dictionary faces, and bad dictionary faces.

Dictionaries require a variety of typogrphic distinctions to act as a systematic way of presenting definitions. Traditionally, serif faces have had a broad variey inclusing italics, smallcaps, fraction height numbers, and oldstyle figures–rich formatting possibilities. Today, there are many more sans that have all the required stuff too.
