Oliver Sacks New Yorker article

July 30, 2010 • eliason

"A Man of Letters," 28 June 2010 issue Thought this excerpt would be of interest to Typophiles. He's talking about how the curious problem that we have a very specialized area of our brain for reading, yet writing is such a recent phenomenon that it could not be accounted for by evolution through natural selection. This is solved by concluding that these neurons were useful for general visual recognition (deciphering the environment), and then were recruited for reading. I wasn't entirely taken with Changizi's analysis of writing systems because it seemed to me that the great variety of forms in 100+ writing systems and the greater variety of forms in nature would pretty much guarantee a large degree of topological overlap. But if systems such as shorthand, designed for speed of writing rather than speed of decipherment, do not have the same kind of topological overlap, then the explanation becomes more compelling. But to come back to Craig's introductory paragraph -- I have not read the whole Sacks article yet --, if the reading area of the brain is specialised, how did it become specialised? If this area involves neurons that were, prior to the development of writing, used for more general topological recognition, why aren't they still so used, and why isn't reading accounted for by general topological recognition? There is another explanation for the existence of these neurons, which is that they were free riders, i.e. that they did not have a specific purpose prior to the development of writing, that they are not explainable by adaptationism, but are simply part of the genetic baggage carried alongside selected for traits. Of course, the existence of free riders, and our inability to distinguish with certainty traits that were selected for and those that are free riders, reduces all adaptationist explanations to more or less probable just-so stories.
