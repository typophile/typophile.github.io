Ecosystem Threat

January 26, 2005 • hrant

I think the balance between typeface crits and "composition" crits has lately become very lopsided in favor of the latter. I remember the trend starting shortly before TypeCon04, and since then there was a short burst of typeface crits, but now it's pretty meek. You could say we're being shown fewer fonts worth critiquing lately, but surely it's a mutual thing; when obviously good designs are virtually ignored by almost all Typophilers, people are clealy less likely to show good stuff - there's probably more loss than gain since you're getting some measly crits in return for exposing your baby prematurely. – Even though it's only positive that Typophile can serve as a forum for composition crits, I think it's fair to say that there are much more people who make compositions than type, and much more people who seem to feel confident engaging in composition critique than type critique. Furthermor, we all have variously limited time to devote to Typophile in the first place, and this means if somebod spends 2 hours critiquing compositions instead of 1 hour, that's 1 hour less to devote to type crits. All this means we have to make en effort to preserve our invaluable ecosystem, where type crits are vital. We can't just build a city with no regard to the environment, otherwise our community won't last in any form. – To me Typophile owes a large part of its prominence to being a singular hub of quality type crits. Now it seems there's a danger that the majority voice of composition will drown out the vital niche minority. So I would simply urge people to balance their time here so that the blood that feeds the whole of typography doesn't become depleted. hhp
from personal experience, it's disheartening to post something for critique and get little or no response. i realize that what i'm doing isn't always everybody's cup of tea, but even just one comment or two is good enough feedback to keep one motivated. sometimes i feel bad and try my best to leave a crit, even though i'm sure there are so many more qualified individuals on the boards that could do a better job than i. anyhow, even you miss one sometimes, hrant. 2005-01-28 15:02:56 • dezcom
Certainly there are huge advantages to doing a crit in person, not least the simple issue of "interface": it's much more efficient to point at something and speak, rather than draw corrections on the computer and type stuff up. But a Typophile crit has its own advantages. Besides the obvious removal of the geographic barrier, there's the fact that it's public; this makes the education "massively parallel" - I know I've learned a lot from reading other people's crits. Also, sometimes a public crit spawns a fruitful discussion of how best to do something, which can't happen with only two people (with a one-way relationship to boot). When Randy puts up (or I should say used to put up) those nice illustrations, everybody benefits. BTW, I won't deny that there are also selfish reasons to do crits, at least in my case: I learn a lot myself just from the process and the formalization; and yes, I do get exposure, hence sales and commissions - but I don't expect to be looked down upon for that by people who do type design for a living! :-) As for the "ego" angle, I think it might be true sometimes, but it's really not very relevant if the results are useful to others - and since a steady stream of people tell me so I have little reason to doubt it. hhp
