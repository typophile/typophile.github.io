2003-12-20 19:16:11 • Stephen Coles
Keywording is all about power. Who controls the marketing process and gets to decide what the product should be like. Getty uses keyword demand to develop product; the process is professionized in the hands of marketers, not the content creator or the end user. (And there is no independent "keywords standards" body representing all stakeholders.) I don't see type design getting that corrupted by keywording, but the writing is on the wall. The question is – whose language, whose keywords are you gonna trust, whose are you gonna use? Whose decision on what is "esoteric and convoluted"? I provided a list of keywords for a font recently: Awesome, Badass, Ballin', Chill, Chronic, Dope, etc… which one of my distributors replaced, in my better interests of course. Without a "no f""k with the keywords" clause in my contract, they can do that. Pity, I'm not sure the typeface in question was the right one to define the new niche, but still…

2003-12-21 17:20:59 • marcox
> i{What ARE today's hot font keywords?} Nick, I don't believe it has to do with keywords being 'hot' or 'dope' or 'what's happening now'. What's missing from much of the keyword data supplied to us by designers is sometimes even the most basic typographic classifications and historical terms (eg: transitional, calligraphic, incised, condensed, etc.) If we could consistently receive these terms in conjunction with a few mood or aesthetic-based terms (eg: roughened, western, distressed, lively, casual, smooth, rounded, harsh, etc.) - it would make our job much easier. I certainly appreciate appropriately whimsical keywords, but not at the expense of more common descriptive terms.
