Any of several Scotch Romans, including New Caledonia as suggested by Kevin. In the days of metal typesetting, the character sets of a font were limited. To set special characters such as mathematical symbols the printer had to resort to an additional font of symbols, sometimes called, particularly in the US, a “Pi” font because it usually included the letter π. I don’t know if this particular page was hand set or in a font that could be used in a Linotype or similar machine. By 1957 photo-composition was replacing metal. So this is also a possibility for your page. For a digital substitute there are specialized math fonts or you could use a Scotch Roman with a good math component. You might look at Nick Shin’s Scotch Modern https://www.myfonts.com/fonts/shinn/scotch-modern/ For more info on typesetting you might start with this wiki http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Typesetting Don

I looked at the maths in the rest of the paper and the typesetting does not meet the mathematical standards of that period. It would also be hard to reproduce formulas that are that badly formatted with currently available tools.

For a historical overview, there is a nice little publication entitled “Mathematical Typesetting” available from Tiro typeworks at http://www.tiro.com/projects.html

There is also Daniel Rhatigan, The Monotype 4-Line System for Setting Mathematics, Essay submitted in partial fulfilment of the requirements for the MA in Typeface Design, University of Reading, 2007 http://ultrasparky.org/school/pdf/Rhatigan_Monotype_4-line_math.pdf,
