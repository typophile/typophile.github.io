2007-04-28 06:20:25 • Lex Kominek
UPDATE: Just spoke with my wife, who represents this company in the US. Apparently the le Crin company (which makes textiles from horse hair tail) dates back to the end of Napoleon's time (1814). My wife was in the archives in France not long ago and found a document from well before the turn of the century. She just told me it had nearly the same logo on it. In fact most I speak with in France believe this is the original logo. Obviously I can stop looking for the font, but what's interesting to me is that the logo is so very similar to Tiffany, yet the logo with the tilted e (almost identical in every way save the lip serif) pre-dates Tiffany by about 150 years. The le Crin company was founded in 1814. Tiffany was designed by Edward Benguiat in 1974. If I am not mistaken the top of the influence design tree (Ronaldson) dates from around the same time as the founding of le Crin. Yet, Ronaldson or Claxton do not have a tilted e. It makes me wonder about the history of tilting the e? Is it part of a movement or maybe a signature foundry style? Anyway, thank you to all who contributed. Tim

The logo may have evolved over the years, changing subtly while still retaining the feel of the original. The Napoleonic version of the logo would probably look the same to most casual observers, but I would bet it uses a different typeface, and most likely a slightly different version of the horse. Take a look at the Coca-Cola logo or the Ford motors logo. These have stayed essentially the same since they were designed way back when, but if you compare a version from today with the original, you'll find many differences. - Lex
