[…] what you guys think are some of the best foundries. Maybe it's more useful to start by looking which "foundry" is offering the best conceived digital typefaces. If you are looking fos strictly typographical styles, I would start by looking at which types you like more, and then see which renditions and stylizations are among the best available as digital type. For example, with Garamond types, or Jenson and Caslon, you may have a lot to evaluate, and then see what works best as a digital version, which is more carefully rendered, which is more solid, or more complete in terms of glyph coverage, etc.

2008-11-07 16:34:02 • Si_Daniels
Well, with that term I tried to compensate my lack of immediate expression in English language… I used it to describe the state of typography (simply seen as the mechanization of the process of setting lettering, not as "fine typography" as commonly used) as it was hit by the tornado of the so-called "digital revolution". Many designers (mostly graphic designers) generated a true "avant-garde" movement on international scale, as type design stopped to be "a brave and arcane business" in the hands of a few, a notion expressed by Matthew Carter in an interview around 1993. Hmm… Try to locate the article "The Digital Wave" by Robin Kinross, from an old issue of Eye Magazine, to look at Rick Poynor's articles in the first issues of the same, and at his book "Typography Now" (issue 1). Locate some Cranbrook or CalArts catalogue, the narratives behind the works of Jon Barnbrook, Miles Newlyn, Jeffery Keedy, Frank Heine, Sue LaPorte, P. Scott Makela, Barry Deck and others in earlier issues of Emigre, and then you may have an idea of all the earthquake these caused in my mind of self-taught designer in process… :=)
2008-11-07 18:24:24 • piccic
My comment about Alias was because I see a lot of David James & Gareth Hague's intutions (but others as well) in letterforms picked up by the designer(s?) of Textaxis. For example, the typeface Borneo has a lot of forms influenced by Alias' MetSys (which, in turn, was based on James' lettering for the early 1990s album covers of Steve Hillage's band System 7). Inercia and Suite owe a lot to FF Dax (1995-1997) and the previous Barmeno (1983) by Hans Reichel; Oneline is heavily influenced by Alias' Progress (2002); Latina Italic owes an lot (in terms of stylization) to John Downer's Triplex (1985), and so on…
