2009-05-12 16:41:46 • Florian Hardwig

Eben, the principle difficulty of proportional tracking is captured in the question ‘Proportional to what?’ Sidebearing measurements capture a distance between the origin point and advance width, respectively, and the left and right extremes of the glyph outline. That tells you absolutely nothing about appropriate optical spacing, which is a complex relationship between three factors: size and shape of inter-letter white spaces, glyph shape and glyph density. The complexity of this relationship – and how this relationship changes at different scales – is what stands in the way of the holy grail of automated spacing.

If we could proportional track text in an optically appropriate way, we would also be able to proportionally space text automatically; indeed, we could erase the mechanical distinction between sidebearing spacing and kerning.

The other thing to note about tracking, by the way, is that all the adjustment is added to or subtracted from the advance width, i.e. to one side only of the glyph (although there may be a mirrored adjustment in RTL text in e.g. InDesign ME).

2009-05-13 21:22:36 • Goran Soderstrom

Kindersley had a quite different approach from Adobe’s optical metrics program. The Adobe program is based on the Hz program developed by Hermann Zapf and URW, if I remember rightly. And it is based on the idea of ‘equal area’ of whites between letters and avoiding closely proximate blacks, if I’ve got it right. Kindersley had a quite different approach, based on the distribution of weight of the black in a letter. As several people have pointed out, most recently Kent Lew, Adobe’s Optical metrics program doesn’t work very well.

An attempt was made to computerize Kindersley’s approach, but it never really got off the ground, except for one brief (and impressive) test, but with a lot of ad hoc stuff being done for the test.

Currently what seems to be better than Adobe is KernMaster from Dutch Type Library, and iKern, a program that its author, Igino Marini, is using as the basis of his kerning service to type designers. These are for the design stage, not for doing on the fly in an application.

I do know that John’s question of relative to what is still very relevant simply because fonts don’t currently offer anything for an app to grab and manage on a percentage basis.

That’s only the tip of the problem, though, because optical spacing involves things other than inter-letter space. So even if you are able to quantify that space in a way that is meaningful and manipulable, you still have to contend with how that space is optically affected by the shape of the adjacent blacks and by letter densities. It isn’t just a problem of quantifying a variable, but of too many different variables affecting each other in different ways and different amounts in different contexts.
