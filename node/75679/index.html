2010-10-27 06:37:13 • blank

Probably because of the recommendation (from Microsoft, as far as I remember), that ascender + descender shall be less or equal to the UPM size on the one side and the want of the designer to make them as great as possible within the boundaries of the recommendation on the other side. But as far as I know, the specifications don’t say, that it is a duty not to exceed the boundaries of the UPM. It is more a rumor, which is particulary caused by the (wrong) FontLab error message.

Edited – from the Recommendations for OpenType fonts:

sTypoAscender, sTypoDescender and sTypoLineGap

sTypoAscender is used to determine the optimum offset from the top of a text frame to the first baseline. sTypoDescender is used to determine the optimum offset from the last baseline to the bottom of the text frame. The value of (sTypoAscender - sTypoDescender) is recommended to equal one em.

While UPM means units per em.

I don’t think, that a sum of descender and ascender, that is greater than 1000 units, violates the specification. Especially because descender and ascender are not necessarily identically with the boundaries of the font boundaries box. But if you absolutely don’t follow the recommendation, it is harder to combine different typefaces. But the advantage of greater sizes is, that you can control the outlines with more precision.

2010-10-27 08:08:59 • Arno Enslin

What I meant is that serif fonts tend to be drawn smaller within the em than sans fonts—thus the shorter cap height.

Ascenders tend to be taller than the caps in Serif fonts more often than in Sans. Theoretically, because of this, the average of all Serif cap hts is smaller than the average of all Sans cap hts.

It is from technical concern, (you don’t want the l.c. ascenders to be clipped off for going above the em), that the caps of any face are smaller then those of another face with cap ht ascenders.
