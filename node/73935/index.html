2010-09-10 17:37:58 • Nick Shinn

Nick: When Fred Goudy taught lettering at the Holme School in Chicago (to Oz Cooper, among others), did he confine students to a single style, because they could pick up the rest later?

There’s a big difference between teaching lettering and teaching typeface design. If I wanted to study lettering, I wouldn’t sign up for a masters in type design. Lettering is about styles and techniques for achieving them. Type design is about mastering a set of skills that can be applied to the production of fonts, largely independent of the style of lettering embodied in those fonts.

I don’t think there is a Reading style in the sense that there is a kind of letter that the teachers encourage students to produce. This is demonstrated by the fact that over the years numerous students have produced designs that clearly do not reflect such a style, and often it is these students who have received distinctions for their work. Rather, I think there are students who have more and less original ideas about letters, and the latter seem to gravitate to some common tropes.

Hmm… I have the impression it doesn’t make sense to evaluate these typefaces side-by-side. It seems to me people go to study to Reading or similar school to learn important things about letters, their forms, history and use, and the people which attend are as much diverse as you can get. In general, it seems to me there are people like graphic designers whose desire is to learn more about the alphabet which aren’t interested in the beginning to strive for originality as a priority for their type design (maybe they will not even design other typefaces). Inversely there are people which are extremely interested in learning about letters, which may go on to design typefaces and keep on studying the history of the written word, but whose typeface may be simply the means to learn better. I envy them, since I never had the patience to learn by trial and error, and my personal works have been more experiments than other… :-)

You can’t suppress one aspect in favor of the other. While I deeply value landmark experiments of the 1990s (i.e. Barry Deck’s, to have just a name), you realize afterwards that you can really evaluate with an understanding, or – better said – a personal knowledge of what you handle. 2010-09-11 15:20:37 • dezcom

Nick, you seem to acknowledge how I explained my use of the term ‘production’, i.e. making fonts, including design and manufacture, and then go on to ignore that use.

I don’t think design is somehow left ‘to be picked up later’ at Reading, but the focus seems to be on skill development and design is studied and practised in the context of making fonts, i.e. of designing typefaces as working products not on drawing lovely letters. Insofar as the graduates hope to have jobs in the type business, I think this is a sensible approach, because the skills they are learning are the skills they will need.

Does the Reading programme provide in itself all the letter design training that a type designer will need to produce not only workable fonts but original, well crafted typefaces? No, I don’t think it does, and the student work seems to me to reflect pretty clearly the prior experience and skills of the individual students. What it does do is to teach design skills in the context of making typefaces, which is something different than teaching lettering which, if you remember, is the original point I made in response to your comment about Goudy and Cooper.
