2009-01-28 00:29:30 • paul d hunt
To make something subtly creepy, what I think of is to take something very normal and familiar, and put it slightly off. A haphazard mixture of letters from two very similar fonts, like Helvetica and Akzidenz Grotesk, for example, tracked widely but slightly unevenly. Just enough that people like us would notice. The average reader would be able to feel that there is something wrong with what they’re seeing, but wouldn’t be able to identify exactly what.

2009-01-28 21:44:48 • Nick Shinn

Jason, cool idea. That could connect to Haneke’s somewhat subversive creepiness in using the (= his) medium. Like the way he mixed film & video aesthetic in Caché: I watched that with a friend who doesn’t know much about movies, and she commented later that it took her a while to realize it was that slight difference in image quality/feel that did a lot to subtly unsettle her. Dunno if Helvetica/AG isn’t too subtle though for “normal people” to notice?

Alternately, his work seems to be a lot about voyeurism and the role of the audience, who is never passive but always, if subliminally, part of the story. I wonder if/how this notion of “drawing in” viewers/readers and making them participate could be translated into a typographic treatment that still works.
