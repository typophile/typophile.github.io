Indeed it does, Chesh.

David, make sure you've actually "cut out" the image: is Photoshop's checkered background visible in the "cut out" areas? If it is, save the file as a Photoshop (.psd) file. When you place the .psd file in InDesign, your feathered edges will be visible, and you'll have the soft transition between photo and background that you're looking for.

2004-07-02 14:11:57 • aquatoad
You put a flat background in your Photoshop file, giving it the exact same CMYK value as the flat background in Quark.

That way, the two instances of the same composite color will merge seamlessly in final output – although a difference may be apparent on screen and in some low-res proofs.

(This is a primitive technique from the early days of desktop, before Photoshop had layers, even, when minimizing file size was crucial.)
