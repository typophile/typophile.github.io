Spacing dcaron and lcaron

February 14, 2012 • blank

I always have to think about how to space the caron side of dcaron/lcaron.

Start with lowercase: do I use a negative sidebearing? In environments with no kerning, a negative sidebearing will at least work fine next to lowercase vowels and spaces, which it is how dcaron will appear most often. The disadvantage is that it will crash into k and some punctuation.

On to uppercase: only one word, not counting proper nouns, begins with Ď or Ľ—ďáblův. So all caps settings seem like a bigger concern, and in such settings it could crash into E, I, U, and possibly O depending on the design of the caron and the O. So in this case should I go with a positive sidebearing?

Don’t use a negative sidebearing! Treat lowercase dcaron/lcaron just like the f: nnďánnfánn.

Comparing this question to an f is erroneous for a variety of reasons. The f has a wide range of form depending on the languages and technologies you expect to design to. Newspapers with no kerning need one solution- a narrow f. Users with access to kerning, ligatures and contextual shapes can use another solutions. If the font is just for a language with no clashes between f and diacritic mark then even more extravagant shapes are possible. In the case of the dcaron and lcaron the range of desirable variation is much much smaller. The f is very big and isn’t going to be misread as easily as a small mark could be if an overlap occurs.

I suggest that it is nearly always best to err on the side of using just space to make your worst case spacing scenario . I say this because it is practical - you can kern your way out out. But also because my reading of the science on the topic suggest that clashes are worse for reading that gaps are. People tolerate gaps really well. The shape of the Caron is relevant too. It is tall and thin. It far more susceptible to being misread/unidentified/ignored when placed next to an overlapping feature than other kinds of marks would be.

Assuming no kerning:

Statistically, the vast majority of letter combinations involving dcaron, lcaron and tcaron are better served by a negative right sidebearing, while allowing occasional failure.

Also bear in mind words that end with dcaron, lcaron and tcaron, being followed by a space or (baseline) punctuation mark. Again, negative right sidebearing works better.
