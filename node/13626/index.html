U&lc the Book!

July 8, 2005 • Henry Hauger

On a designer friend’s coffee table the other day, I came across a big gorgeous book that was nothing but a collection of the best of [[Uandlc|U&lc]] magazine! (For anyone too young to remember, U&lc, Upper & lower case, was this brilliant oversized typography and design magazine that was around from the 70’s until 1999.) In any case, it was a “must read” for graphic designers and I’m baffled that I hadn’t heard of this book before. You can order it straight from the publisher http://www.markbattypublisher.com who has lots of other interesting graphic-design books. (Check out the book about [[graffiti]] in the Paris Underground, it’s fascinating.)
For those of you whom are interested in the lovely book, including myself, we'll have it -- last time I checked -- in the vendor area at TypeCon2005 in New York.

2005-07-09 18:34:02 • tamye
I haven't seen the newly released book (edited by [[John D Berry|John D. Berry]]), but I'm sure there is a lot of background details and stories behind the graphic phenomenon of U&lc. Most of the early editions were representative of the [[New York School]] style of design, made popular by [[Herb Lubalin|Lubalin]], [[Seymore Chwast|Chwast]], [[Milton Glaser|Glaser]] ([[Pushpin Studios]]) to name only a few of the giants involved. Also, it became a gallery of the superb work of [[Tom Carnase]], [[Tony Di Spigna]], [[Ed Benguiat]], again, only naming a few. But the most brilliant aspect of the publication is that it became a "mother" of a marketing tool for the infant [[ITC|International Typeface Corporation]]. This came about through the passionate leadership of Aaron Burns. ITC used U&lc to sell the idea of "Licensing" typeface designs. In other words, it made it a moral issue not to use ITC typefaces unless they had the official "seal of approval". A list of the ITC licensed foundries was published towards the back of each issue. As the popularity of ITC typefaces grew, this added a little pressure for more and more foundries and manufacturers of typesetting equipment to sign on with ITC. What made this so unique, is that ITC through U&lc had found a way of popularizing their designs even BEFORE they were released and available for typesetting. You can imagine how many young designers who read U&lc looked for the very first opportunity to emmulate their heros by specifying ITC typefaces. -------------------------------------------- Yes, I'm old, but I'm back in style!
