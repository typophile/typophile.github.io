2007-08-14 05:37:37 • Stephen Coles

These nameplates were often cast, which obviously requires a mold. The mold would probably have been built by gluing a mockup using three dimensional letters cut on a pantograph machines (which produces beveled edges, necessary for use in molds), in either plexiglas or brass, depending on when it was produced. The patterns used as masters in pantograph cutting were often handcut (with scrollsaw/jigsaw) from wood, brass or plexiglas, based on printed samples.

So you may not find an exact match for this, because printing used to make the masters could have been rough woodcuts, or an old block printing font that was never digitized, and because of the oddities that would be introduced by the skill )or lack of skill) of the person cutting the samples templates.

Long way of saying, Sacker’s Gothic may be as close as you can come.

This plate is actually debossed, or stamped. The back of the aluminum is flat while the letters are slightly (and I mean barely) raised. It almost seems to be a combination of a few Gothic fonts.
