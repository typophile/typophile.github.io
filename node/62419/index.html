2009-09-26 23:29:40 • afonseca1974

I always understood ligature to be a typesetting term. More that one glyph on a single type body.

I never heard it used in relation to handwriting.

I understand that an fi ligature solves the problem of the hood of the f crashing into the dot of the i (except in turkish). I also understand the fl and other f to ascender ligatures solve a spacing and color problem.

I understand the dutch ij ligature because of how those letters are treated as a unit in the language.

I see no reason why a u and an a have to be designed as a single glyph/ligature. There is no color or spacing problem to solve. What other use would there be? If you want to use it to design a logo than by all means go ahead. But I don’t think you are going to find it in any character set in an existing font

And just so you know where I’m coming from I always thought those ct and st ligatures to be an archaic affectation with no modern typographic relevance.

2009-09-27 18:44:12 • omega
A ligature (noun) is a characteristic of typesetting systems: a single glyph representing more than one character. To ligate (verb) is a characteristic of writing: the act of joining two or more letters together in some way. It follows that a ligature may or may not represent ligation, and ligation may be represented by means other than a ligature. From this flows much confusion. I’d say that Boris is looking for examples of ligated ua.

2009-09-28 18:08:01 • omega

When used in text typography those ct and st things draw attention to themselves and just look distracting. Not a good thing in text typography.

That rather depends on the text. I would agree that these forms are no longer appropriate to book length texts, simply because readers are not used to them; there was a time when they regularly appeared in book typography, so they can’t be considered inherently inappropriate: they’re just inappropriate now. But there remain settings in which they can be used sensitively and benefit typography precisely because they are out of the ordinary: they can signify out of the ordinary texts. And of course they can be used to deliberately evoke period typography.
