Capitalizing an all-caps typeface

February 6, 2011 • mpldesign

I’ve been wondering lately about whether its ok to use capitalization on a typeface that is all caps already (i.e. Copperplate Gothic) I was pretty sure that it was something not to do but I see it everywhere (including the business I’m designing for) and it’s making me think, is everyone setting it incorrectly?

I prefer to not but I would like to back my decision with facts. Most of these all cap typefaces have an upper and lowercase but the strokes look thicker making me think it’s incorrect.

Is this just a preference or is there a typographic rule?

  • Matt

If the typeface actually includes an uppercase and a “lowercase” (i. e., a smaller version of the uppercase, as in Copperplate Gothic), I don’t see a problem. It is similar to setting a phrase in small caps with the initials capitalized: I don’t like it so much either but it is fair game.

Subtle differences in stroke thickness are always expected between uppercase and lowercase, because the uppercase needs to deal with a bigger amount of empty space. Probably it has more to do with the specific case of [[http://new.myfonts.com/fonts/agfa/copperplate-gothic/|Copperplate Gothic:]] specially in lighter weights, the “lowercase” is certainly much more lighter than the uppercase and I would not use it because of that (a personal taste, not a typographic rule). But in bolder weights this difference is almost gone.

Now, if the “lowercase” was done using false small caps, that is a different story, since difference in stroke thickness would be huge (≈30%) and therefore not allowed. The funny thing with Copperplate Gothic is that the “lowercase” in lighter weights is so light when compared to the uppercase than it gives the impression of being false small caps…
