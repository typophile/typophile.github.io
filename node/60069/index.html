Stealing From Typophile

July 17, 2009 • evanbrog

I’ve been a member of typophile for a couple of years now. I love the quality of the feedback, the willingness to help, the sense of community. It’s a website that I trust.

However, after a recent forum post for a logo I was creating, I was approached by my boss after our legal dept. contacted him. They were not happy, it seems, with me posting a forum discussion on our logo. As usually happens though, we got a better logo because of it.

They were afraid someone would steal the wording that we were using, and hoped to trademark. Maybe they also worried someone would steal the logo itself.

I don’t know too much about words themselves and how they are protected, I suppose it’s possible for someone to decide to “trademark” a term before we get around to it. As far as the logo goes, it’s copyrighted, always, as our own creative piece–or so my understanding has always been. Therefore the logo would be protected (though, I must admit if our terminology was stolen, the logo is useless).

As I said, I trust those on this website. The problem was that entering the terminology on Google lead to the first result being this thread (that’s how legal found it). So…anyone out there could read the thread, and decide to try and steal my work.

Now I feel this is all highly unlikely. BUT does anyone have any tips on how to keep using this site as a resource (as so many ppl do for identity projects), without risking the work/the terminology?

What can I say to my employer? To our legal dept?

If there is a definite risk–Would ppl in our community be obliging if I asked them not to explicitly write out the words in question in their responses? For example, if I show the words next to the mark in a logo as a JPG, and ask that people refer to them as words, in general, not in specific (“blue lantern fisheries”)–wouldn’t that forum not show up on a google search? Would people mind?

But what if someone did write out “blue lantern fisheries”–telling me it should be centered, or rag right–and now google hits this thread, and I get in trouble.

So you see the problem, there must be a way to be protected without having to annoy people by telling them how to frame their responses. Or maybe their isn’t.

Could anyone offer me some advice, share experiences, etc?

If you don’t want any person on Earth to see what you do, don’t post it on the internet. Not under a fake name, not on private forums, not at all.

2009-07-17 17:13:28 • mili
Your profile identifies you as a freelance designer. When you refer to your boss, do you mean a client? If you’re a full-time employee of a company, you may have signed an intellectual property agreement when you were hired, making anything you create in the course of your 9-to-5 job effectively a work for hire. This may not be fair, but if you signed one of those, your best course of action is to tell your boss, Oops, my bad. It won’t happen again. If this is a logo you’re designing for a freelance client, your contract with them may have some non-disclosure clause in it, in which case the my-bad approach is advisable. In either case, point out that you acted in good faith with the goal of getting the best outcome for your client/employer, and with no intent whatsoever to harm or deceive them. It’s an unfortunate fact of life that businesses base their rules on worst-case scenarios, and therefore eliminate many valuable opportunities because they have a minuscule but real risk attached to them. You are in the awkward position of someone who might have to choose between being right and being employed.

2009-07-17 19:59:20 • Don McCahill

I’ve never heard of anyone “stealing” work from others on Typophile, though I can imagine plenty of people are inspired and perhaps borrow ideas seen here (This does not include any instances of ripped-off intellectual property in the greater type design community).

The majority of our users are honest and respectful people, but this is the world wide web. Anyone and everyone has access, so really, there is no guarantee that your work is safe from copy-cats. (I should also mention that legally, Typophile cannot be held responsible in this case).

Posting client work should always be done with great discretion and yes, Google loves Typophile, so chances are your posts here will get on the first page of any search result. There have been dozens of situations where a designer’s client has contacted us to remove content - usually from the logo/identity critique section. We have also been contacted by legal departments on a few occasions. I’ve seen it get ugly and I’ve also seen people lose their jobs.

It has already been said here, but I recommend removing any proper names associated with the business. Remove it from the threads text and remove it from the file name.

2009-07-17 20:03:33 • Zara Evens

In my book there is no way anyone can steal something that has been published and get away with it. If you described a wordmark or logo on a public forum that counts as publishing and is therefore protected by copyright. In case someone would steal the idea and trademark it (or any other way of registration), you can prove previous use and win litigation. Especially with a lot of Typophiles as witnesses : )

What your legal dept. is afraid of is the cost of litigation and the possibility of getting some dumbass judge who doesn’t get it, resulting in years of drawn out and expensive court cases. But that’s the American Way, I guess (highest ratio of lawyers to general pop., right?)…

A historic event comes to mind, where a Brazilian trademark was established for the wordmark ‘Coke’ by an outsider (because the C-C people kinda forgot to get there first) and that guy had to be bought of for a couple of mil.

2009-07-20 19:42:07 • evanbrog
Don: The circled R is for registered trademarks. TM, in turn, is for unregistered trademarks, and is pretty much just an expression of intent to defend something as a trademark. Companies often stick it on common words and phrases as a deterrent, even when they know that there are low odds of such an ownership being upheld in court if it came to that.
