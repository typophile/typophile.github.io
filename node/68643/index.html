2010-03-23 00:21:12 • typerror

Let’s clear up the terminology. There are established definitions for these terms that are quite precise.

Digraph has a more usual meaning of two characters that as a pair are used for something more than one would expect from a simple sequence of those two characters. For example, in the English word ‘sheath’ the pairs ‘sh’, ‘ea’, and ‘th’ are all digraphs. Digraphs, trigraphs, etc. are strategies used by orthographies to represent more sounds and distinctions than are possible using a limited inventory of single letters. Digraphs may sometimes be considered single characters, like the Dutch ij, but most are considered a sequence of two letters. However, visually, digraphs are usually indistinguishable from a normal sequence of the component glyphs.

When a pair of glyphs are actually modified in their forms as to be distinguished from a normal sequence (so that they touch, for example), then we have a ligature. This criterion has nothing to do with aesthetics. There are typographic ligatures like ﬁ, which is semantically just the sequence of characters f and i (just tweaked so that they don’t clash horribly), but there are also ligatures like æ which in several modern languages is considered an independent character (Note that fi is not a digraph since it behaves no differently from how you would expect the sequence f and i to behave). In modern representations of Classical Latin, æ may represent the digraph ae that would later merge with e. So w represents a ligature in origin that replaced an older digraph uu.

A diphthong is something else entirely. It deals with certain types of vowel sounds, namely when vowel quality changes over the course of a single unit of sound. It so happens that when diphthongs are represented in orthography, they are often written as digraphs (as in English ‘loud’ or ‘show’, but not ‘hi’), and very rarely there are ligatures that represent diphthongs (like æ in Classical Latin and Modern Icelandic, though not for Late Latin nor for Danish and Norwegian), but that’s about it.

So the important distinction is that digraph is concerned with characters, ligature with glyphs, and diphthong with sounds. These terms deal with completely separate levels. A ligature may be a digraph or it may not be one. A digraph is sometimes represented with a ligature, but most of the time it isn’t. Either of these may or may not correspond to a diphthong, sometimes even within the same language. A diphthong is a diphthong even if it has never been represented in writing.

The quote from the OED I cited makes it pretty clear that uu was first a diphthong (“The sound was at first represented by uu”), then eventually a ligature.

No, you misunderstand the definition of diphthong. The sound represented by uu/ƿ/w is the voiced labio-velar approximant [w] or something close to it. In lay terms, it is a semivowel corresponding to /u/, although in English it usually behaves as a consonant so it is more appropriate to call it an approximant rather than a semivowel.

A diphthong is a special kind of full vowel sound that changes in quality from beginning to end. The English word ‘eye’ starts with a vowel quality like ‘ah’ and glides into a vowel quality like the vowel in ‘bit’. By contrast, the vowel sound ‘ah’ maintains the same vowel quality from beginning to end in most dialects, so is called a monophthong. English is quite rich in diphthongs. Here are some words featuring diphthongs: lie, lay, lure, lair, low, leer, loud. For comparison, here are some words featuring monophthongs: lap, lot, let, lit, loot, lean, law.

The sound [w] doesn’t represent a full vowel so it is neither a monophthong or a diphthong, but as a semivowel it can be the component of a diphthong, as in ‘low’ and ‘loud’. You can see that sometimes this is represented in spelling, as in ‘low’, but not always, and a word like ‘law’ looks like it ought to have a diphthong that ends in [w] but is actually pronounced with a monophthong. This is because pronunciation is always changing and English orthography doesn’t represent today’s pronunciation very well (but Chaucer probably pronounced ‘law’ with a diphthong ending in [w], like that in ‘loud’).

Sorry for being picky, but I think it’s important to keep the distinctions clear.

Brian, I think there is only one useful definition of ligature as a noun in the context of typography, and that is a single glyph representing more than one character (whether the glyph in question is a chunk of metal, a cell in a phototype matrix, or a GID in an sfnt font). That is, a ligature is a particular technical solution to the display of multiple characters as a single typeform (the latter is a useful, technology-independent term to refer to the visual units of typographic output, recognising that when we see typeforms displayed on a screen or printed on a page we can’t tell how many glyphs are involved).

Joe: Ligatures are a stylistic attribute and not a fundamental feature of spelling, can we not agree? Nor are separated letters digraphs (QED). If you can’t decompose a character (like Œ or Æ) without causing an error, what you’ve got is a digraph, I contend.

If you can’t decompose a character such as Œ or Æ, then what you are looking at are single letters, not digraphs. This is language-specific: AE in ecclesiastical Latin is a digraph (I believe in classical Latin it was probably a diphthong), and is sometimes written in ligated form Æ and sometimes not; Æ in Norwegian is a single letter and must always be written in this ligated form. Ligation is neither always semantic nor always stylistic: it is the joining of what may be recognisable as separate letters into a single typeform, which may represent, in that form, either the same thing as the separate letters (stylistic ligation) or a distinct letter (semantic ligation).

Of course separated letters may be digraphs; indeed, the majority of digraphs in the majority of alphabets are written in separated form. Ligation of digraphs is actually quite rare, and more non-digraphs are ligated than are digraphs. There are also examples of (separated) trigraphs, tetragraphs, pentagraphs and, yes, hexagraphs (that’s the most I’ve counted). As Brian wrote above, a digraph is any two letters that, in combination, are used to represent a different phoneme than the same letters represent in isolation or in combination with other letters. In English, of course, our use of letters as digraphs is as phonetically inconsistent as the rest of our spelling, so we get pairs of letters that sometimes function as digraphs and sometimes not, and even the same pair used as different digraphs, e.g. CHain vs eCHo.

2010-03-25 16:02:51 • oldnick

Brian: Digraph and diphthong are not typography terms except tangentially

Agreed. A digraph is a grammatological concept, part of the description of a writing system as applied to particular languages. A diphthong is a phonological concept, part of the description of vowel sounds in spoken language. A ligature is a typographical concept, part of the description of how text is displayed.

The word ligature is also used sometimes in description of manuscript text, but I think this is almost always erroneous. There are ligatures in writing, but they are not what we think of as ligatures in type, they are joining strokes that do not form part of letters as alphabetic units.

Tom Milo has a distinctive use of ligature in the description of Arabic text: a connection between letters that normatively do not connect, e.g. a touching connection between a letter at the end of one fusion (letter group) and another at the start of the next.

Joe, I didn’t say that ­Æ was just a separate letter. I said that in some orthographies it is a separate letter. In other orthographies it representes a digraph, but this statement is incorrect:

If you decompose the suspect character and the result is an error, what you’ve got is a digraph

because a digraph by definition is two letters representing a distinct sound* – a grammatological, not typographical, role – , and it doesn’t matter whether those letters are written separately or ligated. Indeed, the fact is the opposite of what you write: if you decompose the suspect typeform and the result is not an error, what you’ve got is a digraph. If the result is an error, as it would be in e.g. Norwegian, then what you’ve got is a separate letter.

What we do with digraphs in typography has no bearing on whether or not they are digraphs, because it is their relationship to phonology not their appearance that defines them as digraphs. Here’s a simple example: in ecclesiastical Latin the two-letter sequence AE is always a digraph representing a single vowel sound, but AE and Æ are equally valid ways of displaying this digraph and mean exactly the same thing.

It’s worth noting that many (most?) digraphs, trigraphs, tetragraphs and pentagraphs represent consonant phonemes, not vowels. [The only hexagraphs I know of represent vowel sounds, but interestingly they include what we normally think of as consonant letters. Irish spelling is a hoot.] And again it makes no difference whether the letters involved are separate or ligated.

  • To a add a nuance, and perhaps more confusion, digraphs etc. are best identified on the basis of representing sounds other than those represented by the individual letters, not necessarily single phonemes. In other words, a digraph etc. may represent a combination of sounds, but different from the combination that the individual letters read independently would suggest. Hence, in classical Latin, the digraph AE/Æ represented a diphthong [ai̯], not a single phoneme, and the Irish hexagraph eidhea also represents a diphthong [əi̯].

Finally, confusion-upon-confusion, in some orthographies a digraph etc. only functions as such in particular preceding and/or following contexts.

2010-03-25 23:05:29 • dberlow

In English, W began life as a digraph VV with the interesting status of a variant spelling replacing the single letter Ƿ (wynn), but which gradually became the preferred spelling and morphed, through ligation, into a single letter, in much the same way that an AE digraph morphed into a single Æ letter in Norwegian and other alphabets.

Yes, it’s per language, and not surprisingly because phonology is language-specific and alphabets tend to make common use of letters to represent the same phonemes only if direct transfer of an alphabet or portions of an alphabet have happened, especially within a language family. When no such direct relationship exists, only fluke will result in the same phonemes being represented by the same letter. Hence, for example, in Polish, the voiced rounded labiovelar approximant phoneme that is the most common signified of W in English spelling is represented by the letter Ł, while the letter W represents the voiced labiodental fricative that we write in English as V. Meanwhile, in Polish, the letter V isn’t used at all, so an analysis if W as a VV digraph makes no sense at all in this particular language context. And so it goes.

As far as I know, W is an English contribution to the modern Latin alphabet, and occurs in European alphabets in a west-east band across the middle of Europe from England to Poland. But the use of the letter varies as it moves east because of differences in phonology, first between the different Germanic languages and then all bets are off when it hits a Slavic language.
