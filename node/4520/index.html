2004-04-30 06:38:04 • hrant
Hrant, the whole point of ClearType is to reduce the need for extensive hinting in the x-direction. The ClearType renderer actually ignores x-direction delta hints. Ross and I have done extensive work with ClearType, and there is no gain in trying to control x-direction stems or proportions: the ClearType resolution gain allows greater fidelity to the glyph form, so optimal screen readability can be addressed in the design process rather than in the hinting. Indeed, if a font does contain x-direction stem hints -- e.g. for the benefit of 300 dpi laser printers -- it is often beneficial to turn them off for the CT renderer, which produces better results without them.

Note that these comments, while generally true of ClearType in its currently shipping incarnation, are even more pertinent to recent improvements that will ship with future MS software.

In fact, come to think of it, even outline fonts aren't always what they seem to be. Consider the necessity for optical scaling: the same outline font looks very different at different sizes, and people make multiple size-versions of a font (or a MM optical axis) to counteract optical effects.

2004-04-30 16:58:56 • hrant
John: your points about ClearType make sense to some extent, but I think not totally. You say ClearType makes superhinting less necessary. I agree. But it can't totally get rid of the need (not at current screen resolutions - not everybody is using your settings :-). You say that ClearType ignores x-hints. Sure. But I can think of two reasons for that, both temporary: hinting at triple the resolution with the color filtering trickery thrown in is much harder, and they didn't want to hold up the other MS products waiting for a first iteration of CT; and they wanted to leave room for improvement*, and could afford to since even a "low-tech" incarnation of subpixel rendering is still a big improvement.

* Leaving room for improvement has in fact become standard practice in the software industry, because you can charge for upgrades.

Basically what I'm getting at is that even with triple the resolution and anti-aliasing, there's some room left for improvement, and that would come from superhinting. But I admit it's possible that the gain is small enough not to warrant the effort.

2004-04-30 18:22:11 • hrant
Well, Hrant, I can't argue with any of that, you're obviously right. Part of the difficulty in this discussion depends on which aspect (or, perhaps more accurately, which argument) we're going after. If we're talking about copyright protection then the points you brought up are major issues (not that they aren't otherwise). I would venture to say that type design is the most difficult copyright question to answer... it may be obvious to you and I, but it's clear that more than a few political types don't see it our way. Most of them can't see past the notion that we're not trying to claim the alphabet itself*, but our own interpretations of it; with that in mind, can you imagine the smoke that'd spout from their ears if they even heard the points we just discussed?

Of course other factors play here, too. What amount of work has to be performed on a (pre)existing object before that work itself is protected as a new object? Perhaps a lessen can be taken from the field of translation: Translations are only granted a "derivational copyright" because they are considered derivative works, and the translator must still gain permission from the original copyright owner to do anything to it. (This is all keeping in mind that, oftentimes, the translator will put in almost three times as much work as the original author.)

This and other issues need to be resolved before most policy-makers are ever going to take the matter seriously.
