2008-01-23 03:38:24 • agostini
Thanks so much for the input! I see the most questions are regarding Helvetica... Well, if you guys have seen a typical church logo, it always seems to have crosses, rivers, mountains, etc., along with fancy serif'd fonts and such. The biggest issue for me was common stereotypes associated with 'church.' I don't believe that crosses, rivers, mountains and all fit any demographic at all anymore. And although real church's don't necessarily target any specific demographic, the identity should be simple and neutral. The reason I used Helvetica as just an initial idea was because of it's legibility from distances. It is more fuller in my opinion, and is easier to read if it was to be on a sign. As opposed to the first logo I've shown that is News Gothic Standard. Also, about the design itself, the original logo before I made the 'old' one was just the character logo that had 'Spring of Life' adjacent to it in a horrible font called 'Rage Italic' I've never been a fan of open logos (logos that have no distinct shape) and 'he' the logo just kinda was standing alone. Anyways, thanks again for the advice! I'll see what else I can do with it! Ty

The letters lif are too tight next to that e. It looks like they have formed a clique and are leaving poor e out. But this isn't important. To me what is important is that even though it is getting better ( it is) I really think it won't become really good until you can describe what you are after and why. Only then can your choices be deliberate. You can always make something that comes close to being a good design by following typographic "rules" or by emulating other designs you like but you can't get past a 50% or 60% success without serious consideration of the context. Until that happens the design isn't grounded in it's purpose. And it will niggle you like a loose tooth or badly fitting clothing because it won't be quite right. I don't mean to brow beat you here. I hope it doesn't feel that way. Quite the opposite. I hope this helps reduce frustration while you are designing.
