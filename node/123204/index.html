Type Made for Kerning

February 10, 2015 • quadibloc

A thought struck me today; during the era of hot metal type, why is it that no one ever invented the following -

Since the kerns for letters like j and Q in some typefaces are very fragile, why couldn’t they have been made more durable by a strategem like the following:

In the area of a type slug below the baseline, for characters without descenders, cut a groove into the shoulder of the type, or lower the shoulder, so that there is room to put a reinforcing base below kerns.

Or simply make the shoulder lower everywhere, so that the part of the type that is in the shape of the particular letter is deeper and hence stronger.

In the case of letters with descenders that are on only one side of the letter, like p and q, the groove or lowering of the shoulder should still be present on the side the descender does not occupy.

An interesting exercise in hindsight! It would make things clearer if you illustrate your idea with a diagram.

I am full of obsolete inventions.

Back before laser printers, mainframe computers used big line printers to produce lots of output on paper quickly. Some used a rotating drum, with all the characters provided repeated at each character position, with a hammer at each character position forcing the paper and a ribbon against the raised character.

Others used a means of moving the characters across the page, so that only one (or a few, since the width of the page would be 132 characters, while usually 64 different characters or less could be printed - but in this case, unlike the drum printer, the characters were often spaced apart by more than the size of a column on the page) copy of each character would be needed.

Of these printers, some used a flexible band, some used a chain of linked slugs with characters, and others used a print train where the hammer pushed a slug that could move forwards to the paper.

If the hammer hit at a slightly wrong time, for a drum paper the line was wavy, with letters displaced up and down; for one of these printers, the character would be moved to the right or left.

Well, the thought struck me that this could be exploited, and a line printer could be made (with extra-wide hammers that would have to be able to be displaced horizontally into more than one position) that could do proportional spacing. Perhaps only crudely, like an IBM Executive typewriter.

And as it happened, IBM made a line printer that moved the characters horizontally, but which used a solid bar with the characters rather than a band or belt or train.

That gave me the idea of using a print drum that moved horizontally to present each character to each print position for printing, and which rotated to make a different character set available.

An impact line printer that could print in Chinese? Along with doing, say, four-line mathematics?

2015-02-14 02:47:37 • Thylacine

Your pictures mostly show conventional kerned type, except in the case of the capital TA, were we see mortised type.

I’m saying that conventional kerned type was recognized historically as being awfully fragile.

One way to deal with it would be simply to make the “letter” part of the type deeper, and the “block” part of the type shallower - that wouldn’t change the nature of the kern, only the proportions of the type body, but it would make the kern tougher.

Yes, though, the “letter” still does have a bit of a base on it that’s above shoulder height. But I’m suggesting drastically thickening that base and lowering that shoulder.
