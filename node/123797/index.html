For a substitute I would use Ambassador Script http://www.myfonts.com/fonts/canadatype/ambassador-script/ a digital version of Aldo Novarese’s “tipo inglese” Juliet typeface. Note the more upright stance is a better match than the inclination of the other scripts mentioned. Clip off some of the flourishing and its fairly close to your card. It even has the crossed z used in your presumably Italian – based on the phrase Enza e Franco and the word Via – Business card. Don

I should have mentioned that most of the old business and other cards I see in this forum are engraved or lithographed rather than a typeface. Looking at the lack of connecting lines, which are easy to do in engraving and lithography, I believe that this is probably a metal jobbing font for letterpress work on cards. In my opinion it is either a simplified Juliet or a font created with the same idea of making a version of English script that would be robust enough to stand up to letterpress printing of cards. Typefaces with hairline connections were notoriously fragile and it made sense to omit them whenever possible. Don
