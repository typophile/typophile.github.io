Take a close look at the work that Bruce Mau has done for Zone Books. (Any title will do, as he’s basically designed them all). If you can find a copy of his book Life Style, he devotes many pages to this topic, and even specifies some 40 or so composition rules that he follows for Zone Books. It is all very sound advice.

Other excellent points of reference are some of the better designed titles from MIT, as well as anything put out by the Princeton Architectural Press.

The work of Richard Eckersley is superlative, but harder to come by. (He did many titles for the University of Nebraska Press, among others.)

With Penguin, just try and pick up one of the older (orange/green/blue) paperbacks for a few pennies on amazon (or at a second-hand bookshop) to look at the layout and typesetting. They’re all just based on golden section rules, there’s a load of information online if you dig around, and here’s [[http://ronin-group.org/misc_etext_tschichold.html|Jan Tischgold’s composition rules]]. Monotype Joanna, Monotype Plantin and Linotype Pilgrim show up frequently, all would be good choices. Joanna and Pilgrim are both Eric Gill typefaces, very good, make very readable book text.
2010-04-09 14:08:47 • Daniel Couper
I should mention that Bruce Mau plainly acknowledges his debt to Jan Tschichold, and there exists a clear lineage between the two men’s respective rules of composition. Both are worthy of close study. Bruce Mau elected to set all of the Zone Books in Eric Gill’s Perpetua, which Tschichold had been known to use to great effect. (Many of the classic Penguin covers were of course set in Gill Sans).

2010-04-10 00:02:17 • uzapucax

Sebastian, the Tschichold/Penguin approach is a great reference - one of the greatest editorial design achievement of XX century, though it should not be taken as an absolute reference per se. Tschichold was a master in book design, and was central in reviving the quality editorial design in popular literature during last century, but one should always consider that he was a man thinking and working some forty/fifty/sixty years ago, in a completely different technological environment. That is to say, for example, that the typefaces he used and championed (in letterpress years) aren’t necessarily the best ones to use today. I, for example, would think twice before setting a book in Perpetua or Joanna: excellent metal types (see Gill’s essay “On Typography”) with a weak digital translations. Same for the Golden Section - setting a double page spread with those proportions can be risky if you don’t mind having a very narrow inner/very wide outer and bottom margins (and you should always consider how the book will be binded - softcover of hardcover?). So, if you’re after some good editorial book design (in the essay genre), first of all I would have a look to your local bookshops and libraries, I’m sure there’s tons of good stuff among Spanish publishers. Then I would consider using a contemporary typefaces; and if you’re after a revival, there are some great recent digital interpretations to consider, both in the Renaissance/Venetian/Garamondish/Humanistic and in the Neoclassical/Romantic types. Finally, here is a quick link to the work of a great book designer (see the illustrated books section in particular):

http://www.matthewavery.com/index.php?/projects/illustrated-books/
