There is a difficulty with categorization. Someone or a group may put type or other things in groups as a means of discussing their characteristics but this does not mean that they will be used by any number of users in the way described by the original category definer. Works of literature are likewise put in to groups–sometimes by a school of thought and sometimes by the name of the prevailing period at the time they were published. Publishers have their own way of deciding which typeface to use and it may have nothing to do with the period of the author or the subject matter written about. It is more likely to be part of the working process of the publisher and which types they own and generally use. Perhaps only books that are self-published, where the writer is involved in the whole process, may more care be taken to match a typeface with an author or text.

Didot and Walbaum don’t get much use outside of display use. In my life I’ve seen two books set in a modern face. Both are contemporary sci-fi. And you can’t find literature in Akzidenz or Clarendon because nobody uses them for books after junior year.

And FYI, Bringhurst’s classification system gets very little use outside of Wikipedia entries, so don’t expect a lot of people to be familiar with the Bringhurst classifications.

I don’t think there’s much figuring to do, Nick. I have the feeling that only a small number of people who design books make any attempt at matching typefaces with the era in which a book was written or with the era under consideration in the text.

The situation is just as ChrisL noted in the last part of his note: publishers or designers they hire have type libraries consisting of fonts they use often, and many rarely venture beyond.

I’d differ with Chris on the matter of self-published books, though. In that realm there’s even less attention made to this sort of matching of type era and text era.
