Approaching it from the other end: The client needs to license fonts as soon as he is handed files (for InDesign, Illustrator, etc) which, for modification or printing or generating PDFs, require that fonts are installed on his computer. But if the client receives files with text converted to outlines or rasterized, or receives PDFs with fonts embedded (please check the EULA for this point), he can modify or print or generate PDFs without fonts being installed on his computer, and in this case does not need to license them.

2009-05-05 13:19:17 • charles ellertson

If it’s a 186 poster book with 200 pages, there could easily be a solid 30-60 pages of writing, which could indeed be a pretty serious bit of writing.

While when I write an academic work on literature I can easily reference multiple works citing only the most relevant part since presumably any major library at a research university will have them or can quickly retrieve them with ILL, pieces on visual works (especially posters which can be have very short public lives and may not otherwise be published in any other place), illustrations are a necessity and may not be available in published form anywhere.

I’d discuss the work some with the author, to see precisely how he wants to go about the work. If it’s a mere collection, then you’ll likely want to ask for compensation, but (I think I lean more to this) if it’s a substantial work that requires illustrations to be meaningful, I don’t think it’s necessary. Actually, just ask him to see a draft copy of the chapter he’s using your book and maybe the introduction too. Contractual obligations with the publisher aside, he’ll probably let you take a look, and from what you read you can decide how to proceed. Liberal arts publishing, at least from my experience in the literature world, isn’t quite as secretive as, say, science publishing.

Alaskan, maybe I can cite a couple of examples from the type community… First example: Tamye Riggs recently posted a request here (and elsewhere) for work using certain typefaces considered to be classic, and the book is due to be published in June. The other example: When Leslie Cabarga was writing his Logo, Font and Lettering Bible, he sent out requests to designers he knew, and he got many illustrations for his book this way. Everyone received credit, and got some exposure in exchange. A win-win situation for all involved, I suppose.

I think that this author has gone about things the right way – he’s written you in advance to request your permission to use your work. Of course, you don’t know this professor the way you might know Tamye or Leslie, but you could find out if he’s published other books – that might give you some reassurance as to his intentions. Or, as Matthew suggests, ask him if you can see a draft, a chapter, etc.
