i seem to remember somewhere here either hrant or sii suggesting licensing fonts via subscription, but i have no idea how to find those discussions, so here are my thoughts on the topic. i have two situations where i think this could be really useful. 1) i am a poor designer who wants awesome type but cannot afford to buy families outright, so i go to my foundry of choice and sign up to a monthly subscription deal not unlike what [[http://www.emusic.com| emusic]] does for music - where for a set monthly fee i can download a certain number of faces, each month my account gets topped up. 2) i am from a community group/non-profit/church/cheapskate company and don't have the funds to licenses quality fonts for the number of users i actually need, so i enter into a subscription deal with the foundry where they charge me a monthly/yearly (whatever) fee to use the fonts on a larger number of computers. eg: my church might need a family of fonts to use for it's identity, but because of the nature of a church lots of the materials are created by volunteers on their own computers, so standard licensing would be too expensive and limiting, but a subscription fee might be workable. i'm sure their are myriad other ways this could work, and problems with it, but i suspect they could be overcome.

2009-04-14 18:38:29 • Jackson
This has come up a few time. The problems are pricing and piracy. Type designers don’t trust end-users to not pirate fonts. It’s likely that people will sign up, download the latest original versions of everything (something most pirate font collections don’t even offer), cancel the subscription, and keep the fonts. That leaves us with two options. One, make up for piracy losses by setting a very high price that nobody wants to pay. The other option is font DRM. Font DRM has a very nasty history and users hate it. Software developers hate it as much or more than users. Adobe has made it clear that it does not want to go down the DRM road again, which means that the DRM has to be part of the operating system, which is unlikely to happen and would only happen in a way that screws everyone involved.

2009-04-15 05:25:12 • blank
As it has been brought up, I'll repeat here things I've previously said or written in public and online, back when I worked for Adobe.... Adobe repeatedly engineered font DRM schemes and then ditched them over the last 25 years. The ones that are public knowledge are (1) the original protection scheme that locked Mac Type 1 fonts to specific output devices (discontinued in the early 90s), and then (2) the encryption and device-tying approach using PACE technology, developed for CID-keyed Type 1 fonts for the Japanese market (phased out starting around 1999-2000, though Morisawa used it for some years after IIRC). The fonts locked to output devices weren't as hard, as I understand it (though that was before my time). But once we had fonts that lived on the end users' computer, and were accessed in applications, the engineering costs of supporting the encryption scheme throughout the workflow were immense, in the multi-millions of dollars. Customers were hardly fond of these DRM schemes either, because they got in the way of legitimate and reasonable uses of the fonts. Ultimately, Adobe decided that DRM for fonts was more trouble than it was worth. There was even a third scheme that was fully developed but never deployed.... I'll never say never, and of course I'm not with Adobe today, but it seems mildly improbable that Adobe would lead the way back into font DRM today. Not that they are morally opposed, but they'd have to be convinced that the benefits outweighed the costs... including the "cost" of frustrating one's customers. Today with font handling being done primarily at the OS level, and with so many apps needing to interact with fonts (and some bypassing the OS to "talk" to the font directly), DRM for fonts would be much harder than it was back in the day. Perhaps somebody should go talk to the folks at LetterHead Fonts who recently experimented with font DRM for a while (http://www.typophile.com/node/30452). Also, on the topics of both DRM and font rental, the folks at Morisawa in Japan could be informative. They have a long history with DRM, and they have a font rental product called "Passport," which is aimed at the student market if I recall correctly. Personally, in principle I think that offering a DRM-based rental scheme as an optional alternative to traditional permanent licensing is a fine idea (that is, offer the same fonts with a choice of licensing models, they can get them without the DRM the "old way" if they want)... it's just the wee technical details that get in the way. But those are some pretty big "details." Cheers, T

2009-04-16 01:56:02 • Sye
Good topic. If you look around you can find a variety of new licensing models being explored. All with the goal of meeting the needs of customers, and allowing type designers & font vendors to earn a livelihood. For example, the traditional font license that includes 5 workstations by default. Some foundries, including FontBureau, offer a single workstation license, which lowers the entry fee. We've done this with our [[http://www.Fontmarketplace.com|Fontmarketplace.com]] site (we offer TrueType fonts with single workstation licenses). Of course the hope is that honest users will be honest and select the number of workstations they intend to install the fonts on from the drop-down menu. Another example is the 'personal use' vs. 'commercial use' licenses. Some type designers and foundries are offering a discount for a license that is only for personal use (to appeal to home & office users much like FontMarketplace). Back in 2003 when Steve Kuhlman and I worked at Agfa Monotype we applied for a patent for a "Font Rental System". [[http://www.wikipatents.com/apps/20040177056.html|Wikipatents.com - Application 20040177056.]] We both left in 2004 so I don't know what happened to it (other than it does not appear to have been issued). From what I remember DRM was an integral component of the system. As Thomas and others have mentioned, in today's market that would meet a lot of resistance. I think the main objective with any licensing system is to make it a win-win for all the parties. For the customer they must see the value, and it must be easy to understand and administer. For the type designer and font vendors it must protect the font software and IP. Some of the comments and ideas posted above are great in that we all must continue to think about how to best serve our customers. If we are not meeting their needs (by not having the right fonts, or making it easy and cost-effective to acquire), then we'll have to continue to evolve and innovate, or face even tougher challenges ahead. So from that standpoint I would agree that the font & music business have many similarities.
