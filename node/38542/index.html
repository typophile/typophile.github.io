It may be appropriate to add to the part 24. Always spell check! “Even if text is given to you by a client, check it.”, that it is not advisable to correct text that is given to you, unless you are asked to do so. You can point out errors to the writer, but do not correct it yourself. Some ‘errors’ in the text might be on purpose, so if you correct it you could ruin the text. Only correct with permission. At least, that is what I have (learned) as a rule.

Also the bit called 28. Indents, I think this is to be decided by the typographer/typesetter, only if he/she thinks indents are appropriate for the particular text he/she is working on. It’s not a rule, because it is not always necessary.

2007-10-29 14:22:20 • Kristina Drake

I think a short list of ‘blunders to avoid’ can be useful, but not a 3 page guide to good typography. That takes a book, like Bringhurst, Felici, etc.

Cases in point:

“Rule 12. Increase line spacing to improve readability in text.”

Too much leading is also a problem.

“Rule 19. Ideal Column width.”

Your recommendation on ideal single column width of 4 1/2 inches is just wrong. Both Bringhurst and Felici both recommend about 2 1/2 lower case alphabets for ideal measure in a single column. The physical length will be affected by the typeface chosen. Also it will vary with leading, etc.

A good relationship between font, measure, leading, number of lines, and margins is a complex subject, and central to good typography. The books devote a lot of pages to it, with illustrations.

Also the writing here is too loose and informal for a guide.

  1. Why put quotes (single) around em and en? The terms are not quoted in the explanation, and quotes seem to be out of place in the rule text.

  2. I would loose “true” in “true quotation marks”. While there are fake small caps and true small caps, I don’t think there are any fake quotes. There are simply quote marks and feet and inch (prime) marks.

9., 10., 28. I think uppercase “not” is over the top. Rule 24 works effectively with a bold word, perhaps these three would too. Perhaps emphasis with italics would be even better?

  1. I am not sure how other people feel about this but Airal, Helvetica, Meta are all huge at 9pt. I’d say 7pt is a more appropriate lower figure. And I’m not talking about special cases (like faces not likely to be used for extended text, or special cases like children’s books). I am talking about mainstream type.

  2. I believe the period after “caps” should be a comma.

  3. I don’t feel this rule is necessary at all. You can never get it right with absolute measures like inches anyway. To me this falls under 16 where you do talk in character count unit (some people express it in ems too).

  4. “spell=check”?

  5. “Whine”?

  6. Quoting em again seems unnecessary. I don’t think an em can be equated with 3 spaces. I would simply loose spaces reference altogether. I would also advise students against using tabs. Indents option in the software is the only way to go, imho.

  7. Just omit…
