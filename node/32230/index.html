Monotypopoly

March 14, 2007 • Nick Cooke

I don't know if this has been discussed previously... What does the type community think of Fontwise by Monotype? Apparently Monotype are presenting this service to major type users in the UK and companies are signing up to a contract. This means that large agencies, publishers or whatever can only buy any new typefaces exclusively from Monotype. I'm not sure that Monotype would do their best to fulfill a request to acquire any typeface from a foundry not owned by them - maybe they would suggest something similar in their library. Of course Monotype are presenting Fontwise as a font management system which makes it easy for large organizations to keep control of licensing and ensure legality. They complete a font audit within a company, but if any discrepancies come to light such as customized fonts, a company could find itself having the extra cost of "legalising the modifications". As many publishers have their own versions of typefaces; eg, having a few characters altered or respacing and kerning I am sure they will not welcome this extra expense, especially if the alterations were done when the license stated that modification is allowed if the original font is licensed for the correct number of users and is not being sold or given away and only being used within that company. Does anybody think this is a good thing, or just a thinly disguised attempt to monopolize the typeface market? They already own ITC and Linotype, and I am sure many smaller distributors and independent foundries will start to feel the pinch as more companies sign up to Fontwise.

OK, in theory I support Fontwise as it ensures that type designers and font engineers are paid for their work. For too long the practice of sharing fonts has been going on. I remember about 10 years ago there was a statistic that for every font bought, five copies were made and used. And that's just in Europe and North America. The moment you go to places where only minimal copyright laws exist and theft/piracy is regarded as an act of valour you will find this ratio rises to about 1:25. Yes we are on the 'signed-up' list at Monotype. But we have no formal agreement with them. It is because we have worked with Monotype in the past and have a good relationship. I too have heard that a Fontwise contract will tie the client to buy fonts exclusively to Monotype. This is of course monopolistic and predatory (and potentially illegal under most trade laws). And that's what I object to. This will eventually push independents out of business as more and more clients will go to Monotype as the first port of call - be that for straight font licensing or customisation. As far as Dalton Maag is concerned it may put us in an awkward position as clients may question our legality. Furthermore, original fonts designed by us will flag up on Fontwise as potentially illegal. I don't know how Monotype handles such flagged up fonts - whether they contact the original designer tracing the source of fonts, or whether they simply demand payment from the client. I know that they contacted me about fonts we have done for a client to establish their rightfulness. Monotype now controls the three biggest font libraries which contain most of the best known fonts. Establishing Fontwise also gives them control over our potential client base, and thus control over our business activities. I am concerned that in the long term the combined behemoth MT/LT/ITC and Fontwise will impact on the smaller foundries and independent font retailers. Companies such as ourselves and retailers will need to double their efforts to put client's minds at ease in regards to legality and copyright if they want to maintain their business. Alternatively, all independent designers will have to 'sign up' to MT's Fontwise to be part of the legal database and give MT a de facto license to retail their fonts. Bruno Maag Dalton Maag Ltd
2007-03-28 11:58:25 • bruno_maag
I very much agree with Bruno, Fontwise is a good idea, but it is being used by a commercial company to enhance their commercial interests. I can understand them doing that, but they may well be tempted, from being in such a dominant position, to simply cross reference fonts that they may not have access to. I’m not saying that Monotype carries out this practice, but I do know that some font distributors have done this, and some probably still do. When a user is locked into a single distributor it is easy for that distributor to say to the user “g-type fonts (for example) are not easily obtainable so to avoid being compromised with possibly illegal versions of g-type fonts – let us license you with fonts of a similar design from our own sources”. So the distributor makes on the new font license, Nick Cooke looses income and exposure of his g-type font library. It is very hard to be an independent font designer without the assistance of organisations like Monotype Imaging to give you wide exposure, and it’s a sad reflection of the font industry that, apart from MyFonts.com, Monotype are now the only boys in town. And what has driven the industry to this position – that’s simple – font piracy. One only has to check the listing in alt.binaries.fonts to get a feel for the amout of font data being passed around illicitly, much of which is original work of independent font designers.
