2008-01-17 12:11:13 • russellm

There are several things at play here. First, I’m not one to defend Steve Jobs, but the Engadget article linked at the top of this thread has him saying that people don’t read anymore, not that books are dead. I have issues with both statements anyway.

That people are reading more and more off screens is just a fact, I don’t think it needs to be qualified further. I’m reading my news online on my computers (an iMac and a crusty old TiBook, alas), and I’m reading technical material (documentation, etc.) and work documents. Do I ever need to print stuff? Very seldom, and I think it’s for the better.

I don’t buy the somewhat luddist position of people who want to feel the paper and smell the ink vs. shabby computers or book-reading devices. I love books, I love paper, I love letterpress too, I wouldn’t be here if that wasn’t the case. But I don’t think anyone can argue that John Le Carré or Dan Brown books, which are printed on cheap paper and are the classical read and then throw in a pile of crap kinds of books, are really objects that we want to carry around in this new century. My father consumes them like crazy, then they end up in huge stacks that just take place and gather dust. Never do they get re-read, because that’s not what they’re after: you don’t read novels like these, you consume them as entertainment. If you follow my point, that kind of disposable stuff doesn’t belong to print, in my opinion. These books don’t have the feel of good books, because they are cheap by design.

That market sector, which is just huge, is rife for being passed onto e-book reading devices, screens, etc. I have been working with an Italian company that imports e-ink reading devices like the iRex Iliad, and I have had one for more than a year now. That is something, like the Kindle, which you can take with you in bed. It doesn’t make any noise, it doesn’t produce light (you’ll need your regular book lamp), it’s as light and as a book. Don’t take my word for it, try to get your hands on one and see for yourself. And consider a device holds not just one book, you can annotate them without harming the original, you can download one while on the road for a fraction of the cost of the paper version, etc.

Also think of the distribution scenarios and of the environmental issues. Paper needs to be transported by plane, trains, wheel, it has weight, it has limited supplies so new print runs need to be scheduled and produced, then there’s the issue of excess copies, etc. All of this problems that we take for granted are simply not there in a digital scenario. People get books more often, pay them less, authors get paid faster and possibly more without the overhead of print production and distribution, and marketing changes dramatically, too.

For books which are truly of value, either because they’re wonderful references (think Letters of Credit!) or just plain wonderful books (the Divina Commedia published by Tallone), I just don’t see what the supposed threat might be. Where form is ahead or on par of function, form has value and I’ll gladly continue to pay for it.

As for typography: technical changes are all over the history of our craft, yet until we somehow come up with a way to replace words that need to be read and written, I don’t see any sort of harm whatsoever. In fact I think the market for screen fonts and other ways to present text on screens will become bigger and more diverse, and that is good news for typographers, type designers and for people reading off screens as well.

If the [printed] book were invented today? A novel concept. Let’s pretend that printing never existed and books have been printed electronically for the last 500 years and are read on electronic readers that were perfected during the first two or three hundred years of electronic publishing.

[seque to futuristic conference room, start with voice over]

I’m a writer and at my publishers office this morning. The publisher, designers and typesetters have my new book all wrapped up in Portable Document Format ready for global distribution. We’re excited. My book is ready to be distributed to the entire world in minutes by just pushing some electrons around.

But one of the designers speaks up and says somewhat confidently, “hold up, I’ve thought of a better way to get this information to the readers of the world. First we’ll chop a few thousand trees and transport them on big trucks to a plant that will grind them up into pulp, wash the pulp with wicked bleach” — he didn’t know what happened to the bleach after the wash. “Then we press the pulp and iron it on huge screens, roll this new paper on huge spools, and put the spools on other big trucks to take them a few hundred miles to another plant. It only takes a month or so and only cost a few hundred thousands dollars.”

“While the trees are being ground, bleached and pressed, another factory is cooking up big gloppy oil and pigment. The plant cooks this glop to just the right consistently by mixing in some sweet solvents — it’s quite a spiritual thing. Then it’s put in barrels, loaded on other big trucks, and transported a few hundred miles to the same plant where the paper was delivered.”

At this point the designer said “they take our PDF that we have prepped here, and they etch these huge metal plates with the design. Then they put it on these big machines that transfer the pigmented glop, called ink, onto the paper. This pigmented paper is then cut up and glued into stacks called books. These books you can actually hold in your hand and smell, or even lick if you want! These paper books are collected and put into boxes, and then, you know, loaded on big trucks and taken a few hundred miles to a warehouse.”

“At that point the books are broken into smaller units and taken on thousands of smaller trucks and airplanes to places all over the world. What about it? What do you think?”

So we think about it. Hmmm? We can deliver this right now with hardly any effort or expense and it can be read on electronic readers that we’ve been using for hundreds of years, edits and corrections can be made instantly, and autoreaders can even read it to someone without effort. Or we can chop hundreds of trees, mix hundreds of gallons of solvents, cook oil into ink, burn thousands of gallons of fuel and employ thousands of hours of manpower. It didn’t take long for us to snort and say “nah, we think not.”

[freeze frame, and wrap with voice over]

I’m sure the printed book will last, for a few more years anyway. But you guys are funny. Do all of you naysayers still hitch the horse to the buggy every morning for the ride to the bookstore? I’m amazed at the lack of vision — the same crowd that dismissed typesetting on PCs a couple of decades ago. And let’s see, who was that guy who saw the potential for proportional type on PCs?

enjoyed this list

Posted By: Steven Poole of The Guardian

I here propose a minimal list of features that any successful ebook device must eventually have. Feature parity with physical books, after all, is surely a reasonable baseline demand. So here is what the electronic book of the future will be like.

1 It will have an inexhaustible source of energy and never need recharging.

2 It will have resolution as good as print. (No, Amazon: really as good as print.)

3 It will be able to survive coffee and wine spills, days of intense sunlight, dropping in the ocean, light charring, and falling completely into two or more pieces, while still remaining perfectly readable afterwards.

4 It will allow me to scribble notes and/or doodles in the margins, with my choice of mechanical pencil or fine Muji fibre-tip pen (black). (Note: typing in the margins with a crappy thumb keyboard is not an acceptable alternative.)

5 It will allow me to riffle through it and thus get a quick, intuitive look at the book’s argumentative or narrative structure.

6 It will allow me to tear off the corner of a page to write down my phone number (or someone else’s).

7 It will display to other people in coffee shops and on public transport the title of what I am reading, so as to advertise my erudition or quirky sense of humour.

8 It will be physically handsome, not drop-dead fugly. (Note to Amazon: for pity’s sake, next time, head-hunt people from Sony or Apple.)

9 Indeed, the books on it will still be designed, by typesetters and graphic artists, so as to feed our aesthetic pleasure.

10 I will still be able to lend or give books to friends, or swap books in and out of the honour library of much-read novels in a Mediterranean seaside bar.

11 I will be able to use the ebook as a reliable flat surface for rolling cigarettes or other leaf-based refreshments, without worrying about debris shorting the motherboard.

12 When I receive the updated edition of the Oxford Companion to Philosophy, I will be able to press the previous edition into service as a stand for the left-hand music speaker on my desk.

13 The ebook will function, moreover, as both bug-crusher and discretionary hat. Placed on my face, it will make a soft roof against the sun on the beach.

14 I will be able to hurl a fatuous tome such as Jeff Gomez’s Print Is Dead across the room without thereby destroying my ability to read any other books.

There. Only 14 features to go before we can all abandon paper. Not too much to ask, is it?

Closer to 2000 years.

The end of print isn’t an overnight thing. I’ve already noticed a serious decline in the last twenty years and it’s just getting started.

On what do you base this observation? Obviously not on book publishing, because the number of books being both printed and sold has been rising steadily. A recent issue of the London Review of Books had statistics for the UK publishing industry: the annual turnover is 2.8 billion GBP, which is only .1 billion less than that of sales of bread. 900,000 books are sold in the UK every day, which is one book for every ten loaves of bread. 115,000 new titles are published every year, and excepting an insignificant dip in 2004-05 the number of books published has risen steadily every year since 1947.

Non-print text media have become extremely important in the past fifteen years, but not in competition with books. If you look at the kind of content that benefits from being in a downloadable electronic format one can see how new media compete with newspapers, magazines, reference books (encyclopaedias, dictionaries, etc.) There is, as yet, little competition with most book publishing.

As others have noted, this may change when display technology improves, but I think there is more to it than that. Every dedicated ebook device I have seen has presumed a pocketbook format, which means that it is essentially only useful for things like novels. Go into any bookshop and take a look at the incredible range of size formats and content structuring in books, and you’ll see how impoverished the electronic reading devices are; they quite simply cannot replicate the complexity of print publishing.

Comparisons to e.g. vinyl vs CD vs online music or different formats of video recording and playback are misleading, because all these involve media that require a playback device. Going from one medium to another is only a shift in the storage format and playback device. Going from a book to an ebook is a different kind of shift, though, because the book is its own playback device. Reading a book isn’t like listening to a recording of someone singing played back on a machine; it is like listening to someone singing who happens to be in the next room. It is worth remembering that typography didn’t significantly change the book, it just made it possible to produce more copies more quickly. Reading a book isn’t like reading an email, which requires the intermediary machine; it is like reading a letter written on a piece of paper. Despite the technology that has been employed in typesetting and printing the book, its relationship with the reader, and hence the reader’s relationship with the author, does not rely on technological mediation.

I live on an island that, for a couple of months each winter, is beset with wind storms. Power outages are quite common, sometimes lasting more than 24 hours. When the backup marine batteries are fully discharged, the computer batteries long since dead, I’m especially glad of my books.
