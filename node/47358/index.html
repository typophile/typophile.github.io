David: I see where the ḥet (ח) & heh (ה) might be confused (sorry simḥa!), but that's a bug in the Hebrew alphabet. If I were turning the surrounding text into a font I might try to differentiate them more, but in this context, with its well-known quote¹, I figured I could prefer ‘pretty’ over ‘more readable’.  That the ḥet (ח) looks somewhat like the mem (מ) from some fonts is harder for me to see; it certainly doesn't look like the mem I'm using! Similarly with the shin (ש) and the Rashi script bet (ב): there's some similarity, but no real possibility of confusion.  The vav (ו)/yod (י) confusion is less excusable, so a bit of background: The style of the surrounding text came from an experiment I'd done in how wide I could extend Hebrew letters and have them recognizable. To distinguish the vav (ו) from the yod (י) from the resh (ר) I chose to make the yod as short as I could while keeping the basic shape and making the vav the narrow exception to the extended style. The `bump' on the shin (ש) and the awkwardness of the ayin (ע) are both also results of this original design. I liked the alef (א) I'd done, but it's only here in the tiny text. Eran/Simḥa: I vaguely see an ayin (ע) in the right stem—where I’d intended yod yod shin (ייש), but I cannot see how the heh heh (הה) on the left looks like a mem mem (םם) to you. I guess I should take it in account anyhow.  … But: Once you know what letters they're supposed to be, do you see them, or still what you saw first? It doesn't need to be obvious a priori; if it works a posteriori it's good too.  (Modern bias?) —Joel 1. Jeremiah 33:10–11: “Yet again there shall be heard … in the cities of Judah, and in the streets of Jerusalem, … (11) the voice of joy and the voice of gladness, the voice of the bridegroom and the voice of the bride”.
