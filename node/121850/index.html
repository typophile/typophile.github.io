Match for typewriter-type for graphic novel?

December 7, 2014 • scillas

Hi! I’m new here (though I’ve nosed around earlier). I’m working on a graphic novel, or actually it’s more like a ‘graphic bio’ – a biography in the form of a picture book.

In the book, there are 3-4 different kinds/lines of text – the narrator’s voice, my main character’s voice – and then excerpts from letters, song texts and poems, written for and by my main character. The 3 different kinds of text change a lot throughout the book, so that I sometimes will have all three kinds on one page or spread. Every page is illustrated and sometimes text is set on top of a drawing.

The two first kinds of text, the narrator and my main character, I think would work with quite many different typefaces, My story takes place mostly in the 1960-1980s, when my character was young – in a beatnik-/alternative surrounding. I’d like the typeface for her and the narrator’s voice to have some warmth, but without attracting too much attention to itself. Maybe slightly handwritten and/or casual. For now, I am leaning towards Cronos, and earlier I’ve looked at Joanna, Perpetua or Legacy Sans.

But, but, but: the letter/poem-texts are causing me a head ache! The only thing that I think really works for them, is using a “oldschool-typewriter”-face (like “Pitch”.) One with a vintage-typewriter-feel. But I don’t think the typewriter-face doesn’t mix very well with any of the above mentioned. The shapes, perhaps, but also when combined with a typewriter face, they look a little (too) formal. I looked at Nexus and Eureka, which both have a monotype-version, that I thought of using in stead of vintage- typewriter-type, but I think those monotypes look too contemporary for my book. Sigh.

Does this make sense? Do you have any ideas or suggestions?

I know this is the kind of thing you should discuss with a colleague, while having a coffee – but I don’t have that person right now. So – if anyone has thoughts, opinions or suggestions on this, it will be very much appreciated! Thanks so much!

2014-12-07 19:02:32 • J. Tillman
It depends on how much “graphic” and how much “novel” the result: if the typewritten text is visually framed, it shouldn’t clash with the rest. If that is the case, I would go for an accurate rendition either historically (which model of typewriter?) and/or visually (a “worn out” face, like http://www.fontfont.com/fonts/trixie). If the lines (pun intended) between the different kind of text are more blurred, you could use either a classic text face with a monospaced cut (like Century) or a proportionally spaced typewriter face (like the ones suggested in the aforelinked thread or http://www.fontsquirrel.com/fonts/lekton). About the text faces you mention, I’m most familiar with Perpetua, and I wouldn’t recommend it, finding it a bit “frail” and maybe even pretentious.
