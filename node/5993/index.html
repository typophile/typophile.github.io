2004-08-09 22:30:06 • Bald Condensed
We don't need them, but for some applications they're very nice. All typesetting up until everything became phototype (then digital) had optical scaling because it wasn't any more expensive than not.

Opticals are especially nice if you're mixing a number of sizes of the same font together, which includes such things as superscripts, footnotes, blockquotes rendered in a smaller font, etc. If you're not mixing sizes of the same font, then you get most of the benefit of opticals by just a more refined font for the larger sizes and a more robust font for the text. An especially nice example of a pair of fonts which harmonize in this way would be Palatino and Michelangelo.

If you mix sizes of the same font without doing any optical scaling, then quality suffers. In particular, smaller sizes are too light, too contrasty (in terms of hairlines being too thing), too compressed, and too closely spaced. Sadly, this is what people have become used to, because it's really easy to do it this way, and rather difficult and expensive to do better.
2004-08-09 22:31:40 • raph
1) Because the way human vision interprets what it sees depends on scale. Think of those optical illusions where you only see the hidden pattern when you're at a certain distance.
2) Because there's a qualitative difference between reading as a pasttime and reading as a chore. It is in the former that formal aesthetics matters most, and in the latter where the intricacies of our reading "firmware" matter most.
