Trying to work out pixel font exercise for students

September 21, 2009 • nstockallen

If one were to assign the problem of making a pixel font letterform to students who are just getting used to using typography, what would be an easy way to have them draw a pixel glyph or two? Draw on a grid in Photoshop OR sketch on a grid and then transpose into Photoshop? Ultimately I’d like them to each draw a glyph and then load them all into FontStruct so they can type with it. They will also make icons to match their letters–like an alphabet book.

Does anyone have a good process, (number of pixels) size to start with and a file resolution to suggest? or a good link that describes this? Thanks!

Give them a week to make a complete alphabet with Fontstruct and another week to make a poster with a hierarchy that incorporates every typeface designed by all students in the class. The results will be horrid, but they’ll learn a lot.

2009-09-22 22:21:58 • David Sudweeks

I find it good to start by defining the intended use of the font, and from there the grid size (the total height in pixels), weight, width and style.

FontStruct is very easy to use and is great for designing bitmap fonts — students can test the fonts on real size on screen as they are designing it. Photoshop can be used to make a nice test/sample document with the generated fonts.

(I have developed a parametric method for designing bitmap fonts, you might find it useful as a reference.)

Hope that helps, Gustavo.

2009-09-22 22:56:21 • gferreira

An advantage to using pshop could be the block brush and block eraser in a small file such as 8 x 8 pixels. Especially since you say their are new to type, using new software could detract from focusing on the bitmap process or letterforms (whichever it is).

Personally I think coloring/erasing/reworking squares on graph paper would be more fun! Maybe let them choose their method.

The task itself could be to choose their name or a word and render it in several different grid heights, in the same “style” as much as possible. I.e. don’t switch from a sans to a slab. That way they have to decide if the higher rez versions reflect their “original” idea for the typeface or if it resembles more of an 8 x 8 for consistency’s sake.

2009-09-24 16:02:51 • Frode Bo Helland

I do the same project in my typography class. I have the students flesh it out on graph paper then develop in fontstruct. My students this year were resistant to developing ideas on paper. For the most part, the initial “hamburgefonts” graph paper concepts were poor…not sure why. I think it would be easier to use .psd than .ai for their initial concepts for reasons listed on an earlier post. My only concerns about using an application to flesh out a concept versus hand rendering is that they will get too caught up in the tool and not the idea.

Hope this helps.

BTW - I do encourage the students to use larger grids as a way of developing more detail so there is more variety.
