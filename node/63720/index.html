[[http://unicode.org/udhr/n/notes_fra.html|This web page]] lists the rather specific spacing conventions used by the Government Printing Office of France. Basically, punctuation marks that consist of two elements (: ; ? ! etc.) should be separated from the closest word to which they “belong” (for example, the last word of a sentence for a question mark, or the first word of a quotation for an opening guillemet) by a non-breaking space. Whether that space should be full-width or thin depends on the punctuation mark.

2009-11-01 15:39:59 • russellm

The standard typographic conventions are more or less the same in France and Quebec, but the word processing conventions are different: as I understand it, in France, the convention is to type a normal space in each case; in Quebec, the convention is generally to leave out the space, except in conjunction with the colon. For both locales, MS Word is configured by default to convert the appropriate spaces into (full-width) non-breaking spaces.

The recommendation that spaces be omitted in Quebec is set forth [[http://66.46.185.79/bdl/gabarit_bdl.asp?t1=1&id=2039|here]] by the Office québécois de la langue française; a small note at the bottom makes it clear that the recommendation takes into account the shortcomings of common word processors (as of 2002), and that if you have a way to insert a thin space, you should use it before semicolons, exclamation points, and question marks.

None of the “official” lists of rules that I’ve seen seem to include percentage signs or currency signs (dollar, euro, pound), but looking through some French language materials it’s clear that these are also offset by space (in both France and Quebec), and there are probably signs that fall into this category as well. An ellipsis is not offset by space, however. Apparently the rule of thumb (“signe double, espace double”) has to be qualified a bit.

I’m not aware of any language other than French where extra space is conventionally inserted around punctuation.

2009-11-02 13:32:39 • ybaggar

From a French point of view:

  • punctuation signs with both a lower and an upper part which take a space before and after (hence the ellipsis does not);
  • the best practice is to have a non-breaking thin space before and one normal space after, although this may cause problems in justified texts (some word processors, some versions of XPress and Indesign shorten the normal spaces so much that they become smaller than the justified ‘normal’ breaking space);
  • whether there is a space between numbers on the one hand and monetary signs or percentage on the other hand seems to be left to the typesetter - however since these signs all stand for words, it would make sense to space them like a word (space before and after).

Also, look at 19C books in English (and many other languages), they are often spaces like French is nowadays. My understanding was that the practice of taking away that extra space was established by commercial publishers (notably of newspapers) who wanted to save space, thus money. I may be wrong!

Last: the book quoted from the Imprimerie Nationale is very good, but as the title says, it is only the rules this printing press used internally and has no pretense of being the norm.
