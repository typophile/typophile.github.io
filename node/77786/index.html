I highly recommend Designing Type by Karen Cheng. It may be a little difficult to find but it has been invaluable to me. This is a good place to start in the meantime: http://www.typeworkshop.com/index.php?id1=type-basics Another thing I've found to be very helpful if you're having trouble drawing a particular glyph is to open up fonts that you think are successful and analyze the letters on a macro level. There are a lot of subtle touches that add up to a character working that you don't notice from a distance. Beyond that, the search feature of this forum works very well if you have a specific question. I also recommend a sketchbook with grid paper or a dot grid (at least to start). It'll help you keep your character widths under control until you get a better feel for it.

Actually, it appears that it's not difficult to find anymore. It's in stock on amazon http://www.amazon.com/Designing-Type-Karen-Cheng/dp/0300111509 Oh, and about the grid notebook: I'm not saying you should always follow a strict grid structure when you design your type. Your eye is always better than rigid geometry (and when you get into it, you'll see that most "geometric typefaces" aren't as geometric as you thought). Certain characters (in order to be most successful) should break the grid. The notebook is just a tool that I've found to be useful for keeping track of measurements. It makes cap/x-height easier to keep track of so you spend less time drawing guide lines, and makes it easier to keep an even stroke weight.
