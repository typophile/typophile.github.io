I doubt seriously that anyone reads an annual report in the same way as they read a book. They might scan the text and focus on the financial statement tabular material to find what interests them.
The type of an annual report should reflect the company it is for. Think of the difference between Ben&Jerry's, IBM, Avon, Harley-Davidson, Disney, Whole Foods, IKEA, Victoria's Secret, Colt Firearms, Smuckers, and John Deere.
I have asked on this forum how much difference does the cold hard data show in readability between a good sans and a good serif face and never been answered. I am looking for a usable measure like: "Serif face x has been proven to reduce reading time for a 100 page continuous text book (a novel) by 5% over sans serif face y."
We all have our personal favorites and those we hate. I get a headache when reading more than a paragraph of Optima. I love the way Bodoni looks in large sizes but tire of reading it in long text. My 2 favorite text fonts to read are Adobe Garamond and FF Din–find me the logic in that pairing. I don't claim to be typical of the majority of readers and don't cram my favorites down my client's throats.
Unlike several years ago when there were far fewer choices, today there are thousands of faces and several of them are good. There is one heck of a lot more to readability than sans vs serif. We don't even know if the serif issue is the most important. I think leading and line length mean much more but I have no hard data to prove it.

Oh well, I'll shut up now.

Oh yes–one more thing–CONTENT!
The nature of the content has a way of either making us want to read more (if we find it interesting) or making us want to yawn and quit (if we find it boring).
If I ownwd a bunch of stock in a company, I would be interested in the annual report and read it. If it were a company that was of no interest to me, I would not read it. This is where design, white space, and photos become a big help.
