Noah, there are a number of challenges facing lefties when it comes to writing, some practical and some cultural. Practically, you have the problem that most writing tools work best when pulled, rather than pushed, which means, for someone working with a left-to-right script, one needs to start on the right edge of most letters. This means, of course, that you need to be a very good judge of the proportions of letters. This isn't by any means an insurmountable challenge, indeed, this, reversed, is the normal way of writing Hebrew: a right-to-left script usually written starting at the left side of the letter. Arabic writers tend to push the pen much more frequently than either Latin or Hebrew writers – hence the prevelance of the flexible reed pen in Arabic calligraphy –, but they also tend to rotate the page, writing from top to bottom, as seen in the short film at the Arabic Typography website (click on the dark square moving across the screen to see the movie; other squares take you to different content).

The cultural challenges are more difficult. In my talk at ATypI in Prague, I discussed the role of ductus in letter identity, and as an illustration wrote the Latin letter a with the typical ducti of three different scripts that have assumed their most common forms written with broad writing instruments: Latin, Hebrew and Devanagari.



[By the way, I usually write Hebrew letters with what are sold by calligraphy suppliers as lefty nibs: they're not perfect for the task – they should be cut on even more of an angle – but they are more convenient than reed pens for quick jottings.]

The red line indicates that, structurally, the three letters are almost identical – so far as the tool will permit –, which is why I don't think a purely structural understanding of letter identity is sufficient. Ductus is part of our conventional identification of letters, and as such is culturally conservative.

The good news is that ductus is not a fixed thing in most scripts, and certainly not in the Latin script. Over the centuries, a wide variety of ducti have been accepted by readers, although almost always as a result of changes in writing implement. The most dramatic evolution in the accepted ductus of the Latin script accompanied the introduction of the split, spencerian nib.

Other scripts have embraced not only different ducti in different styles of lettering, but also varying ducti within a style. The illustration below, also from my Prague talk, shows the range of ducti in the Byzantine Greek style.




In my experience of trying to write Latin letters with atypical ducti, I found that it is actually very difficult. Perhaps it will be easier for you, as a lefty? What I found was not simply that the axis of contrast is reversed but that the angle makes it awkward to render the normal structure of the letters (see the dip in the bold of 'Devanagari' a). What you will find – and I encourage you to try it – is that attempting to render humanist letterforms with a lefty ductus will produce wild and wonderful results, but they won't look like traditional humanist letters. Why not? Because the identity of the latter is not merely structural but determined by their ductus. Also, you will have a much easier time with a narrower nib than I used: I was going to high contrast to make a point.

>Who could actually show some left hand calligraphy?

You mean, who can show a lefthander's calligraphy, right? I have just finished a semester with a class in which three lefthanders made varying progress; all advanced considerably. Of the three, one is on the way to a very good Italic, and with the others, an Antiqua and blackletter (a simplified or early Textura) of some accomplishment.

It is not impossible for the standard shapes of the letters to be formed well by lefthanders. It is equally not possible for all righthanders to form the letters well; as with the lefthander, some skill and, dare-to-say, talent (a combination of sight, judgement, kinesthetics, control, allowance for the tool and materials, and more; desire and an unprejudiced mind, and a pleasure in the making) are necessary.

Noah asked about the (Humanist) ductus – to me, that is the path and order of the stroke, primarily. Pen-angle or bias is secondary. In John's lovely example, the a's paths are identical; the bias of the tool is different. To truly have a different ductus, the 'a' would need to have a different structure.
For example, the 'a' of blackletter is angular; the turns are quicker and tighter; the course of the tool, more direct and straighter. In Antiqua, the paths are made rounder and softer; the turns, slower and more open.
Each share (save the hairline that often closes the upper bowl of the two-storey blackletter 'a') an underlying structure of an arcade and a lower-left bowl, but the shape of the path along which the tool is con-ducted (from L. ducere, to lead) varies enough that we see them as very different styles. This is enough to distinguish the ducti of the two hands.
A radically different ductus for a might be more like that of the Egyptian heiratic A (the sign for the bird, kite) where the path is singular, made with no lifting of the tool from the surface. The belly of the bird is the bowl of 'a'; the beak and head make the arcade. The terminating tail is the spur at the lower left of 'a'. The effect is that of an 'a' with an opening at the right side of the bowl. You will see this ocassionally in some handwriting; the effect is an affectation, but tolerable.

For the lefthander who is frustrated by the edged pen, there is the flexible pen, used for copperplate engravers' script. Quite a market, if done well. As the pen angle is reversed in most strokes from that in edged pen work, and thush tending heavy from upper right to lower left, it is more difficult for a righthander to make; the pen or paper must be held in reverse, or with the nib in a special canted holder. Conversely, a lefthander is naturally positioned to move the pen (always towards, never away, as the sharp nib will bite in) easily and without strain.

Beauty is a complex concept, the subject of a huge amount of philosophical literature. The word calligraphy, obviously, has an explicit etymological link to the beautiful, and the majority of calligraphy tends to involve a deliberate attempt to create something beautiful. Even challenging modern calligraphic works that use non-traditional forms that might be fractured, distressed or otherwise evoke a contemporary ugliness, tend to do so within formally constructed arrangements, and so fall well within the circumfrence of the aesthetic as considered by the philosophers.

If we need to distinguish between handwriting and calligraphy, it must be as activities rather than as products, since there is nothing to prevent handwriting from being beautiful (I only wish mine were). I believe the distinction has to do with the intent of putting down a text entirely for the sake of its content (handwriting), and putting down a text in a manner that gives prominence to its form and arrangement. It isn't actually necessary for anyone to agree on whether a particular thing is beautiful for its consideration to fall within the realm of aesthetics. Beauty is not one thing, but many things, and appreciation of different modes of beauty is both cultural and individual, and develops as we are exposed to and assimilate new experiences of the beatiful.

These are design decisions that must be available to anyone working in letters. These all will ground the work in solid foundations or create an airy fantasy, each fit to its purpose.

As you know, I don't at all advocate strictly edged-pen-like type; the pen is, like a computer, another tool for making. But the things I make – letters – happen to have a long history, both calligraphic and typographic; that history is something worth the time spent investigating and considering when making them.
