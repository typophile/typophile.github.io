One solution is for publishers to commission custom typefaces for which they can purchase rights up-to-and-including perpetual exclusivity. There comes a point where licensing existing commercial fonts for either a large install base or electronic distribution becomes more expensive than commissioning a custom font family. If individual publishers cannot afford to commission their own typefaces, they can join with others to pool their resources (as the members of the SBL Font Foundation have done). Such a solution works particularly well for academic publishers, since they tend also to have specialised requirements that existing commercial fonts may not support.

  • assemble an open library of “open licensed” fonts designed specifically for web and new media use
  • all FMF fonts to be new creations wiki-made by the world’s leading font makers
  • it works like the Wikimedia Foundation and the Wikimedia Commons—except this is a Font Commons, initially for comon fonts long out of copyright, but eventually more and better stuff
  • font maker contributors start fonts and hopefully finish them. Contributors work on each other’s fonts and hopefully finish them. It all takes place online in a wikimedia-powered environemnt. Eventually a big pile of great fonts get wiki-built.

You are sitting in front of a 24" monitor that has 1920x1200 resolution. You are sitting exactly two feet away. You are looking at ten point type that is (let’s say) 15 pixels high at nominal ‘100%’ magnification. This particular type is 100% optimized for this absolutely particular viewing situation.

IF you change either the viewing distance, or the magnification, you will only get optimal viewing if the type parameters (design) change as well.

We can and should be able to implement this. It is what was achieved in letterpress for carefully calculated average viewing distances. “when a T1 font goes outside the 1000 em space, the interpreter automatically scales it to 1000? " I don’t think this happens. The 1,000 em is defined in an 8,000 universe, into which all the points must fit. TT has a similar situation, where it has a huge universal value (16k or 32k?) ‘surrounding’ the 2048, (or whatever your TT em is), that also cannot be exceeded by point positions. Now, if your instructions take a point beyond the universe… you’d have to ask Jefferson Starship, over at MS.

“If so, when that happens, hints are always lost?” In order for them to be ‘lost’, they’d have there in the first place. Hints are only added at output of the product, and are (sadly to this futurist), not used in the design process, only defined there by (hu)man or machine for use at output, or not.

“…have a family in Fog 4/5 outlines that uses floating point points to advantage.” I’m not sure what the advantage is.

“In the display master, there are plenty of small distances like 3.4 units that one really wouldn’t want to round off to 3.” But do round to 3 when you generate T1 fonts… This is not a big deal, this 3.4 to 3.0, unless you have a bunch of 3.x that you want to ‘round together’ and some are more than 3.499999, or whatever FOG not/3.5 does with all that, in my opinion wastefully useless resolution. This extra-super-high-resolution in Fog not/3.5 issue, was stupidly keeping up with a 1990 URW claim that more is more, and if you don’t have it you can’t make it.

It’s now a dead issue, except in Fog not/3.5. The cleverest way to define the resolution of a font is to do so at the resolution or within the range you expect the use to be, but only if you don’t know exact the size/res combination(s). So, if you expect PS use, design on a 1000 em, (not 10,000 or a million), and thus be sure that what points you placed will Stay! until the user needs to scale them. And then, you pray for resolution on their output. Or, if you think their output is a lot less than 1,000 per em, hint. Or finally, if you think their output is a whole lot less than 1,000, convert to TT and a 512, 1024 or 2048 em, and write instructions. All of this is advice to the picky, until resolution fails, not needless to say.

“…what do I have to do to make sure that a really high-res TT font gets accurately printed and not somehow rounded off into converted T1?” Move to Mars, take up ice fishing, or etc.

“- - you would have thought it would all be obvious now - - that problems like this simply could not exist. . . .” Font format development by slow, stubborn, and sustained accretion, leaves tons of problems. It used to be called “Font Wars”, but for marketing reasons, now it’s called “OpenType.”

Cheers!
