Footnote Suggestions

April 15, 2007 • msilverz

I’m typesetting a friend’s writing sample in philosophy, and I can’t decide how to arrange and format the footnotes. (They must be footnotes, not endnotes.) I’ve attached an 8-page PDF. Each page is actually the same page of text but with the footnotes designed in a slightly different style. Here are the three main styles:

  1. Note number outdented; footnote text flush left with main text.

  2. Note number slightly indented, so that note text begins where indented paragraphs in main text begin.

  3. Note number indented, so that number appears where indented paragraphs in main text begin.

Each style comes in two versions: one with a 2-inch rule separating the text from the notes and one without. The first style also has two more variations: one with a period after the note number and one without. In all of them, the main text is Aldus 12/15 and the footnotes are Aldus 10/13.

I can’t decide which of the three main styles I prefer. (An obvious fourth style would be to have the note numbers flush left with the main text.) The first is recommended by Bringhurst, but I don’t think he has academic papers (with their sometimes long footnotes) in mind. I’m worried that the numbers standing outside of the margins draw too much attention to themselves.

Does anyone have any comments about or favorites among these different styles? Any suggestions for doing something different? (I know that the hyphenation needs work.)

Any help would be greatly appreciated!

2007-04-15 17:16:55 • Stephan Kurz

The usual style in the academic world is to use a paragraph indent for the note numbers, unless those numbers are also a superior. Outdenting the note numbers looks OK with loose leaves, but in a bound book, they are apt to get lost in the gutter on a right-hand (recto) page. If you want the note numbers flush to the text page & then block indent the notes proper (flush & hang), that’s fine, though not as common. Cambridge University Press did this for a while, maybe still does.

With paragraph style, the numbers “decimal align” only per-page. In other words, if you have notes 1-7 one page, you do not “clear for two digits.” That happens only on the page where you go from one digit to two, and from two digits to three.

Use of a rule may confuse some people. People reading this will be academics, who are use to footnotes. Usually, a rule is used when a footnote carries over from one page to the next. You always have to start a note on the page where it is called, but sometimes the footnotes are so long, there is no way to page the document without carrying part of the note over to the next page. In such cases, you use a rule to indicate the carried-over note. Three picas long is usually sufficient for the rule, but it can be full measure. If for some reason you must use a rule on all pages, make the normal rule a different measure than the rule you use to signal a carried-over note.

One of the oddest situations I’ve ever encountered was a journal article, where the first line of text called three footnotes. The third footnote took over three full pages to set, even in 8-point type. The forth note was called on the second line of text. After taking it over with the publisher, it was decided to set the article opening, the first line of text, then the notes on the rest of that page & the next two full pages (with rule, of course), before picking the text up again on the 4th page.

Obviously, a poorly written/edited piece. The author would have been better served to either incorporate the note in the text or make an appendix of it, and the editor surely should have pointed this out before accepting the article. But it happens.

Edit:

It is also unnerving for a reader to discover some designer feels that he/she needs “help” deciphering a normal situation. So, for example, you do not need extra spaces between footnotes, just like you do not need extra spaces between paragraphs in the text.

For example, some designers both block indent an extract, and set it in smaller type, so the reader “won’t get confused.” What reader? Most people who read this sort of thing are use to block quotes, they aren’t going to get confused. You might as well put a printer’s fist beside the extract. You wouldn’t believe the number of things I’ve seen by both designers and editors “to keep the reader from getting confused” when it is obvious that anybody reading the text is going to have a college education or more. What usually happens is the designer/editor adds to the reader’s confusion. There are ways to graphically help the reader, but they are usually not by highlighting elements he/she expects to see anyway.

Since you’ve got your answer, I’ll add another way to think about such things generally. Essentially, your question was “how should one handle the element ‘footnotes’?”

There is another way of thinking about the topic, which is, basically, “how should the element ‘footnotes’ be blended into the text page?”

This is probably old-school thinking. I saw Helvetica last night, and was struck not so much by those who did and did not like the typeface Helvetica, but the two schools of thought, modernism and postmodernism. One of the older modernists remarked that you cannot spend your whole life looking at things a certain way and just change. But postmodernism has been around long enough for one of those devotees to walk slowly & have white hair as well; they too have spent a lifetime looking at things a certain way.

OK, back to the page. This is not a simple modernist versus postmodernist point of view; the notion of the type page is far older. I do suspect a postmodernist would disagree with what I’m about to say, but so too could a modernist.

The reason I don’t like flush & hang footnotes is that it weakens the page. The page now has two distinct elements, the body text and the notes. Should these have a different graphic interpretation? I suppose it depends on your point of view. Mine is no, the page is one of the things (aside form the content) that unites these elements. Just like a widow weakens the page, so too do flush & hang footnotes. So would a full-measure rule, which, unlike a rule below the running head block, will not appear at the same place on the page.

The situation is rather different with endnotes, either end-of-chapter or end-of-book. Here, the problem is to let the reader quickly find the appropriate note & reference it to the text. Running heads giving the page number help (Notes to pages 000-00). So to can flush & hang note numbers/notes.

But this is just one analysis, and nothing new. The real question a designer has to ask is whether the design problem is to work to display differing elements, or to work to integrate them.

FWIW

Charles Ellertson

2007-04-16 13:10:04 • Jackie Frant

On more white space between text and notes:

Once again this depends on how you view the page, should you break it up visually, or integrate it. When I design a book interior with, say, 13.5 points text leading, I will use a minimum of about 19 pts b/b between the last text line and first line of notes and a maximum of about 34 points b/b. That is enough variance to allow for one text line to move, such as a widow, or a text line calling another note, or . . .

That is the other thing that hasn’t been mentioned in this thread; if you are setting just a few pages, you can design tightly. If it really is a 200+ page book, you have to allow for the actual manufacturing.
