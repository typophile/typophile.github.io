How many hours to design a font?

November 18, 2010 • cool_hand

I am sure this discussed already somewhere but I could not find it: How many hours does it take to design a standard font? On average? Let's say caps and lower case and numerals, standard punctuation and accents - the same as you would get in a 256 postscript font. Spaced kerned and basic hinting. Format can be OpenType but not with all the crazy ligatures, alt. characters and obscure accents. Did erik spiekermann once quote 120 hours?
How much does a car cost? There are too many variables involved to give a meaningful ballpark estimate. I've seen posts in this forum stating that upwards of eight hundred hours were involved with the creation of one font. On the other hand, I have managed to knock out a few with complete Latin 1252 and CE 1250 character sets in less than twelve hours each, including hand-kerning each and every possible letter combination. It all depends: Tata Nano or Maybach?

2010-11-27 22:00:27 • Alex Kaczun
@Pablo: I will say that design is also related to execution, and the line is not clearly defined. The refinements you describe are "crafting" and are part of execution. There is a certain amount of authorship in crafting and font production in general, but it is not design. Of course, there can be feedback from crafting that necessitates design changes. "Design" has a broad variety of meanings (rather like those other typographic bugbears "modern" and "style"), but I do think there should be some terminological distinction between inventing a typeface and producing it. If not, we have the bizarre situation where someone who produces a digital revival of a face can be credited as the co-designer. Conveniently, the original designer may no longer be around to question the appropriation. Here's what happened to me: I never contested the digital rights to my first sans, Shinn Sans, which were acquired and digitized by Steve Jackaman (Red Rooster), and he credited himself as co-designer. I had originally created three weights of Roman. Steve interpolated two more, and made slanted italics. He also did a considerable amount of adaptation of my original pen-and-ink artwork, which had all kinds of ticks and ink traps (it had been drawn for Diatronic photocomposition), as well as creating the metrics and kerning. However, I requested that he change the attribution, and he was gracious enough to do so, and is now credited with "digitally engineered". That was my suggestion at the time (2002) although today I would probably prefer "digitally crafted".
