2011-03-21 12:18:38 • dberlow
Ick. Never mind the qualities of the typeface, the descriptions of "African-inspired" typefaces seem to be the last places in the world you're still allowed to spew out racist stereotypes. In a sensitive, more subtle and conversational way, I think using traditional art motifs from Africa could work in a typeface, but in general it's horrendously heavy-handed, full of ideas that Africa is particularly rough, primitive, tribal, dark... African design, espeically these days, is probably just as hypermodernist as anything in Europe. I'm not familiar enough with contemporary congolose stuff to really bring that up, but if I had to pick one font to represent (say) Nigeria it'd probably be Helvetica Extra Bold Compressed, probably (unfortunately) with some sort of metallic effect. Here's a blog with dozens of Nigerian record covers, for some more looks of what's happening in Africa right now: http://www.jaguda.com/category/music/

If the target audience's perception of the Congo is rough and primitive, what is wrong with playing to that signification? It's not as if those qualities aren't quite prevalent in the West, where dumb-ass techno fonts, crappy distressed fonts and godawful script scrawls have a lot of cachet. Anti-corporate cred. And then there's Comic Sans. Consider the process by which we pick "Girly" fonts (sexist), "Fun" fonts for teens (demeaning) and "Upscale" fonts (elitist). How is Neuland different? Its sin seems to be the taint of cliché, which is something only the trade is aware of.
