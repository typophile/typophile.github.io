Nah, that’s a different design. Look at the way the endings of the “C” and “S” are cut at an angle in your sample but aren’t in Coliseum, for one thing. Also, the “E"s have much larger serifs, and the weight distribution on the “N” is different.

The question is going to be whether the font in your sample has survived, i.e. if there is a digital version of it. Maybe the guys with the nice type specimen books can help shed some light on this.

Flipping through Jaspert’s “Encyclopaedia of Type Faces” comes up with a typeface called Etienne, with the statement “probably a Wagner & Schmidt face of 1902”. Etienne looks almost exactly like Runic Condensed, which Monotype released in 1935.

To me the problem is that both Etienne/Runic seem more condensed than your sample, and the likely answer is that there is another typeface that was similar to them that we have not found yet. My books get real skimpy going back past the 20th century.

It turns out I have versions of both Etienne and Runic Condensed. [Etienne is shown as “Enge Etienne” in the Phil’s Photo book, and NovelFonts made a version called “Engine”, which I have]. In this image, I widened both fonts to 175%, and flattened Runic Condensed to 90% of its normal height.

Admittedly, this is ‘fudging’, but in the absence of the actual typeface, which may not have been digitized, I think these are pretty close.
