Custom typeface for books

May 15, 2010 • ncaleffi

It is a common feature nowadays for type designers to create custom typefaces for corporate identity, logos, or newspapers. A less seen option seems to be making typefaces specifically for books/publishing houses. In Italy we’ve had the great example of the Garamond crafted in the 1950s by the Simoncini foundry for Einaudi publishing house (still the most used type in Italy’s editorial market). More recently, type designer Luciano Perondi has designed a a Caslonesque custom face for the Mattioli 1885 edition:

And, even more recently, the same Perondi, a gifted Italian type designer, made another attempt with a Baskerville-inspired typeface for Voland, another Italian publishing house; this typeface will be officially launched tomorrow at the Turin bookfair, but I had the chance to see a copy of a new book set with it and, at a first glance, it indeed looks like a contemporary take on Baskerville. Here is a sample anyway:

Can someone else give more examples of custom typefaces made for publishing houses - meaning for setting long texts in novels, essays, poetry books and the likes?

And how do type designers consider being commissioned for a typeface meant for specific editorial (books) use?

Both Dan Carr’s Parmenides Greek & Christopher Stinehour’s Diogenes Greek were commissioned by the printer Peter Koch for [[http://www.peterkochprinters.com/show.php?bookid=3|The Fragments of Parmenides]].

While Stinehour’s design might be considered a display face for the purposes of your question, it was in fact used to set extended passages of Greek in Robert Bringhurst’s [[http://www.peterkochprinters.com/show.php?bookid=67|The Old in Their Knowing]].

Cf. John D. Berry, [[http://www.creativepro.com/article/dot-font-preserving-ancient-wisdom-with-age-old-printing-techniques|Preserving Ancient Wisdom with Age-Old Printing Techniques]] (2001).

That’s a good point: there is little similarity in Lucerna to Veritas (as opposed to none); the two typefaces are closely related, as the design and thinking of Veritas laid the groundwork for Lucerna. (I amended my article to clarify).

Because the management of Tyndale preferred specific aspects of ITC Giovanni, we chose to emulate key design characteristics of Giovanni, which pleased the management team, and allowed us the freedom to do what we wanted with the rest of the glyphs.
