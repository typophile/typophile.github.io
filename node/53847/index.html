Stroke Weight Consistency - U/C vs. L/C

January 22, 2009 • BrooklynRob

Hello I am attaching an image that shows a comparison between a phrase set in Adobe Garamond, Miller Text, and Baskerville. I love the look of Baskerville, and would like to use it for a project I'm about to start, but I'm bothered by the inconsistency of the widest strokes in the uppercase characters vs. the widest strokes of the lowercase letters. The uppercase letters are heavier, and they seem to interrupt the rhythm of the sentence. It's more obvious in longer settings with more lines. The Garamond and the Miller, in contrast, look much more consistent to me and establish a more cohesive, unified type color as a result. I've noticed this inconsistency in other faces also, such as Caslon and Century. Is this considered "bad?" Is it something that used to be considered okay but has now fallen out of favor? Do type designers try to avoid this kind of inconsistency when drawing a face today? In addition to the issue of choosing a face for my current project, I'm interested to know what the type design community thinks of this. I know Baskerville and Caslon are considered "classics," but this unevenness does bother me. Do you think it's so slight that it's not worth considering? Thanks for any thoughts you have, Rob [img:sites/default/files/old-images/Stroke_Width_6294.jpg]
Good questions. "It’s more obvious in longer settings with more lines." ...and at larger sizes than at smaller sizes. The weight difference is made to take up the additional space of the larger uppercase glyph at smaller sizes, as traditional optics demand. This is not considered bad. It used to be required. Many faces drawn today are drawn to suit larger settings, and have smaller differences. Classic sometimes can mean something that has not been revived fully. Cheers!
