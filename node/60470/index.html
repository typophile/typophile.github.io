It’s definitely not Garamond or Cheltenham. The diagonal center stroke on the e looks a little like Deepdene, but the e in your sample is more slanted than digital versions of Deepdene, and your sample seems to have longer descenders. [img:sites/default/files/old-images/deepdene_6016.gif] Can you proof and scan some more capitals? G, M, Q, R, and W are often helpful in identifying a font.

I think this looks like Lutetia by Jan Van Krimpen. I don’t know of a digital version. The attached sample is from the Jaspert “Encyclopaedia of Type Faces”, but the ‘e’ should appear more angled (like your sample), according to another image I have from the Rookledge Type Finder.

Tracy’s Letters of Credit has the original version of Lutetia, with the slanted crossbar on the e, and tells the story of how it was revised. And I’m pretty sure that Mike is right, that’s what it is. Also Tracy tells the story of how it inspired Goudy to create Deepdene, though Deepdene is very different. But similar enough for Victor to reference it 80 years later!
