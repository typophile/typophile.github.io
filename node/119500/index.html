Question about Cyrillic (Bulgarian)

September 13, 2014 • agisaak

I don’t speak any languages which make use of cyrillic, so what seems sensible to me might seem completely non-sensical to those familiar with the script. To me, the alternate forms associated with Bulgarian seem to give the script a more latinized feel than other cyrillic-based languages, which leads me to the following question. Given the more latinized feel, does it make sense to base the Bulgarian letters Ka and Zhe on the Latin letter K rather than on cyrillic? Also, since El often takes a more lambda-like form, should de be given a more delta-like form? To clarify what I mean, please refer to the following image. The top two rows contain (what I believe to be) fairly standard Bulgarian alternates. The second two rows are what I am tempted to add to a face, but I’m reluctant to do so without the opinion of an actual Bulgarian, or at least someone more familiar with Cyrillic aesthetics.

Addendum: the glyphs in my original post are simply Adobe Text. Glyphs not present in Adobe Text are simply quick-and-dirty alterations of existing glyphs to clarify what I’m asking – they’re not finalized glyph forms by any stretch.

Д д Л л should certainly be triangular-shaped. For the minuscule к and especially ж, you should keep the balance in the white, so that the arms would not look too much open. As for the choice of terminals, ball or serif, it is solely a matter of taste. There are many Cyrillic serif faces which do not use ball terminals in lowercase (but I don’t like them). And you should work more on lowercase в. To make it stop falling on the left side, try stabilizing the crossbar. Also try a more straight back, closer to a stem of Latin lowercase b, without the bottom spur.

2014-10-09 10:13:29 • Michel Boyer

Although I like the works of Oleg Karpinsky and consider them interesting, the Cyrillic part in most of his fonts is highly experimental. It may be nice but not it’s not very practical. For example, he insists that Mellnik could be a corporate typeface. In real life, at least here, in Moscow, Russia it would be difficult to find a client that would like to have such unusual corporate font and use it in copies of text.

Also about the difference between letter З з and digit 3: in many fonts made in Russia last 10 years there’s a trend to make the letter З з with two bowls, while the digit 3 with a top right angle. Some consider this construction of digit 3 archaic, but in Cyrillic texts this differentiation helps. Again, fonts of Oleg Karpinsky are breaking all conventions, this one (3зЗ) as well.
