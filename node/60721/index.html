This campaign is brilliant. Absolut has done so many great ads that there’s a coffee table book devoted just to one series. No matter how cool an Absolut ad is the chances of anyone noticing are next to nil. So this brilliant guy comes along, blatantly riffs on Sagmeister, while Sagmeister is surfing in Indonesia, with an ad about being different. And every design geek on Earth gets incensed and demands that everyone stop and look at what Absolut is doing. And for the next year the design police will be blogging about every Absolut promotion in relation to this scandal. The only sad thing is how many designers don’t realize that they’re being manipulated by advertising—am I the only one who went to an art school that made a semester of critical thinking part off the curriculum?

Frankly I don't see the problem with Absolut's ad. Exploring creating type out of found materials or experimenting with type in nature is hardly patented by Sagmeister nor does he have a copyright on the idea. If Absolut had stolen or ripped off his work, fine, I get it, but they didn't. 2009-08-07 18:55:14 • aaronbell
Both videos comprise sentences with words formed from raw materials in a variety of environments. I don't think it's possible to say whether or not plagiarism is involved. (You could trace the "each sequential shot is a word in a sentence" concept back to the Pennebaker 1965 "video" of Bob Dylan's Subterranean Homesick Blues, or earlier. I made a film a bit like that in 1974, with a number of people each saying a word in sequence.) However, when a creative comes up with something that bears a strong resemblance to well-known currently fashionable/popular work, even if there is no plagiarism involved, it's best to kill that baby, for the sake of reputation. But if the "second man in" already has a substantial reputation, or both have equal reputations, they are not so likely to get called on it. It happens in type design...
