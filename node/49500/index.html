May I quote »Keeping Greek Typography Alive« by Yannis Haralambous:

A good friend and colleague of ours at ENST de Bretagne, Prof. Ioannis Kanellos, has started an initiative against this trend, by giving us the opportunity to modelize the typesetting of a very important collection of books : the Aristotelian Collected Works by the German scholar Bekker of the Academy of Leipzig. The typeface used in these books, and especially in the earlier volumes, is a pure masterpiece of 18th-19th century Greek typefaces. It has practically no ligatures ( besides omicron-upsilon and sigma-tau ) but keeps the manuscipt style of earlier Greek typefaces. The placement of accents is amazingly dynamic : in some cases, an accented letter seen out of context seems to be completely wrong. The same letter seems completely natural in the context of a word. We hope that this typeface, called »Bekkeriana« in honour of Bekker, will contribute to the revival of older Greek typefaces and bring the Greek audience closer to part of the ( typographical ) history of its language.

It is a category mistake to think that Bodoni Greek reflects ‘old Greek typesetting’ any more or less than does the typeface used in Bekker’s Aristotelis Opera. A typeface is Greek because it is designed to set the Greek language. Many of the great type designers, Bodoni included, cut Greeks for academic works which are no more or less authentic than Bekkeriana.

Bekker’s Aristotle editions were printed in Berlin in the early nineteenth century and the use of the particular Greek typeface reflect the tastes of their time and the place of printing (and, perhaps even more importantly, the availability of type) as much as the use of any roman typeface. Bekkeriana closely resembles the cursive Greek cut in the late eighteenth century by Alexander Wilson of Glasgow (which has been digitized by Matthew Carter). Like Bodoni, Wilson was a punchcutter in the romantic tradition.

Bekkeriana reflects ‘old Greek typesetting’ only to the extent that it is an old typeface that was designed to set Greek, and its original is much more likely to have been produced on the shores of the North Sea than the Aegean.
