2006-10-07 20:31:14 • Thomas Phinney
Assuming calligraphy as the spring of typography, in old-style typefaces (used as fonts) text figures are usually the same height as a lowercase x, they are not capitals, nor uncial, they are 'minuscule'. The ductus is the number, direction and sequence of the strokes that makes up a character. These days, some typefaces intended for text even make old-style figures the default numerals. FontShop, for example, usually makes old-style figures the default; Adobe doesn't. But it can vary from font to font.

Most of what I read here makes sense to me. I have seen a lot of the exceptions to which people are referring. The ascending 5 can look pretty cool and -- more to the point -- quite normal in some typefaces. My own opinion is that the figures should be made to blend in pleasantly with the text, and that is much more tricky than just following rules. Really, a lot of non-lining figures look bad because the designer just "made the 4 descend" without considering how it harmonizes with text. They can be educational case-studies about what works and what doesn't. 2006-10-08 17:09:24 • Miss Tiffany
I am guessing the process was this. First a decision to shorten the ascenders and descenders. Second, seeing that the terminals on the 6 and 9 would get crowded with the heavy strokes of the eye of the 6 and 9. Hence the idea of using a pointed terminal to reduce crowding. However, in the 235 you have the ball terminals opposite a thin part, so they don't crowd so much, meaning put too much weight together. And an important part of the 'look' of Utopia comes from the ball terminals on the a and c. The ball terminals on the 235 echo and reinforce this, particularly on text figures, which will go with the lower case. Also if you change 235 you probably have to change them on the lining figures, and further lose harmony with the face. Hence this compromise to achieve the goal of making the text figures more compact.
2006-10-08 17:45:49 • William Berkson
Christopher, why do you think the ball terminals were removed on the 6/9? The theories above are as good as any. I remember talking with Robert about it at the time he made the changes, but I don't remember him explaining that. (Which is not to say I didn't just forget!) What's most interesting to me is that there is an overall improvement -- in that the figures just harmonize with the text more. Utopia is a good case for the this particular thread, I think, because the original figures always struck me as a little forced; they conform to the rules of how non-lining figures should be, but they were perhaps lacking design maturity, and never looked comfortable to me. Robert has learned a lot about such things since the early days when he made Utopia, so I think it's reflected in the changes. The figures are, overall, a bit smaller in scale, and all the ascending and descending figures don't look so stretched out. The proportions of each shape are more pleasant. Anyone designing non-lining figures who thinks, "Just change the 3 to align with the lowercase x-height and descender height" might also take some time to actually look at the results. It's often not so simple. Of course the other thing going on with Utopia, I think, is that it's a sort of transitional-modern, and non-lining figures just don't always work as well with such designs. I use Utopia Std for a newsletter I make, and I think the lining figures work better -- so as much as I love Utopia, I still am hesitant to use its non-lining figures. Probably this is why such typefaces -- used so often for magazines and such -- often have more diminutive lining figures or some hybrid. There is less tradition with these more modern styles, and they often benefit from more experimental approaches, at least for type designers with good eyes who are willing to look and think about it.
