*And by “with” I mean as emphasis or to illustrate semantic difference as opposed to being an alternative style of type.

I don’t know who was the first to combine roman and italic in this way typographically, but the concept of using different type styles to indicate semantic or other kinds of textual distinction, like much else that we think of as typography, was exploited by scribes long before the dates you note. There’s a nice example illustrated in JP Gumbert’s brilliant essay ‘“Typography” in the Manuscript Book’ (Journal of the Printing Historical Society No.22 1993): a manuscript dictionary of difficult Latin words, with the words in uncial and the definitions in minuscule. It was written probably at Amiens c.800 AD.

2009-08-09 03:34:31 • typogirl43

…[civilité] was not a success because it was difficult to read.

Says who? Civilité types enjoyed popularity in France and the Netherlands, and even found use in England. Apart from Granjon’s original civilité types — which were cut in a wide range of sizes, a fact that speaks to their popularity —, types in this style were cut by at least seven other known punchcutters, and there are numerous anonymous examples. Harry Carter and HDL Vervliet’s standard monograph, Civilité Types, catalogues 33 different civilité types, the first of which is Granjon’s mediane of 1557 and the last of which is a copy of Breton’s gros romain (original c.1570) cut in 1811. Most of these 33 types were cut between 1557 and 1601. Granjon’s last civilité was cut in 1587, so that’s a 30 year period in which he found enough success with these designs to make them commercially viable.

In cutting types in the civilité style, Granjon was simply doing what Gutenberg, Aldus and others had done before him: implementing typographically the popular writing style of his day. Civilité types fell out of fashion in the 17th Century during the same period that civilité writing fell out of fashion and split-nib styles became popular.

2009-08-14 16:22:01 • ebensorkin

Bill: John, I think the theory that Civilité was given up because of the change in pen styles has problems. Italic was also based on the broad pen, and survived.

Italic is just what you get if you write quickly: your letters start to slant and they become horizontally compressed. The roman/italic distinction is just the same formal/informal distinction that you get in pretty much any advanced manuscript culture. Since the split nib lent itself particularly well to running hands (you can control the thickness of the inter-letter connections independently of letter strokes, regardless of direction; so, for instance, the connecting stroke coming off the top of an o can be the same thickness as a connecting stroke coming up from the bottom of an i, which is not possible with a translation broad nib connection), it was a natural tool to influence a new kind of ‘italic’.

Typographically, this seems to me a key observation that almost everyone has failed to make: the written models of the Fournier, Baskerville, Didot and Bodoni italics precede the models of their roman types by more than half a century, and precede the cutting of e.g. Baskerville’s types by more than a century. We’re used to the idea of romans preceding italics, because that is the sequence in which they were developed as type in the renaissance. But there’s nothing in the manuscript tradition to say that a formal letter always precedes a less formal or more cursive one.

In Barbedor’s Paris writing exemplar of 1647, there is a fully realised unconnected and serif’d italic written with a split nib. It isn’t until 1695 that we see the first printed engraving of an crudely attempted corresponding Roman – by John Seddon, whom I believe is probably due credit as the inventor of the style –, and another few years before George Shelley perfects it.

Now, you go find yourself a split nib pen and try to write civilité with it.

Peter: But why did they change pens?

They liked the new shapes they could make.

The split nib pen requires more skill than the broad nib, but it is much more flexible – literally and figuratively – and you can create much more varied and dynamic shapes. The calligrapher Paul Antonio, who is a modern master of the split nib, ably demonstrated its capabilities at the St Bride conference earlier this year. Its relative difficulty, of course, inspires virtuosity, which is one reason why penmanship became so greatly admired in the later 17th and 18th Centuries (the other reason being that engraving made it possible to reproduce these letterforms in print, leading to the popularity of writing manuals and exemplars such as Bickham’s Universal Penman, only the grandest of a long line of such books).

2009-08-15 06:53:49 • Peter Van Lancker

Of which types are you thinking? The split nib originated in the 16th Century, and was popularised by Cresci, but that is later in the century.

It is interesting to put the timelines of typographic styles and writing styles from 1470 to 1870 side-by-side, and to realise that at either end of the period there is about a hundred years in which the styles reflect each other, in terms of stroke construction. But in the middle there is a period of two hundred years in which they increasingly diverge. Granjon’s civilité represents an attempt to accommodate the popular writing style of the day within the expanding typographic canon. But it isn’t until the mid-18th Century that Baskerville and others bring type and writing back into alignment again by making types based on an expansion stroke construction.
