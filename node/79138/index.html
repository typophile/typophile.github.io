Hello,

I designed some alternate glyphs to lower case and small caps. Making the opentype feature to swap the regular lowercase to the alternate lowercase was easy. I then made the small caps opentype feature. But when I mix both features I don’t get what I expect. The sc -e- is being swapped with its alternate glyphs but the A (which does not have alternate glyph in sc) also swap with the alternate lc -a-. The result is, I get a mix of small caps, alternate lc and alternate small caps. I do something wrong but cannot figure out. I guess I need to create an aalt feature for swapping the regular lowercase with the alternate ones (with 2 classes with the regular lc and another with the alternate lc) and I also need to create a second aalt feature with classes that include small caps in one class and alternate small class in another. But this doesn’t seems to work either :(. Anyone might have some advice to resolve my problem ? I made a pics to explain visually my problem.

Most likely, you’ve got your features defined in the wrong order.

Also, I’m not clear on why you’re using the ‘aalt’ feature here. I’d use a stylistic set (e.g. ss01). ‘aalt’ isn’t normally a user selectable feature – it’s used by things like the glyph palette for determining which alternates are available for a given glyph.

Make sure your ‘smcp’ and ‘c2sc’ features are defined before your ‘ss01’ feature.

André

http://www.typophile.com/node/17887#comment-109421

Also, the ordering of the feature definitions in FontLab Studio’s OpenType panel is important. If you place your “ss01” or “salt” above “smcp”, then “ss01” or “salt” will take precedence when used together with “smcp”. This means that first “a” will be replaced with an alternate “a”, and then “smcp” may try to replace the alternate “a” with an appropriate alternate small-cap “a”. If you place “smcp” first, then it will be applied first, so first “a” will be replaced with a small-cap “a”, and then your “ss01” or “salt” might try to replace the small-cap “a” with an appropriate alternate small-cap “a”.

Both ways are workable, but you need to write your feature definitions to take into account the order of the features in which they are applied in combination.

Illustrator does. It uses “salt”. InDesign on the other hand uses “ss01”-“ss20”. Both also use “swsh”.

AFAIK, the most up-to-date version of the table that lists which OpenType Layout features are supported in various applications is: http://www.typotheque.com/fonts/opentype_feature_support

Another version of this table, along with a great introductory article for writing OpenType Layout feature definitions is at: http://ilovetypography.com/OpenType/opentype-features.html
