I'm hand lettering a wine label and always seem to end up with to much air inside the "C" in "Château". (Probably cause I'm such a lousy typographer!) So, I'm wondering if you know any examples of a ligature combining the two letters. Obviously, there is no need for a ligature to spell out the word, but I'm playing with it to achieve a decorative look.
Sure. They're mostly in Latin American typefaces, as Spanish considers Ch to be one letter, but Porchez incorporated one into Costa PTF (you can see it in the sample on the page). You can see one in Chaco, and a really fascinating capital CH lig in Lineare Serif. And a Google search of Typophile (which next time you may want to do before posting :-) ) turns up this thread on the same subject.

When I was a kid, back in the early 70s, there were a few two-letter combinations that had the same status as single letters -- in Spanish. Ch was one of them. Ll was another. In Spanish dictionaries, words beginning with "Ch" ("charco") or "Ll" ("lluvia") had their own section following "C" and "L," respectively... But in the late 90s, or maybe in 2000, the Real Academia Española demoted these double-letter characters, and they no longer enjoy the same status as the rest of the letters of the alphabet. Nicely put Tiff! My advice regarding ligation would be to think about the balance of the c as it is written and to think of it in 2 strokes not one. Think also about the heavy serif you sometimes see hanging on a Cap C. It's often there for a good reason - and seeing that reason may translate into a better ligation. But to echo Tiff further get the relationships of the letters right. Look at the white spaces. Look at what reads easily and feels natural vs what feel bullied into shape.
