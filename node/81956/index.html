Write-up: Typeface piracy is so common that it’s almost expected, and knock-off typefaces are very common amongst more popular fonts. To prevent knock-offs the designer has to legally copyright their typeface, and to do this they would have to patent and sell each character of the typeface separately; at about £60 per registration and a typeface usually having at the very least 26 characters, this could cost as much as £1,560 to copyright a typeface of only uppercase or lowercase characters – no punctuation or numbers included. When piracy is so common and easy to get away with anyway, spending this much to prevent knock-offs is quite likely not worth it. I think that trying to put a stop to piracy is a losing battle – not only in the design industry, but in the music, film, software and gaming industries too. In an attempt to lessen the effects of piracy in these industries companies usually try to punish those pirating but it usually has little to no impact on the pirates, and instead makes things difficult for honest customers – in turn potentially turning them to pirating too. This is done through things like increasing prices to make up for lost sales, putting anti-piracy ads which can’t be skipped before legal DVD’s, tethering content to an online account so that it can only be accessed as long as an internet connection is maintained, and many other tactics. I think that I can learn from the mistakes that I see in these actions in other industries; these are all attempts at discouraging piracy; I think that instead these industries have to adapt, and to encourage the more ethical option. The biggest problem and influence on piracy is that everything being stolen is easily copied, and therefore seems value-less. I guess a pretty obvious starting point would be to go back to physical work – which brings me back to the idea I had while looking at dolly’s catalogue by Fayherbe / De Vringer – giving the client what would effectively be a physical copy of the typeface as well as a digital copy (which would be sent via email and also come attached to the physical copy somehow). This way you’re rewarding honest customers and making the ethical option a lot more tempting. I’ll look into ways to give the client a physical copy of the typeface later, but I already have some basic ideas after looking at Fayherbe / De Vringer’s work. Again, thanks for any help!

The biggest problem and influence on piracy is that everything being stolen is easily copied, and therefore seems value-less Fonts don't seem valueless to those who pay for them. It's not especially productive to look at piracy as this absolute moral wrong to be eradicated. More accurately, piracy is a cost of doing business. If you cast your eye around the font industry, you'll notice that just about everyone chooses to keep selling fonts despite the costs of piracy. Put it another way: if your thesis fails to explain observable reality, there's a flaw in your thesis.
