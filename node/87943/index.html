Shopping for fonts

December 1, 2011 • rustystew

I’m working on a materials redesign and have been shopping for fonts. I really like how Hoefler & Frere-Jones really explain the features of their fonts clearly.

[[http://bit.ly/t0qfdN|Here is a link to an example features page if you aren’t familiar.]] The features of a font are going to be important in the final decision because the content is very dense and has lots of different situations that are proving to be difficulties with their current fonts. However, I’m not finding any other font houses that really go through it at that detail for the customer, and would like to be able to have more choices than only what Hoefler offers. Are there any other font house suggestions or websites where I can get the information to make informed decisions about a font before I purchase it or worse, present another challenging font to the client?

… any other font houses that really go through it at that detail for the customer

You’re probably right. Though there are some, I believe.

Most independent font designers (like myself, e.g.) or small studios simply haven’t the capacity to set up programming of a more sophisticated font shop infrastructure. We usually try to compensate this shortcoming by close customer relations.

However, I would welcome a progress in this. Maybe MyFonts will still getting better by time, it is still one of the very few (the only?) free selling platform accessible to everyone. There you have a huge selection – with the more exclusive shops you have more comfort but a narrow choice.

I publish a PDF with all Shinntype faces. At MyFonts, look under the “Gallery” link. However, although I describe the typeface features in detail, I don’t always suggest what usage they might be appropriate for, so you may have to come up with that kind of rationale yourself. Here is a some generic BS that may be helpful: Its letter shapes are the result of a design process that brings together fluidity, clarity and rhythm.

Also, search the Typophile Release section.

If you can, get hold of printed specimen booklets: email the foundries and ask. Or attend TypeCon and AtypI conference and get the goody bag!
