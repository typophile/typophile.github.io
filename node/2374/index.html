2003-12-12 21:23:08 • Dan Weaver
Mark & Dan, you might be interested in this quote from an e-mail I recently received from a long-time graphic designer:

"Yes, old engraving companies in Washington DC still use the pantograph for
engraving. The process is very simple: the alpahabet was machined on heavy
steel plates: upper and lowercase; numbers and all the other stuff
(ampersands, etc.). I wrote out the copy backwards in pencil on a long
continuous paper tape. For each letter of each word and punctuation I would
trace it from the steel plate. There was a little gauge where I set the
leading. To find the middle of the plate I used dividers (which is the
reason I don't measure for anything – I just find the middle and divide in
half) – okay so while I'm tracing the letters and moving the steel plate to
each letter another thing, the stylus is etching the letters lightly on
coated steel or copper plate. There was something I used to size the letters
to, probably another lever. It was a contraption for sure. After the plate
was etched it was dropped briefly into a vat of acid. The brown stuff wash
away and there was a light line for the engraver to follow. So the engraver,
a very old man, popped the loop onto his eye, grabbed his engraving tools
and carved the steel or copper plate. Then the plates would go downstairs to
the plate stamping machine. They would strap the plate to the bottom of this
huge shaft that probably weighed a ton, and let it mash down on the paper.
Voila: engraving. These men I worked with were absolutely great titans of a
long lost age: skilled, patient, accurate beyond the beyond. They taught me
my work ethic."

She also worked for years with Compugraphic film type setting equipment. I was amazed to learn we both attended the same high school in Queens NY for at least one year at the same time (1961), so I guess that tells you neither of us is a 'Spring chicken'.
