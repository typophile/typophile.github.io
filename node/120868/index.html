Determining why my font looks bad

October 27, 2014 • AlyMcF

Hi! Back in the 90s I was really into fonts. I even supported Adobe fonts at one point. SO please forgive me that after ten years of Mommy Brain I seen to have lost most of my font skills.

Here’s my situation: I’m publishing a book through CreateSpace, and of course, I would like the font to look good. Currently when I print from Adobe InDesign CS5 to my Brother DCP-7040 printer, the Baskerville font I’m currently using looks not up to standard, IMO. The regular text looks broken up on the thin lines, and the larger, bold text shows jaggies if you look at it closely.

Now, I don’t need this to look fantastic when I print it to my crappy Brother multifunction printer that probably needs a new toner cartridge anyway. I just need it to look excellent when the actual book gets printed. The workflow is InDesign > PDF > CreateSpace.

So toward that I end I could use help determining: –How can I find the font files on my Mac (running Lion OS) and determine whether I actually have a legitimate quality copy of any given font? I am not set on Baskerville, but I have no idea where any of these fonts on my system came from, as I don’t believe I purchased any of them, they just must have come either with the system or some apps. –How can I then embed that font in the PDF I create from InDesign? –What settings in InDesign can I use to ensure high-res printing, either from InDesign or the resulting PDF?

I understand this is a type forum not an InDesign forum, so if you can only answer the first question, that’s cool.

The typefaces that came with your Mac OS should be foundry quality, so that shouldn’t be an issue. As for fonts from other sources, that depends on the source.

Font Book, which comes with your OS and can be found in your application folder, will allow you to look at all of the fonts on your computer and to get detailed information about them (including where they reside on your hard drive).

When you export your PDF from InDesign, even the “Smallest File Size” setting will embed your fonts (usually just the glyphs you used—a “subset,” not the entire typeface) as vectors, ensuring good print quality (ancient Brother printers excepted). The “High Quality Print” and “Press-Ready” settings have more to do with the images and their resolution output than with the type.

Hope this helps….

In InDesign’s Print dialog box, under Advanced, uncheck “Print as bitmap.”

Also try making your text rich black, even if your printer only has a black cartridge. Tjis is more important when printing from PDF rather than straight from InDesign; Acrobat will print pure black as dark grey on some printers.
