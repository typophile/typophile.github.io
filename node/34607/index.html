How far can you bend a letter before it's no longer recognizable?

June 20, 2007 • BradB

What makes a letter a that letter? How far can you bend it before it's no longer recognizable? I know it's a basic question of type design, since there are only so many ways to draw an "a" or any other letter. (or are there?) I've wondered how it works not only for Roman letters, but for Greek, Cyrillic and Chinese.

2007-06-20 16:17:56 • Nick Shinn
Brad, this fascinating and central question is not really answerable, at least not yet, not empirically. But one thing I did during the first TypeCon in '98 was give a talk about my Trajic notRoman font: http://www.themicrofoundry.com/ss_trajic.html _ During the talk I flashed images of the glyphs and asked for quick letter identification. Very informal and not rigorous at all, but the results -as well as the process- I think were interesting to the audience. They were certainly interesting to me! One other thing: Alfred Kallir has surmised that each letter has "acrocratic" features, which give it its being. Presumably these are what can be bent the least. Our Peter Enneson knows a lot about this stuff - do ask for his insights. hhp

What complicates this is that context plays a large part in a letter's identity. So if you play with the shape of an 'A' in isolation, you're nowhere near testing the limits of what can read as an 'A'. The exact same shape can be clearly one letter in one typeface and clearly a different letter in another.
