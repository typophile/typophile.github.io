The Craft of Design

September 15, 2008 • Feelingrandy

“Craft is that venerable ability to shape perfection by seeing the big picture but also being passionate about the details. Today, details can be hard to find when everything is moving so quickly and computers are able to produce a multitude of layouts, but the truth remains, it isn’t the quantity, it’s the quality.”

In the new Folio magazine, I ask some questions about graphic design style and where design is headed. Feel free to read and comment.

Found online here http://www.foliomag.com/2008/where-has-design-craft-gone Thanks

Craft is essentially about serving people (in contrast to Art, which is essentially about expressing one’s Self). If Craft has gone down in value over the past century, it’s because the Self has become too important. Let’s right the balance by spending less on Art.

2008-09-19 13:21:20 • James Arboghast

Craft is skill, which implies both knowledge and ability. Specifically, it is the skill necessary to realise a thing (shape, give form, design, construct, make real). Art is that aspect of a thing that is extra-utile, i.e. that is not functional but is the focus of our aesthetic sense, our delight in beauty and ideas. Art as a class of object, refers to things whose character is wholly or almost wholly extra-utile. Such objects, like anything else we make, require craft to be effectively realised: it doesn’t matter how clever the artist’s idea or how beautiful his conception if he can’t realise it. Just as art requires craft, everything that human beings consciously make is tinged with some degree of art, some extra-utile quality. Humans are aesthetic creatures, and when we make judgements about how to form a thing, no matter how dully functional the object in question, we include aesthetic criteria. Some objects leave little room for art, of course, because their function really does define their form, but seldom is an extra-utile component entirely absent.

Hrant’s definitions of art and craft simply don’t work. I am sympathetic to the criticism of individual expression contra making things for other people, but the use of ‘art’ and ‘craft’ to characterise the distinction doesn’t make any sense and, worse, plays into an individualist and debased romantic view of art as ‘self-expression’ that is historically unjustifiable. As Bringhurst observed in a recent essay, if your self is the only thing your art expresses, it’s not up to much as art. Art expresses the world and the experience of people living in the world, which is not merely individual experience. If art only expressed the individuality of the artist , why on earth would we – would anyone – consider it important or interesting?
