Font choices for a contemporary art institution

July 24, 2010 • Oilybird

I am thinking about fonts to use in the graphics for a contemporary art institution, and I would really appreciate some advice.

It’s a big old beautiful Victorian building, a public institution in the centre of a city, and the programme ranges from younger artists to mid-career figures. The graphic vehicles include logo, posters, leaflets etc. The design has to communicate authority - even scholarship - but with a contemporary / funky edge. (Think: dandified librarian).

I like the idea of using Dave Farey’s Caslon Black as the main display font, EG for the logo of the institution. But could anyone recommend a less funky serif font that would go with it? Perhaps with regular and semi-bold weights, for body copy and titles?

Or does anyone have other suggestions for a combination of serif fonts: a display fonts for the logo; and another font for the rest?

Many thanks.

Logotypes deserves, IMHO, more attention than just being typeset in a generic font.
2010-07-24 01:14:29 • Frode Bo Helland

Contemporary art calls for contemporary type, but also something that is silent enough to let the artworks shine.

How about [[http://commercialtype.com/typefaces/lyon/lyon_text|Lyon Text]] paired with [[http://commercialtype.com/typefaces/lyon/lyon_display|Lyon Display]] by Kai Bernau? While you’re checking out [[Commercial Type]], you should also pick up a copy of Wallpaper Magazine and see their [[http://commercialtype.com/typefaces/graphik|Graphik]] surrounded by contemporary art and architecture.

Another sans that just begs to be used for something like this is [[http://www.lineto.com/The+Fonts/Font+Categories/Text+Fonts/Akkurat/|Akkurat]] from [[Lineto]].

UK based [[http://www.a2swhk.co.uk/|A2/SW/HK]] have a lot of design work that revolves around contemporary art and might serve as inspiration for you. They draw a lot of their type themselves, something you should perhaps consider for your project as well.

2010-07-24 10:08:49 • charles ellertson

These are very useful comments - thank you all.

Frode, the usual way to approach design for contemporary art institutions is indeed to use fonts that feel ‘contemporary’. Commonly this means using sans serif faces throughout - and I’m keen to avoid that tactic. However, there are obviously more subtle ways of being contemporary - and I will look at the fonts that you are suggesting.

Nick, I have thought hard today about this question of the relation to the period of the building - I agree it’s an important issue. I have ditched Caslon Black. And my latest thought is to use fonts that are a bit earlier in origin: a slightly chunky Modern font for the logo, such as Berthold Bodoni Antiqua; and then a version of Baskerville for the titles and body copy.

(Frode, you are of course right that the logotype should be treated separately, and perhaps shouldn’t be set in a generic font. But at the moment the exercise I am doing is a notional one.)
