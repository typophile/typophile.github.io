Just discovered this very short video. At your leisure:

http://www.guardian.co.uk/artanddesign/video/2010/dec/21/art-of-arabic-calligraphy-video

Took some classes on diwāni in Cairo, it’s a bit more swash than what he does there. It’s the kind of hand you can use to make figurative illustrations or just really fancy shapes. I didn’t get that far, though.

In western calligrahy you can get by with a sheet of paper showing a-z, A-Z, 1-0 and some basic punctuation – in arabic, some of the letters could demand one such sheet just to cover all the necessary combinations.

It’s the traditional tool, a pen made from dried reed: http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qalam – I learned using a metal nib, like in european calligraphy, only slanting the other way.

I’m pretty sure there are no traditional hands in arabic calligraphy that use a brush. There’s some very cool modern stuff though. Check out the work of Samir Malik or Nihad Dukhan, for example.

Some calligraphers work very very slowly, some work very fast and use up a lot of paper before they get the strokes just right – Denis Brown is a (european) example of a calligrapher of the latter kind, and you should check his work out as well :)

I think it’s usually an ideal in european calligraphy that the work should look swish and spontaneous, not sure if that’s as often the case with arabic calligraphy. Just look at the Kufic scripts, for example.

2011-01-21 19:24:22 • ChristerMLB

Most of the Perso-Arabic calligraphers I have seen working write very slowly. This is in part because they spend more time pushing the pen than Latin script calligraphers, and this requires a lot of control.

Note also in the video how he had rotated the page and is actually writing vertically. This is very common.
