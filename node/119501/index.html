John Hudson is probably the best person to answer, but I can offer a few opinions.

The lowercase digamma and stigma are both equal to “6” as numerals, but they are not interchangeable. In modern Greek, only the stigma form would be used; the F-shaped digamma is an exclusively ancient form that is only used when transcribing ancient inscriptions or writing about ancient Greek dialects. For a bit of history of how stigma came to replace digamma, see [[http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digamma]]

Typographically, U+03C2 is not stigma (although Unicode claims it is called that in spoken Greek). It is the word-final form of sigma, and never means the same thing as stigma (U+03DB).

The Coptic letters in the Greek block are a leftover from when Unicode was trying to unify scripts as much as possible, before the Coptic block was created. As far as I know, they are never used in Greek.

I believe the letterforms in question existed in Greek writing before they were appropriated by mathematicians, rather than being created by them. Or at least they were used in typography of Greek texts in the Latin-alphabet-using parts of Europe.

For mathematics, Unicode defines the following small phi characters

1D6D7	MATHEMATICAL BOLD SMALL PHI
1D6DF	MATHEMATICAL BOLD PHI SYMBOL
1D711	MATHEMATICAL ITALIC SMALL PHI
1D719	MATHEMATICAL ITALIC PHI SYMBOL
1D74B	MATHEMATICAL BOLD ITALIC SMALL PHI
1D753	MATHEMATICAL BOLD ITALIC PHI SYMBOL
1D785	MATHEMATICAL SANS-SERIF BOLD SMALL PHI
1D78D	MATHEMATICAL SANS-SERIF BOLD PHI SYMBOL
1D7BF	MATHEMATICAL SANS-SERIF BOLD ITALIC SMALL PHI
1D7C7	MATHEMATICAL SANS-SERIF BOLD ITALIC PHI SYMBOL

Traditionally, French mathematics uses upright small Greek letters, that are to be found nowhere in those ranges. Thus it looks like

03C6    GREEK SMALL LETTER PHI
is to be used as the upright version of 1D711 and 
03D5    GREEK PHI SYMBOL
is to be used as upright version of 1D719.

As for actual usage outside mathematics, in many fonts on my Mac the Greek small letter phi has the expected shape of the Greek phi symbol (like a slashed zero).

In 1984, in the TeXbook, Knuth gives the following table so that $\phi$ in a TeX file produces a closed phi in a mathematical formula and, to get the curled shape, $\varphi$ is to be used.

To my knowledge shapes have not changed since then, except for the letter delta for which Knuth changed his mind at some point in time; cf http://www-cs-faculty.stanford.edu/~uno/cm.html (the file from which I grabbed the picture above must thus be more recent then 1992)*

Today, with XeTeX and LuaTeX and opentype fonts, the xits fontface http://www.ctan.org/tex-archive/fonts/xits/ comes with six fonts, the four standard fonts for text and two fonts for mathematics (math and mathbold). With Microsoft Office 2011 for the Macintosh, Cambria comes with five font files, the four standard fonts and one math font file.

I don’t know the inner workings but I would expect that you are free to choose whatever shape you prefer in the text fonts and whatever other shape is better for the math fonts (that is relevant for upright Greek letters used with French mathematics for instance).

*The metafont source file greekl.mf provided with texlive contains the following comment

% This lowercase Greek alphabet was prepared by D. E. Knuth in December, 1979, % inspired by the Monotype faces used in {\sl The Art of Computer Programming}. % (It has been designed for math formulas, not Greek texts.) % The programs were revised for the new \MF\ conventions in 1985.
and there is no indication of changes made afterwards.
