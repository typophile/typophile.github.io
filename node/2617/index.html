> avenier

Avenir is really more of a geometric face in the vein of Futura, thus the name (which of course means "future" in French).

Kristin, if you are including American Gothic faces such as the ones you mentioned by MF Benton, you could also look at Lightline Gothic and Clearface Gothic. Personally, I like to think of American Gothic faces as a separate (sub-)category to the mainly European Realist Grotesks, not least because most of the American cuts have an old-style three-storey "g". ALso, FontBureau have recently released Benton Sans, a revival of this period. In fact, half the FB catalogue is derived from roughly this period, so you may have some luck there for some of the modern interpretations.
2004-01-08 12:35:56 • refusenik
Kristin,

Have you looked at my Sans Serif Font Identification Guide? There are now over 750 families classified by letterform features of 7 key letters (a, e, g, G, M, R and y).

I will leave the genre classification to others (Grotesk/Grotesque, and such terms are too vague and often misused) and try to use a scheme that doesn't depend on such judgments. Look at how much difficulty experts in this group are having agreeing about which are which. In my opinion a classification scheme should be independent of such labeling attempts, because there are too many 'grey' areas in the digital era fonts.

Refusenik, your multiple suggestions are very helpful. I've become fascinated (again!) with font classification systems through this little project of mine. Are you planning on sharing your own research anywhere? I'd be interested in seeing what you've done.

Yes, it's an evil virus, isn't it?

I think Mike Yanega is absolutely correct to state that it is nigh on impossible to classify typefaces in an academically exhaustive way - there are just too many exceptions to every rule you could possibly come up with.

However, I do think that type classification has a place in every designer's universe, especially if it is conducted in a pragmatic and non-dogmatic manner. It can be a great help in making educated judgements about a particular typeface's suitability for the requirements and context of a given job.

My own research, therefore, was simply a way for me to see what is out there from foundry catalogues both online and offline and to find my favourites in each of the categories. Of course, the standard type classification systems are not very useful, so I had to devise my own system of classification by combining, modifying and extending what is out there. I'm happy with the results, but I have yet to devise a way to share my findings. Perhaps I'll get it together one day...

What about you? Is it "just" a personal hobby or are you doing this for a greater good?

FF Super Grotesk is a lovely typeface, in fact it's one of my faves in its genre.

That said (and as if to prove Mike Yanega's assertion that type classification is entirely in the eye of the beholder) Super Grotesk is essentially Futura (Super Grotesk A) with an alternate three-storey g (Super Grotesk B) and a full set of ligatures (Super Grotesk C), so for me it's basically a geometric face with extra features, but without any obvious connection to classic Realist grotesques such as Akzidenz Grotesk.
