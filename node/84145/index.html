Calibri - soon to be the new black?

July 29, 2011 • JDL

Due to Calibri replacing Arial as MS Word's default, it is no doubt soon to be as omnipresent as the aforementioned. However as it, along with the other ClearType typefaces, do seem to be optimised for screen, what are your thoughts on the suitability of the typeface in a printed environment?
Not suitable. I was asked to use it for a book, and although I recommended against it based on prior experience (*) the client "really really liked it on screen". In print, the letter is just way too fat to comfortably read. (*) This prior experience includes using Verdana for print, using Arial for print, using Tahoma for print, and using Times New Roman for print. The common denominator here is not, let me repeat not, that they are Microsoft fonts "and thus bad", but they were all designed, hinted, and optimized for the screen.

However, Microsoft does seem to be a serial offender in not providing full families for its typefaces. The hoary "four styles is a family" mantra, don't cha know. For sans faces, type foundries generally provide their clients with a range of weights, from Hairline to Black. For serif faces, type foundries often provide both text and display fonts: media-specific alternates. But there is still no Georgia Display, no Verdana Light. Would it not be possible, when a Light weight is rendered at small size on screen, to change the font? After all, "ForceBold" has addressed this kind of issue, via creative engineering, from the early days of PostScript.
