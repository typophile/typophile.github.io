[I posted the following response on the ATypI e-mail list, but figured I’d repeat it here for the sake of future reference.]

I’m glad you asked this question, as I’ve seen the term used to describe different things in the context of typography, writing, letterform design, etc.

  1. Some people use ductus to refer to the flaring or “reverse entasis” in the stokes of a letter.

  2. I’ve also seen people use ductus to refer to the kind of meta-design of a letter – its basic underlying structure. For example, in this sense of the word, a single-storey g and a double-storey g represent the same character, but have a different ductus. Other people describe this kind of basic construction of a letter as its “topology”, a term which I would be more inclined to use.

  3. Finally, I’ve also seen ductus used as a parallel to how the term is used in the context of spoken language – to refer to the unique characteristics of flow, speed, direction, and tone of written text. In this sense of the word, it could be said that variations in ductus are what allow you to identify people by their handwriting.

With all that in mind, it seems that ductus is a commonly confused/confusing term, so one would be advised to use it cautiously. Especially because the varying interpretations are so closely related, it is easy to imagine a misunderstanding of any statements which use the term.

Chris —

What might not have been evident from Michael’s response is that when using a ruling pen for this kind of calligraphy, it is manipulated in pretty much the exact opposite way from what it was designed for and how you’re accustomed to using it. ;-)

Instead of ruling in a direction parallel to the blades to get an even, consistent-width line, for lettering like this you generally run the broad edge of the blades against the paper and work more against the direction of the blades, dragging across the page. Varying the angle of the ruling pen in relation to the surface, more vertical or more shallow, will bring more or less blade surface in contact and yield variations in width.

I don’t know how you held your ruling pen when drafting. I know many people held it with a more or less normal pen grip. But I believe most letterers use a grip more akin to how you would hold a steak knife. This allows a great range of whole-arm movement for angling and rotating.

(And I think most letterers grind down the tips of the blades, so they aren’t so sharp and won’t catch on the page.)

If the paper is cold pressed or rough, you get textured edge effects like in Michael’s “ruling pen” example. If the paper is smooth, you generally get cleaner edges, as in the “Scio me” example (the subtler edge effect there looks more like a result of absorbancy than surface texture).

2011-12-13 21:49:37 • typerror

Briän, modulation refers to the variation in the thickness of lines that make up a letter. A truly monoline letter, in which all the lines are the same thickness, would be the only kind without modulation. In practice, due to the needs of optical compensation, accounting for ink or other media factors, etc., even letters that try to appear monoline require modulation.

In writing, different tools and the varying ways in which they are used produce characteristic modulation patterns, i.e. characteristic arrangements of thinner and thicker lines and transitions between them. Since almost all typographies derive originally from written forms, there tend to be characteristic modulation patterns in typographic letters that echo written forms and, hence, particular tools. Hence, the renaissance roman and italic types have modulation patterns that echo contemporary humanist book and chancery hands as written with a broad nib pen held at a mostly consistent angle; 17th Century civilité types have modulation patterns that include features that echo rotation of the broad nib pen; and 18th and 19th Century neoclassical and romantic types have modulation patterns that echo the dynamism of the split steel nib found in contemporary writing.

Two things, at least, should be evident from this:

  1. Making shapes using particular tools can produce numerous modulation patterns that might be echo’d in type, regardless of how the type itself is made (cut from steel, ‘sculpting’ on the computer screen as you put it);

  2. The independent manufacture of type does not tie it to any one modulation pattern or to any particular writing technique or tool, or indeed, to any written model.

I would add to that the observation that modulation patterns derived from tool and technique produce internal consistency when applied across a range of shapes such as an alphabet, so provide a reliable means to avoid arbitrariness and distracting inconsistency. As Evert Bloemsma showed us, though, there are other methods to avoid arbitrariness. A good question for any type designer wishing to work with modulation patterns not echoing written forms to ask is this: why is Legato so good and so many of Fred Goudy’s types not.

Equivalence between vertical and horizontal proportion is disconcerting and to be avoided; this is a broad cultural phenomenon. Chirography implements the principle that stem contrast is natural (in harmony with perceptual processes) and desirable, whether by nib width or pen pressure.

Furthermore, the quality of the implement informs the typeface as a style, modulating the effect of the contrast in a systematic way, providing a consistent tonality that doesn’t have to be decoded and reappraised letter by letter. Rather like the timbre of a nusicak instrument.
