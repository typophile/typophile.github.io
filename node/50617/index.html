“In these old fonts, too, there were more unusual and tied letters than are now common."

This is on page 21 of Printing Types Vol 2, in a reprint from 1962, the 3rd edition.

What do you suppose he means by this? What I think of are a) contextual shapes, and b) Type with two glyphs on it like a linotype logotype. But It may be that there are other possibilities.

Because I have started to see what looks like contextual shapes in a Plantin face I am especially wondering if this is what he means and if there might be some metal evidence at the Museum Plantin-Moretus.

Eben

as far as I can see, Updike relates with those words to the italics (Kanon cursijf and Paragon cursijf) mentioned some lines before – see page 20. If you look at Ill. 201 (between page 14 and 15), you will find a lot of unusual ligatures besides the usual ones (st sp ct ss st etc and so on) and the swashes in the type.

There is a “us” in line 1: Largus, a “as” in proprias, a “ta” in line 2: monimenta, a “at” in line 3: Illustrat, a “is” in line 9: Sudantis, and I’m sure there are a lot more of those ligatures. Although the example is taken from a 1616 imprint, the type was in use at least from 1575 onwards. There are also a lot of swashy alternate capitals in this type.

So I think Updike speaks mostly of those elements.

wolfgang

2008-10-17 22:02:39 • wmayer
I think you are jumping too far in associating Updike’s ‘unusual’ with specifically ‘contextual shapes’, enamoured as you are of the latter. Linking obviously refers, as you note, to the various ligated letters: ff us et ta ll st etc. as illustrated in your second post. But note also the scribal abbreviation in ferroque in line eight, which doubtless Updike would have classified with ‘unusual’, along with swash forms, and perhaps even the long s. Unusual means ‘unusual to Updike’s time and taste’, of course, not unusual to the makers or users of the original type.
