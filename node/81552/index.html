Typography question-- a book of fragments in italic and roman type

April 25, 2011 • theboycrisis

Could someone help me pin down some typographic terminology? I am trying to describe the form of Galloway and Thacker’s book The Exploit. Here’s a preview: http://books.google.com/books?id=_OsaKlegTbcC&printsec=frontcover&dq=galloway+thacker+exploit&hl=en&src=bmrr&ei=7Za1TbjQM8LdgQfD0Km8Cw&sa=X&oi=book_result&ct=book-thumbnail&resnum=1&ved=0CCsQ6wEwAA#v=onepage&q&f=false

As you’ll see (and as they explain in their note “On Reading This Book”), the first major section has fragments in italics which set off small bits of the text– they’re not headings, because they are part of the main text. Is there a typographic term for this kind of form? Or can I just call them “fragments and micro-essays”? Thanks for any help.

I don’t think there’s an exact term for this. They look a little like pull quotes (usually used to mean repeats of a quote in an article printed in large type in the margin or text column, which the main text flows around, so not exactly the right definition) or block quotes / extracts (still not exactly what you have). The term “deck” is also maybe applicable, though, like the other terms, not quite right (decks are introductory paragraphs printed in display size or special typeface). I might go with “extracts,” though as I said, that has a slightly different definition of its own.

They are summaries, at least according to the introduction, which states, “For quick immersion, we suggest skimming Part I by reading the italicized sections only.”

Isn’t that the way summaries work—defined as “a shortened version of a text that highlights its key points”?

Alternatively, they could be considered as subheadings, in that they introduce ideas which are expounded upon in the immediately following text. Paragraph-length subheadings, that is.

However, on a purely typographic reading (disregarding content), as there is no variation in the body text, other than style, it is merely emphasis, which is generally done by going into italic or bold face, or perhaps a sans. It could be compared with a block quote, which is a similar interruption of body text. So how about “block subheading”?
