Ways type can help a small business

June 13, 2005 • vaaaska

I’m trying to put together a little presentation for a small business. While they don’t really believe this statement, it is something that they have jokingly said to me, “all fonts are the same, it’s just ‘fashion’”. It’s an overstatement, but I’m working with it. Having worked for with for and with small business, I can say that they can shoot themselves in the foot because they are intimidated by designers. “They will charge me $10,000 for a logo I don’t even want”, that kind of thing. We’ve all been there…

But we all know there are certainly other alternatives than the logo itself. And some simple guidelines can do wonders as well. I don’t see those as being so expensive.

Anyways, I have a few general ideas how smart typography can actually help a ‘small’ business…anybody have more they’d like to add?

  • consistency - marketing 101, etc…
  • use of guidelines can save lots of time and enforces consistency
  • brick and mortar approach…start simple and build as needed (more design)
  • knowledge of some of these fundamentals can make you a smarter client - work better with your designer (i would hope)

Additions?

My goal, perhaps it’s fruitless though, is to teach them some basic type terminology, how to think about appropriate usage, what guidelines are, and how to consider choosing a good face. In the end, it will be an e-presentation or whatever the kids call it these days. ;)

Or, is this really a dumb idea and not worth the time. I was thinking it could be useful for other clients in the future…

Merci…v

It’s easy and cynical to say that “all fonts are the same”. One could say the same about anything. “All wines are the same”, and it’s true, they do taste pretty much the same (compared to orange juice or beer) – but you come across as uncouth if you admit as much So the same is true of fonts.

If you say “oh, we won’t bother about optimizing some details”, where do you stop compromising? On the positive side, quality is insidious. But the client doesn’t have to care about every little detail, micro-managing it: leave it to the talent to decide what’s important.

From my experience, small businesses are not particularly marketing-savvy. They usually don’t have a strong idea what differentiates their product from that of their competitors, because they don’t have marketing specialists on staff. All they know is they love their thing and do it right.

So the trick is to explain the benefits of a marketing strategy which positions them positively in the marketplace with relation to the competition. Quality, price, delivery, whatever. And if you can convince them of the uniqueness of that proposition, than you can convince them that unique creative is part of the plan, and unique typography and fonts contributre to the design synergy of their market position.

If they’ll settle for generic fonts and typography, that means they’ll look like everbody else and will be reduced to competing at the lowest cutthroat level, on price – and a small business can’t afford to do that, because large businesses with economy of scale will blow them out of the water.

Unfortunately, it’s not only small businesses that have little interest in branding, let alone fonts or typography.

Nick is on the mark suggesting a business marketing approach to your presentation. The only thing that gets attention is a way to improve business.

But before you start to create a presentation to enlighten the company by yourself, I suggest another tact.

Oddly enough, if the business owners hired an outside consultant to improve business, and one suggestion was to revise branding, they would listen. I know… been there, done that.

I’m not suggesting that they bring in a business consultant, I suggest that you bring in the expertise and plan for success by way of reputable case studies. That way you’re not stuck trying to educate or convince them. You just present the facts that good branding means good business.

Here are a couple of articles to get you started.

An excerpt from INC. magazine on the BrandEquity site: http://www.brandequity.com/news/inc_wm.html The article speaks for itself - “In 2000, the first full year after the redesign, the company’s sales rose 20%. In 2001, when most shoe companies' revenues decreased significantly, White Mountain’s sales again shot up 20%”.

Here’s an article by an award-winning consultant: Five Tips to Quickly and Inexpensively Improve Your Brand Identity http://www.womeninconsulting.org/consultants/articles/improve-brand-identity.html It speaks to consistancy of image, use of type, color, layout, etc. accross all material, web site, literature, ads and so forth. The outcome being an improved perception of the company or product by the target market.

You might try searching for articles about branding from publications within your client’s industry as they might hold more weight.

One or two success stories should at least open the door to a few of your ideas. Even if they decide to start slow, or put off revitalizing their image, you bring them proven ways to improve market share and profits.

Good luck.
