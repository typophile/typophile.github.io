Thanks for the book, David. Please send me your address, and I'll mail you one of my books in return. I've read several of the essays in it, and my first impression is that you've simplified the genesis and history of the types in order to create a grand narrative: myth-building. For instance, there is your assertion that Cooper Black had fallen from usage mid century, to be revived by the Pet Sounds album cover, a notion which I disproved in a Typophile thread. With regards to Futura, you state that, "...les premiers succédanés de Futura apparurent dès années 1950..." which is incorrect, as the face was immediately plaigiarized by foundries in many countries in the 1930s, e.g. Intertype's Vogue of 1930. You explain how DIN typ[eface was "...dédié aux panneaux routiers..." which is incorrect, as it was initially a face used by engineers, dating from well before the first autobahn, and its use, upon adoption by the DIN in 1936, was not especially connected to autobahn signage, which had very few kilometers at that point in time, but was in many media such as type and acetate stencils. Nonetheless, I do agree that the format of the book, focusing on well-known typefaces with samples of their usage and an accompanying essay, is unique and fascinating. For the second edition, please include some Shinn types, and I will go a little easier on my criticism :-)
2009-07-10 19:46:12 • Nick Shinn
Hey Nick, you're welcome. First of all, my purpose has never been to create a grand narrative, but to give basic biographical information about type designers at a time it is becoming increasingly difficult to do so (finding today a detailed (as much as possible) biography of Roger Excoffon or Giambattista Bodoni is almost impossible for a regular student, at least in France, unless you have access to rare and old books in some way). I tried not to simplify too much, but the book is indeed an introduction: I also, therefore, tried not to make it look too much boring. I tried to talk about as many type creators as possible, and also not to go too much over 200 pages. If the book contains some unprecise facts, it is because I couldnt get any more precise information at that time. I am well aware that it contains some mistakes, it is after all talking about a lot of facts that take years to confirm, and I had it read by many people whom you can trust when it comes to being precise and reliable in type history; still, some mistakes went throught the net, though not that much. I can actually see that you focused on the mistakes without talking about the 200 other pages of reliable facts - if you know any other book like this one, please tell me so. I guess the second edition will be much more precise and in many ways corrected, thanks to interventions like yours. But still, I am happy to have produced a book which talks extensively about the life of famous and obscure type designers, about the genesis of type creation (even if you consider it simplified), and which contains at least a crash course on how to choose it well, a starting point into developping your own way to do so. And most of all, the layout is clear, rich in colors, not too boring (hopefully), so that it may attract students and other people into reading it. It's not what you consider a "perfect book", it's too simplified, etc... but this book is not for you. You don't need it. It's not perfect, I admit it, but it's better than doing nothing at all, I guess you will admit this. About Cooper Black: all the sources I checked are pointing to the lack of interest in the typeface between 1930 and 1960, and its revival in the sixties mainly due to this album cover. I couldnt find the thread you mention, I'd be happy to read it. About Futura: you are right, I should write the 1930's. I will correct this in the next edition. About DIN 1451: it is widely accepted that this specific variation of the DIN type system (for engineers indeed, as I wrote in my book) was mainly created for use on railways and road signs, a quick look in Google and in the articles of Albert Jan Pol will show you that - my mistake if I believed it to be true. You obviously have access to information which states the opposite, I'd like to see them. David ps: 8 rue de bretteville, 14000 caen, france, and thanks in advance.

David, I looked in some 1960s Playboy magazines from before Pet Sounds in 1966, and immediately came across the Teachers Scotch campaign, among other uses of Cooper Black. If you want to form believable theories about the popularity and usage of faces, it's important to look through the mass media of an era first hand, and also consider the typographic trade practice, i.e. which fonts are being offered by print shops--if they were putting it in their specimen books, it was because it was being used. Pet Sounds was not actually a popular album, in fact its critical acclaim is probably partly due to that.
