2007-02-09 01:55:21 • ebensorkin

Stephen: This has been discussed numerous times. The EULA offered by the vendor to the buyer is the EULA that the buyer agrees to: if you enter into an agreement with Adobe, it’s Adobe’s terms you’ve agreed to, not Linotype’s.

That’s a major reason to buy from Adobe: they often have better terms than the original foundry. Take MVB Celestia™ Antiqua: license it from Adobe, and you can embed it for print and preview (see this list); get it from MVB themselves, and “if you have purchased a Basic License, you may distribute a PDF containing the subset-embedded Font Software to not more than five (5) computers at your one geographical location.” See more here.

Major players like Adobe and Microsoft manage to negotiate more favourable terms for their clients: that’s why you can find editable embedding versions of fonts in Microsoft products that, if purchased from another vendor might not allow any embedding. That’s how Simon Daniels explained it to me.

See this very recent thread on Adobe’s forums.

And, unless the EULA has a clause saying that the agreement is subject to future unilateral alterations (and, sadly, such EULAs do exist!), the agreement you made when the money changed hands is the agreement you have.

It pays to shop EULAs!

Now can you modify Adobe’s Helvetica? Well, that’s one for the lawyers! Maybe it is easier to go with one of your alternates.
