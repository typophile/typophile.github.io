Most American metal type foundries as well as Monotype, Linotype, and Ludlow, made faces along the lines of “Old English.” If I recall right [my source books are buried at home], Monotype Old English was available in a range of sizes.

ATF made numerous faces in this style. Among them are Typo Text, Waldorf Text, Engravers Text, Wedding Text, Shaw Text, Engravers Old English, American Text, and Cloister Black.

Mostly in the USA these were used for “society” printing: wedding invitations, calling cards.

I’m not sure I’d say many of these are actually very close to true Frakturs. European foundries made in metal some faces closer to true Frakturs, and I think some American foundries did also.

As for your desire to “date these sorts”, perhaps you can proof them or photograph them and put up something for us to look at.

English Monotype’s ‘Old English Text’, series 456 in their metal types, was an adaptation, to use a polite word, of the ‘Cloister Black’ or ‘Cloister Text’ of American Type Founders, which appears in their specimen book of 1906.

![](/images/old-images/ATF Cloister Black 1 - 500_6257.jpg)

This was the ATF version, with which Morris Fuller Benton was associated, of some genuine textura gothic types, like Priory Text, that were cast at that time in US and British typefoundries, mostly originating in the 18th century but based on types used in old English printing.

Making the new type meant ‘modernizing and removing the irregularities of’ the historic faces, as Mac McGrew rightly says. It’s well done, but some people might think that all the life of the original type has been eliminated along with the irregularities. I do. But there doesn’t currently seem to be much choice, either among digital types or metal ones.

Fraktur is a different kind of gothic.
