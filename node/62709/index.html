Setting B&H - All Caps, Small Caps, Small Ampersand or Other?

October 4, 2009 • An Accident

Hello, I'm setting a book of short stories and one story contains two slightly awkward initialisms close together: PhD - which has been discussed elsewhere on Typophile and I think is probably best left in mixed case, no small caps. Then two lines below: B&H - a common UK abbreviation for Benson & Hedges cigarettes (nobody says the full name). Elsewhere in the book I have used small caps for things like ATM (on the same page as PhD and B&H), CDs, and RAF; however, with B&H in close proximity to PhD and having an ampersand, I wasn't sure of the best option. • All Caps maybe be 'correct', but I'm worried that it stands out a little too much. • Small Caps looks odd, particularly with the mixed case PhD two lines above. • Uppercase B and H with a small ampersand (part of the glyph set for Garamond Premier Pro) seems to balance the shape of PhD better, but I don't know if it is correct use of the small ampersand. • Something else. Any ideas? Additional information: • There are no other regular ampersands in the book, only one italic ampersand in a title, and one italic ampersand in an italicised film name. • Justified, Garamond Premier Pro regular, 10pt/12pt [img:sites/default/files/old-images/all-caps_3484.jpg] [img:sites/default/files/old-images/small-caps_5678.jpg] [img:sites/default/files/old-images/small-ampersand_4560.jpg]
I don't see anything wrong with Small Caps B&H. PhD is mixed case but so is every first word in a sentence. I think the consistency between ATM and B&H is more important than the visual balance between PhD and B&H (that said I think Garamond Premier has very appropriate small caps for this situation).
