Thread Revival– Legibility/Readability Studies

July 26, 2005 • TBiddy

I've been wrestling with this idea today after Carl Crossgrove brought up some interesting legibility points. I found this thread that Chris Lozos started a while ago that addresses this: http://typophile.com/node/6026 Since I'm in the process of developing a signage face I'm wondering just what makes a typeface most legible. How do you begin to test this? What letterform shapes have the greatest capacity for cognition? Is Century Schoolbook just as legible on a sign as Frutiger? Sans serif, serif or a mix of the two? How much is lettershape cognition based on country or region of origin? Is there any conclusive proof or studies supporting any of these ideas or theories? I thought it was a shame that this thread fizzled out, I think its an important concern.
I remember reading a study several years back that suggested that the comparative legibility of serif vs. sans serif type was based more on the age of the reader than on his or her country of origin. The study concluded that older readers found serif type more legible because that's what they grew up reading; younger readers found sans serifs more legible. As best I can recall, the study is now more than thirty years old. I am fairly confident that the conclusions are still reasonably valid, but with a twist. The range of typefaces available now has expanded public awareness of the tremendous range of possibilities for expressing individual letterforms, so what is legible to the younger reader of today might well seem like hieroglyphics to his or her grandparents. But we're talking about signage here, and good signs aren't intended to be works of art, but rather useful tools, and really shouldn't require decipherment. Probably the best test of the utility of a typeface used for signage is that individual letterforms be unambiguous. If they pass that test, all other design considerations are superfluous.

Research has been done on both country of origin and age. My poor memory doesn't have the details (perhaps it was the Royal College?). From what I remember of the conclusions it seems countries like England and the U.S. were more prone to read serif; countries like Germany and Switzerland were more prone to read sans better. This fits Nick's "what they grew up reading" theory as well. I have not seen a book that compares a large crossection of legibility literature since Rolf Rehe's 1972 book "Typography How to Make it Most Legible". This book still did not satisfy my curiosity then and very much has happened since. The "Typo" readability issue http://www.magtypo.cz/english.html was a good beginning but I still have not seen work that gets at the whole problem enough to be practical. There has been far more research done in signage mostly by engineers. ChrisL
