Watchmakers use their own numbers as a way of making their watch faces look distinctive. These numbers are in the general style of Eurostile Extended [img:sites/default/files/old-images/EurostyleExtended2Numbers_5762.gif] http://www.myfonts.com/fonts/linotype/eurostile/ The simplified treatment of some of the numbers, such as 3 and 4, is found on some of the sci-fi and techno fonts The MAURICE LACROIX wordmark is loosely derived from Avant Garde and similar fonts. consider for example, Hurme Geometric Sans No.3 [img:sites/default/files/old-images/HurmeGeometricSansNo3AsMAURICE_5221.gif] http://www.myfonts.com/fonts/hurme-design/hurme-geometric-sans-3/ Don

To complete Don’s answer, definitely bespoke and “Eurostil-ish” with a techy vibe, softly rounded and with some unicase treatment (/M and /N at least). Unfortunately to me, it’s not so creative nor original as many industrial manufacturers (including high-end watch manufacturers) often use variations on Eurostile probably thinking it could evoke “sports”.

Some related topics with similar “eurostile” digits:

  • Max Bill’s clock: http://typophile.com/node/95364
  • Alfa Romeo dials: http://typophile.com/node/107411
  • Rolex chronograph: http://typophile.com/node/123362

Having a look to [[http://www.myfonts.com/foundry/Typodermic|Typodermic Ray Larabie]] font collection might worth a shot (Korataki, Uniwars, Venacti, Sui Generis, Good Times, From The Stars…). ‘Techno’ DaFont sections [[http://www.dafont.com/theme.php?cat=303|Sci-fi]] and [[http://www.dafont.com/theme.php?cat=304|Various]] would be very interesting to explore as well.
