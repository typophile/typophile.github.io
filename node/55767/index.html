2009-03-12 04:50:58 • hrant

… if the advance widths are [identical]…

They’re quite different, but the lower case stem widths are almost identical (though somewhat warped), and so is the x-height and ascender height.

“this happens all the time without intent”

I can’t speak for other type designers, but in this case I had already designed a serif type for the paper, which they were using with Franklin Gothic, and they asked me to design a replacement sans which matched that serif in style, and Franklin Gothic in weight and copy fitting. So my intent was quite clear. Of course, I would have rather designed something more original (and I did get that opportunity later with the same client), but the challenge here was to do something close yet with a different personality, and without point piracy.

http://www.kltf.de/kltf_otproduction.htm

The Glyph Tweaker tool allows you to distort existing outlines in a number of controlled ways, such that you can e.g. make glyphs wider while retaining stem weights. It doesn’t do it terribly elegantly like Tim Ahrens' RMX tools, but unlike them it doesn’t require an MM font. It works best for very small adjustments, but even these will usually need some manual clean-up if you are trying to produce nicely finished details. But I’m finding the brute force approach useful, even with the distortions to terminals and joins, for looking at what is revealed about the basic structures of letters if, for example, one starts with a high contrast design like Didot and reduce the contrast so that the stroke are more monolinear, or with a low contrast design like Helvetica and increases the contrast to that of a Romantic typeface.

[More of this sort of thing will probably feature in my lecture at St Bride’s next month.]
