Hello,

I am discussing somewhere else in this forum the justifying of some columns of type in InDesign. It is for a magazine that I make which will be printed on a sheet of oversize A2 paper using a lithographic printing process about which I am as yet uneducated and so cannot describe to you.

The design will be similar to a traditional newspaper with a header and then eight columns of type, each two inches wide, running down the full length of the paper. The gutters will be 1/8 of an inch wide and the spacing and leading will be tight. I am trying to achieve several things:

  1. I want the columns to appear as 8 drips of oil or tar, eight black lines flowing down the paper.

  2. I want the columns justified in a particular way so that it looks as though there are cracks in the black lines (see other post).

  3. I want the layout and density of the type to be reminiscent of a newspaper during wartime, closely packed together so that as much information can be printed on the page. Economical with time, paper, space and copy.

  4. I am interested in the type design of William Morris and the the idea of making the type thick and black so that the printed area of the page is very dark. I like the way that he did not like to use paragraph breaks and when he had to he put symbols into the gap to fill it up, he wanted the page to contain a black rectangles and I want my layout and most importantly my typeface to reference that aesthetic.

  5. Finally and again linked with William Morris, I want to use an old typeface which is suitable for newspaper columns/legibility and appears very black on the page but without being too bold or heavy. It has to be a genuinely old typeface and not an imitation.

I hope that this does not come across as a list of demands, that is not my intention, I would just appreciate your advice and discussion on the topic. Having done some reading into it the two typefaces that seem most appropriate are Baskerville and Caslon. I believe that William Morris' own newsletter, The Commonweal, (the only image I could find) was set in Caslon (but please correct me if I am wrong) and I have read, though cannot find a reference now, that he was fond of Baskerville. It would also be worth mentioning Morris' bookface Golden Type, which can be seen here and was influenced by Nicholaus Jenson.

Anyway, I look forward to reading your advice.

Thank you, Adam.

2007-02-10 16:44:17 • adamburton

Adam, Bookman meets your criteria. Try and get over your bad experience with the swashes and approach the problem logically. Accept the face for what it can do, rather than dismiss it on the basis of prejudice. Bookman Medium is a medium weight slab-serif face, which is necessary to get the blackness you specify. Digital versions of most types from the era you reference are too light, and look like they are under-inked. It’s a horizontally extended face with a large x-height. The extension will give you the rivers (cracks) in justification you want, and the large x-height will maintain density.

So to repeat, based on your criteria, you need a face which is:

  1. Medium weight
  2. Slab serif
  3. Extended
  4. Large x-height
  5. Arts & Crafts Vintage

I doubt that any face other than Bookman Medium fits the bill.
