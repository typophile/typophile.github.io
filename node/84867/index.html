Re-branding my company and looking for type advice.

August 24, 2011 • Eric Kelsey

I started a new job recently and I have the opportunity to re-brand my company’s corporate identity. We are a large construction company in the United States.

The typefaces we currently use are Garamond and Helvetica. They are both beautiful and I love them but everyone else loves them too. I was originally debating whether or not we should even change them but I eventually came to the conclusion that I would like to go with something not quite as common. Do you agree? I heard a good quote that helped me make this decision “Different is always better but better is always different.”

I realize that whatever I go with will not be completely unique to my company. I am fine with that. I don’t want to be original at the expense of being good but I would like to at least find something that isn’t on practically every truck, sign, brochure, and ad that I look at.

Ideally I would like to find a hidden gem that is well done and not overused (if such a thing exists). Something that will have credibility with the most experienced typographers, but also looks good to everyone else.

If anyone has suggestions I would greatly appreciate it. The department is small enough and the company is large enough where budget isn’t really an issue. I have a few ideas in mind that it would be nice to have some feedback on as well. I am coming up with options for three concepts.

• Bold and proven. So far I am leaning towards [[http://www.typography.com/fonts/font_overview.php?productLineID=100034&path=head|Sentinel]], [[http://www.typography.com/fonts/font_overview.php?productLineID=100013&path=head|Knockout]], [[http://www.typography.com/fonts/font_overview.php?productLineID=100019&path=head|The Proteus Project]], and [[http://www.typography.com/fonts/font_overview.php?productLineID=100031&path=head|Chronicle]]. Chronicle will be used sparingly, it will be reserved for occasions where we would like to be fancy such as formal invitations. Think of it as business formal. I realize that Hoefler and Frere-Jones fonts are well known but I have not seen them nearly as much as Helvetica and Garamond.

• Family owned, traditional, values. I haven’t really come up with anything for this one yet.

• Gritty and tough. For this one I was thinking [[http://www.christianschwartz.com/amplitude.shtml|Amplitude]] and [[http://www.christianschwartz.com/farnham.shtml|Farnham]]. The etchings on Amplitude give it characters that I think fits and the strong serifs on Farnham convey a tough, sturdy feeling.

Eric,

The Proteus Project would provide a nice toolkit of strong and varied type. I’ll bet you’ll find a fair number of bold slab serifs in the construction industry once you start looking, though.

As for hidden gems, the rapid pace of type development/release these days means there are dozens of offerings from small foundries that haven’t gotten their due. Look at releases from TypeTrust, Suitcase, Process Type Foundry, Village, A2 Type, You Work For Them, Lineto, among (many) others.
