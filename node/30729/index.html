Convincing the non designer Peons of Corporate America

January 19, 2007 • James Scriven

So ive started on two major identity projects. (major on my scale, which is probably small). . .

Im well versed on the design process, etc. But my real question comes to typeface choice. I have tried to explain liscensing and the general design and language of typefaces all to no avail. Both client have simply said why not make my design out of TNR or Arial because they are “free.”

I sample various fonts from vendors and the clients are displeased with the quality and leave the conversation with an “I dont know” show me something high quality. . . Pain.

Any tips on how to convince a client about selecting a nicely fitting and desinged face? Am I barking up the wrong tree and should I bite the bullet in the name of good design, and hope that Ill have a client willing to explore next time?

My final question is regarding the purchase of a face for an identity.

Would I purchase a simple liscense to use a face for a logo? And then have to purchase multiple liscenses for each respective employee to use for the designed stationary, etc.?

Cheers

“why not make my design out of TNR or Arial because they are “free.”?

It’s a valid question.

In the end, the typeface is an integral part of their visual identity. If they value that, then they should be able to accept the typeface as part of the overall package.

“Would I purchase a simple liscense to use a face for a logo? And then have to purchase multiple liscenses for each respective employee to use for the designed stationary, etc.?”

You’d have your own license to create the logo. Once created, however, the logo is then just a piece of art that anyone in the company should be able to use.

If you are using a specific typeface for the stationery other than the logo, then yes, whoever is in charge of creating and updating the stationery would also need a license of the typeface.

2007-01-19 17:09:56 • aluminum

TNR and Arial are not just favored because they come pre-installed on every Windows and Mac PC, they also perform well on screen at small sizes, and have huge international character sets. Replicating the hinting quality and char set in a commercial or even custom font is difficult and expensive. I’ve heard stories of several very large international companies balking at the cost of extending their shiny new corporate fonts to the required char set and level of hinting required by their “peons”.

So rather than fight the system work with it. Look at the commonly installed fonts say the Web fonts - Georgia is nice ;-) - or maybe Palatino Linotype, and build a logo and identity that works with them.

2007-01-24 01:26:38 • James Scriven

James,

I’m with Joe. But I feel your pain, particularly since it’s hard to spec fonts when you don’t own them. Sometimes it’s a matter of having a sit-down with them and explaining the reason for using a non-typical font. It’s hard to make someone care about the finer points of fonts, so know your battle before you go into it and be armed with a solid reason. Make it matter to them. Don’t explain historical models, explain direct benefits: saving space, market disctinction, legibility, reader impact.

Maybe this is all repetition of stuff you know. I find that large samples printed from the font testers that most foundries offer work the best. Select three, create a compelling 20-second explanation of the benefits of each, get their reaction. Then, repeat with a more narrowly selected bunch. You have the power to control what fonts they see and the power to shape their understanding. Don’t worry about showing them extensive text settings. Sell them on what YOU know, not what THEY see. If need be, try to find a PDF with a high-res sample to supplement.

At least, this is how I go about it.

Oh, also consider differentiating between internal memos and external communications. Sometimes only the marketing group/designers need the unique font, allowing them to use a font from the default set for the majority of their users.
