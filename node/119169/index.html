Just a couple of quick notes. Yes to make the two columns equal in width. No need to align the title inside the columns. In general, margins should be wide enough to frame text and pictures, but narrow enough to keep all together. Also consistency is a good thing. You should also check text alignment (and revise body copy in order to consistently use proper typographic elements such as apostrophes, en-dashes, etc.).
2014-09-03 20:58:25 • riccard0
I know you didn't ask for advice regarding this, but your layout is full of misalignments, inconsistent capitalization and punctuation, etc. Most readers won't care if a margin is a few points off, but these kinds of mistakes will be immediately obvious to everyone. Plus the type in the lower right is totally unreadable on my monitor. To paraphrase what I said in an earlier post, I think you're focusing on the wrong things. For a small layout like this, instead of worrying about grids and the exact width of a column or margin, focus on making it an attractive, easy-to-read layout.

Make it black type on white, because people will want to print it. Saying "computer" graphic design is unnecessary; who does graphic design any other way these days? I would put your software skills at the bottom, not the top. Proofread! You have many misspelled words and incorrect or misplaced punctuation marks. Get a friend to read over it for you. How about putting your name somewhere prominent? You can do better than Helvetica. Helvetica may be many things, but unique it is not. You want to stand out. The dark grey type is hard to read. For bullet lists, align runover lines with the first line of the bulleted paragraph. Make it a standard paper size. Many people have a hard time taking "Hotmail" addresses seriously. Some e-mail services are widely seen as more professional than others. Gmail is a safe bet. Organize the document with subheads: Work Experience / Education / Special Skills etc.
