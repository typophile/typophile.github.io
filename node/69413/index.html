2010-04-15 12:26:25 • furberto

The suggestion that this is Caledonia with Electra caps seems plausible. But I suspect it’s a little more complex than that.

After staring at the example for some time, I can’t help feeling that there are a number of very small, subtle details in the lowercase that aren’t quite right — aren’t quite truly Caledonia.

Here are a few examples.

For one thing, that’s not a Caledonia apostrophe.

The tail, upper terminal, and “swing” of the ‘a’ are not quite right. And something about the stiffness of the spine of the ‘s’ and it’s miserly upper counter aren’t quite true either. (Even taking into consideration the vagaries of printing and the low resolution of the sample.)

The angle and overall sweep of the italic ‘v’ are slightly different.

And there’s something about the ear and fitting of the right side of ‘r’ that isn’t quite true, even if you consider that the sample is possibly phototype (a little on the early side, but possible).

Here, Caledonia on the left in black, sample on the right, gray:

  I can’t be certain without a much better quality example and some sense of the actual type size, but I believe the sample is not truly Caledonia with Electra caps. I suspect that it is a European knock-off done for another foundry/technology.

For instance, I’m aware that the Simoncini foundry was accused of plagiarizing Linotype’s Fairfield in the 1950s. I have some documents where Burke, Griffith, Ruzicka, and a Mergenthaler official are establishing the originality of Fairfield in order to challenge Simoncini’s Fancy.

So, it wouldn’t surprise me at all if this sample were discovered to be a European knock-off.

A closer comparison. I’ve cobbled the Caledonia from an 11-point Linotype specimen, printed on harder stock than the Astrolabio example was likely printed on. (And without access to authentic sidebearing info, so differences in spacing should not be considered evidence.)

  It’s impossible to account completely for differences in reproduction. But I remain convinced that the Astrolabio sample is a knock-off of Caledonia lowercase, not actually Caledonia.

A couple details I missed the first time around: the insistent “correction” in the placement of the dot on the ‘i’, and the ‘t’ is missing a certain characteristic swing.
