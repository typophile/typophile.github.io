  It!</a></b>\r\n\r\n<strong>Reviewed by Poltroon:</strong>\r\nThis book started out
  to show how Dadaism and concrete poetry were foreshadowed in the anonymous nonsense
  texts of type specimen books in the nineteenth century and grew into a much larger
  cultural study of the reading habits, politics and amusements of that era. The type
  specimens (many of which are illustrated) are a springboard to a wider discussion
  of many topics from hermits to the alcoholism of printers. Most of the fun occurs
  in the footnotes (there is even a fake footnote that can be deciphered as a reference
  to PALE FIRE by Nabokov). There are checklists of rare specimens in public & private
  collections in Britiain & the US which can be consulted & very useful family trees
  showing how the holdings of various typefounders passed from hand to hand. In addition
  new light is shed on the earliest American wood types cut for display in the 1830s
  as well as some interesting stuff on the graphic appeal of large type.<br>\r\n\r\n\r\n<b><i>Do

  the name 'great', but this is one. The author brings a combination of eloquence,
  learning, and passion for excellence, and the result is a stunning achievement.
  This work seems to be at the top of everyone's lists of books in typography.\r\n&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
  One of the book's greatest strengths is also a weakness, though. Bringhurst has
  the classical taste in art - a taste that treasures the rare combination of serenity
  and elation, and the works of art that give us this feeling.\r\n&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Following
  this taste, he perceptively separates out the Romantic elements that we have come
  to accept as normal, such as bold fonts, grotesque style sans, wide pages, and the
  em dash. And he revives and highlights the classical views of proportion and design
  in typography.\r\n&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; All of this provides a powerful
  new standard of excellence for book typography - and books are really Bringhurst's
  focus.\r\n&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; The weakness of this book is that the Romantic
  taste does indeed have its place, and this taste is neglected. Not only advertising,
  but also any short material is often best served by typography that is not serene,
  but rather provocative. What would quickly become tiresome in a book can be delightful
  in smaller doses, and a different context.\r\n&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; I share
  Bringhurst's classical tastes, so to me the neglect of advertising and the Romantic
  side of typography is no big loss. The Romantic taste is to be found everywhere
  else, so this book is a rare treasure: an exploration of the classical taste in
  an art that affects everyone.\r\n\r\n<b><i>Do you own this book? Add your review!</i></b>\r\n\r\n</td>\r\n<td
