2006-06-18 11:43:34 • timd

Which I believe translates approximately that he was a professor at the national arts and crafts school at Stuttgart throughout the war.

“Staatlich” is this sense means that the school was publicly-funded, but almost all German institutions of higher learning were/are funded by the state. In this case, the state is not the “State” (i.e., the national government in Berlin), but the “state” (i.e., the local province of Württemberg, now part of a larger post-war German state, Baden–Württemberg).

So the school got, and still gets, its funding from the state government, which was/is seated in Stuttgart. It wasn’t a National school, just a state-funded one. Even the Bauhaus, when it was located in Weimar, had the word “staatlich” in its name (staatliches Bauhaus zu Weimar).

I don’t know anything about Schneidler. I can’t say what his politics were. And to try to make judgments based on three-line biographies is impossible and not really something to recommend. But, since he was employed by the state government from 1921–1948, it is probably safe to assume that, when the Nazis came into power in 1933, they did not have a problem with him. That doesn’t mean that he was a Nazi, it just means that he wasn’t Jewish/Handicapped/Openly-Gay/Gypsi/A Communist/A Trade-Union Supporter/anything else the Nazis didn’t approve of. Maybe he was completely apolitical, or maybe he just kept his opinions to himself. Or maybe he loved Hitler and all that, but just kept teaching instead of joining the party or the SS. We can’t know from just a few lines in a biography. He clearly did not become too involved in the Nazi way of being, because he wasn’t fired after the war, but was able to keep working until retirement in 1948, it seems.

She made a lot of pro-government propaganda. Whether she believed everything in it is less important than this glaring act. She tried to get other Germans to go along with it all.
