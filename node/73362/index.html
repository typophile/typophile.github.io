2010-08-19 16:27:42 • jonathanhughes
What is the idea/purpose behind that? It is to offer different "flavours" of the same typeface. The first occurrence of this dates back to the 1930s, when extra "sorts" containing single-bowl g's were provided with some North American sans faces, to give them a more modern, European look. Similarly, Gill Sans was equipped with a single-bowl g by Linotype in Germany in the 1950s. Today, with OpenType, it is quite easy for foundries to put both single and double-bowl "g"s in a font, the alternate being accessible in a Stylistic Set. "a" is another candidate for this duplication: two glyphs for a single character. The single bowl a and g were also provided to create "early reading" versions of some typefaces, Gill and Plantin, for instance. Hypatia, and my Figgins Sans and Scotch Modern types have this feature for "g", National for "a". There is probably a Typophile thread listing all the faces with this duplicity, but I can't find it.

2010-08-19 18:12:03 • PublishingMojo
A binocular g is used to sell more fonts. Even though a monocular g might be easier to read and appear more balanced, a font designer can get more attention by including a cute binocular g. If an alternate g is not included, the font buyer may become leery of the font designer's ability to make both types of g. Font buyers can become nervous or run away if they don't see a binocular g. The smart font designer always includes a binocular g to ensure steady font sales. Some fonts have no remarkable marketable features: adding a cute binocular g to a boring can add up to big bucks. A fancy R, curly lowercase l or ampersand can also be used to sell more fonts, but nothing beats the incredible marketing power of the binocular g.
