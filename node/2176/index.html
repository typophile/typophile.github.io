Origin of "?" and "!"

November 22, 2003 • Alexander Tochilovsky

I am doing some research for an article on punctuation, more specifically on the question mark and the exclamation mark.

What I know so far is that the question mark first appeared on some manuscripts as early as the seventh and eighth centuries. I have also seen a printed sample from the 15th century (1495) that has a small question mark. I am also aware that by the end of the 18th century the rules of modern English punctuation were more or less established.

What I am really interested in finding out is if the original use of the marks was the same as it is today, and how the shape of the marks developed. (In some ways the shape of the question mark is similar to an inverted semicolon, a mark, seen in the 8th century, used to mark a pause halfway between the comma and semicolon.)

I was wondering if someone could recomend sources, or possible places to start researching the origin of the shapes of these marks and the original meaning they might have had.

One very valuable book here is "Pause and effect: an introduction to the history of punctuation in the West" by M B Parkes (thanks to John Downer for mentioning it on the TD list a while back). I once copied some short sections of it - here's some stuff:
- The exclamation mark first appeared in the second half of the 14th century.
- The book doesn't give a firm date about the first appearance of the question mark (except to say that the Castillian practice of a leading inverted question mark started in the 18th century), but it seems to imply that it was during the middle ages.
- An interesting aside: there was once a special question mark for rhetorical questions - one flipped horizontally ("facing" right instead of left).

Also, I remember some Typo-L members once pointing out that the exclamation comes from the two cap letters "I" and "O", while the question mark comes from "Q" and "O"? Not sure.
