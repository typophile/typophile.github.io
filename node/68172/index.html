I just received a postcard for a series of seminar (that is, three iterations of the same seminar on different days) by Edward Tufte here in the Washington, DC area. I find myself seriously tempted to attend. All of the registration methods requested including the reference code on the mailing label, which was PYR, which leads me to wonder: is FontLab peddling its user list to third parties, or is the PYR/Pyrus resonance merely coincidental?

No idea about the lists. I attended an information design seminar with Tufte in Chicago a few years ago, and didn’t notice any pick-up in ‘third-party’ junkmail in the months after. (I don’t recall the event having anything to do with Fontlab, however).

Good seminar, and highly recommended (once), not least because copies of his four books come with it.

2010-03-09 03:21:35 • Jennifer E. Dahl

They were probably jealous of TheVisual Display of Quantitative Information, which he published himself because he was concerned that its quality would suffer if left to others. So yes, he is self-assured, and the book is beautiful, but that doesn’t detract from the serious nature of the topic. Infographics don’t get a lot of creative respect, so anything which raises its status should be lauded, not sneered at.

I bought Tufte’s poster reproduction of Minard’s chart of Napoleon’s march on Moscow. Printed with spot colour on a nice stock, it rightly respects the original.

As someone who’s actually done a few charts, graphs and maps, I found Tufte’s forthright debunking of misleading infographics, coupled with his high production values, to be quite salutary. And with an interest in the history of graphic design, I was fascinated to discover things like Playfair’s work and the Dr Snow typhoid map in Tufte’s book. IIRC, that stuff isn’t in Meggs.

2010-03-09 04:06:04 • Nick Shinn

I saw him at ATypI in Boston as well, and I went to one of his presentations in NYC a few years earlier.

During the NYC talk he announced to the audience that the only typeface you need for charts, graphs and maps, was Bell Centennial, and that one shouldn’t waste any money either licensing or commissioning any other fonts for that sort of work.

During the break people were having their copies of his books signed, and he would joke with each one, and asked them what they did for a living and then personalize the inscription. When my time came he asked me what I did, and as he was looking down preparing the sign I told him I was a type designer. He quickly looked up and I said, “Thanks a lot!” sarcastically. He quickly looked down signed the book and said “Next”.
