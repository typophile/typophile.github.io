You can try [[tesseract OCR|http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract_(software)]] which is free and still has German-fraktur and Slowak-fraktur trained files. But results can always be improved by training with a custom combination of font, lexicon and orthography. Also the ligatures (tz, sz, ch, ck etc.) need special care.

Thanks Helmut for the the software link. I will check it. As for your ID request, on second thought I don't want to lead you down the wrong path with a rash recommendation based on a short phrase from a body text which could end up being a bad match for the full alphabet. One possibility, if you have access to the old specimen books by German foundries, would be to find your matches in them rather than rely on the subset of 19th century Fraktur body texts that have been digitized. I don't know what percentage of have been digitized, but I suspect it's low. As for available digital versions of 19th century Fraktur body texts, I wonder if you are familiar with the major sources. If not you might check the offerings of Gerhard Helzel http://www.fraktur.biz/ Alter Littera http://www.alterlittera.com/ Delbanco Frakturschriften http://www.delbanco-frakturschriften.de/ Ralph Unger at RMU http://www.dtp-studio-unger.de/ and, with others, at Profonts http://www.profonts.de/shop/index.php as well as the standard Fraktur offerings from the major foundries, which can be readily font by using the "fraktur" tag at Myfonts. Luc Devroye's page on Blackletter fonts http://luc.devroye.org/fraktur.html is an outstanding source for possible matches. Don
