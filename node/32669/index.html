2007-03-30 18:27:53 • Chris Keegan
The problems here (and there are plenty) begin and end with the first lines of your post: "Alright this is a plea for help. I’ve had a terrible time in the past with my typography teacher and shes trying to fail me yet again." Trying to fail you? Come on... Your instructor's comments are all on the mark, but, if anything, she's far too generous and forgiving. It seems to me you haven't asked the basic questions and so you're here looking for others to supply you with easy answers; in short, you're looking for someone else to do your work for you. That's the problem. The rest is just lazy typography. I don't mean to be a complete jerk here, but you seem to want the result without the process, and the process involves study, patience and a sense of exploration. Univers for an environmentally conscious document? Why? It's cold and hard and lifeless: how is that supposed to convey an environmentally conscious perspective? Nature's messy, lush, rich, diverse, yet the design here is the opposite: hard, cold, clean, rigid, narrow. I don't know what your guidelines were: perhaps you had to use this font, perhaps only one font, but even in that case there's plenty of room to create a sense of growth and movement; you've done that well with the elements bleeding off the page, but it's not enough (and it's not done all that well -- the "H" on page 3 is bled proportionally far more than the larger "H" on page 5, etc...). My point is simply that the design looks the way you say you feel, and how can anything work under those conditions? Your content isn't just stuff to rigidly arrange on the page, it needs a voice, a personality, a sense of identity, yet at the moment it's just lines and curves nailed into place. Have some fun, take a few risks, but do so with a firm understanding of typographic rhythm and tone. And if you don't know what I mean by that you need to read Bringhurst 5 or 6 more times.

2007-04-01 18:13:50 • sayerhs
Criticism is only useful when it's grounded in a sense of encouragement; that is, when the purpose is to motivate. The same goes for the ability to accept criticism: the recipient needs to focus only on those aspects which are useful (not that this is a simple thing to do). If either side of this equation goes even a bit screwy, it all becomes personal and pointless, and the first sign that it's gotten personal is when words like "bugger off" come into play. "Bugger off" just means "please refrain from being cruel," but it can quickly also mean "I give up." James, don't let it become the latter. Trust that any advice you get is meant to provide help: sometimes it's easy to see how it's helpful, sometimes it's difficult, but a large part of what all of us do is turn difficult tasks into meaningful solutions, or in this case, uncomfortable feedback into constructive purpose.
