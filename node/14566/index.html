“historical German ck and ch ligs, which i’m informed are no longer relevant!”

Ahm no sir! If you set German texts in Fraktur or any black letter typeface, you have to stuck to the old traditional rules since no one has change it. This means at least the long_s and hopefully the common ligatures. The round r is outdated since the time of Fleischmann. Common ligatures are: c_h c_k longs_c_h longs_longs longs_i longs_t longs_z (germandbls) f_f f_i f_l f_t t_z l_l Even more ligatures are possible. Have a look at the original lead typeface of Klingspor Gotisch (not the Linotype digi version).

But anyway, dump, naive people set even German texts like common texts today - and it looks awful. But were is no standard of a Fraktur codepage or a Fraktur keyboard layout. Only a pseudo standard “Dfr” (Deutsche Frakur) is available - setting up the long_s and its ligatures on false glyphs on a standard codepage.

John, how is the status of the Fraktron project? http://fraktron.sourceforge.net/

    In the Christian Gothic (Imprimerie Nationale, 1902), I’m working on, the complete set of ligatures is:

    ff, fi, ffi, ct, tt, ll

    Note that fl, in particular, is not ligated. Obviously, fj is uncommon in French, but presumably it would follow the same rules as fi.

    Additional variants include: final s, final n, initial v. There are also final-swash e, t, r.

    When I release the font, all these ligatures and variants will be included. I’m still undecided which ones to make optional, though - I think that’ll take more experience using the type in present-day settings.

    Paul, there are literally thousands of scribal ligatures used in the original blackletter calligraphy and in Gutenberg’s own B42 type. The most common is any letter with a bar over it indicating a doubled letter. For an exhaustive survey see Ulrico Hoepli’s Lexicon Abbreviaturarum. Horrible cameraphone pictures of it here: 1 2 3. (Bottle shown for size comparison purposes. Urp.)

    2005-08-14 01:51:39 • paul d hunt

    John, the Dizionario di Abbreviature… is by Cappelli; Hoepli was just the publisher. It is often referred to simply as ‘Cappelli’. Another good reference book for scribal contractions is The Record Interpreter by Charles Trice-Martin. This is more specifically British in its focus, being intended as an aid to deciphering public rolls, parish records, etc.

    [The equivalent volume for Greek is T.W. Allen’s Abbreviations in Greek Manuscripts, which is helpfully included in the more recent collection Abbreviations in Greek inscriptions, papyri, manuscripts and early printed books edited by Oikonomides.]

    There is also what looks like a really useful electronic dictionary, Abbreviationes. I have not used this, largely because it is only available for the Mac, but it seems very promising.
