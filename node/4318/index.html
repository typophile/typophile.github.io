1. If you have large solid areas, always add a varnish -- metallics pick up fingermarks far more easily. (Think of aluminum fridge doors..)

2. Type knocked out of a metallic works far better than surprinting (in other words, the reader's situation is usually with the metallic reflecting a darker part of their environment, so metallics appear dark in usage). In particular, surprinting fine black type on a solid metallic is a bad idea, as it doesn "sit" very well on the surface.

3. Always use coated stock -- uncoated can look ghastly.

4. Straight silver can look a little uneven (it tends to pick up the slightest unevenness in the paper stock) -- splurge on the "mixed metallics" pantone book, and try some subtler effects -- more like "pewter".
2004-04-16 19:17:22 • Nick Shinn
Weight and quality of paperstock is criticial when using metallics.

You need to be aware that metallics have a greater degree of opacity than standard inks. As a result, on lightweight or low-grade paper stocks the will tend to show through on the reverse side of the paper or even bleed through the paper itself.

Your printer will generally advise on the minimum grade of paperstock you should be using.
