Upon reading the initial question, Morris Fuller Benton’s Franklin Gothic family immediately jumped to the front of my mind. While I agree that Century Schoolbook and numerous classic woodtype faces have a quintessential Americana flavor to them, among others, I think one would be hard-pressed to find a typeface that has had such consistant and widespread usage since its creation than Franklin Gothic. Perhaps it’s just my newspaper background shining through, but I think the typeface has an elegant simplicity to it, with a touch of flavor, as well. Its heavy variety, for example, has a way of being firm without the artlessness of Impact or any number of unfortunate slab serifs. Franklin Gothic is undoubtably “new” – at least as of the machine age – and I think its lack of pretense speaks to what I like to think of as one of the best aspects of the American character, assuming there is such a thing. In other words, I don’t think the typeface is trying to pass itself off as something it’s not. It’s just a great, utilitarian sans serif family, and I feel its widespread popularity since its creation near the turn of the century (just as the US was beginning to assert itself as an international power) makes a compelling argument for it as the “most American” typeface.

2005-05-24 09:04:39 • Down10

I have the feeling that the classic American faces have a little more virtuoso “drawing” in them than comparable European faces. This would certainly be true of Goudy’s faces, and you still see it in Berlow, Hoefler (especially his serifed), and of course Robert Slimbach.

An appreciation for the beauty of line for its own sake, rather than as mere servant to the overbearing rigor of a design system. At the risk of generalizing, there is something of use to commercial showmanship in this, and American culture is very commercial.

Having said that, in the digital revolution (despite the contributions of LettError and FontShop, a largely American-driven phenomenon), there has been a strain of hardcore experimentalism, where American typographers and type designers have been every bit as theoretical as the Europeans, and not afraid to produce conceptually driven work quite harsh in appearance. Many of Licko’s designs, such as Matrix and Base, have this quality.

So these two leading proponents, Slimbach and Licko, could be said to represent the dichotomy of American culture, the mainstream (corporate) and the anti-establishment.

Joshua, while the typical Cherokee types show influences of Latin letterforms, it is important to note that Sequoya’s original syllabics looked quite different from the types that were made (by non-Cherokees). His original design for the writing system was much more cursive and less Latinate.

All you Mac OS X users have a Cherokee font on your systems: Plantagenet Cherokee designed by Ross Mills. Most Cherokee types are degraded copies of a single early model, and Plantagenet, to my knowledge, is the first design to re-evaluate the typographic history of this script.

2005-05-30 23:24:16 • Nick Shinn

I think “what is the quitessential American typeface?” is an interesting question but alas it is probably going to sit for eternity without an answer that even a 25% minority says “ahh, yes, that is it.”

The reasons for this are deep, I think. First of all, the typographic cross pollination between Europe and American has been intense since, oh about 1850. So there are a lot of types on both sides of the pond that’ve been deeply effected from the other side, e.g. Chelt and Times. Also: Americans commission many more fonts than anyone else, and have for decades, they’ll buy and use types in ways that’d make other nationals cringe (see or hear Spiekermann), and thus, find new design styles through heavy use, misuse and other routes to discovery (see grunge)…whichis why we’re “here” and they are all still “there”.)

This’s been aided and abbetted by American type companies, from the first Cambridge foundry to Linotype, ATF, ITC, then Adobe and into the modern-est era of HTF, FB and P22, US makers are and have historically been among the most active in the world. As users, our Gringos have short fashion cycles, big wallets and questionable tastes, so all these companies find clients galore.

One whole branch the always leaps out at me as “American” is typewriter faces. This example bundles a lot of what I’ve said above into a, perhaps, clear example. Before ITC Typewriter, no one thought of it; I mean we were just escaping the real typewriter and who’d be dumm enough to release a thing so odd as a “designerly” typeface based on such a hideous machine? The rest is history with the amazing popularity of that series.

And then something happened that no one could have predicted and was totally American: (kind of like Bilbo finding the ring), computers needed monospaced fonts so software developers would only have to count characters, and since they all had the same width, you could write code that laid them out even if you were an idiot ( :o ). You could also read your code more easily if it was “all lined up” nice and neat, (I guess). So a new life sprung up for typewriter faces and again, the rest is history, though unfortunately, Courier is everywhere.

Looping back to the point, type is so American that you can argue forever about Dwiggins, Benton, Stone, Frere-Jones, Chank!, without really getting a hold on the tail of the issue, which is that we’re probably a sick society, but new type makes us feel much better, and that is really American.

2005-06-23 21:02:06 • ktinkel

something as innocent as a font design I agree: there are no national implications inherent to a type face. What we are discussing in this thread is common connotations, norms, stereotypes and perceptive customs that we tend to attach to type faces in discourse and while using type in praxis. There are different approaches to lettering and design, though. These are more likely a result of traditions, “schools”, and common conventions than nationality (although national – and lingual – borders are also borders for traditions, “schools” and conventions in typography). Conclusion: When talking about the relative ‘Americanness’ (or ‘europeanness’ or ‘greekness’ and so forth) of type design, do not forget that this quality is always a second order quality. This is not about being politically correct but about trying to understand how ‘type nationality’ works and how we can handle the original question. That question already had part of my suggested clarification in it: fonts that scream “American” – the type face is expected to ‘scream’, i.e. take part in discourse itself and take a position there.
