PANTONE Swatch Books..

February 23, 2006 • dave bailey

How often do you buy these? I got some older ones for free from work when we were cleaning out the office, getting ready to move to a new building. They’ve basically been sitting in a closet and haven’t been opened really so I figure they haven’t really faded. What’s the real deal here? I know PANTONE says you should buy new ones every year but that seems a bit absurd and extremely expensive. Basically I just want to see what designers in the field do about this matter. I’ve now at least got a decent reference for a complete set: 2 process chip books, 1 uncoated chip book and 1 coated chip book. Circa mid 90’s though…if they’re really not worth having let me know.

I use Formula guides, one is about 5 years old and the other 2. I know I should probably change them, but they still work well enough. And I’ve seen older ones in everyday use at some agencies, some of which have really worn out edges and must be at least 10 years old. If your books have been kept out of sun they should be reasonable, but please note, that some numbers might have changed.

A couple of years ago Pantone published the new solid to process guide, which has very different guidelines for CMYK colour mixing compared to the old guide.

2006-02-23 16:56:42 • dave bailey

I’m still using 20-year old Pantone swatches, and they work fine. However, I’m comfortable with colour, have been to a lot of press checks, and have aged with my swatches.

If you’re starting out, I would say get a brand new set of your own. That way you will never have to second-guess yourself, and will have all the newest colors as well. It’s a large sum of money, but you won’t need to renew for a long time (or perhaps never…) And colour is something you use on just about every job, so the expense will be well amortized – unlike certain fonts!

The Process Colour Imaging Guide (comparing spot with CMYK equivalents) is the touchstone, and the most important tool a designer has for seeing, understanding and controlling how printed colour works.

You may also want to consider looking at TOYO or some other color system, if you’re a “power colour” designer – kind of the colour equivalent of using small caps from fonts of exotic independent foundries.

2006-02-23 17:29:31 • Nick Shinn

If you’re trying to be really precise, the Pantone swatch book you want to use is the same one the press operator is using. Offset press ink will look different depending upon how much is being layed down. Unless you’re providing a sample chip with the job, and the pressman cares enough to try to match it, things are a lot less accurate than people generally think.

It’s important to have swatches that match your paper, coated or uncoated, as well as having spot and process samples. Nick probably gets better results from 20yr old swatches and a good relationship with his printer than people who have fresh books and blindly assume their printers are competent.
