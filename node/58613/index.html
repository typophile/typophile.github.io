2009-06-01 23:30:36 • bowfinpw

It’s Monotype Modern, the favored font for math and math-heavy books at the time. It’s been largely supplanted by Computer Modern, the TeX adaptation of the font. Note that the official Monotype digitization is not optically scaled, but the Computer Modern version is.

This book is about calculus of variations, which I reference extensively in my thesis on interpolating splines. I looked at a bunch of references and decided on Elsgolts instead, largely because his presentation of the Euler-Poisson equation was much more direct than most. Note also that this is a republication of the original 1952 edition, and Dover, as is typical, reproduced page images rather than retypesetting the text.

@bowfinpw: And yes, the “CONTENTS” is a different font, possibly Baskerville. Also note that the boldface is a not-quite matching font, I think it’s one of the numbered “Monotype Old Style” ones, but I’m not 100% sure.

2009-06-02 00:20:38 • dpm

Heh, interesting. Yes, the Monotype digital will be impossibly high-contrast (hairline serifs way too thin) at normal text sizes. However, you may not be entirely out of luck. There’s a second digitization that’s quite a bit more robust. You could use this for text and the Monotype for larger settings, and get fake scaling. However, if you have lots of subscripts or other small type mixed in with the main text, I’d personally find the lack of scaling to be disappointing. I expect it from Times, but not from this font.

Best of luck!
