Typeface Anatomy

February 13, 2008 • Homeschool teacher

I recently began to teach my granddaughter the alphabet and the sounds of the letters and I soon realized that many of the letters that she saw in books were different from the ones taught through my teachers' guides (for writing). Most of the fonts in books are or resemble Times New Roman, while most children are taught to write one of several styles (D'Nealian, Zaner-Bloser, McDougal/Littel, etc) that resemble Arial or Comic Sans MS. The lower case letters, especially "a" and "g" can be very differently formed. She needs to be able to recognize the Times New Roman "a" for reading, but write a different form of "a". I would like to explain and show her more about the anatomy of letters. Is there a typeface anatomy poster out there? If so where can such a poster be purchased? Alternatively, I would be interested in a single page showing typeface anatomy - even one that I can print off the Internet. Thank you for any suggestions.
She doesn't *need* to write the simpler forms of "a" and "g" - those forms are taught because they are regarded to be easier to learn to write. Hmmm. I've been meaning to write a blog post on this, actually.... Cheers, T
