2004-01-09 23:02:23 • Si_Daniels
Si, that BBC article reads more like the stuff of horoscopes -- hard to take too seriously. (Then again, it's just the article and not the actual study.)

I can't help thinking of color psychology as it may relate to the subjective area of type selection. Those color studies are interesting and have merit, but I rarely defer to them as 'truth.' I would have to consider any psychology of typography study in the same way (especially if it's as trite as the suggestion to "use Courier for a resignation letter.")

For example, we've been culturally trained to believe red is the color of love (valentines day, roses). But color psychology suggests that blue is the color of love (private, caring) and that red is the color of power (can't argue with that). We've been culturally trained to recognize blue as corporate (stable, trustworthy). We tend to think of yellow as bright or warm (fresh or sunny) while color psychology suggests yellow represents control or conflict.

So, these readily contradict each other. Both of them may be right in their own way, in their own context. For me, the selection of color (and type for that matter) are all about the context and the audience.
2004-01-10 01:34:39 • Joe Pemberton
Type & Layout: How typography and design can get your message across - or get in the way
By Colin Wheildon

This is a recent work, very practical for art directors who are interested in getting the text read, rather than just looking good.

Wheildon is an ad man, using direct marketing as his laboratory, just as the famous Claude Hopkins (1910s) and David Ogilvy (1950s) did before. What they would do is a split run, and vary one typographic feature between the two (say do one half in serif face, and the other sans) and see which got the better response.

However Nikolaj, you seem to be interested in the emotional effects of typography.
Usually, pyschologists and researchers such as Wheildon look at the mental mechanics of reading, in particular legibility and readability.

And there's a reason for that.
Any piece of typography has so many variables, that it would be impossible to isolate, in an experiment, the emotional effect of:
-typeface
-leading
-alignment
-measure
-size
-page layout
and of course there is the meaning of the text to consider.

This is the preserve of art and design, not science.

I feel your need. It seems that most writing about typefaces discusses:
- historical reference
- description of features/classification
- technology
- politics of design
- cultural theory
- readability/legibility
- circumstance of its design process

I've written a lot about typefaces, but it's very analytical (note my penchant for lists), and that seems to be the norm -- journalistic, commercial. So, which writers let the subject get under their skin?

Jon Coltz, for one, at daidala.com

Any other suggestions?
