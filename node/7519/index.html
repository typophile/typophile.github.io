2004-11-11 02:28:14 • hrant
I think the identification of neoclassical type design as the stage between baroque and romantic is fairly obvious, both in terms of the chronology of Baskerville's work and its relationship to contemporary art, music, architecture, etc. It is precisely the ability to put type design in the broader cultural context that makes this art-historical terminology so much preferable than the specialised terminology of 'transitional' and 'modern', which are meaningless in any broader context.

Robert's real piece of brilliance in this area is, I think, the identification of rococo type design: the final flourishing of baroque before the restraining order of the neoclassical. This has contributed to a new interest in the work of Rosart and Fleischmann, which is very welcome.

2004-11-11 17:41:46 • hrant
Hrant, I think I found another book for you to look at. This is a sweet little small edition, I think. It is called Von der Romain du Roi zu den Schriften J.G. Justus Erich Walbaums: Eine Schriftstudie, which means–"from RdR to the typefaces of J.G. Justus Erich Walbaum; a study." It was written by Gustav Bohadti, and published by H. Berthold AG in Berlin and Stuttgart, 1957.

It has a print of RdR paired with Granjean's true italic, from 1702. And then the same text is printed again with RdR and Alexandre's true italic, from 1712.

Then come copies of specimen pages from Fournier, Didot, and Bodoni. Here's the cool part: it has one print comparing text set in RdR with Fournier, Didot, and Bodoni texts.

And there is also a lot of text about Walbaum and H. Berthold AG, but that isn't as relevant to the forematter about RdR.
