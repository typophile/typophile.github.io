  Warren Chappell, Robert Bringhurst (Contributor)\r\nPublisher: Hartley & Marks;

  $24.95&nbsp;&nbsp;<b><A HREF=\"http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/0881791547/typophile-20\">Buy
  It!</a></b>\r\n\r\n<b>Reviewed by Paul Stanley:</b>\r\nThe title is only partly
  right. Short this certainly is, relative to the ground it covers. But it is hardly
  really a history \u2014 more a sort of chronology with comments. It does engage
  to some extent with technical developments, but it doesn't attempt any real cultural
  or economic analysis. Overall the tone is antiquarian rather than historical, and
  Bringhurst's sententious style grows, to my mind at least, tiresome here. Although
  it does cover territory other than books, it does not do so thoroughly; its focus
  is very much on high typographical culture. It can be read with some pleasure by
  those who are already interested in printing. But I am underwhelmed by it. There
  surely is a fascinating history of the \"printed word\" to be written; but I don't
  think this is it.<br>\r\n\r\n<b><i>Do you own this book? Add your review!</i></b>\r\n\r\n</td>\r\n<td
