Perceptual Illusions

May 23, 2008 • Emma Ford

Following on partially from the posts on http://typophile.com/node/44841 the area of perceptual illusions is very interesting in font design.

By perceptual illusions I mean the phenomenon such as the letter “O” looking smaller vertically than the other characters in the set even though mathematically it may be the same height. So to correct this we increase the vertical size of the overall character from the top and bottom. But by how much.

I have contacted an academic professor who specialises in perceptual illusions and he has said that for any pure perceptual illusion the only way to make corrections is by perceptual judgment. In other words there is no mathematical formula which can be used to correct the illusion unless statistical experiments are conducted and conclusions/judgements are formed using statistics.

Font designers come across this all the time and make judgements to correct these illusions. But even through there may not be an mathematical formula has anyone got any general rules which are used when these corrections are made. Including how to spot potential illusions such as sharp points such as the sharp bottom point on “V” may look smaller than other characters if vertical size is the same.
