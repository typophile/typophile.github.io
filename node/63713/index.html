For an artist, names of pigments work quite well as reliable bench marks. Zinc White is the colour of zinc oxide and thalo blue is the colour of Pathalocyanine. There is no ambiguity because one is referring to colours of pigments that remain consistent all the time.

But in the design world, names of colours, such a coral are too ambiguous and subjective to be useful. There is no common benchmark for what they mean. As a matter of interest, different suppliers of materials for the sign industry have the same or similar names for some very different colours. One company’s sunflower yellow is Pantone 116, another’s is close to Pantone 130 which is more orange than yellow. You can never specify a colour by a descriptive name, you always have to use an industry standard such as Pantone, hexidecimal, RBG or CMYK values. My sky blue could be your tropical blue, but Pantone 299 is always Pantone 299.

2009-11-01 11:20:07 • bendy

Color naming is about as far away as you can get from exact science, which makes it interesting to study on other levels. It’s highly culturally specific too. For example the boundary between green and blue differs between different cultures/languages, or the importance of differentiating light and dark blue. And it’s interesting to see which hues are attributed their own names while others aren’t (German for instance has a commonly used word for a certain very dark gray, anthrazit, which I realized when in Japan other cultures don’t have a special word for).

So go ahead and learn color names, but if you want it to be practically useful, stay within one system, and one that makes sense to use in your specific context. Just be aware it’s going to be one system, arbitrary like all the other ones. No god-becoming involved.
