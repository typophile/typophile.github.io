According to D.B. Updike:

“The end of the sixteenth and the beginning of the seventeenth century was signalized by the appearance of Shakespeare’s Plays, both separately in quarto, and collectively in folio. [ . . . ] The folios were printed in double column, with the text in roman and the names of the characters in italic; and although the prefatory matter was set in handsome type, the body of the work had from a printer’s standpoint no particular typographical interest.” [Printing Types, Vol II, p.129]

England did not have as distinguished a typographic tradition at that time, not to the same extent as Italian, French, or Dutch. I would expect that most Roman types of that period might have been imported from the Dutch. And native attempts at types that weren’t blackletter from that period were generally influenced by the Dutch styles, I believe.

If you’re looking for something digital that suggests the roman types of that era in England, you might look at [[http://www.typography.com|H&FJ’s]] Historical Allsorts or other revivals of the Fell Types (Igino Marini did some, IIRC). Also, someone once made a digitization of one of romans cut by François Guyot and used by John Day (ca. late 1570s, London).

“Also, someone once made a digitization of one of romans cut by François Guyot and used by John Day (ca. late 1570s, London).”

That’s Day Roman. Unfortunately, the font has only the regular weight, no italic, but it’s nicely done and is available for free.

“Day Roman is a digitally redrawn version of what has come to be historically known as the “Two Line Double Pica Roman”, a typeface designed by 16th century French punchcutter Francois Guyot, and used in numerous books between 1535 and 1570, most notable of which are J. Steelsius’s printing of The Bible (1541) and Frisius (1551), Gillis Coppens van Diest’s printing of Erasmus (1544), Georgius (1544), Serlio (1550) and Horatius (1552), and Rotarius’s printing of Livius Brechtius (1549). The type was also used extensively by H. Dunham, and later J. Day, in London (the name Day Roman is simply a reference to J. Day having used the type).”

A bit late to help Sven, but for the record –

Not long ago I studied the text font of the First Folio and found it was most likely set in Pierre Haultin’s second pica roman. (More information on this available at my Typophile blog.) I have one more type family to add to the excellent suggestions on this thread so far: MVB Verdigris, which has an italic inspired by that of Haultin.
