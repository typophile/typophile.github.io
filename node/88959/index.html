Typography trends for 2011: in retrospect

January 10, 2012 • Nick Shinn

While we engage in soothsaying for 2012, would it also be possible to identify the trends of the past year?

**

I don’t know how typical Shinntype is, but here’s our retrospective:

Our best-selling “new’ retail typeface was Bodoni Egyptian Pro, an upgrade to a face that had been selling steadily for over a decade. One of the reasons it did well was because it was e-mail marketed to those who had already licensed the “legacy” version, and quite a few decided to upgrade. (New weights, features and language support had been added.) Also, the slab genre, which had become very popular in the previous decade (notably through the Clarendon revival, the success of Archer, and the influence of The Guardian), was still going strong.

We also had some good corporate sales of Beaufort (another face designed in the ’90s).

Our largest commissioned design was in the rounded sans category—a genre, like slab, that has been popular for a while.

Webfonts has emerged as a new market for us, but not huge. We expect it to grow, but it’s hard to say by how much.

**

As our established catalog continues to perform, I decided to move away from developing large-family faces in generic styles, to making more novel display faces.

**

What has been the experience of other foundries/type designers?

As a layman I would like to through in a question for the forum: In fashion around the world the Grunge genre and the early 90s have now a revival for at least a year already. Does this trend spill over to the graphic design world? Did it impact your type sales if you have something in that category? Did you notice this trend? 2012-01-10 18:06:54 • flooce

Thanks for the post. I am very happy with the sales of display fonts, specifically Egyptian, Clarendon, most of the slab genre, etc. For me, this has been a good year and I hope that it will continue. So to answer your question, yes display fonts of the older sort, i.e., nineteenth century in origin have been good for me and if I see a trend in this direction, it certainly is in the slab genre, if evidence of this can be found at myfonts. I like to divide the type world into display and text. A few can make great or at least very good text faces but this is hard. If they are good they will find an audience, and some on myfonts and other places developed in the past year are quite good. The display genre is important because many designers, I think, are always looking for unusual display types, thus for example the development of very heavy slab or sans serif fonts. I also see a proliferation of calligraphic types, many quite good.

I hope this is an adequate answer. Thanks Nick.

2012-01-13 00:08:37 • butterick

Sorry, I think you and Chris are thinking of Hammersmith Bridge, from where Cobden-Sanderson dumped the Doves Press type into the Thames. The association of St Mary’s Putney doesn’t have anything to do with type, but rather, in a way, with the Occupy movement. At least, that’s the reason for starting my walk there. In 1647, the church was the site of the Putney Debates, where the Grandees and Levellers in the New Model Army debated the constitution of post-Civil War England. It is one of the great moments in radical history, when the notion of electoral suffrage independent of property were given voice, famously in the statement of Colonel Rainsborough:

For really I think that the poorest hee that is in England hath a life to live, as the greatest hee; and therefore truly, Sr, I think itt clear, that every Man that is to live under a Government ought first by his own Consent to put himself under that Government; and I do think that the poorest man in England is not at all bound in a strict sense to that Government that he hath not had a voice to put Himself under.

I figure a walk from one of the early sites of radical political history to the most recent, 365 years later, will have something of the flavour of a pilgrimage. Ironically, the route takes me up the King’s Road and past Buckingham Palace. London’s good at imposing historical significance.
