I’ve read that [[http://new.myfonts.com/fonts/linotype/gill-sans/gill-sans/|Gill Sans]] has no contrast at all. (E.g. [[http://www.mediumbold.com/04_thinking/type/typeterms/index.html|1]], [[http://okaydave.com/projects/saintpetersburg/typography.html|2]], [[http://issuu.com/naomifarrar/docs/sans_serif_11|3]], [[http://www.foamtrain.com/glossary/ccc.html|4]]) Assuming this is correct:

I understood contrast derived from modulation of the stroke width, and I clearly see variations of stroke width in Gill Sans (where the curve of the lowercase ‘n’ joins the upright, or the upper part of the lower storey of the lowercase ‘a’, for example). This inplies to me that Gill Sans must have some (albeit subtle) contrast.

What have I misunderstood?

Many thanks Dave Higgins

First of all, it’s important to realize that:

  1. People simplify to understand.
  2. Mathematical equality of vertical and horizontal thicknesses results in the former appearing thinner.
  3. Things look thicker when they touch, and this effect increases with the acuteness of the angle they join.

And #3 is where modulation (the way I like to define it) kicks in: the thinning of joins to avoid visual “clotting”. In contrast :-), contrast means the difference in thickness (and not just between verticals and horizontals) between various parts without factoring in #3.

If you look at Futura for example, it tries to be totally zero-contrast* but to achieve that the joins (like in the lc “a”) are thinned (modulated) a great deal.

  • Something I actually wouldn’t attribute to Gill Sans.
