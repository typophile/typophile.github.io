{Hey, you knew it was coming…}

Prestige? The number of entries to the TDC2 keeps falling year after year. So I'm sorry to say that I'm being proven right: it has major flaws. In fact three of them:
1) The cost to enter is simply too high for too much of the world. As a result, proven hotbeds of type design talent (like South America - just check out the Morisawa results) get shut out, making the results of the competition less valid. The TDC needs to implement a variable fee based on country of origin, exactly like ATypI membership has now become. The way the TDC uses the word "international" is the way baseball uses "world series"…
2) The judging is too rushed (one weekend!), and this results in the ever-present bad choices among the winners, discouraging entries. Every year there's at least one winner that the bulk of people agree was totally misguided. It's tempting to think that every competition has this problem, but really, in the case of the TDC it consistently seems to go too far.
3) The judging is too "clubby". The TDC insists on flying the judges to and lodging the judges in friggin' NYC. Total waste of money.

The solution to #2 and #3 is exactly what Critique magazine did for their wonderful (and free!) competitions in the late 90s: screen the entries, weeding out the really bad stuff (optional, actually); send the remaining entries to the judges, instead of the other way around; give the judges plenty of time, like a month. Since fonts are adequately shown in one color, on a flexible and flat surface, you would save a lot of money (hence reduce fees) by simply rolling up the 11x17 sheets and cascading them from one judge to the other. Or you could ask people to send five copies to speed things up. Or you could send groups of entries to each judge and get them rotating. And hey, do conference calls if you think interaction among judges is important. ANYTHING is better than donating money to the airlines and hotels.

There are in fact other problems with the TDC competition, but they're not as bad as the three above.

–

Morisawa was super prestigious, and deservedly so - and it even allowed submission by emailed PDF! But yeah, it's over. Bukvaraz was wonderful, and another one would be even better (it could allow itself to start looking forward rather than back). Then there's the Linotype contest, which is now giving out cash prizes (although there are some strings attached). Note that all of them except the TDC are/were free.

–

The ideal competition would have modest (and country-sensitive) entry fees, be held every 2-3 years, and give a choice to each winner of prize+strings or just glory.

Mimosa's? Huh?

Stubborn? Why because I don't agree with you. I have been listening to your suggestions on how to run a competition for 6 or 7 years now, and for the TDC, with our publishing deadline, the idea of sending work around the world to a set of judges so they can have as much time as they need, does not work.

I've run your idea past judges of the competition, and all have had the same reaction… "no way would I want all this stuff coming to my studio."

You seem to think that a weekend is too short a time to judge. After every competition that I have been around, I have asked the judges if they felt they were rushed or needed more time. Every one that I have spoke to has answered the same way. They all felt they had plenty of time.

Of the total number of entries, about half are dismissed by the judges in the first hour. So if you have 120 entries, 60 are gone. Of that next 60 half of those go in the next two hours. It doesn't take four people a month to decide on the remainder.

All the judges have very clear ideas of what is good and what is not. The discussions are very lively, and I wish everyone could eavesdrop because it is a valuable experience. I remember Matthew Carter saying that if he was in town the year following his judging would it be alright if he came by to watch, because he felt it was such an enjoyable experience.

Right there is another reason why it is important to have judges in the same place. They get something out of it as well. I think that bringing someone to NYC and putting up in a hotel for a couple of days is a small honorarium to pay in exchange for their expertise, and commitment.

As to Kyle's question about Hanging Fees. No one has ever refused to pay them. Since the hanging fee requirement is spelled out in the competition rules, they come as no surprise. People whose work wins seem generally happy to abide by the rules they have accepted. Considering the cost of other avenues of advertising and marketing, I consider the hanging fee that covers the cost of being in the annual and the shows to be a bargain, considering the reach they give the work.

If someone did refuse to pay I think it would be up to the circumstances. IF there were special circumstances involved then I think the club and the winner could come to some understanding. But if the person just refused to pay and offered no reason then I would take it in the same way as someone who tries to welch on a debt and they, most likely, would not be included at all.

Every year when someone brings up the TDC Type Design Competition Hrant begins his litany of complaints. I have tried to explain the reason the competition is run the way it is. I have grown weary of doing this every year for the last 6 or 7 years. This is the last I will speak of it. If Hrant thinks he has the answers, I encourage him to start his own competition. It would lovely to see him actually act on his complaints.

> the judges as a body were coming to a decision

Yes, this is admittedly valid - I for one am a big fan of communication. But:
1) It's just another factor that has to be balanced. If the cost of getting the judges together is preventing meaningful competition results, then maybe it should be tossed? Or here's an idea: don't use one of the world's most expensive cities to host the thing! And here's another: do the judging where type people are going to congregate anyway, like at TypeCon.
2) It doesn't preclude doing part of the judging remotely/individually. There might even be an advantage to having a hybrid method, so judges can think both individually and with the benefit of the others - different people think better in different circumstances. So you could send copies of the entries to the judges, give them a week or two to build some preferences and pro/con arguments, then get everybody together to nail down the winners. In this way you have the communication, the shmoozing, and the better results too, without spending more (like you could make the final stage one day instead of two).

There's so much room for some nimble, intelligent improvement, instead of this seemingly monolithic and exclusionary thing we have now, I'm just surprised that the TDC competition doesn't seem to be adapting, even though the drop in participation is the strongest possible warning signal.
