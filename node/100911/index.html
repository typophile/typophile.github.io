@Hrant: To me writing system support should not be related to Basic/Pro/etc. I would like to agree, but if one is publishing Latin, Greek, and Cyrillic versions of a typeface, the standard practice is to not label the Latin as such, eg: Pentimento Pentimento Greek Pentimento Cyrillic Pentimento PE (Pan-European) If I named a font “Pentimento Latin”, those in my primary (99%) market (Latin) would be perplexed and put off licensing it, because most people think “Latin” refers to the ancient language, not the present day writing system. So I presently favor the above system, with “Pro” describing fonts with OpenType features. BTW, check how foundries such as TypeTogether, Rosetta, Parachute do this -- as well as companies such as Adobe, Microsoft, Apple and Linotype -- and of course FontFont, who really upset the apple cart.

2013-02-27 17:25:45 • ferfolio_2
Just a historical quibble: "Expert" came from the PostScript Type 1 days, where encoding was by PostScript name, not Unicode. And given the hard-wired encoding of Mac & Windows, the so-called "expert sets" of the time had most characters mis-encoded (i.e., mis-named). To this day, when I see a font advertised as having "expert" characters, I just assume it's screwed up. You might not want to have "expert" and "encoding" in the same blurb/sentence.

2013-02-27 22:38:30 • George Thomas
I concur with the mal-use of "Expert". This used to be an indicator that the font contained Small Caps, Oldstyle Numerals, and a smattering of ligatures. In those days only "fi" and "fl" appeared in 'normal' fonts; the rest had to be replaced manually. A tedious and error prone process (so the term "Expert" may not have designated the font, but rather the level of experience needed to use it correctly). These days, I expect these extras in what has become known as "Pro" fonts. Adobe makes a clear distinction: its Type 1 fonts that were converted to the OTF format, but did not magically gain (lots of) ligatures or other OT features, are called "Std". Those that do -- irrespective of the number of scripts --, are called "Pro".
2013-02-27 22:45:15 • Theunis de Jong
I wouldn't call them screwed up except from a usage standpoint You know, actually, they were. Standard PostScript was set up by Adobe, and Adobe had a glyph list of proper names for all characters. Not as useful as Unicode's numbers, perhaps, but at least a Standard. But if you remember, the OS systems for Windows and Mac had a hard-wired encoding, so to get a character that wasn't in *their* (different) list, you had to give it the wrong name. Those of us who set type out of a DOS box weren't so limited, but we were few. When you start lying about encodings, everything becomes font-dependent. Sad thing is about half the presses we work for today still think that way, and some still stick to their old Type 1 fonts with mal-named characters. Just how many ways can you lie about what Greek character goes where, about which font had an omacron character in the odieresis slot (so the damn *characters* would change if you changed fonts...) I call it screwed up, and if we're talking about *fonts* rather than *typefaces* I think relevant. Edit: To address the original question (malformed or no), I think Nick is about right. These days, languages covered is the big question, the goodies are just assumed, and can be shown as cute little OT icons where the font is sold.
