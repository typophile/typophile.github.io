2006-05-13 22:23:42 • hrant
Readability is about continued reading, legibility about deciphering individual letters and words. There is a tradeoff between the two. The latter is probably best served by widely-spaced, monoline sans-serifs, for instance, but the former by contrasting seriffed fonts. Here are some rather random notes on what makes for readability: they're hardly the product of research, and may all be nonsense. Think of pattern not rhythm - i.e. robust and taut white shapes, not stem distance regularity. Construction of stems, bowls, counters, cross-bars, diagonals, and how they join matters more than detailing. Limited but clear contrast assists differentiation and adds life, about 3 or 4:1 being ideal. Differentiation within a small range in many aspects of the design will obliterate a consistent chirography; however chirography provides two (broad and pointed pen) admirable starting points for design, and since real calligraphy is never so geometric as, say, Minion one can learn from it especially once one has internalised the effects of miniaturisation. Variety matters especially around the top of the x-height. bpdq bowl-height should be apparently less than o's height. Vary axes for differentiation, both exterior versus other exterior axes and interior versus exterior axes. Vary textures for interior and exterior outlines: rigid and angular in one, rounded in the other. Basic forms may be selectively differentiated by e.g. giving c 16th-century Roman capital serifs, or e a terminal beak, or h a rounder right leg than n. Text lowercase baseline serifs should be only minimally cupped, if at all. The primary purpose of lowercase serifs is to limit lateral interference: they should be long and strong. Near-equilateral triangles are not good enough. Perceived blackness flows into stems from intersections and serifs; smooth colour-gradations require the management of intersections. Thus the horizontals of n and m should meet the left stem immediately below the serif. Distance between stems of n and m should be a touch smaller at the bottom than the top if the characters are not to seem inflated by the open counter at the bottom. There should be no superfluous neural spiking in reading - the text should be transparent. Difficult letter-combinations, such as gg and ra, may be ligatured. x-height for 12 point should be about 55% of h-height, and descender length about 55% to 75% of ascender length. Examples of quasi-Aldine proportions: ----------------16 pt---11 pt---8 pt x-height--------4.5-----5-------5.5 Italic Cap------5.5-----6-------6.5 Roman Cap-------6.5-----7-------7.5 h-height--------7.5-----8-------8.5 p-height--------7.5-----7.5-----7.5 Leading---------1.5-----1.5-----1.5 In small text, small irregularities, white and black, should be relatively larger. Both nearest points and stem-distance matter in spacing. At 12 point, optical space between characters should be about three quarters of that within the characters' counters. At 8 point it should be about seven eighths.

For the typographer, optimizing readability doesn't just depend on the choice of typeface, but on other variables such as size, leading, line length, paper stock, page size, layout hierarchy, margin size, amount of text, and who's doing the reading. Changing any one of these variables affects the others. In the real world, one is faced with deciding between typeface A which looks better at 9.5/13, and typeface B which looks better at 9/14. Unless laboratory testing develops an index of readability for all the typographic variables, it's of no practical use in any but the most banal typographic projects. For standard formats on standard equipment, such as templates in Microsoft Word, running on Vista with ClearType fonts, reading research has no doubt been useful in font development. But if people generally want some research-proven rules to follow about what typefaces are best for original layouts and design, that's not possible.

Ben Lieberman (1967) The Sheedy test is interesting because with a variation in testing, it might be able to achieve the differentiation. Before I explain how, just let me note that the first thing is just to establish experimentally the validity of the distinction: to show that faces that are equally good when it comes to reading a word in a short flash are still different in readability. Here I think the place to start is not with fine distinctions between text faces that are acknowledged to be excellent, but just to establish the difference between more and less readable faces that are equally legible. What is interesting in the Sheedy study is that readers were able to compensate for lower legibility and make the differently spaced words equally readable, as far as speed. As you see in the Lieberman definition, there are two aspects in readability. One is reading speed, which should be easy to test. The other, which I think will give a bigger differentiation between faces, is on ‘ease of absorbing the message’. This I think it the comfort factor that others have already noted they want to see tested. Here’s a suggestion how. There is a certain point when reading when we get fatigued and have more difficulty in absorbing the message. I would suggest that in more readable text the fatigue point would come later than in less readable text. Thus if you have fairly demanding material, you could test comprehension people of a similar education on reading material on an unfamiliar topic. The test could be tried with different people at intervals, and get an idea when the factor sets in. Then try setting it in say 10 point Helvetica with a long measure and little leading, and 10 point Times New Roman (or Minion)—or equalize for x-height. Then do the test to see whether the fatigue factor sets in later with the text face verses the titling face. I am assuming here that Helvetica and Times New Roman will test as equally legible when individual words are flashed. If my theory is right, then you should see a clear, repeatable, differentiation between readability and legibility. For example, you could take the Sheedy study and do the same variation in spacing, and test this time not for speed, but for the fatigue factor. I think you may well get a variation in the fatigue factor with the different spacing even though people were able to compensate and read with equal speed. In other words, my hypothesis would be that the effort to compensate for less readable text is fatiguing. I am quite confident that it is only a matter of designing the right kind of test to capture what many type sensitive people see. Kevin, here is another area to test, which Peter Enneson and I have been e-mailing about. The scrambled letter tests refute the idea that we read by the external shape of words only. However, Peter has argued that while we don’t simply read from the external outline, we do read from the whole pattern of salient letter characteristics in a word—joins, open and closed counters, ascenders, etc. This is different from a process of first identifying letters--even simultaneously in parallel--and then going from there to identify words. Because we have evolved such a phenomenal ability to identify human faces, I find it plausible that at the very least we can recognize ‘th’ or ‘oo’ within a word and map that pattern--and perhaps even many whole words--directly to sound and meaning without going to the intermediate step of identifying the letters. The interesting possibility here to me is that if Peter is on to something, mastering reading may involve a transition from letter by letter reading, to reading by groups of letter patterns, or whole word patterns. In other words, the mastery process would be not a matter of increased skill in processing already identified letters in a particular order, but the actual visual pattern made by the letters. The problem is how Peter’s view could be possibly tested? One idea I have suggested to him is that the same test could be done on children with different degrees of mastery of reading. One idea is to increase letter spacing to the point that it is disruptive to expert readers. My idea is that this would not slow down the beginning reader significantly, as he or she would be doing the letter identification, but the expert reader would be doing less letter identification, and more pattern recognition, and so would be slowed down significantly. The idea here is that the word as ‘pattern of letter features’ is dependent on tighter spacing than simple letter identification. And this test on different levels of mastery would reveal it. As I don’t know the literature on reading, I suspect that this test I am suggesting may well be too crude to be a crucial test. But I think that using different levels of reader skill, or those with reading disabilities, in tests would be the best shot at seeing whether pattern recognition beyond single letters is an important factor in reading expertise.

Almost all of the physiological and cognitive activities involved in reading are unconscious acts.
