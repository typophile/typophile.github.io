September 5, 2003

I just noticed Palatino and Aldus by Herman Zapf were mentioned as great type faces and Herman Zapf suggested as one of the greatest living type designers. And this is interesting: The general consensus in the type community in Sweden (as I've understood it) is that everything Zapf has done is more or less a definite No-no. He is considered too mannered, too calligraphical and, thus, unreadable and unusable. Beside the fact that legibility shouldn't really a matter of taste (but always seem to be) I find this interesting: If this view of a certain typographical touch is restricted geographically, I am curious of what other countries have on their black list. What do YOU think?

2003-09-05 21:47:50 • hrant
I admire FW Goudy for a good many of his types, mannered or not. Many designers will flat out declare "I like Goudy" or "I hate Goudy", referring to Goudy Oldstyle, and not knowing that the man did so many more types than the one he is best known for. The squashed "g" in Goudy Oldstyle was thanks to ATF, whose drawing department head, in hand with the marketing people, ignoring Goudy's vehement protests, deemed that the type would sell better with short descenders. Goudy's original drawings exhibited a much longer descent. Lanston Type Co. of Finland has produced both versions, which you can see on their website. The effect makes it an altogether different type. Type and their designers are too subjective a thing to bother getting defensive about, and certainly defending FWG will do him no good now. It's an accomlishment to leave this planet with one good typeface to a person's credit. We can thank Goudy for a good many: University of California Oldstyle (Californian a few years later by Lanston and in the last 15 years a derivative by ITC called Berkeley). Deepdene, Kaatskill, Bertham, Goudy Modern, Forum, Kennerley Oldstyle, Garamont. That a few of these never reached a national market is due to the shortsightedness of the foundries of the day. The designs themselves are solid and wonderfully structured. One must also consider that the man was making type that would sell in 1925, and didn't have the advantage of being able to extrapolate on designs that hadn't happened yet. Goudy was attempting to break new ground, based mainly on Venetian sources. If he wandered from time to time into an ornamental mood, that, too, was very much in vogue. Look at Robert Weibking's Artcraft (Ludlow, and BB&S). FWG was making new and stylish designs (some perhaps too stylish designs), but he was trying. In addition he did these single-handedly, designing, cutting matrices, and casting fonts for sale. With no other help than that of his wife, he carried on like this for decades, at the same time warding off criticisms of "too commercial, self absorbed, vain and arrogant." Everyone else was busily making the soulless "moderns" that brought the look of book printing in the pre-twenties to a sorry decline. William Morris railed against the poor quality of book-making, as did Eric Gill. Bruce Rogers (who adored Goudy and his work) had no love for the type that was available to a book, designer in 1915. Young designers and students have the benefit of the information he left behind with his "Typologia". Certainly no other foundry at the time was forthcoming with any of the internal secrets of the type-making process. Goudy was and is accused of repeating himself. I think many designers do that. It's in the personal quality of a human creature's line. You can look it up, because I can't recall who it was in Alan Haley's book, "Hot Designers Make Cool Fonts" who stated that every time he drew an "a" it looked the same as the last one. My God, I've gotten passionate about it. I'll try not to let that happen again. Jim Rimmer

> The squashed "g" in Goudy Oldstyle was thanks to ATF Then thank you, ATF! :-) Making the descenders notably shorter than the ascenders in a text face isn't a matter of taste or marketing, it's a matter if "ergonomic" reality, the way language actually uses the letterforms. The "g" is the benchmark because its descender needs the most vertical space to elaborate itself - as a result it suffers first/most. But it has to suffer aesthetically for the benefit of the x-height and ascenders, for the benefit of the font as a little machine, at the cost of its own painterly prettiness. Text fonts are a lot less about aesthetics than some people might like.
