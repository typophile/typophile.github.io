2011-11-09 22:31:54 • oldnick

Interesting, as fancy could mean in a way “done in an elaborate way”, or as well “having a flair of luxury”. For the first category the typeface would need to be rich in details, for the second it needs to have a certain feel to it. So of the sake of discussion I think you need to clarify your definition of fancy.

While for example Jenson does have a lot of details, for some of a general public Bodoni in bold and all caps might read “fancy”, as we know it from the Vogue cover.

To answer with my personal feel, I consider Cycles, by Sumner Stone, as fancy. Following that are Andron, Arno, Williams Caslon, Dante and Sabon. Interesting enough, they are old old style fonts, but I guess that is my thing. However I am not in a type/graphic profession.

2011-11-10 10:22:47 • Andreas Stötzner

I wouldn’t use the term ‘fancy’ to characterize Centaur. ‘Fancy’ connotes, at least for me, ornament or excess. There’s great attention to form in Centaur - which happens to be old-style form -, as there is also in Optima, Frutiger, Futura, Gill Sans, Hans Eduard Meier’s faces, etc., etc. The design of Centaur is in its way, spare - it has a sufficiency, not a superfluity.

You might call Brioso fancy, though, or at least busy (in comparison, Centaur is stately). Ditto for Arno.
