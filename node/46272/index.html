Found this type in an elementary school book first printed in 1969 (German publisher). The type must be from the late sixties. Any suggestions highly appreciated.[img:sites/default/files/old-images/informal_type_1_3851.gif]
This seems to have a strong similarity the Meier's [[http://www.fontshop.com/search/?q=Barbedor&x=26&y=11|"Barbedor"]] italic, but the g is wrong, and other details don't match, even though the feeling and style seem very similar to me. He also did "Syndor", but it is also no more than similar. The calligraphic style also made me think of the Zapfs, but Gudrun's "Nofret" and "Christiana" are only superficially similar. If it's German and pre-digital it may be hard for many of us to locate good samples of typefaces from this country & time. For me, Jaspert is about my only resource for this. Where is Georg? - Mike Yanega

I think I found the answer. I looked in a German book of Schreibschriften and found a font called "Prima", which my notes showed was also included in the Jaspert book. It was a Stempel typeface from 1957, designed by Martin Hermersdorf. The text says that this "pen-drawn type was used by the Moritz Diesterweg educational publishing house in reading primers". I overlooked it before, because it was included in the scripts, and I thought it was an italic face. - Mike Yanega

2008-06-15 03:04:02 • bowfinpw
Nice job, Mike! Just confirming: Global Type also credits [[Karlgeorg Hoefer]] with being the designer of Prima. Martin Hermersdorf was a student of [[Rudolf Koch]]. He reworked Koch’s Offenbacher Schrift, a German Kurrentschrift to be written with the broad-nipped pen. Bavarian schools used this ‘Koch-Hermersdorf-Schrift’ as a secondary script in the 1950s. Hermersdorf also is the author of several books and was a member of the Iserlohner Schreibkreis. As far as I know, he hasn’t designed any typefaces.
