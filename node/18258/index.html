Well, everyone I know seems to define "first-things first" a little differently, and I suspect that there are as many "ideal" ideas for a Type 1 class as there are Type 1 instructors. I can only say what I learned in Type 1. We learned to group some typefaces in five, very basic categories (sans serif = Futura, old style = Garamond, transitional = Baskerville, modern = Bodoni, slab serif = Serifa). We also learned how to properly space letters next to one another, how to space words, and lastly, how to space a few lines. That was it, and actually, that was quite a bit of work. We had a series to accompanying type history/terminology lectures. The stuff went over my head, but later, after having it repeated to me several times over the next semester and a hlaf, I got it, and was glad. btw, my course was offered by a "respected" art school ;-) hope that helps…

Without knowing the approach of your instructor, an initial "practical use" of type could lead to a better understanding of the mechanics provided that the class assignments are critiqued on the basis of fundamental typography and not "graphic design" criteria. That could stimulate class discussion and participation as analysis after the fact with the intent of redoing the assignment with new found understanding of fundamentals, followed by another group critique. As I said, I don't know the planned curriculum or stated outcome of the course (this should be clearly stated in the school's course description), but several instructors have voiced their personal frustrations to me regarding the building of effective and viable typography courses. Incidentally, I have just devised an interesting class assignment for beginning graphic design and typography students. It's in the early stages and not field tested yet, but the intent is to help students develop a sensitivity to type [[color]] and how to use it as an element of page layout.

There are as many ways to teach as there are to learn. Questioning your teacher's lesson plan is one thing, expressing what you hope to gain is another.
