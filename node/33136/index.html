Desktop Publishing Vice or Virtue

April 18, 2007 • jordy

This subject, “desktop publishing,” has been discussed many times before but I am curious about the reactions graphic designers and/or font designers have had to the advent of technology driven design. Has it been good or bad for design, has it allowed a lot of mediocre “artists” to contribute to the field whereas previously they would never have had an interest in the black art of design? Much has happened since I saw an article I think in Publish magazine about the postscript printer, the gist of which was how it would change the graphics field forever. Has the introduction of publishing and designing software like PageMaker, Quark, Illustrator, Fontographer, etc., had an influence on the democratization of design? Obviously yes, but for good or bad? Now that design is accessible to everyone with a computer and the cash to buy the software can’t everybody be a designer as I believe Rudy Vanderlans declared in an issue of Emigre? And if so what does this mean?

2007-04-18 17:19:08 • ill sans

It’s given designers much more control than they had before, but in many cases more control than they wanted. Designers are now expected to know how to set type, where before that was left to typesetting experts. Whether this is liberating or a burden depends on how much control you desire.

It’s also compressed job turn around times to nearly nothing. The idea of getting copy from a client and have a finished design on press a few hours later would have been unimaginable. Unfortunately, this increase in production speed has not necessarily given designers more time to work, it has more often allowed people earlier in the production process to start later. (I have seen this happen over and over any time I’ve figured out a faster way to do something–an in-house stat camera, using FedEx, etc.)

One other thing: You never have to worry about making things square. In fact, it requires extra effort to make things crooked. The opposite used to be the case with paste ups and mechanicals.

2007-04-18 18:39:43 • dezcom

DTP is just the culmination of a trend that started 40 years ago. Compugraphic and others started selling typesetting machines based on the idea that “a secretary” can set the type now. Of course, the people who bought the machines found that the “secretary” had to be trained to the point where she was a typographer, not a secretary, and was no longer willing to work for secretarial wages.

DTP is just the newest tool. It (fortunately) does not confer skill or talent on the user. Heck, now you can design type with a $600 piece of software. That doesn’t mean that the type will be good.
