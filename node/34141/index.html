OpenType feature access to unusual characters

May 31, 2007 • Nick Shinn

This is a question for graphic designers, art directors, and typographers in general.

In my experience, it’s difficult to find special characters in a font. For instance, it was annoying to replace the vertical “hash marks” in a font with the correct, angled inch mark from the Symbol font, but now in OpenType fonts, it’s even harder to find the character in the glyph palette, as there are so many.

Here’s what might be helpful in an OpenType font.

It’s pretty straightforward to use the “Superior” feature for these:

® becomes “registered.alt” – a small, superior version of the character † becomes “dagger.alt” – a small, superior version of the character ‡ becomes “daggerdbl.alt” – a small, superior version of the character

But would the following be useful as “Stylistic Alternates”?

' becomes ′ “second” " becomes ″ “minute” x becomes × “multiply”

  • becomes − “minus” l becomes ℓ"liter"

becomes ☞ “fist”

These seem reasonably intuitive. Is there any reason not to implement them? Anything else that could be added?

I’ve already broached this subject in the “Build” forum, but now I’d be interested in the opinion of font users, rather than font developers.

Nick, despite it’s really a nice idea and I can understand its usefulness, I disagree. Is there any reason not to implement them? Yes, there is: semantics. ‘×’ just isn’t a stylistic alternate of ‘x’, just as you wouldn’t put a ‘∂’ in some ‘stylistic alternate (greek)’ slot of ‘d’—if there was.

Once you’re on a level where you care about correct differentiation of apostroph, second etc., asking for a reasonable use of the glyph table isn’t too much. As Miguel pointed out, ID lets you compose your own glyph set(s). With ID5, you can save your search/replacement routines. And now having GREP, there is not much one could miss, is there?

Run a search on ‘cipher(s), contingently followed by white-space, followed by x, contingently followed by white-space, followed by cipher(s)’ and replace the ‘x’ with the proper ‘×’ surrounded by the desired whitespace. Safe it and you never need to worry about that again. And certainly you wouldn’t want to have some arbitrary linking of glyphs in your font.

(Talking about the glyph table: I don’t want to be ID-specific—there is cross-application PopCharX for the Mac, and a lot of similarly nice stuff for the PC, I’d bet)

Too difficult for amateurs? Possibly, but then the amateur really doesn’t give a doop about the ℓ and its companions (and he/she will be overexerted with the access of stylistic alternates aswell, no?)
