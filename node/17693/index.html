I would write it like this:

Not since the addition of chocolate to milk has there been such an unusual yet tasty combination.

The #1 rule when writing ad copy is to keep it simple. The same applies, or should apply to all kinds of writing, eg:

Good writing is simple and easy to understand.

You don’t need to say any more. Adding words to that sentance or trying to be more specific—or clever—would only complicate it and make it bad writing. Stick to the point. Most attempts to be clever produce self-conscious “writerly” prose. A staggering number of internet users exhibit this trait, trying to mimic what they think is the scholarly authorative tone of professional writers. It’s a plague. Blog owners are the worst afflicted.

Keeping it simple includes a simple approach to punctuation. Where possible leave out commas. I understand why you put those commas where you put them—they seem like they should be there when you analyse the sentance structure. But they don’t really need to be there. Inexperienced writers tend to add commas when reading back what they’ve written at a slow, analytical speed. The falacy is that people reading your text don’t read at that speed; they read as fast as possible because they’ve left the oven on at home and need to catch the 5:17PM bus.

“Seemingly” is best left out for 3 very good reasons. 1) It complicates the sentance. 2) Ad copy can’t be ambiguous. The product either is an unusual yet tasty combination, or it isn’t. No two ways about it. To convince readers and make them absorb the message you must be decisive. 3) “Seemingly” adds another word to the count. A slogan has to be breif.

em dashes could be used as they would draw more attention to the word tasty.

Depends. Being a big user of the em dash I often recommend them in favour of commas. Will this be set in a text font using text setting conventions, or in display type using display typography?

Not since the addition of chocolate to milk has there been such an unusual—yet tasty—combination.

That is more effective, but you end up hanging on “combination”. Here’s the solution:

Not since the addition of chocolate to milk has there been such an unusual—yet tasty combination.

An alternative is to use color and size—make “tasty” bigger & louder, sans em dashes.

You could also spice up the writing to give it context and make it less technical, more colloqial. Who added milk to chocolate?

Not since they added chocolate to milk has there been such an unusual—yet tasty combination.

The ubiquitous, ever-present, anonymous “they”.

What else could you do? Bung an ellipsis on the end to add a sense of mystery & intrigue, the air of inuendo. There’s more to this icy-cream ;-) than the ad reveals, but if you don’t buy one you’ll never find out. Good ad copy is always ambiguous in some way. What you don’t say is as important, sometimes more important than what you do say.

Not since they added chocolate to milk has there been such an unusual—yet tasty combination…

Acceptable to who, and in what kind of publication? In a technical paper maybe. In a magazine article or newspaper em dashes are less used than commas by editors. In a novel em dashes are widely used and accepted by readers.
