Scale is too small and somewhat blurred for me to say for certain, but from what I can see this looks like it was set in a 19th century style of typeface called Scotch, originally designed by Richard Austin for Alexander Wilson and Son in Glasgow. 2015-04-25 14:15:38 • Joostmarcellis
In 1889 newspapers were parsimonious with space. The article was probably printed in a nine point or smaller lining Roman, which is the same Scotch Roman under another name. The term lining means that descenders are short, which is a feature of Scotch Roman. The digital versions suggested capture the letterforms in the newspaper article, but are based on larger point sizes. Note from this compilation of a lining Roman cut in the late 19th century how the thin stokes become more and more hairline as the point size increases from nine to twelve To correctly reproduce the article we should have a digital Scotch Roman based on a small point size. Unfortunately I have not been able to to locate this creature. Don

Nick Shinn’s Scotch Modern is based on an earlier version of Scotch than the one in the article. However the differences – such as the base of the 2 – are not major. Nick Shinn’s Scotch Modern has a lower contrast than the Monotype digital because it used a ten point source – which makes it a closer match. However it is only part way there. It is possible to mimic the appearance of the article by putting a parallel line around the letters of either the Monotype or Nick Shin versions of Scotch Roman. As a final note, bear in mind that the text of newspapers printed with metal type looks darker than digital types modeled on the metal type, or light pressure specimen proofs, because the ink spreads during printing. Don

The version of Scotch Roman in Modern Extended uses more substantial semi-hairline strokes and would give better color than Scotch Roman MT or Scotch Modern [Nick Shinn]. It also has larger serifs. Both features are a better match to the 1889 newspaper article. However, letter width of Modern Extended is narrower than the Scotch Roman in the article, which is matched by Scotch Roman MT Don
