Edited: This clunkly blackletter was sold in Britain and the U.S. as Tudor Black. It was a very common font. Even the Economist Magazine used it for headings in the early 20th century. The design dates from the 19th century when it was created as as a Victorian romantic Gothic revival with little basis in historic blackletter other than some inspiration from Spanish rotunda. I have been unable to determine the name of the designer or the original date of the typeface design, whether named Tudor Black or Psalter Gotisch. Also I don’t know which came before the other. There is some variation in some of the capital letters among the versions sold by various foundries. The Edinburgh Press probably used the Miller and Richard Blake version although it could perhaps be the one from Stephenson Blake. Miller and Richard even created an italic version – a blackletter monstrosity. Don

As I said, every foundry had a version, sometimes more than one. The design had a long run and there were plenty of opportunities for tinkering to avoid accusations of piracy. Also there was no guarantee that different point sizes would have the same design. They were often cut separately by hand rather than by mechanical scaling. The smaller scale versions of complex typefaces – and this one is not simple – had to be simplified to deal with the problems of using metal type. Don
