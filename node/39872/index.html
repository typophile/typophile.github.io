December 7, 2007 • Frode Bo Helland

I’m about to print a book using coloured text for footnotes and qoutes, as well as coloured underlines. Does anyone have any experience doing similar things? What colours did you pick? I’m after whats working well with lot’s of whitespace.

My initial idea was using magenta, since it’s both contrasting and readable against white paper. Also, I might need to use a laser printer - and any other colours besides C, M, Y & K will output a lower resolution. (Am I right?)

I’m lusting for a bright red.

A big question is whether it’s a PostScript laser printer or not. (For this purpose, even third-party PostScript emulations are fine.)

If it’s not a PostScript laser printer, or you use a PS compatible laser in a non-PS mode such as PCL, whatever you do will get sent as RGB data by the printer driver and converted to CMYK at the printer end. It’s impossible to get really nice solid colored text in this case. Sorry.

If it IS a PS laser, you need to be using a PS driver, and an application that allows you to set your colors as CMYK colors, and to turn off color management for your CMYK color space. Then you can specify solid CMYK colors. For example, a nice bright red might be 100C-100M-0Y-0K. As long as the printer’s registration is okay, that would print out pretty well.

Being an Adobe guy, I know how to do all this in InDesign, but not QuarkXPress. Last time I looked, Word’s internal color model was strictly RGB. What application are you using?

2007-12-08 02:33:36 • Linda Cunningham
Your best bet for color reproduction on a laser is to get a Pantone CMYK palette, find a color you like, and print a sample of the nearest ten colors to see if you can find something that matches it. Some colors look great on a laser and some look dreadful—it’s really just a trial and error thing. I have a KonicaMinolta laser printer and the color is pretty terrible overall; when I want good laser color I use a Docutech system or just have it run off at a local print shop that keeps their high-end Xante machines in tip-top shape.

2007-12-10 19:07:35 • dezcom
Good practice in a high-volume color copy shop dictates that the machines be calibrated every morning for color accuracy and registration, so the earlier in the day a job runs, the better it stands a chance of being as perfect as the copier is capable of achieving. Ambient humidity plays a factor, too; on very damp days, high-speed copiers tend to jam more often, which can screw up the calibration. On the other hand, if the air is too dry, there’s a problem with jams caused by static build-up, so–during cold weather–earlier in the day is better, too…
