2010-10-12 23:41:54 • EK

Bill: I think I understand the differences now: The Israeli law allows font designs to be protected by copyright…

The difference is always easier to comprehend if you avoid phrases like ‘font designs’.

A font is always a particular technical implementation of a typeface design, and that is true whether you are talking about trays of cast metal, sets of Linotype brass matrices, Berthold glass photo discs, Letraset rubdown sheets, or an OpenType digital font file. A typeface is a design, a font is an implementation. Both the design and the implementation may involve original creative work, and different jurisdictions recognise that and protect that work as intellectual property in different ways. The recent Israeli ruling apparently establishes that, under Israeli law, a typeface design may be subject to copyright protection. This is different from e.g. US or Canadian law, in which the design is subject a different category of intellectual property protection (design patent or design registration).

Why problem?

There is a reason why the judge Gruschkopf had mentioned different cases. In the Interlego case Supreme Court judge Meir Shamgar added the artistic or artistry criteria because his aim was to prevent one side from gaining a monopoly if the case was judged on functionality alone.

There are several artistry criteria:

  1. the choice option where the buyer has the option to choose from several products on the shelf.
  2. the creator’s intention.
  3. how the public accepted/viewed the product.
  4. willingness of the public to pay for that product [if they think that the product is good and worth the expenses]
  5. aesthetic
  6. artistry

Now the judge Gruschkopf said in section 52 that effort by itself is not enough. This is clear, why? Let’s say that a person is “designing” typefaces by tweaking other designs, where no intention or care is given to other aspects of the design; so this kind of case won’t hold water.

Judge Gruschkopf is saying that a typeface could and should be under the umbrella of creator rights. He understands that a typeface design is not a one day task/job, and the designer should have a long time to hold the rights to the designs in order to gain profit! And by the law (Duration of Copyright, section 38): “Copyright in a work shall subsist during the life of its author and for 70 years after his death, subject to the provisions in this chapter.”

BTW, the judge is not accepting the view that a typeface that was designed in the digital era is subject to a different law treatment than a typeface that was made by trays of cast metal.
This is also based on the ruling of Shamgar in the case of Interlego.

Rich: This has me confused. (You know, I confuse easily.) For a design patent, you have to have drawings, right? Which, under your definition is yet another implementation of “the design”. So, if these are all “implementations” of “the design”, where does the actual “design” exist?

A set of drawings (or even a printout or PDF showing a typeface, which is a typical US design patent submission document these days) is not an implementation of the design, it is a showing of the design. An implementation of a typeface is a font, i.e. a functional tool for setting type. What distinguishes the design from the font is that the same design can be implemented in multiple formats or technologies, and even if the manufacturing demands of these technologies affect alterations to shapes, the overall sense of the design remains intact and identifiably the same. It is in this distinction, and the gap that it opens between a protected design and a protected implementation, that the lawyers make their money.

what makes all the versions of baskerville valid new and unique versions?

They’re not all new or unique relative to each other, but some are new and unique in themselves and none of them are slavish copies of a design by John Baskerville and John Handy in 18th Century England. They’re all interpretations of a style, because the original Baskerville design only exists in artefacts produced by lost fonts, i.e. books, and you won’t find any single instance of a letter on the page that is identical to another. The original design is obscured behind the printing process, and hence any ‘revival’ is a work of interpretation and production of a new design, not of direct implementation of the original design.

In my view, most PostScrpt, TrueType, and OpenType fonts, need multiple versions of the same font, a same weight, to be best viewed at different sizes.

Larger point sizes needs tighter sidebearings, or narrower inter-letter spacing, than small point sizes which need wider sidebearings, or wider inter-letter spacing.

Larger point sizes can have letters that have greater details, while smaller point sizes must have less details, to be legible and conducive to comprehension.

Smaller point sizes too need design adjustments in certain letter like the shin for example. The spaces between its vertical strokes must be farther apart. On the other hand, the spaces between its vertical strokes in a larger point size must be closer together. As the point size increases, the closer these vertical bars get to each other. As the point size decreases, the farther apart these vertical bars get to each other.

Koren has many details in the designs of its letters. For example, look at the aleph and the gimmel. They are complex. Many letters, like the chof and peh, have unusual angles. These are details which draw attention to the eyes of the reader.

Hence, this design works better at larger point sizes, and do not work well at small point sizes, like Biblical text.

The sample I saw was fairly large text. At that size, details are pleasing to look at.

Fonts like Romm Vilna, Rashi, Hadasa, David, and the fonts of Tzvi Narkiss, are excellent for small text. Romm Vilna had different designs to appear at larger sizes. Rashi, Hadasa, David, and the fonts of Tzvi Narkiss, simply do not work at larger sizes. Koren could work well at larger sizes.

Israel, Hebrew on the right or left doesn’t make a difference as far as the eye jumping from one side to the other, if the reader’s eye jumps from the end of a Hebrew or English line to the beginning of a line in the other language.

If you jump from the beginning of a Hebrew line or an English line to the beginning of the other language—which the imperfectly bilingual reader will want to do sometimes—then Hebrew on the left and English on the right is shorter, and better.

I don’t think the reader has any reason to jump from the end of an English or Hebrew line to the end of a line of the other language: he or she isn’t going to learn anything that way. That would be the only situation where the eye jump is closer for Hebrew on the right, English on the left.

Israel, I only use VOLT for OpenType Layout work, not the Adobe FDK tool. I only use the Adobe tool for hinting PostScript fonts.

Basically, if the Adobe tool supports the OT command I think it’s called SIZE, then the GSUB replaces each letter, that’s it.

The OTL ‘size’ feature is not a GSUB feature and it will not substitute different glyph sets within a font for different text sizes. The ‘size’ feature is very problematic, and it isn’t supported in any major software that I’m aware of (maybe in XeTeX?), although you can use the Adobe FDK to define the ‘size’ feature in your GPOS table (but you can’t mix VOLT and FDK workflows). The ‘size’ feature uses a GPOS lookup to store some information about the sizes at which a particular font is designed to be used, such that, in theory, an application could choose the appropriate font from a large family containing multiple size masters. It’s a hack: an official hack but still a hack. It makes use of a table usually accessed late in text layout for store information that an application needs even before it determines what font should be used for the text. It should be replaced by a better mechanism more likely to be implemented by software developers. Since size specific designs are predominantly implemented as separate fonts, rather than as variant glyph collections in a font, I favour something like a SIZE table that an application could access long before it needs to be dealing with GSUB and GPOS.
