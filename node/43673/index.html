I'm trying to decide... and I want your opinion!

March 29, 2008 • blakehaven

Some of you may remember me from a few days ago when I wrote a really long post about working in the industry of graphic and web design.

The resounding response on a couple different forums was that although working in the industry would be easier with a design degree / diploma, it isn’t necessary.

So my next and probably final question is this. From the standpoint of someone who doesn’t have a lot of money, what do you think the better decision would be. Investing in a 4 year program where I’m going to learn little of relevance, but emerge with a great piece of paper, or attempt to find work with a local studio as an intern where I can begin to network.

One of my concerns is that having no formal education could be devastating in 10 years if there were great shifts in the industry that leave me with no real credentials and severely limited working potential.

I look forward to hearing your opinions, you’ve all been very helpful so far.

Did you see [[http://www.typophile.com/node/32459|this thread]]?
2008-03-30 03:20:28 • eliason

I went to college for everything BUT design. Afterward, I just took the minimal technical courses at night until I had a portfolio. The night design courses, with 30 or more students, were a complete waste of time; the teacher was less talented than I was! Everything I know I learned on my own or on the job. We are all self-taught every time we get another software program and we all keep up on our own.

Mentors and internships are the key way to get into most fields; if an art school gives you access to the right mentors and internships, then that’s your justification for going to art school. Strong portfolios lead to more work as you network and become appreciated. No client ever asked me about my formal education; they just want to evaluate my samples.

2008-03-30 14:57:22 • mondoB

Even as screwed up as the school I’ve been at is, I still learned a hell of a lot from studying under the lot of talented and successful working designers who teach there, much more than I did from my internships. Michael Beirut wrote about there being two kinds of design programs, crazy fine art programs where students learn about art and how to do wildly creative work, and schools that help students churn out a really slick portfolio that will get them a job. If you go to one of the former, you’ll learn a hell of a lot and that paper really will mean something. The important part is making the effort to find the right school.

The downside, of course, is having to put up with batshit crazy design teachers who have boring clients and live vicariously through their students.
