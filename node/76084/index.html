2010-11-08 16:48:52 • noono24

Fakery? Gutenberg and Schoeffer set the tone with their simulacrum of the quilled page.

Poliphilus was a great fake, a 20th century Linotype face able to create a facsimile Incunabula page. My Scotch Modern attempts the same thing, for digital+offset to mimic mid 19th century typography (see present issue of Eye magazine.)

Chalet was launched with a fake provenance.

Oneleigh is an historical fiction, designed in the style of 1920s historicism, seventy years later.

Script fonts fake handwriting. Duffy Script fakes the random irregularity of a hand-drawn script with programmed multiple contextual alternates.

But I don’t think these kinds of fakery are what you had in mind, your topic seems to be more about protecting consumers from piracy and plagiarism.

2010-11-11 19:00:09 • JamesM

Not only handwriting of course, but also hand lettering. Haven’t you made a few of those yourself?

The process is commoditization, as the skilled products of one generation of technology users (e.g. scribes with pens) are automated and turned into a commodity (type) which imitates what went before. It’s one of McLuhan’s Laws.

In early Photoshop, it was possible to mimic “airbrush” effects, but difficult, as layers and dedicated filters did not exist. Then the effect was commoditized in pre-set filters.

But not everyone is beholden to using others' tools to make their thing, some make their own tools.

**

In typesetting, tasks which were done manually – justification and kerning – have now been automated. Contentiously, InDesign’s “Optical” kerning fakes the “Metrics” kerning in digital fonts, which in turn fakes the manual on-demand, instance-by-instance kerning of foundry type composing.

Nick, you seem to know a lot about the subject?

I wrote a series of magazine articles 2000-2005, some of which address the role of technological change in type and design culture, which IMO is the Big Question. They’re at shinntype.com, under “Writing”.

2010-11-11 22:02:17 • ben_archer

…the fact that I have availed myself to modern technology does not mean I am “faking it” any more than any other production method …

I wasn’t suggesting you were. I’ve argued above that with each generation of new technology, much of the fresh output mimics the effects of previous technology, and is in that sense fakery, adopting the appearance of something that it is not.

When people use a script font that is strongly informed by hand lettering skills, they want some of that cachet without paying for a custom job, and quicker and more immediately flexible. At the same time, script fonts have their own dialog with type and type history. It is a horse of a slightly different color, because the typographer can experiment with the setting typographically.
