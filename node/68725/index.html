This is from an essay by Firmin Didot published in 1783 and set in his Cicero italic (12 point) - a text where, supposedly, Firmin shows his mother the peculiarities of the typeface. There’s an interesting treatment of the lowercase v - when paired with a, like in the word “avez”, it gets a different shape. Like this:

The v in “vous” is the standard shape; the one in “avez” is the contextual glyph, narrower and without the left top tail. The reason seems to be having an homogeneous kerning. I wonder if other typographers ever applied such a trick. Below is attached the full page from the Italic essay.

When I was talking to the late Kay Amert, she said that I should look at the italics of Simon de Colines because they were full of contextual and alternative glyphs. I have yet to do this but I expect that she is right about this. However I will be looking at them quite soon to see if they bear on my work.

In the meantime here are my contextual v shapes from my not yet released Reading MATD type - Arrotino.

The blue portion at the serifs is the portion contextually removed. The middle one has a bit of both sides removed because it has two diagonal neighbors and the ones on the left and right just the the side next to a diagonal letter. All the diagonals in Arrotino roman have this behaviour when set against a fellow diagonal (y, v, w and x). The idea is to be able to present the letter optimally in all contexts. The example shown is unlikely to be a final solution for this letter as it was put together fairly quickly - more in order to test the code to control it than as a matter of highly deliberate letter design. Still, could a little thing like that make a difference? I think it can - yes.

2010-03-25 02:23:17 • Michel Boyer
I’m thinking about doing something a bit like this Didot v in my Brill Italic typeface. The style is derived – more directly than most types – from 18th century formal cursive writing, but I’m forced to make a pointed rather than a rounded v because the rounded form is a separate letter in IPA and some orthographies. My default v is quite similar to the Didot initial form, which of course causes spacing or collision problems when following some other letters. I probably won’t use the variant form after every letter, though, only after those in which problems occur.
