February 12, 2008 • nicholasgross

G’day guys,

I’m in the process of designing a textbook and it’s going to be a big one of about 550 000 words. I’m just not sure what dimensions to go for, I know it needs to be big to take some pressure off the softcover spine but how big? Are there standard sizes that get used for textbooks? In the past for smaller books, I’ve designed them at approx B5 (250x176) but it will need to be bigger than this. cheers

The size of the book will depend on the press it is printed on. Where I worked for a few years, we were primarily 6x9 (inches), but that was sheetfed. Big runs on a web press will be different, and you need to find out what press size will be used, as they can’t change the cutoffs, and prices really jump when you have to trim about 30% of the paper as waste because your book was a half inch too big for the press used.

Can you find out who will be printing it, or the probable quoters?

550,000 words? Way too big; get them to do a re-write. Cut the modifiers. Something.

In addition to Don’s suggestion, you need to consider the subject area, the kind and number of illustrations, the amount of color, and the audience. I’m guessing that at 550,000 words this is for well-educated users who can read well. Is it pretty specialized? Also consider run length. If it is a long run, Don’s probably right that it will be web printed.

Surely there must be some models out there you can look at.

As an aside: In the good old USA there are national standards for design & production of public school textbooks. Administered by the National Association of State Textbook Administrators / NASTA. These are largely driven by standards set by some of the biggest states, like California. I have heard textbook designers comment about the large pain in the butt it can be to deal with these standards. But meet them they must.

http://www.nasta.org/

I’ve not done textbook design, but I consulted with our state history team when they did our new state history textbook a few years ago. My colleague who ran that project has this to say about the NASTA website:

“Their website isn’t particularly user-friendly (or professional-looking), but this is the organization that set the guidelines we followed for “Northern Lights”–kerning, line spacing, etc., for various grade levels. Those specifications came to us via the design firm we used in Texas, rather than from NASTA directly.”

have fun

2008-02-13 04:19:18 • Scalfin

At 1000, you will be looking at sheet feed or digital press options. Web is not very efficient at this sum length.

Back to the 550,000 words. Do some tests with your sample pages and try to forecast a page count +/-5%. Then check with the printer and see if the book will be of reasonable size. It is very difficult to bind books thicker than 3 inches (and equally hard to read them.)

For marketing and financial reasons, you should consider making the book a series of three volumes. The benefits are: a) you only have to get a third of the book finished at a time (to production stage, you will want the whole thing written); b) production is facilitated when the editor is working on part 3, the layout person is on part 2, and part 1 is on the press; c) you can start selling part 1 while you are still working on parts 2 and 3, getting some money in to finance the rest of the work; d) you can probably price the books higher in aggregate than you can for a single volume (particularly if the parts are coming out in different fiscal years for the purchasing libraries).

2008-02-13 14:41:26 • Don McCahill

Look at other books in the same discipline. The industry treats each discipline differently.

Norton anthology has a small trim size for page count, but it is a nice size to sit on your lap between classes.

Janson or Gardners have a large trim size and pretty much require a desk.

Also consider the Instructors Manual and other items that may be packaged with the book, including consumable workbooks.
