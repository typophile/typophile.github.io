Typesetting TMs with periods

June 5, 2003 • cordpork

Gang,

Way back as a junior lackey, I was taught to typeset trademark symbols (hereafter referred to as TM) at the end of a sentence thusly:

"…so make your voice heard: call Acme.TM" (then kern the TM close to the period, rather than:

"…so make your voice heard: call AcmeTM."

Was I duped? Comments? Is there a reference guide that may discuss this vexing issue?

Thanks in advance.

CP
The best reference is your own eye. What looks best? Which
method conveys meaning without distraction? I usually
kern the period so the "TM" is above the period and still
close to the "Acme". Crazy dedicated typographers do the
same with quotes over periods.

2003-06-06 00:29:44 • Miss Tiffany
I think it is incorrect to separate the  from the
Acme with a period intervening. No matter what. The
implication in your approach (both 1 and 3 in your
example) is that the entire sentence (or at least the
clause "call Acme") is the trademark.

I would set the sentence order as Acme., then
kern the period under the , but not so far as
your #3 example. This way, also, your text is not
"broken" when repurposed into an environment
without kerning.

That's my opinion as a designer, typographer, and editor.

>Crazy dedicated typographers do the
same with quotes over periods.

Crazy dedicated typographers who use well crafted
fonts shouldn't have to, as the quote-period kern pair
should be present in a good font. I also include kerns
for period-quote in my fonts. I have not, however,
heretofore built a kern pair for trademark-period.
But perhaps I will include it from here on out. (And,
I suppose, period-trademark as well, just in case.)

– K.

2003-06-06 05:40:48 •
I am much less concerned with implying that a period or an entire sentence is part of the trademark than I am with that lonely period floating off by itself!

In general, I would pull the TM much closer to the word Acme than you have in any of the examples shown, CP. Then I would tuck the period in so that it would match any other e. combination.

I'm more concerned with the flow of the sentence as it is read. The TM is going to be ignored by the reader, so I try to make it as inconspicuous as possible while still meeting the legal requirements of the corporation.

JMHO
