Actually, there are no general rules for roman and italic. Many historic typefaces had no italics and either italics created later or the roman was matched with an existing italic. The general trend for the last fifty years is to design a complementary italic for each roman weight. If your design is a true text type, then you have some options based on the nature of the design. You neglected to mention a type classification for our reference. Sometimes the italic weights can rival the roman version. I always felt Matthew Carter's ITC Galliard italics were truly outstanding. Just some thoughts to ponder. Yes, I'm old, but I'm back in style!

2005-06-11 01:49:40 • Eric_West
If you are designing an old style typeface, it depends on what your intent is and what applications the face is more suitable for. If the design is primarily for book publication and lengthy text, you might try a more traditional approach. It always helps to do a little research on how some of the best type designers resolved the same problem. For traditional book face examples, review these famous versions of Garamond: Stempel Garamond™ from Linotype http://www.linotype.com/1500/stempelgaramond-family.html Please make sure to read the descriptive text below the samples as it illustrates how many source specimens and how many iterations a design can go through. Berthold Garamond http://www.bertholdtypes.com/bq_library/90076.html If you want to review the full character complement and text samples, download the Old Style pdf samples from their home page: http://www.bertholdtypes.com/home.html These specimens will clearly illustrate how the italic cuts can differ and how the character shapes and widths complement the roman. If you are taking a more contemporay approach and feel your design is better suited for advertising, brochures, medium to short text applications, try looking at ITC Garamond. ITC Garamond (from Linotype) http://www.linotype.com/664/itcgaramond-family.html Again, read the descriptive material below the samples. It illustrates how intended use will greatly affect your design decisions. Of course the final decision on how to approach the italics is up to you. You may be able to take a more innovative approach and perhaps give the type world something fresh and exciting. Good luck. Yes, I'm old, but I'm back in style!
