2007-08-10 13:43:17 • Grot Esqué
Don’t convert. And don’t use any profiles either. Your printer has to put your pdfs through the RIP anyway. The settings of his RIP software are set—or at least should be—to match the profile of his press, while each single press and every print job needs a different profile: rubbers, inks and paper type used, pressure, density, print speed, temperature and relative humidity of the press room, &c. Al these different parameters affect dot gain and so they can‘t be predicted by your PhotoShop or other software, but ought to be adjusted, ideally, minutes before press run. If you convert your imagery from RGB to CMYK colour space yourself, valuable colour information will be lost, so that the final conversion in the RIP won’t have all the data to get the most optimal colour match. This procedure is being advised by engineers at Heidelberg Druckmaschinen AG. If your printer complaints you gave him a pdf in RGB colour space, either he is not knowing his craft, or he needs an update of his RIP preflyghting software. So, why are these profile conversion filters built into Adobe applications? Ask their marketeers.

2007-08-10 15:05:15 • aluminum
Seconded, there is no BEST way. But just converting by whatever default model and then adjusting is probably the worst advice one could give to you. Keep in mind that ANY process in the manipulation of an image will reduce the amount of information in it. The basic problem in RGB->CMYK conversion is that the first model uses Three (additive) colours, while the latter (subtractive) uses four. The first three colors, C, M, Y theoretically could be easily derived from the respective RGB-values. Now the problem is that the fourth color, black, has to be determined somewhat artificially, and that the way black is calculated will have massive consequences on C,M,Y. There's several basic models to determine the amount of black, like UCR (Under Color Removal), GCR (Grey Component Removal), good old Skeleton-Key, etc. All these work pretty differently, the set of plates will look very different, depending on the conversion method, and will give completely different results in print.
2007-08-10 15:43:23 • franzheidl
Your options would be: 1. Just fiddle around, proof, adjust, proof, adjust, evetually print. NOT recommended. You won't be happy nor confident in the long run, if the results are good, it's just good luck. You could have bad luck, too. 2. Theoretically, you could try to integrate into a color workflow via profiles. That would make sense if you had control over your input devices and the RGB images to start with. Keep in mind that profiles aren't arcane magic, they just help how to reproduce a given color in a given color space in another color space. If your input image is bad, profiles won't make it any better, they can just help you to reproduce it most exactly as bad as was before. 3. Educate yourself, calibrate your screen, talk to respectable and experienced printers, etc. Probably the best way, but time consuming. 4. Find a printer and/or prepress company who will do it for you. Color conversion and prepress things in general are most of all a question of experience.
