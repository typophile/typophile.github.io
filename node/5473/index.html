This article completely fails to understand and explain the situation, instead quoting people (eg from Razorfish) on a subject (font economics) they know nothing about.

(Yeah, we should get a union -- except as self-employed workers, that would consitute a cartel in the US, which is why illustrators and photographers don't have unions.)

If Chank's font is used by a third party (a small design firm) to create a piece of artwork, say, the wordmark for a campaign, for a large corporation, he "sells" one font licence to the supplier, no matter how many times the wordmark is reproduced by that supplier's client.

If the large corporation installs his font on all its corporate computers, so that all its in-house marketing departments can use it for ongoing typesetting for their ID and marketing, then he can sell a multi-user licence for a far higher price. And if the typeface has a family, that helps sales too.

This is, one would assume, what happened in the $6,000 Ocean Spray "deal" (ie contract) mentioned in the article, but the writer doesn't explain why "negotiated directly" is so much more lucrative, leaving it open to suggestion that it's all about Chank's mythical Elvis-like charisma and Donald-ish powers of negotiation.

Chank has the right idea. As long as fonts are durable tools, type designers will have a tough, competitive retail market .

If we can make fonts totally ephemeral consumable fashion items, that's a better much market.

Rockstar fonts is a start, but the real money is in licensing deals with the celebrity du jour. Anyone know Paris Hilton's email address?

Or, just suppose, Joe Teen buys a new pair of Shaq-endorsed sneakers, and they come with the new Shaq blackletter font (on mini-CD) which he can use in all his emails (or school essays).

Jim -- do you have the rights to Beckham's latest tattoo yet? Are you negotiating with Cristiano for space on his chest? Get on the job, man!
