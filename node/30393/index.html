2007-01-08 01:16:42 • matthew_desmond

Cagliostro would be an excellent choice for the name, a recognition of Cooper’s great wit and love of language. Cagliostro (the man) was a notorious character. Among his many skills was forgery, hence the juxtaposition here with Actuality, as well as the showing of the two italic “t” ligatures. A two-word type specimen poem! Cagliostro is a lovely type specimen word in its own right.

BTW, Dave Farey has described Highlander as more of a general piece of Ozishness, rather than a revival of any particular Oz alphabet.

I wonder what kind of a typeface this “Cooper Sans” would have ended up, if it had gone throught the mill at Monotype, Linotype or some other foundry? On page 81 of the Book of Oz is a comparison of the original lettering of Packard, and the typeface made from it, which might be a helpful benchmark in assessing the degree of regularity you wish to add, in going from lettering to type.

I recall discussing this alphabet with Richard Kegler some years ago, we had both considered making it into a font. I don’t know if he took that idea any further; I didn’t, although I have always considered it to be an astonishingly beautiful design.

Cagliostro was an important Mason, master of the secret meaning of letters – another allusion. No doubt there was some connection between masonry, the Black Art of printing, and the brotherhood of commercial artists.
