Opinions on business card?

November 28, 2006 • mpvk

This is a business card for an event planner looking to diversify from mostly non-profit, performing arts-type organizations.

I’m being rushed through this business card design, and I’ve been looking at it for too long.

Basically the “NH” mark can’t be changed, the angled grid is a must and I can only really change fonts.

The idea is to contrast the mark using a script or serif with something more “organic,” but not “too frilly.”

So please, someone say anything to get me thinking about these boxes differently–thanks!

The typeface contrast in B and E work best for me. But all that background in black is a bit much. Can you have “Event Studio” in black on gray? What if the NH (as in B) was the only reversal and the diagonal lines were black? Or maybe NH EVENT STUDIO could be white on black with everything else having a white background? Way too much black now.
2006-11-28 04:57:18 • pino

If you can remove/thin down the gridlines, that will make it look less visually busy. Because you’ve got a lot going on with the lines, logo etc., I would discount using swash fonts and use something plain because otherwise things will fight with each other, as you’ve got at the moment. What about using Helvetica?

I actually like the black on the card, it’s different. I would try using a warm black or deep rich dark colour to add some warmth to it - a lot of angles seem to make things look quite cold and clinical, so a warm colour might balance it out a bit.

2006-11-28 08:38:11 • Duckworth

I agree with Simon about thinning the lines and warming the black.

The Avenir variant does it for me; all the others look like the typestyle is fighting the underlying aesthetic. The role of that type should be to support the mark, rather than detract from it. Good luck.

2006-11-28 11:54:59 • all about seb

Thanks for the replys.

Pino: Yes, it’s a lot of black. The card is printing in one color so I’m doing it this way until the type is decided. I’ll play with the lines and boxes and look at ink/paper combinations again later. I started down that road and all the variations were leading to a “freeze-up” of sorts, if you know what I mean.

Ben: Busy, yes. I’d like to not have every part of this card be boxes, lines and triangles, but we were getting to a point where there was a lot to look at and decisions were getting harder to come by. The card is just the first part of an ID package, but the rush means I’m picking type for the ID to work across everything while actually doing just the card.

Seb: The type needs to contrast because we’re trying to say “reliable, organized, exacting/personable, elegant, approachable.” What with the mark being Avenir (and decided already), I’ve got to use either a contrasting type or some “organic” element(s?) paired with the mark (which the client has already indicated a predisposition against–not that it still couldn’t work).

So you see my limitations, now. This card is basically a rough that’s just being used to try-out type indended to contrast the mark. It’d be better to do outside the context of the card, but she wants to print this thing all of a sudden, so it’s turning into a bit of a mashup.
