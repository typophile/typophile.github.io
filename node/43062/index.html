This is kind of related to the “How much of a font do you draw out by hand” thread in the Design forum, but I posted it here because it’s more of an attitude question than a design one.

How accurate do you make your vectors?

Just because it is possible to place node accurately to a 10 thousandth of an inch, do you? If you need precision and can have it, why not take the the time to get it? Right? Well, maybe, or does too much close scrutiny get in the way of the overall visual effect you’re after.

I tend to go by the dictum that it’s right when it looks right, but at times, I find my self fussing of over details that you wouldn’t find with an electron microscope. On a computer it’s possible to work to a much higher level of accuracy than output devices are capable of delivering, so what’s the point?

so where do you… Uh, draw the line.

When I’m working on my fonts I’m excruciatingly anal about everything—I can’t expect to ever start doing it for money if I’m not obsessing over the details. But when it comes to less important stuff, and I’m in a hurry, I tend to work several times larger than final size and scale down to hide imperfections.

Rounding node coordinates down to whole numbers has one very basic implication: they save computer resources. Long decimal fractions take more memory both in disk storage and RAM, and are also more difficult to calculate. Granted, that impact seems insignificant considering the massive leaps in computing power of modern systems, but it still adds up.
2008-03-12 06:36:54 • cuttlefish

It depends upon what you’re working on. Because of the facility to work so much larger than actual size (and divorced from any real physical scale), it is hard sometimes to keep the end result in perspective.

For example, take the case of a type design destined for text at 12 point, to be output at 2540 dpi. Twelve points is approximately 0.1667 inches. Multiply that times your output resolution and you get about 423 imaging units (toner dots, whatever) for 12 points.

If you’re working with a 1000-unit em, then that’s 2.36 em units per dot. If you’re working with a 2048-unit em, then that’s 4.84 em units per dot.

So, for a text face the practical truth is that tweaking a node one or two units either way probably won’t even register as an actual difference in output.

It can be hard to keep that in mind when you’ve got that glyph zoomed in to 1600% and you’re neurotically finessing that seductive curve at full size on the screen.

– K.
