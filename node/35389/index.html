I came to type three years ago with no formal training in art or graphic design. I can report that it took me two years before my eyes would tell me not only that something was wrong, but how to correct it. This was by constant constant drawing and re-drawing, and in FontLab comparing to outlines of typefaces I admired.

Existing typefaces are the one indispensable teacher, and the font programs that give you the outlines are like x-rays. And you can paste one outline over another to compare. There are no secrets, except those in plain sight–which do take a lot of looking and thinking to understand.

In a sense, all learning is self-instruction. The difference is whether you have guidance. I think guidance can save you a lot of work and grief, but it doesn’t have to come only from a formal program; there are other avenues.

That being said, the advantage of a formal program if it is good–and Reading and KABK seem to be good–I would think is that ideally you will get all sides of the type design: the technical, the artistic, the craft, the historical, the commercial. Most people I think will find one or another area more difficult to get by self-instruction than another.

2007-07-24 18:45:29 • Nick Shinn

I am certainly getting the message that you want some structured, authoritative guidance. I understand that desire. What may not be clear from Simon’s message is that previous to about 10 years ago, there simply was NO PLACE such as you imagine; which is to say that in the 20th century, ALL type designers were self-taught, more or less. Now, undoubtedly someone will come along to nit-pick about that statement, but as a generalization, I can safely say that most type designers were never trained; they used the momentum of their own interest, sustained over years, and a lot of time spent looking, drawing, looking, reading, looking, drawing, digitizing, etc to develop their skills.

There really is no magic bullet or neatly-encapsulated program in general; the possibility of a type design education is a very new thing. But people can get involved in type design from nearly any direction; there are mathematicians, calligraphers, architects, signpainters, dancers, advertisers, graphic designers, stonecutters, and programmers who have found their way successfully into type design.

It sounds like you intend to then use the skills in type design in your business. I think you are already positioned to do this better than almost anyone; you have clientele in graphic design, you have experience in that industry, you have a connection to trends and client taste, and you have proficiency with graphic design tools. There is really very little left for you to acquire or add to that mix to successfully build lettering, type design and custom type into your practice. It’s really true; as scary or unsupportive as it may sound, simply start doing it. There is no licensing board!

Peter, I was at a similar stage before I looked into coming to Reading for the MA — a lot of experience with design and typography, and perhaps just enough knowledge to be intimidated by type design itself. For me, the decision to go back to school had a lot to do with wanting to focus on type design without the distraction of other work once and for all. It’s a luxury to turn one’s back on earning a living for a while and devote all your time and attention to something new. Hopefully, the investment will also have a good return. What we get at Reading is advice from smart and talented people (which you can get elsewhere) and access to some really fantastic collections of historical artifacts (which you can see your own of you wished to), but most importantly we get the time to pour all of our energy into learning and a lot of support for doing that as efficiently as we can.

It’s very, VERY self-directed here, which is important to consider. It’s not step-by-step instruction in calligraphy, using FontLab, or writing OpenType features. We get a lot of history, a lot of perspectives on how things have been done and how things are being done now, but it’s up to us to figure out what to do with all of that information. That’s great for some people, frustrating for others. (Well, it’s been frustrating for all of us at some point or another.) If we’re doing good work lately, it’s really because we’ve gotten the chance to keep at it, and we’ve gotten a lot of good feedback on the things we have tried.

2007-07-25 12:51:25 • Christoph Knoth

An apprenticeship/internship is an good option, if you can find one. I went through the Reading MA years ago, and now occasionally lecture and give design crit there, but I’ve also taught a few apprentices. I personally prefer the long-term apprenticeship method. However, an apprenticeship tends to mold your knowledge around that of your ‘master’. If the ‘master’ is someone like Robert Slimbach or Matthew Carter that is a very very good thing. But it can be rather one-sided, and the expertise and tasks you may learn may only cover a portion of the knowledge needed for an independent type designer. It can also sometimes hold you back if your growing design sense differs from your master’s.

The big benefit of a place like Reading is the breadth of voices speaking into your learning and work. I vividly remember times when Gerry would suggest I make this or that change, then have Gerard say the opposite! It forced me to understand why they suggested what they did - their experience, knowledge, personal taste, etc. - and come up with my own sense of design purpose. Now I find myself being a third or fourth voice, and helping the students in my particular strengths.

I designed type for 9 years before going to Reading, and the main reason I went was my own dissatisfaction with my work. I needed a stronger theoretical and historical foundation, and regular, expert review of my stumbling efforts. That is very possible through an internship, but just be sure that you still have a variety of voices around you.

Sure, why not.

Are these even mutually exclusive? Just like applications grow more feature rich based on consumer demand, the demands for font technology will only rise. Users will be interested in highly automated, open-type loaded, multilingual faces that are suitable for text, but workable into display proportions. Super-superfamilies that extend beyond latin-based glyphs, that you can dial from historic to pomo, come after that. There may be some major innovations in font management interfaces that is required to permit that demand to blossom, but it will happen.

And the people at Reading now may then be those most qualified to lead the teams needed to design and implement typefaces of that scope.

Thomas hits the point squarely. A few years back, we used to hire interns from design schools. The problem was, by the time you had them trained well enough to help you, they were gone. The other end is, the time you spent training them was time you could not spend doing work so if you were in a crunch, they likely sat around bored until you met your deadline. It was tough meeting your mentoring obligation while making your deadlines. The interns that showed the most initiative and who were self-starters tended to get the most attention. Those who sat quietly, rarely got as much attention as the self-starters. The message is, if you are not given enough to do, do something on your own as a learning problem and ask for a quick crit. The other message is that design is a business that has to complete work to prosper. You have to be on the plus side, not the minus side, in your efforts as an intern. This is not easy and takes determination and team spirit.
