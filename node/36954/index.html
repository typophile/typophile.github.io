Polychromatic Polyglot Packaging?

September 10, 2007 • blank

Are consumers pretty good as discerning between languages on polyglot packages? I am putting together an english/spanish package and I am concerned not only that separating languages by color could be overkill, but that some people might find it insulting.

Most of us are used to reading these kinds of packages. Pick up any shampoo bottle and tell me how much you get inundated with superfluous French. Most bottles have French mixed in with English in the ingredient list. Some have French in slightly smaller type than English in the small paragraphs that describe how your hair will flail and whip like the mane of horse.

If its medical then by all means make distinct zones. Otherwise… I’m not sure.

In Europe most packages have the fine print in the same font/size/style, with enclosed country/language codes for quick scanning. The main logo/front design will be primarily in one or two languages. So for example a Portuguese company might have:

apple juice | apfelsaft | عصير التفاح SUCO DE MAÇÃ ZUMO DE MANZANA jus de pomme | succo di mele | яблочный сок

With the bold capital text in display face, coloured etc, with the parenthetised text in a sans font. The ingredients would then go:

(P) Agua, maçães (E) Agua, manzanas (GB/IE) Water, apples (D/A) Wasser, Äpfel

I would upload a picture of a package like this, but I don’t move to Europe for another two weeks and I can’t seem to find a picture already online.

But I agree with Mike, most people in the US are used to reading packages bilingually and trilingually after NAFTA. The one thing to be careful about is market regulations. I want to say that in Quebec the French has to be within X size of the English. (Actually, I’m almost 100% sure of such a regulation, I just don’t recall the details). You said this is English/Spanish, and I don’t think Mexico has a similar law, so you could either do equal treatment of the two (often by doing the front in English and the back identical to the front in Spanish) for products equally destined for the US and Mexico, or a subordination of the Spanish, assuming a product destined for a generally English speaking public.
