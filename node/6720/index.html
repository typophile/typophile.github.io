It's very telling that this thread has evolved into discussions over religion. I'll bet John could have predicted it would happen.

Here's my take: Who we choose to work for and who we'd turn away is ultimately a personal decision. For every person (or studio), it comes down to what they're comfortable with and whether they can look themselves in the mirror at the end of the day (and whether looking at themselves in the mirror is even important to them at all). It seems the real danger lies when we try to enforce our personal standards (whether based on opinion, religion, personal experience, morals or ethics) upon others.

And then there's the First Things First manifesto published in Emigre #51. As designers, we hold within our reach the power to shape the way people perceive the world around them, and it's my opinion that we ought to use our talents to try to make the world a better place. (Yes, the definition of 'better place' will vary from person to person.) The FTF manifesto suggests a move away from promoting abject consumerism of inessential products in order to focus our efforts on cultural and social change. I can get behind this, but I also recognize that I need such clientele in order to make a living and feed my family. So I choose carefully who to take on so that at a minimum, we're not aiding an organization that is negatively impacting the world.

As a founder and partner in a design firm, it was important to me to align with partners who shared a similar goal, so that we may avoid conflict over selecting design opportunities that present themselves. Has anyone out there been faced with a decision in their studios in which the partners differed over taking on a specific client? What was the outcome?

I recognize there are plenty of designers out there who have no qualms about taking on Corporation X or Y, and could care less whether they make the world a better place… Who see no need to distinguish between clientele. It's not my place to decide what's "good" and who's "bad" so I pledge that I won't disrespect you for your choices in the event we differ - all I ask is the same courtesy. There's plenty of work out there to keep us all busy, and as Tiffany said, at least this way we won't be competing.

Finally, what's your thought on taking on Pro Bono or non-profit work? Personally, I think it a good thing that design firms take on some work in order to help organizations that never could otherwise afford the expertise. It's also a great way to stay grounded and avoid having every design project be driven by the almighty dollar.
