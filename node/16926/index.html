2005-12-21 21:27:37 • Addison Hall

I’ve been using the HP Designjet and Epson for years. Personally, I favor the HPs, particularly with regards to color accuracy. Most service bureaus I’ve worked with also favor Designjet for proofing so, when my agency brought prepress in-house, we decided to go with Designjet as well. We really couldn’t be happier. Just be certain to purchase the EFI Designer Edition RIP, rather than the HP solution.

Oftentimes, people will say they favor Epson because of all the papers available. I think this argument is moot these days, as the Designjet can self-calibrate to any paper. It’s my personal opinion that most Epson advocates have not used a Designjet – at least not lately.

As far as duplexing goes, good luck. No large-format I’ve used is good for duplexing. You will be stuck feeding paper through twice.

2005-12-22 04:51:41 • jupiterboy

I used an HP DeskJet 1220c/PS for years, and it was great – worked out of the box with near contract proof colour accuracy via the Adobe PressReady software. PressReady was discontinued a few years ago so I started looking for other options and settled on a DesignJet 10PS (with HP’s RIP rather than the EFI one). I bought it from eBay so it arrived without software – not normally a problem in these days of online driver downloads and updates. Oh dear. HP’s support site is a horrific rabbit warren of out-of-date information and the software itself, when I finally managed to get hold of it, is utterly half-assed. See my comments here.

The big plus side of the DesignJet range is that they use dye-based inks rather than pigments like Epson. This allows a larger colour gamut and reduces problems like bronzing and metamerism. The big downside is that on papers other than HP’s own very expensive and hard-to-find range, dye-based ink fades pretty quickly. With the new Epsons, you can print on pretty much anything and get a near-archival lifespan, and there are loads of third party papers available that are nicer and cheaper than Epson’s own range. The bronzing and metamerism problems that Epsons were known for have now been pretty much solved with the new Stylus 2400, apparently, so even though I don’t have personal experience of that model, based on my experience with HP I’d recommend the Epson. Prepress houses agree, at least in the UK – most guys I speak to now actually call digital colour proofs ‘Epsons’!

There’s a review of the 2400 here, which points up another advantage: great black-and-white prints. B/w prints from my HP come out green, calibration or no.

2005-12-22 19:05:59 • dezcom

My perspective on evaluating these printers is different, because I’m actually writing the software to drive them myself, rather than using what’s provided by the manufacturer. But in a comparison between the Epson R2400 and the HP 10, there simply is no comparison. The latter uses only four inks, of which the black is 18 picoliters (a huge drop size by today’s standards). That’s why your grays come out greenish - they’re printed almost entirely with CMY ink, where on the Epson they’d be primarily “light black”.

Also, the mechanical accuracy of the 10 series (I had a 110nr) is nowhere near that of the Epson. For type work, I’m absolutely in love with the 1440x1440 mode of the Epson, and, while I was initially skeptical that it was really hitting that accuracy, I’m now convinced. By contrast, the HP has visible imprecisions even at 1200x600.

Early Epson pigment inks did indeed have problems with gamut, bronzing, and metamerism. But the latest generation is very good indeed. Bronzing can be a problem, but choosing the right paper helps a lot. As for gamut, I found the CMY inks on the 110 somewhat watery, making rich red-browns and midnight blues hard to attain.

Metamerism is a trickier question, and I really wouldn’t want to make definitive pronouncements without doing spectrophotometer measurements, but the greenish cast on the 10 series may also have something to do with that. Try viewing the prints in different light (flourescent, incandescent) and see if the cast stays the same. I know the earlier Epson dye printers (I had a 1270) had really bad metamerism, so pigment is not always superior to dye.

If you do go for the HP, get the 30 series, which is six color and has a reasonably good black dot size - that’ll improve text rendering considerably, at the very least.

In any case, enjoy! The quality of sub-$1k inkjets today is absolutely incredible, compared even to high-end pro models from a few years ago. I’m sure that whatever you end up with will suit your needs.
