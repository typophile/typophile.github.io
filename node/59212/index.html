Using my Serif Guide the closest font I could find in terms of certain key letters like the e, g, b and y that I thought were unambiguous, was Hollandse Mediaeval, and there are a number of disconnects there, including the y and the A. The lettering appears to have been drawn before etching (or whatever process forms the raised leters), and so may have been a custom design. The sign was apparently made in 1996, but they may have tried to use old American types as models. If so, I haven’t incorporated them in my Guide yet.

I’ve finished reviewing Mac McGrew’s book, and found no 20th century type that matched this. I don’t have books showing much of anything earlier, and McGrew’s book does include 19th century types that had 20th century issues by foundries.

By the way I only found about 10 non-slab serif families that had a single-storey g. Certainly not a common feature. Now if they rotated the ‘e’ as Jan suggests, maybe they used a backslanted version of an italic g?

It’s pretty hard to ID something if modifications like this are to be considered. Might as well say it’s a custom type design.
