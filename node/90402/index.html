2012-02-27 14:56:59 • dightondesign

@dightondesign: I would begin by complementing you on your use of visual rhetoric in your first attempt (grey & sans) ie: many different things coming together to make a whole, unity &c. I would then ask you you explain your reasoning behind:

a) the choice of colour b) the choice of font c) the choice of dimensions

Lastly, I say “OK, that’s one idea, now put it aside and do a 180°.” Too often I see students get stuck on a single concept and push it around for days. You’re done with this one. Stop, and come up with another. Do this 100 times. In one day. On paper.

2012-02-27 15:10:15 • Chris Dean

Frode, thanks a lot for the link to that very interesting article. It strongly supports group interaction, and gives very interesting conditions under which they work.

I think the article’s criticism of the original ‘Brainstorming’ idea is somewhat off the mark, though. That is because, contrary to what he says, it doesn’t prevent criticism. It just separates the brainstorming and criticism phases. So the tests he cites don’t really critique the original idea, at least as he reports them.

The specific test he reports in which separated individuals did better is what I would call a ‘puzzle’ rather than a problem. It has very limited, well defined boundaries and solution. To me brainstorming is most important when the situation is more unclear and fluid and needs conceptual definition.

I think it is also possible that in art, having an individual’s vision guiding the process, with others' critical input in many cases is better than a strictly group process. The whole question what group process is best for what kind of problem is really interesting, to me at any rate.

On Fran’s brief, the main thing I would urge is to look for a custom lettering solution rather than a type solution. The Rudolph Koch work has a totally different feel from the Sister Corita work, but what they have in common is that they are custom lettering—and wonderful.
