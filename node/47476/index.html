2008-07-16 21:45:40 • Jens Kutilek

Stephanie,

Actually, it happens fairly often. If you’re the designer/typesetter & text files have been supplied, you can fairly quickly set a lot of the text. Often, there will be a setting where things just work better – say, for a particular text, 10.6/13 x 25.5 (or 25p6 in NEWSPEAK). I’ve been known to play with measure in 1/4 pica increments, and as your client suggests, type size in 10ths of a point. The optimal leading is also affected by the measure (well, margins) & type size. All this can lead to specifications not possible with old typesetting systems, even computer-based ones.

When you get the right sizes, things just sort of click. The spacing looks right, you don’t have too many widows, too many hyphens, etc. etc. Perhaps less an issue with poetry, though you still have the visuals of measure, margins, & turned lines to accommodate.

FWIW

2008-07-17 08:15:32 • P A Hardy

“Is there a good reason we don’t use strange font sizes like 10.6? "

When working with newspapers and other publications to define the perfect blend of size, leading and line length, we land on fractional point sizes all the time.

When working with screen resolutions other than 72 and 144 dpi, operating systems and thus applications, automatically use fractional point sizes, almost exclusively.
