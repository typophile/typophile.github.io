2007-01-20 16:35:51 • begsini

I agree. Avoid the gimmicks.

Things to consider when sending your CV and or portfolio to potential employers:

  1. They receive dozens of CVs, so they don’t want to spend a lot of time interacting with yours.

  2. If it is free of typos, decently designed and your experience meets their expectations, they will put you in the “contact for interview stack.”

  3. Keep the document simple. No color, no more than one page. Just a nicely designed, easy to navigate typographic document. Anything else will be seen as gimmicky.

  4. As stated above, “gimmicks” can work. But it has to be so amazing and innovative that the only next logical step is to hire you right away.

Sounds to me like you’ve come up with a solution - the cube - and now you are in search of the concept. This rarely works out well.

“I have two ideas for my CV. I am designing one that is typographically exciting and another that is interactive.” “I need to make it look exciting.”

Again, no.

A CV should be:

  • easy to read/legible
  • standard format
  • nothing else

Do not overthink the CV. It’s a practical piece of design. A definite form FOLLOWS function object. Let it be what it is. Pick a couple of nice typefaces, do some careful typesetting, proofread it, and call it finished.

As others have said, gimmicks are REALLY hard to pull off…mainly because a thousand people before you have already tried. Remember, designers are pretty much in the business of gimmicks (at least at some point in their career) so the odds of you coming up with something unique enough to trump not having the CV readable are slim to none.

Consider your CV like you would your wardrobe. You COULD show up to your interview in thigh-high leather, a pink boa, and purple tu-tu to try and ‘stand out’ in the crowd. But no one is really going to hire you based on your wardrobe. They just care about your experience.

“Both of these sorts of people are more susceptible to cool than HR people.”

Graphic design isn’t about ‘cool’, though. It’s about ‘communication’.

The last time I did portfolio reviews for college grads, I was sent one student who everyone was raving about. The kid was ‘cool’ and had really nailed down the MTV-esque style. It was a slick portfolio.

Then we got to a piece that had the same great MTV-esque style and he commented off-hand ‘yea, it’s good, but the client really hated it. Whatever’

I start to look at the piece and notice it was a brochure for a septic tank company.

At that point, I realized that he wasn’t a designer. He was a highly skilled and talented decorator. Alas, he wasn’t able to grasp the context of the piece and apply the appropriate design solution.

If I see a ‘cute’ resume, I think ‘this person doesn’t understand the basic functionality of a resume’ and would probably then label them a poor designer…albeit perhaps a great decorator.

IMHO, of course.

Thats a very good point. There is not a single solution to this. I’ve seen gimmicky resumes that were failures because the gimmick had nothing to do with the rest of the work. If you’ve always wanted to work somewhere that is obsessed with cubes, squares and gemotric CV’s, send them your cube, so to speak.

If you want to work somewhere well paying, classy, boring and bureaucratic, send them 12pt TNR with broad even margins, and no abbreviations.

It is up to you, but if you go for a gimmick, make sure it is one that actually communicates something about you, the company, or both. If it doesn’t do that, skip it and save your effort for something else.

Hi Miss G

I’m coming to this thread late, but thought I would relate a true story…

Years ago I designed and produced a 3D cube Christmas gimmick for our clients' amusement in the festive season. The cube was about 3 inches square on each side and was self-tensioning with an internal rubber band, so that it effectively assembled itself as it came out of the envelope. The only information it had on the outside was our company branding, our closing dates over the holidays, and a bit of seasonal decoration. It had no other function, but required four colour printing, a custom-made die, heavy-duty laminating and a lot of (expensive) hand-finishing. We ran less than a 100 off. The clients, almost without exception, got pissed at the office parties and played football with the cubes. In most cases the ‘goals’ were, yep, the waste paper baskets.
