What exactly do you want to do with OpenType?

Do you want to convert your outlines of characters, with the various effects from different programs, and import them into a font editor to produce an OpenType font to use in MS Word or Adobe InDesign to produce your desired results?

To do so, you must think outside of the box.

A typical font editor can not produce the same effects that your can create in Corel Draw, Adobe Illustrator, or Freehand. Rather, a good flexible font editor can make a font which creates characters that look like the desired results you showed in your link.

Like most people, you think that OpenType is the key to almost everything.

OpenType is merely a new sophisticated font format, used by many font editors. Most font editors can not offer all the advanced features of OpenType. Maybe in another 5 years. Maybe 10. Maybe never.

MicroSoft and Adobe have created advanced tools to harness much of OpenType. One is called VOLT; one is called a tool kit. Each is not intuitive and easy to use.

To succeed, you must know its capabilities and limitations. Then, think of a way to achieve your goal with those limited capabilities. That’s thinking outside of the box.

Over a decade ago, I created a font with characters like those that appear in your link. I did it in Fontographer, now from FontLab at www.fontlab.com by creating all the different graphic shaded elements as different characters. Then, I pasted composites of them in different character locations for the alphabet. The solid black or white elements I drew in those locations.

Even though it looked like a mess, after I created a PostScript font, it printed as desired. PostScript fonts in those days did not render on the screen until after Apple and MicroSoft came out with TrueType, ad then Adobe followed with Adobe Type Manager.

Today, we have MicroSoft and Adobe offering OpenType. I won’t get into the advantages of OpenType over TrueType, but it’s only time for Apple to offer a new font format, code-named, “Bric-or-Brac”, or Everything-You-Could-ImagineType. I got a beta. Boy, it makes OpenType look like rotten tuna fish!

sk1n_w4lk3r,

the various shading parts for Federal are all in separate fonts. The Federal fonts are released in OpenType format, but layering and color effects are not OpenType features. You need to do some assembly.

If you install all the fonts from your download, you should have the following items in your font menu:

Federal FederalSix FederalNine FederalTwelve FederalEighteen FederalReserveNote

The first, Federal, is the font with the regular, unshaded forms. Use this to set the initial text and get the size and position right. The numbered entries, Six, Nine, Twelve and Eighteen Line are sets of shades with different levels of detail.

For instance, in the Eighteen menu you will find 5 fonts with shades and parts for the 18 line version:

Bevel DShadow Diagonal HShadow Horizontal

Bevel is the three dimensional looking body. It makes the letters appear as if cut out. DShadow contains the diagonal (D) shading for the shadow. This fits behind the bevel. HShadow is the same, but now horizontal lines. Diagonal and Horizontal contain the shading for inside the letters. These need to go on top.

Your download also includes a FederalManual.pdf in which the layering process is shown step by step. The Sample Art folder contains 4 illustrator documents with lots and lots of examples of layered words in all sorts of different combinations and color schemes.
