Looking for a "timeless" font for a book design project

February 7, 2011 • Easily Amused

I came across an interesting project and wanted to get the opinions of the gurus here at typophile.

It’s a novel length fantasy - 600+ pages - and it needs designed.

Things to keep in mind: There are to be two versions of the book - one to be read and one to be looked at (it’s a self-aware object — also read as: deluxe edition) The book (text) is about the book (object) and its writer. Moreover, the book spans from about 400 B.C. to the 1960s. Finally, the pen “writing” the book was stolen from the book of fate and is writing in its own distinctive hand.

I’ve been struggling with font choices. I normally use a historically appropriate family and run with it. In order to do so on this project, I’d need a timeless or better yet, an immortal font.

It’s an interesting concept and I thought I’d throw it out into the forums. Would a full-out calligraphic solution be appropriate for the object? If so, what would the “readable” book be set in to coordinate? The book not only spans centuries, but also jumps from continent to continent, making references to many of the greatest civilizations in history. An entire chapter could be set in Chinese in the object and it would be correct… but should it be? In short, I have so many options that my head is spinning.

What would you do?

Is this for a personal, academic or professional purpose?

2011-02-08 05:01:13 • dezcom

Clearly, Times Roman is right out. So, unfortunately, is, say, Cloister Lightface. And Univers would clearly be the exact opposite of what you want.

I would say that one might sort of squeak by with, say, Caledonia or Garamond. A typographer would peg them to a particular era, but they don’t strongly suggest a particular era to the general reader. But I’m pretty sure you want something a bit less ordinary than this. (Weiss Roman and Palatino are a notch more decorative, still not heavily temporalized, but they’re still fairly routine as well.)

The suggestions so far given here are excellent ones, but if you want to use a typeface that is more conventional than a calligraphic one… then, although Cloister Lightface or Jenson is not feasible (great for a deluxe edition of something like Lord of the Rings, yes, but not this) I think the closely related typeface Centaur might do very nicely.

2011-02-08 22:43:51 • Easily Amused

All out calligraphic? I can’t think of any calligraphic script that wouldn’t be a very obvious “font” over 600 pages. The ones I’ve seen with lots of alternates and ligatures are either very unformal or way too formal for text. So, in short: the kitch pitfall is definitely present.

A few “object” faces that come to mind: Trinité, Brioso and Maiola. I also second Beorcana. Of these, Trinité is perhaps best suited to handle both the “book” and the “object”, with it’s variants - no. 4 italic for the most extreme. With Brioso, there’s the obvious pair Kazuraki for Hiragana, Katakana and quite a bit of Kanji (I’m no expert on Chinese, but I seem to remember that they share at least the Kanji signs with the Japanese).
