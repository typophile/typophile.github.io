Well, I am the eternal student!

Distortions that:

  • Accommodate visual “clotting” (at small sizes). Typically long and shallow.
  • Trap actual ink spread preventing rounded inside corners. Like in Retina (for print).
  • Keep outside corners sharp (which I call thorns). Common in the photo era.
  • Trap emitted light preventing inside corners from bloating. Retina onscreen?

Any others?

So, the theory in your second kind of trap is that unless one Retinizes 90 degree intersections, the ink, which is really applied in a miserly fashion, in a process running at really high speeds, will go somewhere :) it don’t.

In JHs presentation he seemed compelled to use a twilight zone blurring effect to simulate what does not happen. I did press tests of this for Poynter Agate, and not being one to put all kinds of shenanigans over clients to impress them with stuff that ain’t a problem, I put my traps where they do help, in acute intersections, did grades that take care of the rest and poof, on to the next issue.

The theory being revived here is from completely different age, when Matt Carter made Bell Centennial, a bitmapped family of styles for a printing process completely unrelated to modern newspaper agate.
