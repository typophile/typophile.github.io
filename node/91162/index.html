Best way – at least, to see what’s current – would be to go to a book store & browse. If you want to see books with a sans as body type, stay with art and travel books, at least in the States.

2012-03-21 00:09:32 • Hector Muñoz

Kievit and Caspari and work very good for long copy, even for a book, they are somehow close to garamond in proportions, structure and maybe even color. Quadraat Sans has a better color and better modulation. These three faces also have good italics.

The thing with sans serifs is that most designers are used to set them a bit tight by means of negative tracking and in the case of body copy you need them to set them normally and with more leading that if you were setting a serif face because the visual cohesion of the line is weaker due to the lack of serifs.

Also light sans faces are no good for long body copy and disastrous in books, you need the lines to have good color.

Hrant, it may not kind, but it is fair. The evaluation of performance of any product falls on a bell-shped curve. If it is a manufactured item, somebody gets the good ones where the numbers are close to the zero defects point. Others don’t.

To put a point on it, if you ask the guy who bought the Yugo that falls at the extreme left of the curve, he’ll tell you it’s a great car.

Type, esp. type as software, doesn’t have those manufacturing issues. But acceptance is also a bell-shaped curve – in me, for example, you’ve found one of the few people who doesn’t like Gotham. Doesn’t make me either right or wrong, just far out on the curve.

So asking for “best,” even with an exhaustive survey, just gives you that curve. Buried in that math, of course, is evaluation. As Nick said, if you include a fair bit about “best for what,” you shorten up the curve. A general “best” is just a poor thing to ask for, anyone working beyond the level of a neophyte should know that.
