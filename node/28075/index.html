Hello everybody, I'm designing a book for a literary club. Actually, it might be more accurate to call it a journal, a collection of short fiction. This is not a high-tech affair - I'm going to use InDesign (with which I have a little experience, but I'm very much an amateur), but it's going to be bound and printed at a local printing shop, which means it will probably be laser printed. I'm looking for a serif font that is suitable for continuous text, extremely readable/legible even in modest sizes (say, 10-pointish, though the actual size will depend of course on the typeface), that works well with laser printing, and that complements the subject matter. There's a secret here of real use to you. Some Adobe Open Type releases, like Warnock, offer optical weights, so that instead of Warnock Pro regular, you can choose Warnock Subhead, which is a shade cleaner and thus will come out of laser printing as clean, or cleaner, than the regular optical weight. Minion Pro, Adobe Jenson, Chaparral--same option. If you find yourself setting 9 pt for long stretches, try ITC Stone Serif, it's a superbly readable workhorse I've used for many such jobs.

2006-09-07 18:26:12 • quickbrownfox
Jessica, re: your last question: It's a matter of flavor, of tone. Caslon is very sensible, reliable, some would say boring and old-fashioned. Warnock is more electric, crisp, modern. Another face like Paperback is more tough, hard-boiled, flat-footed. Others have more flavor, or different flavors, and this aspect of typography is what interests book typographers. It's not really different from display typography, but the flavors have to be subtle. I have read books set in Trump Mediaeval and it's a nice dark color, but the shapes are so peculiar and exotic that it's a little distracting. Your material is gritty 70's cop crime romances for women (to oversimplify). What is the appropriate tone to overlay on the text, via type choice? Some might say Century Schoolbook is appropriate here, or newspaper types. But your choice can also influence the tone. For example, if you chose Fournier, it is still readable and serviceable, but more delicate, old-fashioned, and might draw out the romantic element of the stories. The type's own tone or flavor could be a contrast or counterpoint to the tone of the writing. This might all sound like a nonsensical wine-snob discussion. It's like that; if you're into the details to the degree that you're using descriptors like warm, lively, quaint, aggressive, serious, then you're perceiving the qualities typefaces add to the text they're representing. If it doesn't matter, or if these influences are undesireable, use types that disappear: Times, Caslon, Baskerville, Janson, Caledonia, Sabon. I think it's nice that you're curious about this.
2006-09-07 19:47:31 • crossgrove
My suggestion: print out some type specimen (in high dpi, please) and see it for yourself. Two things to watch, among the many, are:
  • The overall color (read: blackness) and texture of the paragraph.
  • The 'butteriness' of the typeface, or how easy it is to read through the paragraph. Remember that fast reading aren't always appropriate, because text matter that is to be scrutinized by the reader (like a serious essay or technical material, for instance) will require a face that follow suit. Furthermore, there's that "We read best what we read most" adage, so this observation can be subjective.
