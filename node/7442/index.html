2004-11-03 16:11:37 • pattyfab
The way I look at it, current font prices, which hover around $30 per font, are a good value. I base this on the price of a sheet of Letraset in 1980 which was about $10. If you take into account inflation, this would be about $25 in 2004 dollars. Modern fonts are a much better value than a sheet of Letraset (unlimited characters and sizes, the characters are never crooked, never crack, space themselves automatically, huge selection, etc.) and yet they cost only a little more. Consider also that a fonts for typesetting machines back then cost hundreds of dollars.

I think that the perception that some have about font prices comes from the fact that you get them for free or almost free with operating systems, software bundles, etc. combined with all the free fonts given away by hobbyist font designers. Both of these cases are unfair comparisons to retail fonts. In the first case, this is like saying toasters should be cheaper because your bank gives them away when you open a new account. In the second case, hobbyist fonts are not usually very well made or complete.

Ultimately, the price has to be low enough that customers will pay it and high enough that font developers can make enough to make the effort worthwhile. It seems like it's there now.

The question I have is: If font prices were substantially lower, would substantially more people buy them? In other words, could font developers make more money if prices were lower? For instance, if a font is sold at half price, I'd have to have more than twice as many orders because my operational overhead would go up (more transaction costs, more potential support costs, etc.).

When a type designer designs a typeface or family for retail sale he/she doesn't know if it will sell or not. You just have to hope that the fonts will catch somebody's eye.

It is a speculative and time-consuming enterprise with no guarantee of success. I spend months designing a font or family. One particular typeface in my collection (I'm not saying which one) has been on sale for a while and hasn't sold one copy.

Now if this was the first font I had designed for retail I would have been very discouraged by the lack of any renumeration and would have probably given it up as a waste of time and effort. Fortunately, this wasn't the case, and I manage to make a living out of type design.

Fonts need to be not sold cheaply so as to encourage designers in their future efforts.
