Choosing a typeface for academical text

May 12, 2009 • daniel_poeira

I'm working on my PhD thesis and it struck me that I have been using Times New Roman for years without ever questioning the almighty manuals that most brazilian academics follow. Most ABNT (the brazilian ISO or DIN) manuals say that fonts must be either Arial or Times New Roman, but I'm willing to break this rule. After considering using a typewriter or a DOS font, I began more serious research on what substitutes could I use to make my text look exciting and smart. I started to research the subject and discovered that Yale solved the problem in a very simple way: they designed their own typeface: http://www.yale.edu/universityprinter/typeface/ I'd like to ask you all for more sugestions on what typefaces could be used for a PhD thesis, and if you know the rules used my other universities. Maybe even what faces were used in certain famous works, etc. Thanks, Dan

It is a mistake to think there is an "academic look." Different typefaces work well with different subjects. Even in your field, (Cinema?), I would choose different fonts depending on the subject. For example, if your thesis is "Romantic Comedies starring Doris Day," it would likely get a different font than "Film Noir, 1955-60." As to Times Roman, It too has appropriate and inappropriate uses, though today, it seems few think it has any appropriate uses. I think it is decried for two reasons. The first is it is used a lot, and boredom sets in "The Designer's Bored and the Reader Pays" might make a good dissertation topic. The second and more important reason is the font hasn't moved gracefully from metal to photocomp and digital. Probably because everybody who's capable is bored with it.

2009-05-26 23:11:29 • William Berkson
Well readability is or course important, but for academical texts it's also necessary that the typeface has certain specific characters -- at least in my opinion -- to name one example, superscript digits from 0 to 9 (and not only 123, like lots of typefaces) for footnotes. But preferably also optical sizes, so you can use a proper optical size font for footnotes at 6 to 8 points or so. Since these kind of texts potentially contain lots of numbers, like years and dates, old style figures would also be nice. But also tabular figures for things like tables.
2009-05-27 22:47:34 • Quincunx
I imagine you'll be using Microsoft Word; perhaps more important than the font choice is the proper use of paragraph formatting, leading, etc. If you want your paper to stand out, don't use Microsoft's default settings, which are typographically atrocious. Don't double-space after punctuation (if that's how you learned to type, use find/change to change double spaces to single spaces), don't type multiple spaces for indents, etc. Learn to use the paragraph formats pane, and make adjustments as necessary. You should read "The Complete Manual of Typography" by Jim Felici, and visit: http://www.designingwithtype.com/ Good luck!

Dear Daniel, ABNT really doesn't specify which fonts you should use. You should check your department rules (which might not follow ABNT). Actually, I learned that ABNT rules even accept numbered citations (which most people in Brazil don't even know). The choice you have to make is if you want to use free/opensource fonts or commercial ones, or if you will use a real typesetting software (like InDesign or Latex) or Word. I use latex with Xetex (for fonts) and the ABNT package, which does all the dirty formatting work for me (biblio, citations, quotes, front page, etc.), and so far I've been trying Arno Pro for text and Hypatia Sans Pro for titles, with good results.
