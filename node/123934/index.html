Does anybody use dingbats anymore?

March 19, 2015 • mjkerpan

I’ve collected bunches of dingbat fonts over the years as part of various packages and apart from playing with them to see what contents they have, I’ve never really used any of them. Simply put, they’re a lot harder to place and work with than vector clipart. Does anybody use them in day-to-day design work or are they mainly just fun little relics of the metal era?

Simply put, they’re a lot harder to place and work with than vector clipart.

Are they? Their advantage is they flow easily with the text and can be styled liked text. The trouble is of course to access the right glyph. But the more recent dingbat fonts have Unicode support and the glyphs can easily be accessed through glyph palettes or copied from various sourced and then pasted. Wingdings, Webdings and these fonts might be outdated, but on the flat icon style is currently very popular – on the web with fonts like Font Awesome or fonts from Symbolset, and in print with fonts like FF Dingbats.

I use checkboxes, scissors (to indicate a cut line), arrows, and a few others occasionally. But many dingbat fonts consist of symbols you seldom need.

I don’t think they’re hard to work with, but generally work best when tiny. If I need something bigger I’ll use something more detailed.

I originally made separate ornament fonts for Fontesque, but when FontFont upgraded it to OpenType, the ornaments were subsumed (i.e. buried) in the alphanumeric fonts.

Originally (1993), it was the early days of DTP, and vector art was having a heyday, as bitmapped images were much harder to process in the workflow.

Perhaps there is a similar situation today with regards to emojis—they being the an emergent form suited to a new medium (the high-res mobile device).

Even quite recently, there was a vogue for ornament fonts, observable in the MyFonts best-seller list. But ﬂourishes now seem to have migrated to be included in the fonts.
