I used to have a number of books produced in England under the War Standards act. These are cheaply produced, and have flexible card covers: somewhere between a trade paperback and a hardcover book. Colour printing appears to have been limited also, since the books have very plain covers. Perhaps colour was reserved for specifically morale-boosting books. Interestingly, the cover typography of these books tends to be very plain and almost generic, as if the designers were keen to signal their commitment to wartime austerity measures even if it made no practical difference in the cost of production. War Standards book production in Britain continued for some time after the end of hostilities.

I'm not sure what the practice was in England during the war, but in the US, "Nazi-made types" were banned by the Graphic Arts Forum, "a group of democratically minded graphic arts and advertising people." The banned typefaces included Bauer Bodoni, Bernhard Modern, Eve, Futura, Kabel, Neuland, Trafton, and Weiss.

Reference on this is The Book Collector's Packet, 3:11, December 1939.
