If you are really interested in designing swashed forms, you have to develop some skill with a calligraphy pen. That means practice of the basic letterforms, and the basic swashes, so that the hand and eye make them–in beautiful, sweeping strokes – rather than the brain.

There are lots of lefty calligraphers.

Normally, lefties keep the paper with baseline parallel to stomach and either “push” (risking smearing) or “hook” (cramps hand and wrist). Using a standard nib is difficult, and the purpose-built “lefty” nibs aren’t all that satisfactory.

In the years when I was teaching calligraphy, a few of my lefty students had success with this unusual approach: hold pen (in left hand!) fitted with standard “righty” nib (Brause, H&B), place paper with left edge close to stomach and right edge at far side of desk. It takes a while to get used to the change in perspective but the payoff is that your hand is completely relaxed and all the nib slant variations can be done with relative ease. Might be worth a few minutes of experimentation, at least. Doesn’t work for everyone.

On quite a few occasions, I heard horror stories from lefty students who had attended parochial schools in the early grades: the teachers forced then to write with their right hands, using adhesive tape to fix the pens in their right hands. Yuck.
