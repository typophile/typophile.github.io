Artistic solitude

December 29, 2005 • Kristina Drake

So here’s a question I’ve been mulling over – not exclusively related to type, but I think it relates, because I think there’s creativity/artistry involved in type design and typography. Just looking for other perspectives on this.

I’m a writer more than an editor. I find it extremely difficult to write poetry when I’ve been around others. I require a lot of solitary time to get my head and creative gears tuned to language, and if I’m not alone for long enough stretches, it just ain’t gonna happen. Being busy doesn’t interfere, as long as I’m alone.

What’s your take? Do you feel your creative work requires solitude? If so, what do you do to make sure you get it?

2005-12-29 04:07:14 • dezcom

I don’t need solitude in order to design type – or write, for that matter –, but I think other people may want to keep their distance some of the time. When I get myself into the middle of a design problem, I’m liable to get irritable and cranky until I’ve found my way back out again. If I have to leave off work on something part way through, I’ll be distracted and ill at ease until I can get back to it. But on the other hand I’m happy working away in the middle of a noisy airport terminal, and don’t mind having other people around.

It is a long time since I have written any poetry, but I suspect this would benefit more from solitude than type design or, for that matter, other forms of writing.

2006-01-12 04:43:08 • hrant

Which sort of runs parallel to my view that poetry for example is more about expression than communication.

It is a bit of both and neither, and most importantly something else entirely. Poetry is linked to song, and hence to music, and like music and song it seeks direct emotional and epiphanic connection. What happens at the point of connection varies, but very often it involves a shared insight that is arrived at by the reader rather than, as in expository prose, explained to him. This could be considered a kind of communication, but it is a whole lot more powerful than most of our nattering to each other.

Funnily, I’ve known a number of fine prose writers who wish they could do what poets do, and a number of good poets who wish they could do what composers do. I’m not sure who composers envy. Maybe angels.

Formal instruction of language could possibly even harm true learning I think.
