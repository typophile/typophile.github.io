I would like to recreate this 1940-era design, but would like to use the original fonts if possible. I think it is practical to structure this post using the FAQ available on this board. Can I help with the identification of my sample? As a matter of fact yes. Tell us whatever you know about your sample that might be useful. Information such as the country and age of the sample, and whether it is from a web site, or from a scan of printed material. (For example, if we know something comes from an advertisement published in the 50’s it rules out a lot of possibilities.) The sample is printed for the Dutch and/or Belgian market, considering the spelling of the months. It is not sure if it was actually printed in Holland or Belgium, as it could have been imported from a foreign source. The font was designed in 1942 or before. I think there are actually two fonts, one being sans-serif (the months), the other is a serif font (used for the days and the title: 'Kalender 1942'). Can I have a go at it myself? Identifont: resulted in a font from 1965. I cannot rule out that I have given a wrong answer on any of the questions as I am not experienced in typography. MyFonts’ WhatTheFont?!: I got 8 results but the were all negatives and did not resemble the sample. Furthermore MyFonts’ keyword search: Waters Titling proved a close match, that is however a contemporary design. Mike Yanega’s Script Font Identification Guide: I suppose it would be a Formal Non-flowing Script, but I could not identify the font I am searching for on his site. FontShop’s TypeNavigator: It resulted in Bitstream Carmina Light by Gudrun Zapf von Hesse (1987). However that font is contemporary and notably different from the font I am searching for. However, as with Identifont, I cannot rule out that I have selected a wrong option. [img:sites/default/files/old-images/kalender_3560.png]

Xcel is very good for making up tables like this, but has a limited degree of control over format compared to a graphics program. In a program like Illustrator, the degree to which you could match the spacing and arrangement of the type and other elements would only be limited by your ability to measure and observe.
