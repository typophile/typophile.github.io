Alternative to Minion/Myriad, Sabon/Syntax?

June 11, 2011 • Feliphil

Hello,

what would you propose as an alternative to the common Minion/Myriad- and Sabon/Syntax-combos?

The application would be mainly technical project reports, several dozens to sometimes a few hundred pages, typeset with LaTeX on A4 paper, in two columns or with wide margins, 10 to 12 pt, with loads of tables, figures, “in-text-markup”, etc. Sometimes read as PDF on screen, but mostly printed with “office” printers (laser). Also, as secondary applications, presentations, letters and similar “office” documents. European readers, German, English, French language.

The criteria would be:

  • Readability. Should be as easy as prose, as far as technically possible under these conditions. Whether these documents get actually read or not makes a significant “competitive advantage”. In the industry nearly everyone uses a certain “text processor” for these applications and certain “typefaces”, together with a certain type of “layout”, and as a result, the documents are simply never actually read.
  • Features: Both serif and sans should be available at least as regular, italic, semibold, semibold italic, with oldstyle figures and small caps at least in the regular weight, both upright and italic. A variant appropriately spaced for headlines would be nice to have for the sans.
  • “Garalde” look (and “feel”) for the serif. Preferably as bright as possible without compromising legibility too much.
  • The sans should also be suitable as main text typeface for certain applications.
  • Opentype postscript flavor for integration with LaTeX.
  • The typefaces in question shouldn’t be as common as Minion/Myriad or Sabon/Syntax, that’s why I am asking here. ;-)

I’ve had a look at the “similar fonts” at myfonts.com and identifont.com already, also at the families of “companion” sans and serifs at identifont.com. And while I do like quite a few of the sans typefaces listed there (Celeste, Charlotte, Freight…) I haven’t managed to find a matching serif typeface that I really do like. I definitely do not like the serif members of “corporate identity” families such as Thesis, Corporate ASE, Nexus, Generis, Meta, etc…

Any proposals?

Sincerely,

Wolfgang

2011-06-11 19:58:15 • Andreas Stötzner

Garaldes with italic small caps and regular/semibold weight are, among others, Lyon and Pona, MVB Verdigris has no semibold, but does have italic small caps. It is a rather dark text font though.

As for the Sans, any humanistic Sans would work. Nick Shinn’s Sensibility, Stella, Mundo, PF Centro Sans, Depot New. As well the Office 2007 bundled Clear Type fonts Corbel and Calibri are part of the humanistic “Frutiger-alike” genre. New and interesting is the stressed Ideal Sans.

Be aware that I am a layman too and this is not an expert opinion.

2011-07-01 06:36:00 • Andreas Stötzner

Epic seems to be more a reinterpretation of Sabon, with new italics, than any other Garamond-type. This is interesting, as somehow Epic seems to be more close to Sabon than the revival of Sabon Next. In any case I am not qualified to assess the quality of how the typeface is crafted (outlines, spacing, kerning, etc…).

As well it seems to me that Epic is different than other typefaces (and Sabon) in the size and position of the German Umlaut dots in comparison to the i dot. I mention it as it seems like we share the same mother tongue.

All in all it seems to me a useable typeface.

Something else that fits your criteria is FF Celeste, which has a [[https://www.fontfont.com/fonts/celeste|serif]], [[https://www.fontfont.com/fonts/celeste-small-text|small-text (serif)]] and a [[https://www.fontfont.com/fonts/celeste-sans|sans face]]. Celeste takes more inspiration of the modern era. It is a good text face though and very suitable. It does not need to be a Garalde. (EDIT: I just saw you mentioned Celeste in your original post. You said though that you did not find a matching serif you liked. That means you did realize that there are Freight and Celeste serif versions, but you did not like them, right? Just in case you did not notice.)

Did you try to ask the designers of the serif typefaces, which fitting sans they recommend?

As well there is no real agreement on how to pair typefaces. I think it was Hrant here in this forum who said once that one should not aim for all too similar metrics and x-height.
