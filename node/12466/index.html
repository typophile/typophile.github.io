2005-05-17 07:26:46 • hrant

A little light entertainment in the Arts section.

Overall, the article is just that, and I think John’s ‘moronic’ is way too harsh. It does give the idea that an interest in fonts can be quite enjoyable, which is great.

But these sentences were for me bad journalism:

“Graphic designers, who often have fonts to sell, can be cranky about where their p’s and q’s come from, and they seem to be getting crankier by the minute. Maybe it’s because there is less and less demand for original typefaces;”

First, I would think that actually a very small percentage of graphic designers produce and sell fonts. Second, if the demand for original typefaces was falling off I think I would have heard about it on Typophile. My impression is the opposite.

The tone of ‘oh those whacky people’ is standard journalistic fare, and not a serious issue, though I think it is shallow. Someone at the New York Times who makes claims about the type business should really feel a need to check their facts, which she obviously didn’t.

My wife is an economist, and in economics the term of contempt for an article that is substandard is “journalism.”

I just recently talked to a fine journalist who has covered one industry for much of his career, and he says he regularly has to explain to serious people that he is not like what they see on TV - he actually understands what he writes about. He thinks the big problem is that journalism school is the worst preparation for journalism, because you come out being ignorant of every subject.

This wouldn’t be so bad if as a rule journalists had humility and were willing to learn, and wanted to learn.

This particular article is such a relatively mild and harmless example of the problem that I can’t see getting too worked up about it; the phenomenon generally is a really grave problem,
