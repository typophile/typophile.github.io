Going back to the roots of a script?

September 19, 2005 • dan_reynolds

In Helsinki, I began thinking about scripts and their origins, and how everyone seems to have their own idea of what “authentic” might mean.

For instance, one designer told me that many Irish have seemed to conclude that Irish-script unadulterated by foreign influence (meaning English influence…) can only be found before 597. But then I thought that the Latin script itself was a foreign import to Ireland, which probably came after 400. So authentic irish script only existed for two centuries… a small window that closed 1600 years ago! And (some) people still long for that today!

Also, a designer of Hebrew types mentioned that there is newfound interest in the writing used on the Dead Sea Scrolls, which are almost 2000 years old. Do you have to go that far back to get “authentic” Hebrew?

For Latin, as we use it, I guess we only have to go back to the Italian Renaissance (beginning of U&lc writing and typesetting…)… what’s your authenticity date, for anything?

For Latin, prior to the cancelleresca corsiva hand (Arrighi’s corsiva writing manual, La Operina, published in Rome in 1522), you can probably go back to RUSTICA developed from the square Latin capitals, (though “Gallos Fragment” dates rustica to about 22 BC, and suggests rustica probably predates square capitals as a bookhand).

After that I guess Carolingian as mandated by Charlemagne (AD 768?).

2005-09-20 10:57:31 • timd

Authenticity has to be seen as a continuum, I think, and it is inevitably a relative term: a way of making distinctions between forms that are more or less embedded in the continuum. This can also mean different things for different scripts. So when, for instance, I talk about ‘authentic Greek’, I’m thinking in terms of an historical continuum in which certain details of letterform are present across multiple, historically evolved styles, and contrasting this to a recent and generally Latinised set of design conventions in which these details are missing. When Tom Milo speaks about ‘authentic Arabic’ he is not talking about details of letterforms at all, but about the fullness of the morphographic system of traditional Arabic writing, as contrasted to the simplified subset of letter interactions dictated by technological limitations of typesetting systems.

The question of ‘authentic Hebrew’ is an interesting one, because of course what most of us think of as Hebrew letters are actually Aramaic, and were imported into Judaic culture during and after the Babylonian exile. Prior to the exile, the Jews used a structurally identical but visually very different north semitic alphabet that was common throughout Palestine/Canaan, and which most Europeans think of as ‘Phoenician’ (although ‘Ancient Canaanite’ is a better term, I think, because it was widely used by different linguistic and ethnic groups, not just the Phoenicians). For a time, following the exile, the two alphabets appear to have been used side-by-side, but by the time of the writing of the Mishna, the Aramaic alphabet had come to be considered the only form appropriate to the writing of sacred scripture, and the old Canaanite script had ceased to be used by the Jews. So the ‘authentic’ Hebrew letterforms of the Dead Sea Scrolls are imported Aramaic letters, with a non-Hebrew pre-history and a post-exilic evolution that includes the formal square script which most people identify with Hebrew, the Rabbinic hand, and the informal cursive.

2005-09-20 13:30:41 • John Hudson

The question of authenticity of scripts hinges most on your view of traditions, and secondarily on type-specific issues.

So here are a few generalities.

  1. Traditions are greater than any individual in them. You have great individuals like Louis Armstrong in jazz, or Garamond in type, who seem to define a tradition. But when you look into the history, you always find that though they are great they built on a tradition that was already strong.

  2. There are no ‘pure’ traditions. All traditions have mixed and diverse influences on them. As John explains, the current Hebrew script is a variation on Aramaic, not ancient Hebrew script. The English language is a ‘pidgin’ or ‘creole’ French–native speakers of a Germanic tongue who couldn’t wrap their minds around French grammar.

  3. There are none the less great periods where a tradition gains a power and coherence that has a certain internal logic and force to it. And messing with that internal logic often results in something worse rather than better. To change it in a way that offers new possibilities that are as good as the old is quite difficult. So for example, it is possible to define ‘classical’ music, or ‘dixieland’ jazz. Similarly people define ‘Geralde’ ‘Transitional’ ‘Didone’ styles. It is easy to take such styles and change one aspect and have it look much worse. That is because they are well thought though and have a certain internal logic.

  4. In spite of the internal logic of a great style, people will always come along and figure a way that does offer new and good possibilities, even though they won’t be ‘authentic’ to the style. Traditionalists will cry foul, and they will be right, but also wrong, because the new style has something to offer. Also because of the on-going creative changes in styles, defining a style like ‘classical’ or ‘transitional’ will always be somewhat slippery and tentative.

In sum, there is such a thing as authenticity, and it is valuable. Understanding the ‘rules’ of a strong tradition is a strength. But violating the rules can also be a good thing, even though most often the result is worse rather than better.

I also treasure fine work that is a creative response to deep understanding of a tradition–Matthew Carter’s historically influenced faces are examples. So as far as substance, I am in agreement with you, John and Peter.

I do object to these uses of ‘authenticity’ and ‘hermaneutics’, though, because they are part and parcel of a dishonest and anti-rational campaign to bully people into accepting the odious philosophy of Heidegger, who uses the terms in this way.

Heidegger tried to base ethics on ‘authenticity’, as a rival to both traditional religious approaches, which do not derive obligations to others from the self, and the Socratic approach of ‘enlightened self-interest,’ which attempts to arrive at obligations to others from a true understanding of social realities. Heidegger tries to spin ethics purely out of the self, it seems. The effort fails, and in the process the notion of ‘authenticity’ is stretched beyond the breaking point.

When you use ‘authenticity’ in the Heidegger stretched manner, you are not necessarily endorsing Heidegger’s views, but I think it muddies the waters of rational discussion because such uses are ‘slipshod extensions’ (Fowler’s term) of a technical and tendentious term that you may not in fact agree with.

With ‘hermeneutics,’ the situation is much worse. Hermeneutics originally means the process of analyzing and interpreting the meaning of texts, particularly sacred texts, and does not address the question of truth or falsity of the text. Heidegger and followers extend the concept to analysis of society, tradition, human nature, etc. This Heidegger move slides over onto issues of truth and falsity, the territory of the sciences, and this is deliberate as Heidegger is anti-science.

The effort to build an alternative to science could be fair enough, except that science deals with evidence honestly, and Heidegger and followers deal with it dishonestly. Rational debate about the impact of evidence is the only way to resolve conflicting claims about reality in a way that transcends individual opinion. But doing hermeneutics in the Heidegger style does not brook rational discussion of evidence. The key assumptions of Heidegger are already smuggled into his hermeneutic method of analysis, and it declares itself the only valid method. This is, as Popper noted, a betrayal of the Socratic tradition of critical discussion in the search for the truth.

I observed this bullying, question-begging method at work when my daughter took a college course on literary theory. She was required to compare texts using the prescribed method. But the prescribed ‘hermeneutic’ method already assumed that texts have the primary purpose of imposing the will of the author’s power group on the reader. Actually the meaning of the text was not being discussed, only its political purpose, and that was not even open to discussion.

How is this relevant to authenticity in type design? I think it is misleading to call an honest piece of work that results from study of an historical example but is quite different from it ‘authentic’, as you, John, seem to want to. Galliard is not an authentic digital version of Granjon’s type, and doesn’t claim to be. And a person being authentic, in the sense of honest, free of pretense, is something different than the question of authenticity of a version of an old design. The person can be honest and unpretentious, and the result—Galliard—not authentic yet very good.

To me the conflation of ‘authentic’ and ‘good’ muddies the waters, and implies that violating the authenticity of an original design is bad. I think there is nothing wrong with messing with an old design, providing you are honest about it. The result may well be superior, especially for contemporary needs. Authenticity can be a valuable quality if the original had a strong idea and coherence, as I said earlier. But authenticity is way overrated as a virtue.
