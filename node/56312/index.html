Problem of the digital world

March 26, 2009 • Stefan Seifert

Hi everybody,

I want to share a thought I had at this moment. Well, I know, I am quite alone here, thinking that digital creation has some major general problem that Design in any analog form (hot metal, drawing ecc.) had not. Lets take it from another side ;-) Does anyone know the problem either in design with Beziérpoints or also in spacing letters that one unit to the left is too left, or one unit to the right, too right? Do you know what I mean? That sometimes it seemes that we just can’t find the solution we wanted because we always only have the choice between two ways, and not EXACTLY the one we wished to have. I hope I explained that.

Now my thought: In wikipedia (yet German version so it makes no sense joining this here) I read that the number that is the result of the “golden section” is an irrational number (1,618033988…) impossible to come close to with two rational numbers (so in the text). What does this actually mean? Bingo. If one knows that everything we are doing in a digital surrounding is down by 1 or 2, i.e. 1+1=2 and so on.. so that we always ONLY have sections (doesn’t matter in the end how fine they are) that are divided by rational numbers, or if you want 1+1+1 and so on. We can never have 1,618033988…! This may be philosophical but I truly believe it has some meaning.

Are we able to do beautiful things within a world that isn’t in fact able to catch the irrational (number)?

Salute Stefan

It’s called “the grid” and we’re stuck with it. :-(

For something to be repeated it needs to measured. A mathematical concept like a circle can never be perfectly implemented because we are constrained by the media we wish to represent it with. So even if I use a compass to draw a circle on paper, I can zoom in further and further until I can see the very un-round graphite scattered around the paper pulp. A circle is just an idea.

Ultimately we lop-off the bits that are deemed to small to distinguish, which hopefully conveys the idea without too much scrutiny. So if you are going crazy over a pixel here, a pixel there, maybe it’s best to zoom out a little and look at it the way it will be presented to another person.
