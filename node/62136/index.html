The use of the em-dash to signify a pause or trailing off at the end of a sentence may be an American phenomenon. Emily Dickinson used dashes everywhere in her handwritten manuscripts, and in some editions, editors left the poems that way rather than substituting more conventional punctuation. When you're writing with a pen or pencil, it's often easier to make a dash than to choose between a comma, semicolon, or ellipses. I don't know if Harper Lee wrote To Kill a Mockingbird on a typewriter or not, but if she wrote in longhand, she may have simply defaulted to dashes, and her editor chose not to change them. I was always taught that in type, em-dashes should be "tight," but that may be discretionary, like the spacing after a period.

The convention in dialogue in movie scripts is, IIRC, that an ellipsis indicates that the speaker doesn't finish the sentence. A dash--usually a hyphen--indicates that the speaker is interrupted.

The use of the em dash in this manner was far more common a couple generations ago than today. For some reason, this style of punctuating has fallen out of favor. But The Chicago Manual of Style, 15th ed., still covers this usage:       §6.90 Indicating sudden breaks. An em dash or a pair of em dashes       may indicate a sudden break in thought or sentence structure or an       interruption in dialogue. (Ellipsis points may also serve this purpose;       see 11.45.)

When I worked in a sociolinguistics project on a large-scale corpus of spoken French in Ottawa-Hull in the mid-80s, we had a transcription convention no doubt derived from the "interruption" function of the em dash: a "false start", where the speaker would start and then stop and restart their thought, was signalled by a double hyphen (our closest approximation at that time, when the Macintosh 128 was the new gizmo on the block).
