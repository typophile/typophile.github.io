You can do that easily in your font editor software. Just cut (ctrl+x) the outer contour of your glyph and use the preview mode. The countershape will be filled in black. Make any necessary adjustments and paste the other contour back. But I think you should also take a look at other fonts. Look at their curves, the BCPs… see what works and what doesn't.

James, I personally don't think that's completely true. A lot depends upon HOW you look at it and what you EXPECT to see. If you see it upsidedown (think about a brochure laying on a table), you'll be surprised about how it will look then. Then it might not 'look good' anymore. Invert a character and you'll have the same effect. A lot of mistakes you will not see in small sizes. But use it big and you can be shocked. You expect to see a perfect font, so automatically I think the brain 'corrects' the mistakes. It's the same with grammar mistakes. Some you don't see because your brain correctes them, but they are there. It's in the nature of fonts that there are a lot of ways on how to apply them. If a font contains some very small curve-mistakes that would mean that you limit the user in their creative application of it, because they could not use it bigger. That a font might have been optimized for small sizes does not mean that you can't use it big, or inverted, or vertical, or only the counterpunches, etc. I for myself do that very often in my work. So for specific applications I agree with Dukes statement, but it seem to me to be only half of the truth. In this statement I miss the 'but…'. ;-) Ren

2004-01-20 00:00:20 • Nick Shinn
I'm not sure what the 'in reverse' test would tell you about a design intended for typical black on white use, other than that it probably isn't optimal for white on black. Luc(as) de Groot and Erik Spiekermann have both developed fonts that are specifically intended for white on black or backlit signage, and these designs are optimised for this purpose. In particular, if you have two signs side-by-side, one black on white and one white on black (or similar high contrast colours), you need to make the letters in the white on black slightly heavier and sturdier in order for them to look the same weight as the black on white.

In the development of ClearviewHwy our research showed that fonts for Negative Contrast applications (Black letters on Light backgrounds) had to be slightly heavier and a bit more tightly spaced than the those for Positive Contrast applications (White letters on Dark backgrounds) to appear the same. One of the problems with current regulatory signs in the US is that they use a set of fonts designed for Positive Contrast, in Negative Contrast applications, resulting in the type looking too light. >I'm not sure what the 'in reverse' test would tell you It's not about the functionality of the font, but about the functionality of the design process. Designers get so close to their work, they lose perspective, seeing it negative is a strategy for getting a little jolt of objectivity. Two related techniques I use: Rotate the character 90 degrees (useful for examining relative contrast of vertical/horizontal stems and the curves between them) Look at a print-out in a mirror, or held up to the light from the verso of the printout. Do the same, upside down. (The paper that is, no need to stand on your head.)
