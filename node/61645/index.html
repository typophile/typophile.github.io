I can’t answer the whole thing right now, but will add something.

Aside from the specifics of type, any kind of custom element distinguishes a company or publication even if it’s on the subtle side. This can include a logo, trademark, a music jingle, lettering, type and so on.

An example of not so subtle would be the Coca Cola logo. It is so ingrained into culture that just small elements of that design are recognizable to non-designers. An example of subtle is the background music of McDonald’s TV ads. Little hints of rhythm and melody allude to it and then you already can hear it about to resolve into their trademark jingle. The subtle look of a typeface that may not jump out at you right off becomes familiar after a while thus reinforcing the brand.

2009-09-03 17:51:00 • Charlie Zinno
A commissioned typeface can certainly help support a brand identity, but a big reason that typefaces are commissioned nowadays is to save money. A site license of an off-the-shelf typeface for a large company can be quite expensive, and can have legal ramifications if it is improperly done. Commissioning a typeface can cost less in many cases and take care of the potential legal issues.
2009-09-03 18:07:05 • Mark Simonson
Types have been commissioned nearly as long as printing has existed. The first punchcutters were artisans who cut punches for stamping letters and images into coins, jewelry, and other stuff, and printers started commissioning them early on. The earliest example I can think of is Aldus Manutius commissioning Griffo to cut type for him, but it seems likely that printers in Mainz were doing the same thing before it was sacked and everybody left town.
