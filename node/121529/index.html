Questions about non-letter side bearings

November 24, 2014 • stanev

Hi everybody,

I’m working as a font developer, usually my job is to take a Basic Latin set (alphabet, sometimes also numbers and some punctuation or symbols) and turn it into extended Latin, Cyrillic etc.. - a full set. I usually work with sans serifs, slabs at the most. I use Glyphs 2.

The problem: I’m not exactly sure what the side bearings (sb) of non-letter symbols should be - the side bearings of @, #, 1, 2, &, . : ; " and so on. I realise every font is different and there are no rules set in stone, but I know that sb of the alphabet symbols are based on H, O and n,o (we’re taking standard, not fancy in any way ghotic fonts), Is there a similar system for the non-letter symbols? Based on the period, for example? How do you go about your fonts? Please share experience and thoughts. :o)

Numbers would use the key glyph “zero” and their spacing would be dependent upon their character set, either tabular or non-tabular. Symbols in general can be set up using the key glyphs they would normally be used with, and the same principles apply.
2014-11-24 16:34:24 • George Thomas

I look at how these glyphs appear in text strings of what I consider to be the most typical usage. For instance, the @ symbol most often occurs in email addresses, with lower case letters either side. While I weight /& as a lower-case glyph, I set its sidebearings (and kerning) for cap acronyms such as “V&A”.

It also helps to study how the sidebearings are handled in solid, well-established typefaces similar in genre to what one is working on.

2015-01-16 11:23:55 • stanev

  1. What’s the “original” symbol? Do you mean the registered trademark? The standard for copyright is to be full-size and centered on the cap height. For registered, it is to be a superscript position. Plenty of people get these wrong, but those are the correct choices for almost all fonts.

  2. Which specific symbols do you mean?

  3. Non-italicization is open to debate, for sure. But I certainly have a list of symbols to not italicize, borrowed from work I did at Adobe.

  4. That’s a choice by the designer or foundry, but in general I don’t see much point in it. Why put in bogus character support? I’d only do that if I had a specific client or customer need for it.

  5. I don’t think I have ever seen such guidance thoroughly enunciated. But you might check Microsoft’s character design guidelines, or Karen Cheng’s book. Maybe the old Monotype type design standards have it, I’d have to look. (That is internal and unpublished Monotype material. Assuming I’m not confusing Mono and Lino here.)
