2008-04-05 01:28:21 • Michel Boyer

more questions than answers

That’s inevitable. The more closely you look at any busy scene, the more complicated it becomes, and the more myths accumulate. The name of Garamond (or Garamont if you prefer) is thick with them. But what is extraordinary is how much more is now known with some certainty about about the types being made in France during the period 1520 to 1550, when Paris and Lyon were among the greatest European centres of printing, compared with what was generally available when Updike first published in 1922, and how many of the original documents on which existing histories are based at second or third hand still survive and have now been published.

That date of 1510 for Garamond’s apprenticeship to Augereau, which has been around since the 18th century, is based on the reminiscences of a member of the Le Bé family, punchcutters and typefounders. If it is to be believed at all, it makes more sense to take it, as Vervliet suggests (in his piece in Typography Papers, 2007), as the date of his birth. Then other events fall into a more believable pattern. Learning punchcutting in his twenties from Augereau, who is burned in 1534 for heresy. Then working for another punchcutter, Pierre Haultin, and for printers in Paris during the later 1530s. And finally a certain date, the contract for the royal Greek types for Robert Estienne, in 1540. To get that job, he must have by then have shown he could do good work.

More than one punchcutter was making types based directly on the De Aetna of Aldus in Paris around 1530, and these types are the models for the classic ‘Garamond’ style. Augereau is one of them. Among the other candidates are Francis Gryphius. And Constantin (whose first name we don’t know). Who? Read Vervliet. Colines never quite took to this new model.

And as for what we think we do now know about Garamond, look at John Lane’s scrupulously researched and well-illustrated essay in the handsome specimen printed in 2005 to launch Adobe’s Garamond Premier Pro. If copies are still around, perhaps you can get it if you buy the font. But by now maybe Adobe could consider putting a version on line? It would be appreciated.

One of the most recent of Vervliet’s essays lists and shows the 51 typefaces that appear in Paris during the first three decades of the sixteenth century: ‘Early sixteenth-century Parisian Roman types’, De gulden passer, jrg 83 (2005), pp. 1–88. And there are some others that go back to the early years of the century.
