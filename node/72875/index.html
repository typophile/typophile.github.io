2010-08-04 02:36:29 • phrostbyte64

Good on you.. here’s some things to think about:

Identify your cause. Are you into politics, aid work, the local arts scene?

Start networking, especially if it’s local causes you’re into. You mind find someone from your existing client base is also involved in charity work, can you do anything through them? Or if you’re viewpoint is more global, get online, there’s loads of volunteer networks out there, here’s a couple:

http://www.volunteermatch.com.au/ http://idealist.org/

People may be posting their design needs on there.

Secondly, think about what you want to get out of it for yourself: Are you looking for an opportunity to flex your creative muscles away from commercial pressures? Think hard about that because you’re still offering a design solution to someone else’s problem, regardless of whether there is a fee involved. If you have an idea that’s waiting for the right client to come along, why not get on and develop it anyway? If you open it up to collaboration (make it open source in other words) then probably the right recipient will come along through the glorious interconnectedness of the interweb.

Finally; fees… If you’re serious about offering specialist design services to the non-profit and charitable sector as a commercial venture (for want of a better word) or as a second revenue stream, then you should treat it like any other target customer base.. build a portfolio, make a name for yourself, look for ways to get on their rosters. research who commissions design regularly, and which agencies do the work; that will give you a taste of what might be involved. But even then, be prepared for marked differences in corporate culture from what you may be used to in a wholly commercial context – not least decisions by committee, politically correct considerations and stakeholder interest.

Alternatively, you mentioned fundraising. It’s possible that you could approach your chosen cause with your great idea, and offer to raise your fee yourself – through normal activity like marathons or stunts or whatever, but be careful this doesn’t just look like self interest. For most of Joe Public, justifying design fees would be a tall order, even heavily discounted ones. Better to look at where your chosen cause gets their funding from and make an approach to collaborate with one of their existing supporters (but make sure you have your cause’s approval first). You could also think about match funding: both you and your cause agree to meet half of the fee each – they go to their supporters for the extra, you donate your time.

As for fundraising itself, look at the work that the people behind building letters (http://www.buildingletters.org/) have done. You could do something similar, that is to say, work out a way that your design skills could generate donations and build a system to involve the type community, in other words, get entrepreneurial. Forget taking a cut for yourself though.

Last but not least, to cover your costs if that is really important, work out what proportion of your working month you will spend on Pro Bono work, and hike up your normal fees to compensate.

2010-08-04 07:12:41 • Anthony Noel

It’s extremely difficult to run an “ethical” business, because that’s not the purpose of business. It’s the purpose of business to make a profit, and of government to regulate that process so that it’s not too damaging. Jane Jacobs discussed the mutual exclusivity of this relationship in Systems of Survival.

Self-regulation is corrupted by vested interests.

People (“consumers”) part with their money for price, quality, or service; virtue (e.g. fair-trade organic) is a harder sell. Given the difficulty, you need exceptional business skills to make a “good” business work, and you will always be putting yourself at a competitive disadvantage to those who play by the existing rules, or cut corners.

Therefore it is better for idealists to make a healthy income within the system, and use that wealth (and/or their professional skills) as 100% gift to idealistic organizations run completely by volunteers.

I would be wary of supporting “establishment” do-good organizations with salaried staff, because these organizations generally rely for their funding on sponsorship (from various corporate sources and government bodies), as well as having an infrastructure to maintain, both of which compromise their integrity. Having to meet a “quota” of sales or income is, as Jacobs argues, fundamentally non-ethical. IMO, grassroots shit-disturbers make the most difference.

“They sentenced me to twenty years of boredom For trying to change the system from within” –Leonard Cohen

2010-08-04 15:09:50 • Nick Shinn

you’re still offering a design solution to someone else’s problem, regardless of whether there is a fee involved.

Yeah, sometimes designers doing pro bono work assume that their client will give them complete creative freedom. But clients – even pro bono ones – expect you to come up with a design solution that they like and which effectively communicates their message. They don’t care if it’ll look nice in your portfolio or not, and they won’t hesitate to say “I don’t like that design.”

So it’s important to pick your clients carefully and to discuss expectations before you start work. Show them examples of your work to make sure they’re comfortable with your style. Explain your working method. Find out if you’ll be working with an individual or a committee. Ask them about their budget for things like photography, and ask who the printer is going to be and what the printer’s expectations are (they may be printing the job for free or at a low cost).

And if you’re expecting a lot of creative freedom, make sure they agree to that from the start of the project.

It is very rewarding to invest my daily work in projects that help people. I feel like my work is leaving a lasting legacy - not for me, as fonts can often be unnoticed and taken for granted - but for the millions of people trying to hold on to their language and culture while becoming citizens of the information world.

The cost of this decision was not small, for me or my family. Because most of the funds given directly to SIL go to supporting development projects, most of the staff serve as unsalaried volunteers. 2010-08-05 09:47:20 • gaultney

Nick, “corporate” needn’t be pejorative. It can mean well-organized, paying for experience, being willing to hire professional services as needed (IT staff, accounting, even designers), etc.

If we think that, say, human rights groups fighting discrimination should be exclusively done for free, there will be a big imbalance in our society with government and business being well-funded and having tools the nonprofits don’t have. A few highly motivated activists with weak institutions will have a hard time achieving results. And what about helping homeless people or providing low-cost health services to poor communities (less of an issue in Canada but a big one in the U.S.). Only volunteers? It’s hard to have sufficient scale and impact with all volunteers. They burn out (unless they are wealthy already w/o need for a “day job”) and the organizations will tend to not have organizational structures in place to make best use of resources of donations (even donations of time from volunteers) due to burnout and turnover.

If there is an emergency or short-term crisis, yeah, volunteers can make a huge difference. But if we’re talking about dealing with a long-term problems, we need long-term action. And if we’re talking about advocacy against, say, well-funded corporations on an environmental or health issue, you usually have to pay some people to succeed. I think in Canada about 7% of the economy is from nonprofit activities and in the US a little more.

BTW, in most places “non profit” means that any money raised above costs must go back into programs that help people, rather than into the pockets of owners or investors. So if a nonprofit runs a surplus one year, they have to do something useful with it that fits their mission.

Good point that the phrase is lame - defining something by what it’s not. Some other phrases that avoid that problem, but are not great either are “civil society organizations,” “private public-serving organizations,” and (for local groups) “community-based organizations.” Also “private voluntary organizations,” but that term is very misleading since a lot of PVOs have paid staff and only the board is voluntary.
