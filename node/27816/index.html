2006-08-22 15:04:01 • dezcom

Some of the letterform features are a result of the medium: slate, which is very brittle. But apparently it’s also very weather-resistant and that’s what makes New England headstones so special: they last for ages - so the letterforms are very old (in the case of Copps as old as the 17th century I think). So the disjoin in the bowl of many of the “g"s and that one-sided “f” are probably done to combat the brittleness. I also remember seeing a wonderful “B” with it’s middle join not reaching the stem.

BTW, that last “i” is funny: it looks like the guy put a placement mark for the tittle, but never actually made it.

2006-08-22 19:47:18 • hrant

do the graves really point north?

They did seem to point north more ofthen than not. Lots of graves were opposite so they pointed south. There were fairly few east or west pointing ones. Pointing/facing north might make sense because it would reduce the number of freeze/thaw cycles the carving would be subjected to. Or there could have been another reason - like a desire to point towards water maybe. Mostly I metioned it by way of advice for future photographers because they might want sideways light - or more somewhat more diffuse light to help capture more detail. Hrant is right that a really cloudy day might give you dull looking images. Of course a light diffuser might help…

The “y ligature” is “ye” or “the”.

More from the Oxford dictionary (on my Mac):

ye 2 |yē; ðē| adjective pseudo-archaic term for the : : Ye Olde Bookshoppe. ORIGIN graphic variant; in late Middle English þ (see thorn ) came to be written identically with y, so that the could be written ye. This spelling (usually y e) was kept as a convenient abbreviation in handwriting until the 19th cent., and in printers' types during the 15th and 16th cent., but it was never pronounced as “ye.”

2006-08-23 03:41:20 • Mark Simonson

As far as I know the “y” was subbed in for the thorn directly because the English started off by importing their type from Holland, and were too cheap/lazy to add proper sorts for the thorn. By the time Caslon started making type from scratch it was too late. And I think they initially used a vertically flipped “y”, before no longer even bothering with that extra effort.

This is quite a prominent case of technology affecting script. If they had been as concerned with their culture as they were with the fast buck, the English script would probably still enjoy the richness and elegance of the thorn (and eth).

Here’s a ref: http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternative_spellings_of_%22the%22
