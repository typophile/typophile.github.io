2009-09-22 14:01:46 • Tim Ahrens
My point was two-fold: - In the 90s there were still grotesques, neo-grotesques, geometric sans and even humanist sans being designed and released that did not fit any obvious "neo-humanist" mold. What it is fair to say, and may be all that Tam was trying to say, is that some particular style of humanist sans became much more popular. However, one would need to define the characteristics of that style. - I would be very interested in how one defines the characteristics of this "neo-humanist" style, as to whether said characteristics are particularly "new" in the 90s or have antecedents in the 80s and earlier. I'm expecting the latter, but it depends on the definition. For my part, as long as we have somebody calling themselves "Hypatia" in the thread, I'll note that the past decade or so saw a resurgence of geometric sans, which was largely a revival, stylistically: Avenir came back into vogue, and new typefaces like Gotham and Neutraface were innovative and well-executed, yet also in the style of previous work. (My* own Hypatia Sans was intended to be part of that geometric sans resurgence.) Cheers, T * I'll continue to call it "my" typeface as a shorthand, and because the main ideas and all the outlines of the upright were my doing, but I owe a huge debt to Robert Slimbach for constant input and guidance, Miguel Sousa and Robert for doing the kerning on the upright faces, and now Paul Hunt for finishing the italics.

Hmmmmmmmmm… the postings in this thread are quite interesting and informative and yet my original question was “Which kinds of typefaces are likely to succeed the Grotesques and Humanist Sans in the future?" As no one actually gave his views about this particular topic, I thought a bit about it and this is what I came up with: 1. We could have a new type of Sans Serifs based directly on 'almost upright' handwriting and enhanced structurally according to the principles of Graphology. 2. We could alternatively have Sans Serifs based on curvatures and other structural details found in industrial objects, such as cars and even architectural buildings. 3. The Geometric Sans on the other had might evolve from experimental typefaces and ideas such as those presented in Letter Spirit: http://www.cogsci.indiana.edu/farg/mcgrawg/lspirit.html 4. New type design algorithms will be invented and their use will give rise to a whole new species of typefaces, Serif and Sans Serif. I look forward to hear your own views about it.

2009-09-23 17:16:32 • Hypatia
Pointing out obscure experiments done by cogsci or tracking the multitude of typefaces available seems like an unreliable way of anticipating what typefaces will "likely succeed" (I do think that is a good rephrasing of this question). A better way of looking at this is by seeing how the neo-grotesques/neo-humanists address the problems that exist in typographic settings. I still think that Scala Serif/Sans as well as other early [[http://typophile.com/node/29924?|super families]] are the most notable contemporary examples of how the sans serif can be used in a complete typographic system. They address the problem of the lack of harmony that exists in a setting where non-linear reading occurs (as mentioned by [[http://www.keithtam.net/documents/sanserif.pdf|Tam]] p.12), without being overly ambitious in creating a sans serif that aims to bear the full burden of body copy. I think that when one neglects looking at how typefaces are being used, the explanation of their success—or potential success—becomes too speculative.
