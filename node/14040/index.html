Metaligatures

July 21, 2005 • hrant

(No, not ligatures for FF Meta...) One of my fascinations in the field of "visible language" are shapes that stand for more than one letter. It's interesting to consider how they combine, what parts are "important", and how the recognition of the components is weighted (intentionally or not), when alone or even more interestingly in the contexts of words. I can't stop thinking of this topic, even in banal places like Dairy Queen, where I daydream weather a logo that's a "D" with a tail would work (or maybe it would drive their loyal customer base back to the mountains.) Below is something I did when working on Trajic notRoman, years ago. But more than show what I've done (not much anyway, really) I'd like to collect and compile examples you guys might have seen. Please let me know of any metaligatures that you think are worth thinking about! (Oh, and as always I'm open to terminological suggestions as to what to call these things.) hhp

2005-07-26 12:45:36 • oldnick
Here's a logo I designed in 1977. http://www.oktransportation.com The idea, which is a mainstay of the "compressed" ligature, is that where there is a repeated vertical stem, one is removed in a kind of "negative kerning" exercise, and the remaining stem does double duty. In a logo, this provides a point of visual interest. There is a similar idea in the "fi" ligature, where the ball of the f serves as the dot on the "i", but the purpose is different: it could be considered as the removal of visual stutter. Another category of compressions: as in the Avant Garde ligatures, where the purpose is to enable justification of tight-set headlines.
