2010-03-19 16:21:33 • Stephen Rapp
My impression is that the crossbar of “Æ” also isn't broken in most native handwriting. So the handwritten forms are just a guide, and for typographic forms different considerations come into play. To risk pointing out the obvious, the crossbars of the uppercase letters A and E are almost always simple horizontal lines, while this is not the case for the lowercase a and e. So it makes sense to me that different rules may be used for the uppercase and the lowercase. Speaking as a non-native and not having actually tested this, I think I would find both forms of “æ” equally readable.

2010-03-29 16:47:29 • Nick Shinn
People generally read what they expect to read. I know and it took me some time to see the trouble with "Gretkzy". On the other hand, if someone writes Montrèal instead of Montréal, I see the mistake right away and in general I find mistyped French accents in English publications quite disturbing. With diacritics, we don't seem to see what we are expected to read, and we can in fact be quite demanding, though they tend to be relatively small. With œ there is an additional difficulty that makes the question more interesting. Contrary to æ where it seems that students are taught to write the character in a single stroke, I was never taught to write œ in any special way, as a single character. I don't think that students neither in Québec, nor in France, are taught to write œ differently than oe, i.e. as two separate letters. The distinction is, so far as I know, introduced only in carefully edited texts; it is typographical. When you receive a file filled with œ, you can almost be sure they were put there by Word or some other text editor, while typing. There is no œ character on the keyboard. As a consequence, we are not trained to recognize that character, and it is thus not obvious that the Arno Pro Italic "æuf" would not be judged acceptable by many customers. I would thus feel like experimenting and establishing statistics to see what percentage of people would see no problem with æufs in Arno Pro Italic. I don't think we use the same mechanism as when we read "Gretzky" when it is written "Gretkzy".
