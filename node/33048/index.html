From Eats, Shoots & Leaves by Lynne Truss: Until the beginning of the 18th century, quotation marks were used in England only to call attention to sententious remarks. Then in 1714 someone had the idea of using them to denote direct speech, and by the time of the first edition of Henry Fielding's Tom Jones in 1749, inverted commas were used by printers both to contain the speech and to indicate in a general, left-hand marginal way that there was speech going on. (There follows an example of this that I'm sure would be mangled if I tried to transcribe it here. Speech was in single quotes, and every line break within a quotation required another open quote at the beginning of the next line.)
