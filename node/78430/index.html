MoMA Get's Faces

January 26, 2011 • sevag

Hello dear Typophiles,

I just received this via email from my much admired professor Dr. Klimis Mastoridis and would like to share it with you as well.

“MoMA has just acquired 23 digital typefaces for its Architecture and Design Collection. Some are of everyday use, like Verdana; others are familiar characters in our world, like Gotham, which was used in President Obama’s election campaign, or OCR-A, which we can find at the bottom of any product’s bar code; and others are still less common, but exquisitely resonant, like Walker or Template Gothic.”

You may read the full article here.

2011-01-26 22:39:40 • Andreas Stötzner

How can a museum “acquire” something with no capital value? This is listism, and not much different from the commentary of anyone who has written an opinionated type history, from Updike to Blackwell.

The return on investment—the value added—from which the museum profits in admission fees and collateral revenue (catalog sales &c.), is produced by the curators.

I am in two minds of how good this is for type designers. On the one hand, being propped by MoMA enhances our status, but on the other it devalues our work. Let’s face it, “acquiring” typefaces is a cheap way to put on a show. I don’t see any House Industries fonts on the list — their licence (for, say, the brilliantly Postmodern Chalet, a suitably modern arty type) may have been a bit prohibitive, perhaps gearing fees to gallery admissions!

It also undermines the position of the museum, because it opens the door for virtual curation. Now anyone can be a curator.

Fontshop’s Best of 2009 was “hand picked” – will its Best of 2010 be “curated”? I hope so!
