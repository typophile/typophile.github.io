Excessive vertical scaling ... when did this fad become popular

October 5, 2006 • soren_olsen

I make logos. A potential client of mine has a logo that has been ‘designed’ this way:

1. Write the company’s name in Argo

2. Scale the result 200% horizontally

3. Throw away the dot over i, or crop the ascenders, or both

4. Voila!

A logo like this may seem trendy at first (to some people), but 10 years after, it is hopelessly outdated.

Could you please tell me (I am gathering ammo for the designer/client meeting): when did we first see these examples of bad typography? Must have been in early 90s or what?

2006-10-05 19:38:15 • soren_olsen

when did we first see these examples of bad typography

Progress is often the result of incorporating “mistakes”, which are made possible by new technology, into the canon. That is where the paragraph indent came from, to go way back in time. Negative leading and fitting of text in the 1970s were made possible by photosetting. Scaling distorion is a desktop-publishing phenomenon, because it’s easy for computer-using designers entrusted with typography to “squeeze to fit”. And hey, it looks new, different, and interesting – or at least it did 15 years ago, if you didn’t find it horrifying.

But perhaps your client is into a retro-vernacular-1991 kind of thing?

“Ransom Note” typography was popularized by Jamie Reid in his designs for the Sex Pistols in 1977, and used by Neville Brody in The Face magazine (before he designed his own typefaces).
