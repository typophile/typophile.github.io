I always felt Herb Lubalin was set free on his spacing from the 1950s and the start up of the typositor. That was freedom from lead type. It allowed creative folks to place their letters where they wanted to - without using the knife.

I knew many designers from that era - and can even remember a few late night discussions of “letterspace tighter – tighter – tighter…” *

*It wasn’t called “kerning” back then -

2007-08-29 17:58:52 • Hector Muñoz

Yes, there is overlap between ‘Finer Points’ and ‘Elements’–I’m sure Bringhurst knows Dowdling. Also I think Dowdling I think overdoes it on the tightness of word spacing. –The main point of the book is to combat overly loose word spacing, which was apparently common in the metal Linotype days, when the book was written.

However, it does go into more detail on a number of issues. It’s a nice book, but not a ‘must’.

Incidentally, [[http://www.oakknoll.com/detail.php?d_booknr=92771&d_currency=|Book Typography]] by Mitchell & Wightman is excellent and quite comprehensive on type setting for book design. It’s a bit expensive, but does cover everything that Dowdling does, I think, and a lot more.
