Hand-lettered in 1954. This is near the end of about two decates when this style of lettering was popular. Looks like it was made by a broad nib pen rather than a brush, has a thick and thin/high contrast character, has a crisp, precise appearance, and is regular in spacing and slope. So what digital fonts meet the job description. None completely but here are a few that are part way there.

Robert Hunter Middleton’s Coronet Bold from 1927 http://www.myfonts.com/fonts/mti/coronet/

Quick, designed by Howard Allen Trafton in 1933. Bauer sold it as Quick in Germany, as Trafton Script in the US, and as Etoile in France. http://fontsinuse.com/typefaces/31841/quick Versions: Parfum http://www.myfonts.com/fonts/rmu/parfum/ Pacific Script http://www.myfonts.com/fonts/scholtz/pacific-script/ Trafton Script [Edited: Deleted link to House Of Type version of Trafton Script.] There was also a version from Solotype with the name Trafton Script but I can’t find it.

Another 1930s design: Troubadour http://www.myfonts.com/fonts/rmu/troubadour-pro/ Digitized by Ralph M. Unger who also did other similar fonts like Carla and Ronde.

Duktus http://www.myfonts.com/fonts/eurotypo/duktus/
