2011-09-22 19:31:11 • aluminum

As for the term ‘brand’ I think most people in marketing/design industries have very mixed feeling about it. The term has been abused greatly to refer to visual marketing when many feel that the real definition of a corporate brand falls more into the public perception camp.

I am of the latter and feel that brands aren’t created but rather earned. We can make a visual asset to help represent that brand, but if that brand hasn’t been earned, then it’s merely lipstick on a pig. Sometimes that works. Sometimes it doesn’t.

I think a lot of businesses use the term ‘rebranding’ now as a couched way of saying “we treat our customers like crap…let’s make a new logo to see if we can fool them a bit longer.”

On the subject of typefaces and typography for political campaigns:

Hoefler and Frere-Jones added serifs to Gotham, making a slab-serif version, at the request of the Obama campaign: http://www.typography.com/ask/showBlog.php?blogID=261

On the subject of branding as a form of manipulation:

I love typography and design but I hate advertising and commercialism. This probably explains why I mainly design books — and ideally only the interiors at that. In this capacity, design is not really about selling anything, but only providing for a suitable, unobtrusive, and subtly allusive reading experience. At the risk of sounding corny, it is not about making money; it’s about making something beautiful. Design can be used, as it often is, to manipulate people in order to make money or spread ideas. I feel that though this is design’s most obvious use, it is often a misuse. However, I will gladly design pieces that do exactly this for causes I believe in.

“Branding,” by the way, literally refers to heated metal stamps (brands) that were used to mark cattle and even slaves as belonging to their masters. Brands are deliberately created to cement the identity of their owners into the minds of consumers or the general public. So, to get back to Alaskan’s original question, what might interest me is a politician without a brand — one whose identity is strong enough that not every poster has to match in order for people to understand who it is evoking. Of course, this might never work. It does contribute to having a unified group of supporters if the campaign has a strong visual identity. But was Obama’s first campaign, which was widely described as effective “grass roots” marketing, really so grass-roots if the identity was determined on a national level by a single group of designers?

Do campaigns, in other words, need branding? Did George Washington have a “brand”? Is there a better way of designing in support of politicians than by stamping a logo on everything?
