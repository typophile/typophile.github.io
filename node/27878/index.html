…so, you need to fine-tune your client presentations in such a way that you get your clients to request the new typefaces ;-)

On a more serious note, Mr. Red, there are other ways to show fonts to clients without having them built into your presentation boards or slides. These include specimen book pages, print-outs from the online PDFs that many designers and foundries have on their websites, or jpgs taken from the letter-setters that most foundries offer online too.

Try to get your client to sign-off on your overall concept first. Then say that, in order to get the message that they have already approved just right, you’ll need to select the perfect typeface [together]. Oftentimes, at this stage, clients will be willing to look at typesettings outside of the of client visuals, since they have already signed off on the photography, layout, etc.

Also, if you are just putting together first round comps, you can always use a typeface you already have in that, and then try to get the client to sign-off on a more specific font later. This has worked for me quite often. For instance, you can substitute Adobe Garamond with a more specific Garamond later, Futura with a newer geometric sans, etc.

On another note, some foundries do offer trial versions of their fonts, but not the same way your stock photo supplier offers images. FontShop seems to offer one weight of a family as a free trial download about every month. Many foundries will give you fonts on a trial basis if you call and/or e-mail their sales and/or marketing departments and let them know specifically what you are looking for.

For instance you could call them up and say, “Hi, I’m working on a new ad campaign proposal for Coca-Cola in the US Northwest market. We’re making new billboard designs and want to show the client our layout in Avenir and Candice. Can we use trial fonts for this presentation?”

This comes across much better (and as much more believable) than an e-mail that says, “Can you e-mail me your newest fonts so that I can spec them for some project, etc.?” Once you buil up relationships with foundries and/or designers, this process gets easier, too. To a degree, its about trust, openness, and communication.
