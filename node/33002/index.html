April 12, 2007 • joeking_tp

What is letterspacing?

How do you kern letters ‘by eye’? Jim Krause in ‘Type Index’ on page 90 says to ‘try focusing on sets of three (adjacent) letters at a time;’ ‘squinting helps accentuate “dark spots” and “holes” within the word-’

‘Thinking in Type’ by Alex White on page 365 says to flip a headline upside down so it’s shapes become noticeable or by imagining equal amounts of water poured between characters. In the example he gives he has the spaces between letters as grey on the x height of the letters. So you are supposed to compare unequal/strange shapes. He uses ‘Avelas’ as an example. I am not sure why he doesn’t include the ascender or rather the parts of the letter that extends past the x height in the equal spacing calculation. Why does he not include the bowls and the negative space of the letters in the calculation (e.g. the ‘A’ has a space - the ‘counter’)? He provides a chart of most significant kerning pairs on page 364.

‘Av’ Would equal spacing be optically okay spacing? If you measured from the ‘A’ to the lowercase ‘v’ would that be the same for all letters?

Try doing the Krause method by printing the work out and covering up all but the three letters you’re working on. Space them evenly. Slide the paper over one letter. Repeat. After you get them all, pull aside the cover sheets and tweak by looking at the letters as a whole.

I use the squinting method, by squinting you lose the ability to see colours and the eye uses rods which discern black, white and grey. The aim is that you don’t see dark or light groups but a generally even grey. Since you cannot (well you can, but not easily in running text) alter the shapes of the letters you have to accept the counters and work on the spaces between them, all the while bearing in mind that you don’t want to affect the readability of your piece (so, for example, you have to accept that an rn combination is going to have an open space and by closing it you will raise the chances of it being read as an m). Optically even does not equal mathematically even, so your Av sample wouldn’t hold true, for example, Av might need loosening while ve might need closing. I suspect that practical experiment, as in so many things, is better than reading about it.

2007-04-15 12:21:06 • joeking_tp

I am not sure how blurring a hole (squinting would cause some blurring) would ‘accentuate’ the hole.

Blurring is indeed what you want, although the eye can discern black and white in low-light, mostly you see shades of grey, while losing the sharp edges where characters meet spacing. With a black on white headline by squinting you should be able to compare the lighter inter-letter grey patches and easily pick up areas whether they are dark or light. Depending on size and use, you would normally close up the large light areas and open up the dark areas so that the areas are equal(ish), bearing in mind that you don’t want to reduce (or increase) the inter-letter space to such an extent that it hinders reading. 90pt at arms length might be too close/big to get the effect. It doesn’t work for all, an alternative might be to look at it upsidedown.

“90” looks ugly in this case? Or does it clearly differentiate between the numerals? Which is more important, harmony between characters or the message they carry?

2007-04-16 08:32:44 • timd

Watch out for light/dark balance onscreen; it’s not the same as on paper. Light-emitting, low-res monitors give a very different impression, halation is actually an issue. But you certainly can use photoshop, or your eyes, to approximate a blurred view of type, and that is useful for finding light or dark spots in the overall texture. Older techniques: print a sample large, then walk all the way across the room to look at it. Or, get a reducing glass and look through that.

I think what’s crucial about the idea of letting the eye be final arbiter, is that so many people prefer to rely on logical rules and formulas of one kind or another, and the more you rely on some kind of logic, the less you are seeing the actual fit of a typeface. The eye is what will be using the type in the end. It’s like designing a chair. Coming up with an elaborate rationalization and concept for why a chair will be comfortable is no substitute for sitting in the damn thing to test it out. We use these tools with our bodies. The smoother, more comfortable they are, the easier it is to use them subconsciously and do our work (or reading or dining).
