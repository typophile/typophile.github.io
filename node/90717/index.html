2012-03-24 22:04:17 • hrant

Hrant sort of acknowledged this, but I feel compelled to point it out again: the term “talus” is a complete Hrant neologism.

Don’t get too hung up on it. No professional type designer that I know uses the term. Most won’t even have heard of it (and of those who may have encountered it on this forum, I can’t think of any who have chosen to adopt it). I don’t even know anyone who bothers to refer to “internal leading.”

In metal, the measure of the body of a piece of type was it’s point size (and vice versa). When set without any intervening leading — or, as it was called, set “solid” — the distance between baselines was the same as the point size.

[This is assuming, for the sake of keeping things simple, that this is a setting of the same font on all lines.]

For practical reasons, metal type faces did not usually have extenders that reached to the very top or bottom edges of the piece of metal. So there would usually be a little extra space, such that descenders on one line and ascenders on the line below didn’t crash. (Hrant has chosen to coin a special term for this extra space.)

For the Linotype machine, this extra space had a prescribed minimum. I don’t know if foundries or Monotype had their own standards for this.

In digital type, there is a virtual corollary to the body, but there is no physical constraint or requirement to keep parts within the bounds of the body. Different type designers take different approaches.

Also, since we’re no longer stacking little metal blocks upon one another, the virtual body has come to have less and less technical significance. In Postscript Type 1 fonts, it was still pretty explicit (in the Ascent and Descent values). But with TrueType and OpenType, there are multiple values describing various vertical metrics, all used by different platforms and applications to determine vertical relationships.

So, these days, setting text without any explicit linespacing measurement is not necessarily the same thing as setting metal type solid and you may or may not get a value equivalent to the point size, depending upon the platform, software, and preference settings.

Of course, if you explicitly specify 11 pt linespacing (or leading — however the app chooses to call it), then your application should definitely set it with a baseline-to-baseline measure of 11 points.

2012-03-27 13:39:44 • Don McCahill

Of Talus Hrant says ‘I didn’t make it up. I wish I remembered where I got it, but I’m pretty sure it was from a “professional type designer” :-/ sort of book. It might have been DeVinne.’

It’s a normal French word, and I think the text was more likely one by a man called Fournier (his Manuel typographique, 1764), someone who could be called a ‘professional type designer’. Talus is the French for ‘slope’ or ‘angle’. He is writing about punches and matrices and metal type.

‘Le Graveur a encore une chose essentielle à observer; c’est de ne point donner trop de talus aux lettres, soit dans l’intérieur par le Contrepoinçons, soit à l’extérieur par la lime. Ce défaut procure au Caractère, à mesure qu’il s’use, un épaisissement desagréable.

In his English translation of Fournier Harry Carter makes this:

‘There is another thing against which the punchcutter should guard, that is giving too much shoulder to the letter, either with his counter-punch or outside with his file. this defect gives the character an unsightly thickening as it wears down.’

‘Shoulder’ is Joseph Moxon’s English word for the sloping profile of the punch.

The punch must have a sloping profile or you would never be able to get it out of the copper after striking it to make the matrix, nor would the type cast freely from the matrix. (But as Fournier says, if the angle is too obtuse, the type thickens up as it wears.) This profile naturally creates a slight distance between the face and the edge of the body of the metal type, and unless it is allowed to project outside the body in casting and is rubbed away (something that was often done laterally with round letters like o, which would otherwise have too wide a ‘set’) it makes a natural distance between, for example, the descenders of one line and the ascenders of the line that follows. Which is generally what the designer needs to allow for in order to avoid the clashing of descenders and ascenders.

Talus is just the word French professional punchcutters like Nelly Gable use today for ‘slope’ when they talk about their work.

2012-03-28 16:15:47 • Don McCahill

OK, terms for the parts of metal type became confused with the changing of technologies. I grant that there is a sense of ‘shoulder’ that was sometimes used in modern images of metal type, as Bill says, for the area “from the edge of the face to the front edge of the shank”. A beard and a shoulder are different things, or so I should hope. ‘Beard’ was used in the period of the hand press for the projecting square edge of the type which could pick up ink and spoil the impression. So the projection was often trimmed or ‘bearded’. Later, with machine printing, ‘bearding’ no longer made much sense, so the square projection was given the name ‘shoulder’ (perhaps mistakenly, but just possibly because it was a name with typographical associations that was no longer in current use).

‘Shoulder’ in Moxon’s original sense, which is how Carter was using it (and although Fournier had a word in French, he was clearly puzzled to find an English one for it), applied only to the sloping profile of the punch and had little (though still something) to do with the detail of the cast type. Talus may seem to be an archaic survival today in typographical terms, but I know that it is still current among punchcutters because I have often heard it used by Nelly Gable when she talks about her work, and her predecessor Christian Paput used it too. The word puzzled me and drove me to try to find out what it was that they were referring to. I was delighted to find that it meant something to Hrant and was therefore in a kind of current usage, even (via this thread) an international one. It seems to me that once one works it out, the talus does indeed account, rather neatly, for the built-in distance between descenders and ascenders on which this thread has touched.

An image that I hope is worth sharing, if I can get it to work.

It’s a small detail from the plate made by G. Quineau for the Description des Arts et Métiers in about 1696, showing the system of related type bodies invented by Sébastien Truchet for the romain du roi. The heading to the plate reads ‘Calibres de toutes les sortes et grandeurs de Lettres’, meaning something like ‘measurements of all type body sizes’. The detail, maybe the first time anyone ever tried to show such a thing, gives you a diagrammatic view of a single piece of type, showing the ‘œil’ or x-height of the letter, the length of an ascender and a descender, both of which, of equal length, are called the ‘queue’ or ‘tail’, and then, above and below the letter, a small ‘empty space’ or ‘vuide’ (modern French ‘vide’) which was left on the face of the cast type in order to stop the descenders from above hitting the ascenders rising from below.

Didn’t always work in practice, of course, because getting the right distance between the lines of type depended on having moulds that fitted the matrices correctly, but at least this was the idea. (The whole plate is shown on page 49 of the catalogue we made for the exhibition at Lyon in 2002, Le romain du roi – la typographie au service de l’État.) In digital fonts, what is clear and precise is that all fonts have a defined baseline and a defined ‘em’. In a specification such as 12/14 the first number is the scale of the em to be printed, and the second the distance between baselines, end of story.

Bill, please forgive me for relentlessly chasing terms that happen to have survived from metal type into the different world that this thread is mostly concerned with. I won’t go on with it for ever. But I originally contributed because I was fascinated to find that traditional terms like Nelly Gable’s talus, which I hear her use when she cuts a punch in steel, are still in use. And yes – to answer your query – the talus is indeed seen right through the process, from the punch and the matrix into the cast type, and it normally produces a vide that makes for a slight inter-linear spacing in metal type.

In traditional typefounding, in which the steel punch is struck into copper to make a matrix, the strike naturally has the same talus or slope. Since the cast type has a sloping profile that exactly reflects the shape of the punch, it has a ‘space’ above and below the face which gives it the slight built-in leading which seems to be the subject of the current debate. (In many traditional designs, the tops of ascenders and capitals align, but admittedly many current designers have gone back to the reduced caps of the De Aetna, etc., which provide some extra spacing between lines.) You can get rid of the slope in lead type if you want to by using a mould that puts the face at the edge of the body and rubbing away any surplus sloping metal from the casting. But that’s a chore that’s best avoided. As I said before, you do this laterally when casting letters like o to get them to fit well, but see what trouble Fournier made for himself – in the example I gave – by doing this clumsily in the vertical dimension.
