How much uniformity in old-style revivals?

January 17, 2007 • Nick Shinn

The fastidiously authentic Garamond Premier departs from its model in one noticeable respect. For while the details, shapes, and proportions of individual letters are extremely faithful to the originals, their proportions relative to one another are not–especially with regards to x-height.

For instance, in Garamond’s type the top of the bowl of the “d” is much lower than the top of the “u” or “g”. The same feature is apparent in other old styles, such as Jenson’s.

Revivals of the early 20th century repeated this trait, e.g. ATF’s, and some played it up–Goudy’s Garamont, of course. Robert Slimbach’s revival, while containing a similar degree of micro-detail and variability of detail (compared with, say, ITC Garamond) is much more uniform in x-height.

This feature of metal-era old style type design may relate to the imprecision of letterpress printing. For even in the best examples, there is an element of “bounce”, in which the letters are not precisely aligned vertically. By varying the vertical dimensions of different x-height characters, bounce is camouflaged; at least, that’s my theory.

By the same token, digital type, with its great precision of placement, coupled with the constraint of vertical alignment zones required by hinting procedures, favours a uniformity of x-height.

The situation raises a number of questions:

  1. To what extent is the varying x-height of the original old style types an integral part of the design?
  2. Does it have any reading benefits?
  3. Was it done for the reading benefits, to camouflage bounce, for some other reason, or a combination of reasons?
  4. When a revival emulates certain qualities of a previous technology -- as almost all do, where does the redesigner draw the line?
  5. Would there be any merit in taking things a step further and using Contextual Alternates to simulate a degree of bounce, and at the same time incorporate significant irregularity of characters' vertical dimensions?

And in answer to your particular questions.

  1. [To what extent is the varying x-height of the original old style types an integral part of the design?] It seems to me that the Old Gang very consciously made each size just right for its optical presence on the sheet (I’m thinking of the dramatic differences in Janson from 10- to 12-pt, for example). Maybe they were so astonishingly good at what they did, using nothing but simple hand tools (and did they even have crude magnifiers in the 1400s?), that the slight differences in size from character to character were just as intentional.

  2. [Does it have any reading benefits?] Based on my experience with perhaps 30 books in the past few months, I say absolutely yes! Hence my musings in the quoted text of my previous post. These were set in linotype, but I think the effect is very similar.

  3. [Done for reading benefits, camouflage bounce, other, combination?] Can’t comment on their intentions. Results very compelling, however.

  4. [When a revival emulates. . . where . . . draw the line?] Yowza. Here’s the crux of it all. I just made another comment over on the “setting poetry in Electra” thread because I think many digital versions of classic types are too emaciated looking. If you look there you’ll see a sample of digital and metal Electra (12-pt) side by side. I would welcome the “imitation” of ink squeeze in digitally-composed and offset-printed books in these faces as I think it would improve their readability.

As a book designer rather than a type designer, for me it is all about the content of the text, how readable it is, and how much the type helps to convey the spirit of what the author is saying, far more than the fetching upswing of a particular glyph. Apart from foundry specimen pages, these letters never exist outside the context of each other!

Therefore, the more readable and accessible we can make these “revivals” in the range of 10- to 12-point, the better. And in the case of treasured favorites of mine like digital Bembo, Electra, and Caledonia, the digitals have never begun to approach the warmth and readability of the metal. Therefore I have used them a lot in metal but rarely in digital. Is it too simplistic of me to think that this warmth could be easily attained?

  1. [Would there be any merit in . . . Contextual Alternates?] I don’t understand the implementation of the technology, but it certainly seems appealing to me.

Nick, as you know, I talked about this in my talk at TypeCon 2006 on my Caslon revival in progress.

Generally speaking, in my experimentation I found that variation at the x-height can be good if there is systematic reason for it. For example both Adobe Garamond (I don’t have Premier yet) and my Caslon–and the original Caslon, though not with full consistency–have the stems at x height ( of nmr etc) going higher than the overshoot height of the o.

Also the cross bar of the t and f can be lower than the top of the x.

If there is some visual reason in the design why the d overshoot should be less than the o and b, sure go for it. But that kind of stuff didn’t work for me. (Italics are another story.)

Such variation can look junky, and less readable, which is what I found when I tried more variation, without a strict visual logic to it.

For example, Bruce, I do not think that the jumpy baseline of the worn Linotype machines is responsible for the warmth of some of the metal types. After experimenting with it, I became convinced that it is a false trail. Of course that is just one person’s opinion, but I have actually tried it, though not extensively.

where does the redesigner draw the line?

There is no general answer to that question. What to adopt and what to change is a creative decision that depends on the designer’s goals, analysis of the problem, and aesthetic and craft sense. That’s why superb revivals like Slimbach’s Garamonds and Jenson are not so common.

The bowl of the one story sans g is often raised from the baseline so as not to crowd a short descender-eg Frutiger. It doesn’t look weird because the visually the hooking descender and the bowl are a unit, and you don’t get a direct comparison with the bottom of the g bowl and o etc.

The serif on the d can be lifted or smaller in comparison to the bhkl, also so as not to crowd with the bowl. It could be that Garamond lowered the d’s bowl for the same reason, but maybe Slimbach thought it was a bad idea because of the break of x-height uniformity.

What font has a p bowl raised off the baseline? I don’t know about that. In an old-style p the foot serif is relatively flat, unlike the triangular serif at the top of the d, so there isn’t the crowding problem.

I do it a lot. Well, OK, maybe not off the baseline, but raised on to it with no overshoot. Here’s something current. It’s necessary because things get a bit tight in the bottom half of the p, compared to the top of the d, say, as the descender is shorter than the ascender, and the serif is wider and on both sides. The inconsistency of the “overshoot” doesn’t matter, as the presence of the descender makes that irrelevant.

2 humble cents from a neophyte:

The slight variations in the text do, I think, make a difference, particularly when reading poetry; “perfect” digital text is so efficient and easy to read that, at once, it lulls the reader to sleep and encourages skimming. The slight variations (of all kinds) in metal - whether intentional or by happenstance or whatever - have the effect of slowing the reader down. And whereas digitally-based fonts seem like counters holding/standing for content, the original metal impressions somehow close the gap between content/meaning & typeface. A least, this is so for me.
