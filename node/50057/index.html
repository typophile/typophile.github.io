2008-10-05 23:59:39 • Merlin

The term “glyph” came about in the late 1990s I believe. It means something like “sort,” but does not have quite the same connotations. In modern (Unicode) usage, a Greek capital “Alpha” can be the same “glyph” as Latin capital “A”, but they are different characters. It would be odd to use the term “sort” here; the notion of having both Greek and Latin in a single metal font would be unusual. Caslon Greek would be thought of as a different font in the old days (“old” meaning before the 1990s). In type cases, they would be thought of as different sorts only because they would be in different cases. But if you ran out of A’s, you could use an Alpha & not loose meaning.

And while all logotypes are sorts, not all sorts are logotypes. “Sort” doesn’t specify what is unique about logotypes (two letters on one body). It would be the same as saying we don’t need the term “ligature”, because “sort” or “glyph” is sufficient. (see note)

By the way, there were logotypes in systems other than Linotype. With foundry type, a Q with a long tail required a “Qu” logotype.

It may be that in the past few years “logotype” has come to mean a logo consisting only of type, I don’t know. While words can change meanings even in formal usage, that would seem to be a bit far. Sort of like expecting everybody to know what “dudine” means.

(note): A “ligature” means tied letters; by extension, used where elements of the two letters touch, such as the glyphs fb, fk, etc. The individual characters in logotypes (old usage) were not tied; nothing touched, hence not “ligatures.”

2008-10-06 14:37:46 • charles ellertson

In addition to the two-letter sorts that Charles has explained, I believe that “logotype” also referred to catchphrases and other complete words set as a single metal sort. (Hence the derivation of the term.) A printer’s glossary I have from 1924 gives the definition: “Logotype – Two or more letters, or a whole word, cast on one body.”

In some cases, a company would have had it’s name specially cast up as a single sort, perhaps in a variety of sizes, to facilitate the setting of advertising material, especially once the practice of custom, identifying marks became a common part of business practice. This might have been more frequently a zinc etching rather than a proper type-metal sort, but I think the term was still applied.

I believe that’s how we’ve inherited the usage that is pretty common today, where a company’s distinguishing wordmark is referred to as a logotype.

– Kent.
