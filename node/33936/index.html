Trying to think about this more deeply: It seems that the graphic-vs-textual logo decision is now the central one here. I personally prefer the former in general, but as others have mentioned a text-only logo might actually be preferable; as it often does, it leaves things to the customer’s imagination, which can be great. In this case especially it also has the distinct advantage of being less costly in terms of cash, materials and/or potential eventual profits. On the other hand: hopefully the savings aspect does not over-ride everything else; and a graphic approach might in fact be better if the aggregate customer perspective is focused enough for a graphic logo to nicely focus things to the benefit of sales. Each of us can and does have opinions about what “social butterfly” and “enfant terrible” connote, and some of us even feel comfortable gauging a general connotation among the prospective customer population, but the degree of divergence in the connotation is qualitatively much harder to gauge, and this I think is what would determine whether a graphic or textual approach would be better. Ergo: if you believe that the cost of having a graphic logo is too great in relation to the possibility of a graphic approach being ineffective or even confusing due to a large divergence of what those label names connote, then you should go with a textual approach.

What happens then however is that you ideally should have two different fonts for the different lines. The highly convenient thing about Perla here is that it does have two cuts - one dark and one light! A question however is whether the light cut is strong enough to hold its own. So you might do better to choose a different font for one of the lines.
