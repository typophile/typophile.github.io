Measuring success

October 10, 2006 • Kristina Drake

Hi,

My unit (publications) is participating in a University-wide endeavour to measure success. In short what this means to me is that I have to provide my supervisor with the criteria by which he is to measure my personal success, as well as suggestions on how to measure the success of our unit as a whole.

So I’m wondering how you all are measured or evaluated in your jobs. What elements are taken into consideration? It would be most useful for me to hear from people who hold editing/proofreading and page layout positions. Obviously, certain factors will be company-specific (ensuring that the success is in line with company goals), but what else? Is your performance evaluated based on how correct the publications are? How many typos slip by? What about measuring success at being creative?

Any input would be appreciated!

As a freelancer, my clients evaluate me on how quick I do it and how much the print shop charges them – they usually trust me to “do it right” already. If I get the job done on schedule and make their print shop bill as small as possible, they come back. If not, not.

My accountant gives me a performance review – if I’m making more money than I’m spending, I’m “successful.” :-)

In your case, I’d be excessively wary of standards like “make x typos and you’re sacked” – if you’ve got the job, it’s because you passed their editorial test before they hired you, and it’s a moot point (we all make mistakes sometime – we’re human!). I suspect things like cost control and “customer service” are much more important (to them) measures.

2006-10-10 23:11:44 • nicholasgross

Thank you. I guess my employment situation isn’t typical of this industry. I’m not working on contract; I’m unionized and permanent. “Measuring excellence” is part of the university’s new 5 year plan.

For sure the measurements can’t be like those for data entry, which is precisely why I was having difficulty. However, I quite appreciate my supervisor’s point: if I don’t provide the guidelines by which to assess my success, someone else will, and it may be along the lines of x number of mistakes gets me canned.

I’ve included below what I came up with. Thanks for the input, and of course, I’d be happy to hear comments… I have a feeling this will be a work in progress.

Thanks, K.

Measuring my success

On message Do I edit and design with an eye to clarity, accuracy and unity of message? Are my designs consistent with University branding and visually compelling for the target audience?

Are my designs clear and readable? Do they contribute to or hinder the reader’s ability to find answers and information? Does the publication’s design/language fit its intended purpose?

Are my writing, editing and designs up to the standard of excellence the University wishes to project?

Accuracy Do my publications contain errors and typos? How many? What (if any) impact do my errors have, financially or otherwise? Have I done my best to ensure accuracy?

Adaptability and responsiveness Am I flexible and able to adapt to new/changing technology, methods, and target audiences as required, and within reason? Do I set reasonable limits?

Do I use creativity in solving problems?

Do I strive to improve? Do I improve? Does my work address/correct issues raised with previous publications? Do I correct my mistakes and take steps to ensure similar errors do not recur?

Overall contribution How am I to work with? Am I flexible (again, within reason) with regards to work schedules and tasks? Do I assist the team or do I hinder? Do I set necessary limits? Do I contribute to the unit’s success?

Measurement tools (Keeping in mind that opinions will vary.)

1-End user surveys: recruiters, students, counsellors, graduates, applicants, professors, etc…

2-Feedback from telephone services, or other front-line services about ease of use, clarity or other questions and comments resulting from the publication’s language/presentation.

3-Casual feedback from other areas.

4-Feedback from peer competition events. (I believe K. was submitting the faculty booklets in an award competition of some kind. This kind of response can tell us how we measure up against our peers, whether others in the industry think we’re doing a good job or not.)

5-Self and peer evaluations.

6-Checking admission statistics against the unit’s target areas for adjustments to be made in the future. (What has or has not had an impact, where do we direct our efforts?)

2006-10-11 19:15:41 • Kristina Drake

Might be a little verbose – I’d be tempted to turn “contribute to or hinder” into simply “contribute to” – but certainly a good start.

The “Are my writing, editing and designs up to the standard of excellence the University wishes to project?” makes me a little nervous, though: if there’s an extensive style manual that is rigourously adhered to, fine, but if there isn’t, it become a pretty amorphous concept.

I’d also want to see something in there that also mentions cost-effectiveness: for example, you find a way to do a great piece by creatively imposing it to print one side one colour and the second side four, instead of both sides four, and save a ton of money in the process. Doesn’t hurt to qualify being able to improve the bottom line.

As someone once remarked to me, “The person wanting this job done will want you to do it fast, right, and cheap. Always tell them they can only have two.” Might as well get credit if you can get close to three…. ;-)

FWIW, I don’t envy you having to do this: I know it’s the backlash from Enron, but trying to wring “accountability” from intellectual and creative processes seems to frequently result in a lot of BS. That’s not to say that there shouldn’t be some way to objectively measure the difference between two employees, but all this palaver about “excellence” simply strikes me as the latest management fad proposed by someone else with a book to flog.

Anyone else remember TQM?

2006-10-13 17:16:04 • Linda Cunningham

“Measuring excellence” is part of the university’s new 5 year plan.

Did someone from the business school come up with this slogan? Sounds like MBA-buzzword-BS to me…

if I don’t provide the guidelines by which to assess my success, someone else will, and it may be along the lines of x number of mistakes gets me canned.

“x number of mistakes” is not a measure of success: it’s a measure of failure. Héctor is entirely correct: measuring success depends entirely upon setting goals and achieving them. Goals, according to standard MBA-buzzword-BS criteria, must be…

  • specific;
  • measurable; and
  • attainable.
Each project is different; each project has different goals. Ideally, the person who hands you the project should draw up his or her OWN list of goals, and your success should be measured against how well you do or don't achieve THOSE goals. If you don't have a clear idea of what you're supposed to accomplish in the first place, you shouldn't be held accountable if the end product doesn't meet (or exceed, to continue the buzzword-BS argument) the requisitioner's expectations.

Of course, this approach requires extra work from the people who want work from you, and it requires that their goals meet the criteria listed above. Chances are, even if you were to institute this regimen, you would have a difficult time getting complete, accurate and realistic information from your internal clients…which is why I said, at first, that it sounds like MBA-buzzword-BS to me…lofty, commendable, proactive, but basically marketing hype, and nothing else.
