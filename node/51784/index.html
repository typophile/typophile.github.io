Paying Twice For The Same Fonts

November 17, 2008 • RahimSnow

The Problem Many of us have wanted to license a complete font family but cannot afford to do so at the present time. So we purchase only one or two weights that we really need.

But later when we are able to afford the whole family, we are told that we have to either purchase the whole family at the full price (essentially paying for the single weights we purchased earlier all over again) or we can get a nominal discount.

Fontshop, for example, allows you to pay the difference if you buy the single weights and then you buy the whole family later inside the same month. The issue with this is that if you can’t afford to purchase the whole family right now, that situation is unlikely to change inside a month. It may take you many months or even a year or two to be able to do it.

Though I am sure this makes a lot of business sense from the point of view of the business, it is not as friendly & helpful as it could be from the point of view of the consumer.

One Happy Solution In the economic climate we all now live in, I want to point out [[http://www.vllg.com/tastetest.php|Vllg.com’s “taste test” policy]] as one example of what a truly upgrade-friendly consumer-oriented purchasing policy looks like. Their policy states:

If you purchase a single weight (or more) of any typeface we offer, then return later to buy a family set, we will credit you the amount of the original sale.

How the program works

We know that you might want to try out one or two weights of a typeface to decide if it’s right for a project you’re working on. While the MudTyper and PDFs are very useful, there is nothing quite like trying out a typeface yourself.

So, you visit vllg.com and license your “trial” font (or fonts) and work with it for a while. A couple of hours, a couple of months, or a couple of years.

If you decide you want to get a family set, just come back to vllg.com and purchase a license online. Send us a note on the checkout page or via email and we will deduct the original cost of your “trial” fonts from your family order.

A policy like this encourages us to purchase more fonts from them to try out, knowing full well that they will allow us to pay the difference if we want to come back and get the whole family, even if it is years later.

What do you think of this “taste test” policy? Do you know of other upgrade-friendly type foundries that are willing to do what they can to help the customer not pay twice for the same fonts?

This is quite funny – while shopping I think I noticed a valid analogy to what I’m trying to explain. Please bear with me.

In the office supplies section of the supermarket I saw some pens for sale. One pen costs €1.90. The pens are available in three different colours – red, blue, and black – each available as single pens. There’s also a package with three pens in the three colours, one of each colour. This package costs €4.90.

Customers have two options. Either they need the three colours, or they don’t. Actually a third possibility would be that they need one colour straight away, but they’re not sure if they will need the two other colours in the future.

Adding the price of the three separate colours results in a price tag of €5.70. As mentioned above, the package with the three colours is priced at €4.90. There are a number of reasons for this price difference of €0.80.

  • If you buy the three pens in one set you only go through the cash-out system once, so it takes less man-hours, effort and administration to move the same amount of product.
  • Part of the price includes packaging and presentation in the store; having the three pens in one set uses less packaging, less effort to put in the store, and takes up less display room.
  • As buying the two first pens already costs €3.80, the third pen only costs €1.10. This may persuade the customer to buy the three pens in one package anyway, even if he/she is not sure he/she will need all three colours.

This means the decision is all up to the customer.

  • The customer buys the three pens in one package because of the reduced price, even if he/she may not be sure to need all of the colours. But hey, the last one costs less, so it's a bargain.
  • The customer only buys one pen. If he/she wants to complete the collection he/she has two options. Either he/she ends up buying the two other colours as well and will have spent €0.80 more, but those expenses are spread over time which means they have a lesser impact on his/her budget. Or the customer decides to buy the whole package, which is a bit silly because (a) he/she now has four pens, with two of the same colour and (b) he/she will have spent €1.90 more.

The analogy with fonts works on all counts.

What I’m about to write now is based on deduction and my experiences with working with FontShop. As I’m an independent contractor I have no actual numbers to support my theory, so I won’t fall in the same trap many others have fallen into and present them as facts. This is what bugs me most – for all the theorising and speculations in this thread most of you simply don’t know. I at least am willing to admit this, and I’m better informed than most of the non-insiders (the insiders being Village, Sharon, Nick, Jens, Ivan, Stewf, Co, and Thomas Phiney; I hope I didn’t forget anybody).

Basically it all comes down to pricing and profit margins, and system of distribution. A foundry is a more direct link to the customer; there is an extra step when purchasing through a vendor/reseller. This additional step means the profit margin is less, because the foundry specifies at which price a font package should be sold and the type designer keeps the same royalty share (BTW this means the “my purchase supports the artist so you get the most money” is a non issue, really). The lower profit margin should be compensated by the higher volume of product sold. Why do type designers sell through vendors/resellers then and not directly? Read Typographica.

I think it’s easier for a foundry or a co-op like Village to grant their customers extra discounts like the Taste Test scheme because they have a bigger profit margin per product sold – they are likely to have sufficient financial elbow-room, and implementing this kind of scheme is less complicated as they move less volume. Mind you, I said it’s easier, but it’s not always possible. Certain foundries prefer to set their prices lower to have a competitive edge, and as a result their profit margin is not sufficient to support this kind of scheme. It’s all about offsetting operational costs against profit and volume – how many people does the foundry or co-op employ, what are the development and maintenance costs of the website, how much technical and sales support does the foundry provide, etc.

Large vendors have much, much higher operational costs. For example just take a look at the sheer size and complexity of their websites, accommodating tens of foundries and tens of thousands of fonts, all with their different set-ups and constellations. All these foundries have their own policies, so trying to devise a general discount system is not realistic, and implementing foundry-specific discount schemes means a considerable investment in development. What a large vendor can do are specific coupon actions, like FontShop is doing now in Font Magazine and with the industry-specific newsletters for people (not just customers, but anyone with a FontShop account!) who completed their professional activity in their profile (BTW this also dispels the notion that large vendors do nothing with regards to pricing). These discounts provoke a dent in no-one’s but their own profit margin, not anybody else’s – unless the foundry requested a promotional action and are willing to lower their prices of course. Now this much I know for sure because I recently discussed this with FontShop BeNeLux: to reach a break even on the current promotion consisting of a 15% price reduction (not even extra profit) they need to sell 40% more fonts.

So basically it’s up to the customer to decide if they prefer to have their own private discount for returning purchases, or if they’d rather go for the convenience of having a large selection of foundries and fonts available in one spot with occasional discounts – even if they haven’t purchased anything previously – and added benefits like free swag, information, blogs et al at the disposition of the entire community. Granted, this is a bit of a caricature, but I wanted to restore the balance a bit. ;^)

I hope this makes a number of things clear.

One thing that I don’t think has been addressed is that, aside from all matters of policy, a scheme like this is much less straightforward to implement on a large, preexisitng site than if you’re making that decision from the start of the design process. Earlier in the thread, Mr Jenkins from Village said:

The Taste Test has been Village policy since Day One. In fact, it was always our policy at Thirstype in Chicago too.

I actually remember this being the case when Village launched their site (which is an excellent example of a site which is both beautiful and practical, while I’m at it). As somebody who works primarily on the web (only occasionally in any kind of development role) I can vouch for the fact that it’s a whole different thing to implement something like this once a site or application has been out in the wild for even a short while. Updating an exisiting system always comes with its own challenges, and there’s no guarantee that the data stored about customer’s previous purchases will be enough to launch something like this right off.

In this scenario, what do you do? Start the new policy only on later purchases? This isn’t necessarily a situation that will look like a goodwill gesture to existing customers. So at this point, a technical difficulty can become an issue of policy - all of which will take time to resolve.

These are just a few points - obviously I make no claims to know anything about the inner workings of any of these suppliers. Just trying to bring something to the table that I haven’t seen mentioned elsewhere. Certainly not to take away from Village’s policy, which I think is an excellent idea. But this is always the advantage that smaller, newer businesses have over larger competitors - the ability to implement bold strategies quickly and effectively, due to having less ‘baggage’. Village should certainly be praised for having such a customer-friendly policy, but I don’t think at this stage the larger sellers should be demonised for not following suit.
