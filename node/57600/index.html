Technology's effect on type?

May 2, 2009 • k.brideau

Beyond its use as a tool, has the computer affected type design?

In the process of researching a paper on the New Typography and its claims of correspondence to the world, i’ve been struck by the importance of photography to the movement. Not only have i run into accounts of design history that basically disregard anything that happened between Gutenberg and Niépce/Daguerre as unimportant, but photography is identified as a perfect match for the New Typography given its objectivity, clarity, and lack of individualism. Whether these characteristics are applicable, it’s clear that the ways in which photography affected worldviews in the late 19th and early 20th centuries had an effect on this particular theory of type design.

It’s got me wondering what sorts of effects computers have had upon type design. Not being a designer myself, i’m curious if folks have an opinion on this. Particularly if there are characteristics of the computer (such as its reliance on numbers and patterns, its use in networks, its close ties to scientificity…) that have had an effect on (your or other) design in recent decades.

Computers have had a rather dramatic impact on typography. Read Poyners No More Rules to see an evolution of graphic design after the designers started doing the layouts and type themselves.

2009-05-02 20:14:45 • Nick Shinn

For me, the most important thing is that, back in the day, if a someone designed a new font, it would take a long time to be able to test it, let alone see how it would look in different sizes. And after the font was ready, if someone was willing to publish it, it would take a titanic amount of work (and costs!) to make it available for use.

Today you can design everything by yourself, test it quickly in a lot of different ways, and make it available for the entire world in the blink of an eye - without having to cast tons of metal to make your font usable.

This faster and cheaper way of working with type design allows much more experimentation, and what we see today is that instead of just slightly redesigning a previously available font, type designers are free to create whatever they want and see how it comes out.

By the other hand, this is misleading people to think that all you need to design a font is a Mac and some spare time… no need to study or read anything… :|

2009-05-03 10:16:05 • Frode Bo Helland

All we really have is a change in tooling. The questions that have some slight interest is how people respond to new tooling, and the inevitable effect of commercial practicality.

For example, we could recreate much of machine-set letterpress with computer composition. But we don’t. Is that because it would take more effort than the result is worth, or because machine-set letterpress, in order to be commercially viable, itself involved compromises we would like to abandon. Etc., going back & back.

And the answer is both.

You can chronicle such changes, but it isn’t terribly interesting save for historians, and I suppose psychologists.

If that is meant as a personal, psychological statement – what motivates a type designer – then no quibble.

If not, the point I was trying, and obviously failed, to make was that if we chose, we could reintroduce most of the effects of the hot metal composition with computer type. Just a couple of instances – a randomization program could be used to introduce slight variations in letters, mimicking the variation from casting individual letters in a mould.

Ink spread could be compensated for. And on & on. Given that, the question could be asked: Do we not do this because the original “look” was itself an an aberration? Or if it was desirable, it must not have been so desirable as to be worth all the work to replicate it.

You can argue that some of look of metal type was an aberration, occasioned by the technology,
but an equal answer is that many of those “aberrations” could have been banished with the technology of the time; they were not because the cost, both in type machines and time, couldn’t be commercially justified. And it was that commercial considerations that brought about that technology to start with, not aesthetic ones.

The technological change (now to computers) may have occasioned designers to move in different directions, but they could have moved that way anyway, albeit at a much higher cost.

Sorry, I believe tooling from a market perspective is essentially an historical, not aesthetic matter. Except, as always, for personal motivation.
