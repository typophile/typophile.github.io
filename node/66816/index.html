Hi John, welcome to Typophile.

Adam Twardoch has explained the Bitstream story in an older thread:

[…] in the 1980s, […] [a] group of type designers including Matthew Carter left Linotype and started Bitstream, which was to become the first digital type foundry in the world. They digitized original Linotype typefaces, but Linotype refused to license the original trademarks to them. So they used names derived from their own classification (Humanist 777 being Frutiger, Swiss 721 being Helvetica, etc.). Bitstream licensed the names from some other foundries, so some of the fonts used original names (e.g. Futura), but those copied from Linotype, Monotype and Berthold designs usually did not. However, these still were very faithful and high-quality digitizations, not cheap knock-offs like many many other companies offered them. […]

[In 2003], Linotype and Bitstream signed an agreement which finally ended their dispute over the designs. Linotype officially “licensed” the designs to Bitstream (but not the trademarks). This means, that prior to that, Swiss 721 was an unlicensed clone of Helvetica, and now it’s a licensed version of Helvetica, but sold under a different name. […] This is handy to know as this sort of confirms that the Bitstream versions really are derived from the same original sources.

Later in this thread, others who (I believe) do know the ropes of digital type history, like John Hudson and Gerald Giampa, elaborate on this.

For your second question: it depends. If the design in question really is in the public domain, you might be allowed to do your own version, provided that you don’t build upon someone else’s copyrighted data, or use a name that is trademarked. Better get professional legal advice. However, the most important question remains: why ‘design’ something that already exists? Make sure to read [[John Downer]]’s essay Call It What It Is: “‘Me Too’ fonts, or ‘Copy Cat’ fonts, as they are called, tend to focus on opportunism rather than on originality. These don’t rate as revivals because they don’t revive.”

2010-01-30 12:44:30 • Florian Hardwig

Today, there is not much excuse for copying fonts, since fonts are sold in TrueType or Adobe Type 1 format for use on all sorts of equipment.

But the reason type designs were copied - by nearly every typographic equipment manufacturer - in the old days are simple enough.

If you were going to purchase a Monotype caster and keyboard, or a Linotype machine, or an IBM Selectric Composer, or a Compugraphic phototypesetter… if you couldn’t use it to set Times Roman or Optima or Caledonia or Futura or Kabel, or any of the other fonts that were, at one time or another, all the rage… its usability would be very limited. Since, on the one hand, companies were loath to license their highly popular type designs to competitors (and it’s not as if Linotype manufactured Monotype matrix-cases, or font discs for Compugraphic phototypesetters, and so on) - and, on the other hand, at least in the United States, type designs enjoyed neither patent nor copyright protection, well, they got copied.

The names of typefaces, though, did have trademark protection. So this is why so many alternative names for the same type design emerged.

2010-02-01 15:10:43 • Si_Daniels

type designs enjoyed neither patent nor copyright protection

Actually, type designs have enjoyed patent protection — specifically, design patent protection — in the United States for well over a century. In fact the very first design patent issued in the USA was for a typeface, in 1842.

Now, design patents may be expensive, and offer a limited duration of protection, so most type designers might not bother… but they most certainly do apply to typefaces. They still apply today, or at least recently, as was demonstrated in the summary judgment of the Adobe vs SSi lawsuit.

2010-02-02 06:49:09 • Uli

criminals who deserve criminal penalties

The image to which you linked refers to copyright infringement. That is a tort, not a crime. Adobe and Linotype are permitted by law to sue people who infringe their copyrights, and that has to happen before enforcement takes place. Infringement for resale can indeed be a criminal act, and other crimes can be associated with copyright infringement in connection with obtaining access to the copyrighted work to copy it (i.e. in the case of movie piracy): but font designs are not copyrightable in the U.S., so this is not relevant to those acts of font copying.

design patent protection

True, I’ve seen some of these old design patents. My understanding was they failed to hold up in court as an effective way to protect font designs however, or, if they did work, they were limited to ornamental fonts and not basic text fonts.
