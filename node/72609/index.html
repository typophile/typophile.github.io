Generally speaking, optical center is slightly above actual center, so your top margin should be smaller than your bottom margin and, according to general proportional rules, the outside margin should be slightly larger than the inside margin.

Garamond is a reasonable choice but, given the presbyopic nature of your readers (like me), ITC Garamond Condensed (larger x-height) at 12/14 might be preferable…or not.

I agree with Oldnick: top margin must be smaller and bottom larger. If you will have footnotes, this is even more needed because you have more graphic elements at the bottom of page.

Garamond has a rather small x-height. So 11.5/14pt or 12/15pt will produce better reading experience to your public. With larger text you may use just two wider columns.

Scientific publications usually adopt footer indication about article title and/or author name and/or edition number and date.

Geometrical schemas to construct page margins are presented in detail by Robert Bringhurst in Elements of Typographical Style. van de Graaf’s is one of them.

Anyway, I never agreed about these schemas because they produces margins too large without a real logical reason. To have a geometrical division of the page is not enough (in my opinion, of course) to waste so much space on paper.

Grid systems are useful to layouts with many elements, especially newspapers. In your case, this is not necessary.

I did a sample using these margins: Top: 18mm, bottom: 24mm, outside: 16mm; inside: 30mm.

You can see it in [[http://if.pro.br/psy.png|small]] and [[http://if.pro.br/psy-full.png|full size]]. The InDesign file is also [[http://if.pro.br/psy.rar|available]].

I used Berthold Garamond in 11.5/14pt because this is my favorite Garamond revival. It’s just an example (text is from Deutsche Wikipedia page about Psychiatry).

…people speak with certain disdain about the ITC Garamond versions…

There is indeed snobbery directed at ITC Garamond. It is said by some that a face with such a large x-height has no business appearing in book text. ITC Garamond was also extremely popular in commercial work (the old “trade” stigma) in the 1970s and 80s, as well as being Apple’s display face (in the Condensed version), where it acquired the negative connotation of being the face of desktop publishing in the 1980s, when DTP signified cheap and nasty typography.

However, if your client is going to challenge you with a Lacanian peccadillo, I would say that warrants a non-conformist response.

For the exact size and leading, and whether you choose Light or Regular weight, experiment and trust your judgement, but in my opinion the Light is far more interesting. I prefer it without much leading, as the extenders are so short.

With Light for text and Book for footnotes, fixing the optimum relationship could help structure your page.

2010-07-27 18:44:21 • jacobsievers

Lacan mentions Garamond only once in his Écrits, in Seminar 5, page 501, in L’instance de la lettre dans l’inconscient (The Instance of the Letter in the Unconscious):

::: Or la structure du signifiant est, comme on le dit communément du langage, qu’il soit articulé.

Ceci veut dire que ses unités, d’où qu’on parte pour dessiner leurs empiétements réciproques et leurs englobements croissants, sont soumises à la double condition de se réduire à des éléments différentiels derniers et de les composer selon les lois d’un ordre fermé.

Ces éléments, découverte décisive de la linguistique, sont les phonèmes où il ne faut chercher aucune constance phonétique dans la variabilité modulatoire où s’applique ce terme, mais le système synchronique des couplages différentiels, nécessaires au discerne­ment des vocables dans une langue donnée. Par quoi l’on voit qu’un élément essentiel dans la parole elle-même était prédestiné à se couler dans les caractères mobiles qui, Didots ou Garamonds se pressant dans les bas-de-casse, présentifient valablement ce que nous appelons la lettte, à savoir. la structure essentiellement localisée du signifiant.

-

::: Now the structure of the signifier is, as is commonly said of language, that it is articulated.

This means that its units-no matter where one begins in tracing out their reciprocal encroachments and expanding inclusions-are subject to the twofold condition of being reduced to ultimate differential elements and of combining the latter according to the laws of a closed order.

These elements, the decisive discovery oflinguistics, arephonemes; we must not look for anyphonetic constancy in the modulatory variability to which this term applies. but rather for the synchronic system of differential couplings that are necessary to discern vocables in a given language. This allows us to see that an essential element in speech itself was predestined to flow into moveable type which, in Didots or Garamonds squeezing into lower-cases, renders validly present what I call the “letter”-namely, the essentially local­ized structure of the signifier.
