Lots of folks recommend against using them in dates, Opting to omit the ordinal suffix from the numeral, but spell out the suffix when the date is spelled out in letters.

{ I’m going camping on July 12, and staying outdoors until the twentieth. }

Which is a style I’m learning to appreciate. Though, that doesn’t account for other ordinal numbers. I’m looking particularly for information about street ordinals.

2008-06-29 05:42:52 • keoxa

In English, this is entirely stylistic. All publishers I know steer clear of raised ordinals. It is viewed as archaic and fussy. (Despite MS Word’s propensity for raising them – faux raised ordinals, no less.)

Many OpenType fonts are starting to support them regularly with {sups} or {ordn}. But American publishers generally don’t accept them in regular use.

There are other languages – like Spanish, I think – where raised forms are considered more linguistically proper. Thus the inclusion of ordmasculine and ordfeminine in standard character sets (but, inexplicably, no accommodation for some plural forms).

– K.

Oxford’s Hart’s Rules says “Use words for ordinals in … numerical street names”, with the exception that “the convention for Manhattan addresses is to spell out numbers for avenue names and use numbers for street names: the corner of Fifth and 59th”.
