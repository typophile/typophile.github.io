2011-01-13 02:52:49 • Stephen Rapp

The appendices to Bringhurt’s Elements of Typographic Style are very useful for this, though I admit I don’t remember if he lists Unicode values for all of them. And of course, this is only for Western alphabets. You could also try the Unicode site itself — for instance:

http://www.unicode.org/charts/PDF/U0300.pdf

Though the code tables don’t identify languages that the diacritics are used in, Bringhurst does, so between the two resources you may have what you need.

2011-01-13 13:47:59 • kentlew

The Wikipedia article about [[http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin-derived_alphabet|Latin-derived alphabets]] is the nearest single source I know (I’m assuming you are searching mainly for Latin script languages).

For deeper information, you can take a look on each letter and language article published in Wikipedia. Each [[http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A|letter article]] brings a table with diacritical combinations.

About transliterations, there is an [[http://transliteration.eki.ee/index.html|excellent archive]] made by Thomas Pedersen. It’s almost hidden in the Estonian Language Institute site, the same cited by Kentlew.

I’m concluding a survey about glyphs needed to support all Latin-script languages and also phonetic systems (IPA, APA, etc.). Unhappily, I did not keep information about the languages, just took the glyphs.
