A large part of early T26 fonts were all produced as collages of existing forms. On one side, it was a direction for research, on the other side, I was horrified by what T26 started to do, especially considered I was going to release with Emigre, which has always been painstakingly precise and thoroughly conscious in ethic matters.

I have to say, however, that most of Jim Marcus typefaces were not so bad, they showed a quite original research in this “collage” aesthetic, despite the disputable methods of approach. Problem was they offered types with incomplete character sets, produced often with little or no care. Among the T26 catalog, however there were true gems, like Frank Heine Amplifier and the original Tema Cantante by Eric Lin.

2009-01-13 22:57:26 • typerror

I have not said the various aspects of those works are ethically fine. I just noted what was happening, on a general level, and the difference lies precisely there: who produced the most interesting work developed it to a degree which no longer relied on original elements of inspiration to be considered a theft or even a copy.

Especially if you say this one is from the work of a renowned calligrapher. For his typeface “Masoch-Dirach”, for example, Jim Marcus “hacked” the forms of that typographical masterpiece which is Peter Behrens' Behrens Antiqua, presumably from printed sources since no digital version existed. It’s largely questionable how this approach could make sense. As you say, as long as you do not differentiate enough from your source, and produce a simple alteration, it’s really deprecable and that’s why I hated so much T-26 when they came to the scene.

I did not expect such a bad thing, and it trashed the whole efforts Rudy Vanderlans was putting into having those new experimentations recognized, when they were conducted on a path of absolute ethical integrity (P. Scott Makela “Dead History” was entirely re-digitized to make his type research a work on its own, as much as Brian Schorn “AddMorph” was never released because it depended on the digital data of Adobe Trajan). Now we pay the price…
