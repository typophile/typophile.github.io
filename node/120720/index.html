Martin: "Infant" typefaces are text typefaces that are meant to structurally mimic the very earliest handwriting of small children. The theory is that by making the symbols close to the ideal shape they're taught to write (or rather, at that age, draw) it makes it easier for children to learn reading. (I don't have the exact studies at hand, but I seem to remember from elsewhere that this is not actually verifiable, and may even be outright false.) If at all, they differ from "schoolbook" or "primary" typefaces by being even simpler in construction and, where possible, by being less cursive, and more constructed, just like a four year old will have more constructed writing than a seven year old. [[https://www.myfonts.com/fonts/mti/sassoon/|Sassoon]], a typeface developed by a child psychologist, has an "Infant" version which is upright, has a low X-height (to clearly differentiate between the two cases), and is stripped down in a reductionist way, and a "Primary" version which has a slight slant, taller X-height and several other more handwriting-mimicking features, like a looped k and a q with a tail.
