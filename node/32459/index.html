Is Design School worth it?

March 22, 2007 • supermanism

I’m a sophmore in Design School in NYC, and lately, I’ve just been thinking about how much of a waste of money it is.

I have two studio classes in my major (graphic design) that seem like I just go for the attendance. One class in particular, the teacher is often late and we have a quick class discussion about the work we put up, then class is over.

Don’t get me wrong, I enjoy being in college, but overall, I look at the work I’m doing, and its just almost worthless. I’m usually towards the top of the class, but most of the things we’re doing are exercises or small projects. A lot of non-real world stuff. Often times we have like 4 weeks to do small projects too.

I know I’m improving, but still. All the ideas are mine, and I’ve had to learn a lot of the programs' tricks on my own. I guess its no different from liberal arts programs though.

So, I assume a lot of other people on the board have been through design school.

I know this wasn’t the best written, but maybe someone can reassure me its worth such a large amount of money.

Short answer? Yes, it is.

Long answer? Not every school is the right fit for every student. Some people will become great designers without having set foot in a design school Others would fair better at one school, rather than an other. Since you are only in your Sophomore year, perhaps it is not too late to transfer to another institution, if you feel like you’d like more intellectual challenges and/or student–teacher interaction.

2007-03-22 09:05:08 • dan_reynolds

You really only get better through practice and curiosity. What the school offers you is a potential social network and access to a variety of expensive programs and other tools. In between the assignments, exploit this access as much as you can while you still don’t have to buy the software yourself at the prices designed for businesses. College is an environment in which to tinker with lots of different stuff at low risk. Occasionally, you might even learn something useful from one of your teachers, but don’t count on it. The social network will be more significant.

The key: Do whatever you can to get work in the field before you graduate. Because when you graduate, you’ll be competing with everybody who graduated at the same time as you for the same few jobs. And not long after you graduate, your diploma will be worth jack diddly zero.

I’m going through the same issues with my design school, and so are a lot of other design students across the country. This came up a few issues back in Comm Arts; design schools are teaching curricula invented for the mid-twentieth century, but it’s 2007. From the conversations I’ve had with design teachers about this I know that they are aware of the problem, but most of them don’t know how to get past it. They don’t understand that the new generation of designers really don’t need a month or two and fifty revisions of sketches just to design a book cover.

There are some younger teachers who get it, teachers who came of age when Postmodernism was exploding, but it’s going to be another decade or two until they’re deciding how design schools function. Until then, design students just have to suck it up, learn what we can while in school, and then move on with our lives. The important thing to remember is that there is still a universal method of dealing with the problem: everybody needs to get together a couple times a month to drink beer, eat pizza, and kvetch.

Others have already said it above, but I too think school is worth finishing. It’s a luxury to be able to approach a problem the way it’s done in school, and you will miss that once you’ve graduated and have a “real world” job.

Carl and James, from the few years I have been out of school (3) I have learned that the most valuable asset a designer can have is the hability to envision. Envisioning implies understanding a problem, thinking a creative (original and effective) way to solve it and planning it’s realization.

This means that it is more important to know what has to be done than being able to do it (specially if you don’t know what to do). The highest design positions are focused on envisioning and directing the creative work of others. Those with only the ability to make the tricks get to be production artists.

Design education will try to teach you how to envision but also will give you other valuable tools such as a title, communication theory, semiotics, history classes, marketing understanding, technical training, social networking, production experience and a program to grow by an adequate path (not in a chaothic and crippled way).

Don’t expect fast outcome, take the time needed to train you for a lifelong career. I passed the first year of design school drawing models, taking art history courses, doing color charts, line grids and abstract excercises. It was until the second year that we were able to tackle some “real life” design projects and made corporate identity until the last semester (out of 8). Otherwise it’s like entering the karate class and wanting to do the ninja stuff on the first month.

If you can afford it I would recommend design school, if not I would recommend trying to follow the program by your own. As said above if your current school doesn’t fulfill you change it, you are grown up.

2007-03-24 14:27:49 • Diner

The question of quality of the instruction is an important issue. And I cannot address the question of the quality of design schools in general, nor of course of ‘supermanism’s school in particular.

However, as a former college teacher I can speak to the other half of the equation: student’s role in his or her own education.

The students I enjoyed most were those who had been out in the world of work and returned for further education. They had these characteristics:

  1. They knew why they were studying. They didn’t just drift into studies for lack of another option, but had a goal.

  2. They were willing to challenge the teachers, including me. When I say challenge, I mean regularly ask thoughtful questions, including asking what is the benefit of studying what you are studying. I don’t mean to do this in an angry or impertinent way, but rather in expectation that there is an answer, and you want to learn it.

  3. They know that the teachers are working for them. They are paying the bill, and they seek out instruction. If one teacher is not the best in something they want to learn, they find another. If that person is not in their school, they will go to a person in another place–school or private sector–for supplement, or change schools.

In sum returning students tend not to whine about their education, but to seek out excellence in instruction or go elsewhere. This quality of initiative, rather than passivity, is one of the best things a student can have to get the most out of school.
