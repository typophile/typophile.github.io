So far, we have the following proposed definitions of creativity (with the caveat that the people who put forward these definitions may not be committed to them):

Darrel: the ability to create

Me: a presumed enabling property of beings that create

Jack: the ability to let go and be open to multiple directions and new mental wanderings [cf: Russell’s suggestion that ‘uncreative’ people are ‘afraid to make mistakes, or make fools of them selves’]

Christopher: a neurophysiological process in which the brain forms new associative neural pathways

It seems to me that we either need to distinguish mental creativity or ‘creative thinking’ from creative acts, i.e. making things (as Chris put it with admirable clarity), or find a way to unify the mental and physical phenomena.

I don’t find the romantic view of mental creativity helpful, since it relies entirely on metaphors (openness, wandering, road maps, blue-prints, highway); it may be an example of creative thinking – and perhaps even evidence that creativity is not something that can be nailed down analytically –, but it is also a demonstration that metaphors tend to lead only to more metaphors.

Chrisopher’s proposed definition seems unromantic, but it strikes me as begging the question to apply the label ‘creativity’ to the formation of new associative neural pathways, since the formation of such pathways does not necessarily correspond to what we commonly understand by creativity as e.g. observable in the making of things. If a definition requires abandoning common usage in favour of a novel scientific application, we have to acknowledge that we are no longer talking about the same things. Both common and specialised usage of terms may have their place, but one can’t be taken to trump the other.

This leaves us with the notion of ability or enabling property, another word for which is talent. As I said above, I think such a property is presumed on the basis of observation: we see people creating things or we see the things that people have created, and we presume a property in the people that enables them to create things, and we label this property ‘creativity’. Like most enabling properties, it has to be innate at some level; even virtuoso performances requiring highly developed enabling properties rely on innate properties that in enable such development. So I don’t think creativity could be taught, as it were, from scratch, as a purely cultural transmission without a pre-existing natural transmission of an innate creative property. It follows then, that if the phrase ‘teaching creativity’ has any sensible meaning, then it must imply fostering or developing of that innate property.

It may also imply, in many situations, simply getting the hell out of the way.

I don’t want to get bogged down in definitions, as ‘creativity’ and ‘problem-solving’ are both vague enough terms to argue endlessly without getting anywhere.

We make our ideas clear, as C.S. Peirce said in a great essay, when we relate them to something concrete, to action. In this case what is clear is the problem that thinkers have been working on for over a century now: what is involved in the creation of innovative and excellent new work, whether in be in the sciences or arts? And we have paradigmatic examples, like Mozart’s and Beethoven’s musical works, Leonardo and Michaelangelo’s paintings, Newton and Einstein’s scientific theories, or mathematical breakthroughs of Euler and Gauss.

The question is, What mental process is responsible for these great works? As I said, much of the work on this issue is within the framework of problem-solving. Students of Oswald Külpe, whom I mentioned earlier, created both the Gestalt and Würzburg schools of psychology. Most of the work post-WW II was directly indebted to the Würzburgers, such as Karl Bühler and Otto Selz. Popper studied under Bühler, and Herbert Simon, Nobel Prize winning Economist who was also a leader in early computer simulation of intelligence, acknowledged his debt to Selz. (Selz’s own career was cut short by the Nazis, who murdered him at Auschwitz.)

The computer simulation work is definitely limited, and the Gestalt school–also a problem-solving approach–did some things that still haven’t been full incorporated into more modern psychology.

One of the ideas of both the Gestalt and Würzburg schools is, I think, that there is a continuum between the most ordinary and routine problem solving, and great creative works. I suspect they are right that that there is a difference of degree, not kind, between the ordinary and great works. So there is no “secret ingredient” that genius’s have, though they may have stronger imagination, focus, persistence and what you might call synthetic ability.

On the question of struggle, I don’t think that that is a necessary part of it, though I do think time is involved. To take a simple example, Popper pointed to the optical illusion of [[http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Necker_Cube|the Necker Cube]] as an illustration of the idea that the brain is constantly hypothesizing in an effort to solve problems.

When we stare at the Necker cube it will keep reversing as to which corner is closer to us. Popper takes this as showing that the brain is trying to solve the problem “what is this?” and because it lacks information, the visual system keeps trying one hypothesis and then another.

This is on the level of perception, and so much more so on the level of cognition, and the processes behind writing a symphony or painting a picture.

Kent, I don’t think that there’s necessarily an emotional struggle. For Mozart, as you say, there wasn’t, and with Beethoven there was. And Bertrand Russell was like Mozart when it came to writing: he would work it out in his head, and then it would just flow out of his pen, with little or no revision.

But the theory, as I hope you see from the example of the Necker cube, is that problem-solving is so fundamental a human process that greater or lesser emotional struggle is just one thing you might study, not an essential feature. And a lot of the problem-solving work is unconscious.

What I would be interested in is what John or Chris thinks the problem-solving framework leaves out of the creative process.

I’m not at all stuck to this model as being the whole story, but I do know that it has proven very fruitful.

Bill, Chris,

Perhaps part of the reason for our disconnect – and at this stage I’m more inclined to think of it in these terms than as a disagreement –, is that the experience of creativity varies significantly, and while there are parallels between creativity in the sciences and in the arts, there are experiential differences that should be acknowledged and described. While a problem solving framework may be used to describe any creative act if one’s concept of problem solving is sufficiently general, it seems to me that such a framework fits the experience of creativity in the sciences more easily than it fits that of creativity in the arts; that is, for a scientist, creativity feels like problem solving, whereas for an artist it might feel quite different. I’m not suggesting a general division, by the way: one has only to read the accounts of artists talking about different works to realise that their experience of creativity varies.

Rilke’s account of the initial inspiration of the Duino Elegies in 1912 is a classic case of something that, at least experientially, doesn’t seem like problem solving. In retrospect, if one were committed to a problem solving explanation, I think one could come up with one even to account for this flurry of inspiration, but for Rilke it felt like something else. Interestingly, after the initial burst of inspiration that resulted in the first two elegies and fragments of the others, the writing stalled for the better part of a decade. What this indicates is that a conscious problem solving approach without the inspiration was insufficient to this act of creation: no amount of working on the poems as if they were problems that could be solved by effort, skill and intelligence would result in their completion. It was only in 1922, in a ‘savage creative storm’ that Rilke not only completed the elegies but also composed the Sonnets to Orpheus. Again, I’m not suggesting that no problem solving framework can be built around these creative acts, but that to the poet they were not experienced as problem solving but as a ‘storm’.

I suspect people in the sciences – or for that matter in business – experience creativity as problem solving because they have predefined goals independent of individual creative acts. Because the defining characteristic of art is extra-utility (which may include ‘self-expression’ but that is not the defining characteristic of art, as most of the world’s artistic heritage demonstrates), there tend to be fewer predefined goals, and hence the genesis of the creative impulse is less easily grasped.

While a problem solving framework may be useful in many ways, I think we need to be able to talk about creativity in terms of the experience of the people who are creating; indeed, we need to be aware that insisting on a framework that fails to take into account that experience may damage and inhibit creative activity.
