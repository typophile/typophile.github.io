2007-07-12 23:40:02 • crossgrove
If you do design work for hire, your current situation is--unfortunately--one that you will encounter all too often. If your artistic integrity is more important than paying the rent, you can simply tell your clients that their ideas are total crap, thank them very much, and walk out the door. If, on the other hand, you've put a lot of effort into the project and feel that you ought to be compensated for it, you have to give the people who sign the checks what they want, even if you do, in fact, think it's total crap. In the former instance, you write off your losses and preserve your self-respect; in the latter instance, you collect your fee and leave that particular project out of your portfolio. Bummer...

2007-07-13 05:25:15 • Robert Trogman
we stipulate in our contracts that the client gets 3 rounds of changes. After that, they are on the clock. Makes compromise a lot less painful when you know that for every time you lower your standard, you ring the cash register. Of course there's always a chance you'll end up with something you're happy with after all those rounds. In that case, the extra $$ is the icing on the cake. Not to sound crass, but we put the brakes on unlimited changes years ago - after rounds and rounds of seemingly endless revisions. The idea of 'what is finished' could be a dissertation in itself. In terms of whether or not you can put it in your portfolio - we keep 2 portfolios like criminals keep 2 sets of books. You get the idea.

2007-07-13 15:41:49 • Nick Shinn
I have certain "rules" too, like Remy I usually put up to 3 revisions in the contract, after that I bill hourly. But I can be flexible depending on the job - if it's something I'm proud of and want to be associated with I'll be more generous with my time and effort. But I'll also be quite clear with the client if I feel it's not moving in a good direction. And I have walked away from jobs where I have felt that either it's being designed by committee (and the result is not good) or the client and I are not on the same page creatively and are not likely to agree on a solution. Or if the client just can't articulate what he/she wants and I feel like we're both spinning our wheels. Not everything I do ends up in my portfolio, but if it isn't going to be a portfolio piece I better have gotten well compensated. I also look at the big picture - the easy, high-paying jobs help subsidize the more laborious ones. I'm way underpaid on a job I'm doing right now but it's a real feather in my cap so I'm sucking up and putting in the extra hours (even cut my vacation short a day). I tend not to include work that wasn't used in my portfolio. I don't think unresolved jobs (which often require explanation) put your best foot forward. Ditto with spec work. But that's just my opinion. As an art director, I was always interested in the portfolio pieces that were successful both from a design and a client point of view. Unless you're a student of course.
