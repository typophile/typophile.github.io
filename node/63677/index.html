Hello all. I’m pretty new to the world of design, but decided to make the jump into freelancing after a recent move. So far it’s gone quite well, with a couple of mistakes from ignorance, but nothing too major. But now there’s a client I’m trying to design a book cover for, and just can’t seem to get it to the final level. Book: Face to Face; on friendship/hospitality, based on a series of sermons. The tone is very easy-going, but also direct and exhortative. Stipulations were just that the cover not be too obvious (i.e., two faces).

The client liked my idea for the cover, but hasn’t been happy with my versions of the idea, I think mainly because it doesn’t have as much impact and interest as they would like.

Here is the third round of revisions (apologies for the huge size of this post). I’m afraid these won’t be warm enough. Any suggestions? Should I start over?

Hi, I’m try to put some thoughts about these down here, and I’ll try to be constructive, so don’t stop reading after the first sentence ;)

I think they are alright, but…a bit stock photo-y…and clean… and boring… Don’t know about the plates, I would think this is a book about food. And looking at your plate seems the opposite of looking at ones face. Then again, if the client liked the idea of the plates, you gotta try to work with it. I think the covers lack visual impact, if I were to see this among other books this wouldn’t jump out. Maybe it’s not a kind of book that has to stand out, but think about how a book would look from the other side of the room. Generally I think the problem is that it’s all to neat and clean and unadventurous. It doesn’t ‘feel’ like a good conversation (engaging, exciting etc). It’s hard to put to words how to go about and experiment some more with this, even if it’s just a rough idea. Put together a few covers in a limeted timespan, all with different ideas explored, not just one idea like these. Don’t be too fussy about it, they don’t have to be ready for print just yet. You could also do this fase on paper, if you feel more confident with that. In this way you will hopefully get one or two covers that you like, for whatever reason. Then you can continue to explore in this direction. But even in this fase try to be a bit bolder. Do things you never thought you would do. After this fase you can work on details, but not at the beginning, as it will kill anything you try to do (I’ve been there :))

To make a long story short: these are too clean and neat, be bolder! Think about the idea with the plates, try some different things with it, but if it doesn’t work: throw it out, no use trying to make something work that just doesn’t. And most importantly: Have fun and don’t take it too seriously!

I hope this helps you a bit. I don’t think it will help you to give very detailed feedback on each one of these, but I hope you post your progress here, to see how you’re getting on. Good luck!

2009-11-02 23:54:03 • BEH

I think your flexibility and willingness to branch out definitely showed in the 2nd iteration, where you introduced the vase and the door.

I agree that the vase is the strongest composition, thus far. The 3rd round, I think, was a little hasty.

My very limited experience with this sort of work is that you really want to stay in the sketching/conceptual stage as long as possible.

Another thing to consider is the subject of your composition, other than the title. Sometimes it is OK to make the title the subject, but almost all illustrations/photos are bound to become the subject, and so you want to make sure your subject has some depth and character. For example, I think the concept, layout and composition of the frist vase is excellent, but it would need a more refined illustration. Of you coul keep the same concept at use a photo of a vase. (I don’t think the optical illusion is nearly as important as a solid layout). Vases have a nice way of shaping positive and negative space.

I think the concept of a design “subject” is relevant here. The subject needs to be one of four things.

  1. Typography (i.e., title/author)
  2. Illustration
  3. Photo
  4. a combination of the above

And ideally, it would be a depiction of a person or a place.

What this means is that a good book cover designer has access to the necessary illustrations or photos.

For instpiration, check out The Book Cover Archive.

I also have a potential book cover client, and the theme is Bolivian finance… so rest assured, I feel your pain.

2009-11-03 02:42:20 • dirtcastle

Just a nontypographic thought…

If you go with the two-plates-on-a-table-idea, make sure the shading indicates that the light source is the same for both plates. Right now it looks like one is convex and the other concave (or more like the bottom one is right-side-up and the top one is upside-down). If you need to repeat symmetrical objects (as opposed to shapes), don’t just flip them, or this problem will arise.

Good luck!
