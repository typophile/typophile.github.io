Glad to see something of a local flavour being discussed. I’ve talked at length with friends here (Ireland) about this typeface as it puzzles me greatly. Apologies for the verbose posting.

The original Córas Iompair Éireann (CIE) corporate typeface was designed by [[Tony Fahy]] in 1999/2000 and was called CIE2000 or CIE2K. You can check it out here. To be honest it is quite ugly.

Subsequently, (I have heard) it was cleaned-up by another Irish designer. Whether this new typeface is the cleaned up version, I don’t know. They both share some similar characteristics.

To add to the mystery, the new typeface is used by CIE in all of their annual reports (available here) under the name Nexus Sans. The typeface is obviously not Nexus Sans by [[Martin Majoor]].

Sorry to drag up an old post, but I thought I’d answer Conor’s question about the Nexus Sans typeface currently used by Irish Rail (Iarnód Éireann).

In 2003 I was commissioned by a company called Creative Inc. to audit and redesign the CIE 2K font, originally designed by Tony Fahy. The redesigned font is called ‘Nexus Sans’.

A absolute condition of the redesign was that I had to conserve the basic characteristics of the CIE2K font.

First I carried out qualitative research in three areas:

  1. how the CIE 2K font was being used,
  2. whether its design was suitable for that use, and
  3. an evaluation of its standard of technical execution.

The research showed that CIE2K was conceived as a display font, not intended for use at small sizes, or for station signage for that matter. Regardless of the aesthetic qualities of the font, it suffered greatly from regular misuse and inappropriate application. Irish Rail had received negative feedback from staff and customers about the font’s legibility. A vivid example was the truck driver who crashed into a railway overpass and claimed he could not read warning signs (set in CIE2K) of the height restrictions.

With six different weights, I felt that CIE2K was too complex for use as a cross platform corporate identity font. My redesign, ‘Nexus Sans’, specified just three weights to make it easier to use and added basic glyphs such as the euro currency symbol as well as optional tabular figures for setting timetables.

My redesign sought to keep the references to brush-drawn Gaelic calligraphy, while making letterforms and letter-spacing more consistent. The font is now comfortable to read at small sizes and less idiosyncratic in appearance.

I also recommended that ‘Nexus Sans’ be restricted to printed corporate communications, and that a separate font should be considered for the special requirements of online and signage typography.

One year after I had finished and named the font, Martin Majoor released his beautiful ‘Nexus Sans’ in 2004. An unfortunate coincidence, but not significant since the Irish Rail corporate font is not sold commercially, and I hope people would think better of comparing such dissimilar designs.

The Creative Inc website shows some examples of Irish Rail Nexus Sans and the excellent Brunel font used for station signage.
