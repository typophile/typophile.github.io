2006-10-26 22:02:55 • hrant

Or try this text: The adequacy of the spacing of a pair depends on what other glyphs it is likely to be adjacent to. By the same token, the adequacy of the spacing of one side of a glyph depends on what the other side is doing. The most prominent example is the “r”: when you know that the right side is always going to be a little loose (because otherwise it means its black body is dysfunctionally narrow and/or the beak crashes into bunches of things) then you make the left side slightly loose as well, to -gently- propagate the (un)even color. More subtly, the inevitable looseness on the right of a “k” (relative to the “h”) means that it benefits from a slightly looser left. Mind you, we’re talking about a distinction of ems you can count on the fingers of one hand; ignoring this refinement (what I call “intraglyph color propagation” - please don’t shoot me) will make nothing stand out too much, since there are bigger (unavoidable) spacing imbalances “built-in” to any (conventional) font (and to some extent, alphabet). But like a somewhat long right foot-serif on an “r”, it helps just that little bit.

The “r” is to spacing what the conventional binocular “g” is to structure: both are overly-large spikes that need to be fixed. For the latter it’s easy: just open the bottom bowl. But for the former the only good solution I can think of is unpalatable to most type designers: make the beak ascend (à la original Cheltenham). On top of fixing the spacing it would also make for better boumas.
