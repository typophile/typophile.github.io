p.s Added first mock-up sample pages

  • Before you pick your trim size, type size, and margins, look at all the poems in the book and see how long the longest lines are. You may not be able to avoid line breaks altogether, but design to avoid frequent line breaks.
  • Pay careful attention to leading.
  • Look at the lengths of the poems, and design your trim size and margins to avoid either excessively short pages or too many page breaks. When you do have a page break, put the break where it makes sense in the poem, not just where you run out of room.
  • You'll have a lot of paper showing, so choose a good quality paper, off-white rather than bright white.
  • Don't use running heads at all, and don't use folios (page numbers) unless the book is longer than 40-50 pages. Pages of poetry usually have a lot of white space, so running heads and folios are much more conspicuous--and distracting--than they are in books of prose.

    • My first book design assignment was a book of poems. Good luck, and please post some sample pages here when you’re done.

    Let me add a few things to Mojo’s very sensible comments.

    PAGE SIZE is very often dictated by the publisher. Even publishers most sensitive to poetry will often tell designers what the page size is to be. Publishers have their own arcana on this matter, which they consult during séances. When the publisher tells the designer the page size it almost inevitably creates difficulties (OOOPS: I mean “challenges and opportunities”) for the designer and/or compositor.

    PAPER is also often chosen by the production manager at the publishing house. If this book will be printed by a book printer, paper choice will be further limited by papers the printer keeps in stock. Few books of poetry will have sales enough to warrant a special order of paper. Mojo’s right: natural or off-white is best.

    FOLIOS need to be discreet in size and placement. Head to the library and look at a lot of poetry books and folio placement. This can be a tough nut to crack with poetry books, thanks to the white space Mojo mentions.

    RUNNING HEADS cannot be automatically dismissed. A poetry anthology may require them. Even at times a book by a single author may require them. This may be more of an editorial call than a design call.

    Designing poetry books can be a lot of fun. In my current job I miss doing poetry. At times designers get lucky and end up collaborating with editors and poets, engaging them in discussion and showing alternate examples of settings. It often reduces the profit margin on design & composition even further than its already low state. But you may find some excellent writing and you may make a pal of a poet who gets a good design. I’m eager to see what you come up with. Good luck; have fun.

    Ha, ha. However, for those who might not see this as a quip, I want to point out that the publishers dictate page size based on press specifications. If you add even a quarter inch in dimension to a page, you make it too large to be printed economically on a certain press. The result is that the job has to be printed 8 pages at time instead of 16, effectively doubling most of the production costs.

    It may seem like arcana if you don’t know the procedure, but in fact it is simple math. And economics.

    2009-07-20 14:45:08 • will powers

    I’ll echo what Will said. Having also been involved in some of these “calculations” when I was Creative Director at Storey Publishing, I will say that there are a number of factors which make up the “arcana.”

    Production economics are an important factor. Those being met, then design considerations and suitability to the content may come into play. But market expectations and trade considerations will also play a key part. (Even down to what will fit on the shelves in a particular section of a bookstore, as Will pointed out; some sections will have taller or shorter or deeper or shallower shelf sections – think art books vs. fiction.)

    You could theoretically have the exact same book (same title, same content, same production costs and price point) but markedly different proportions – say, one tall and slender, the other nearly squarish (remember, I did say “theoretically”) – and one will be deemed more sellable than the other.

    As with any design destined for the marketplace, there is a delicate dance to be found between going with the flow and standing out from the crowd.

    2009-07-21 16:19:20 • kentlew

    Paper can comprise half the cost of manufacturing a book, so publishers and printers are keen not to waste any. 6 by 9 inches is an economical size because most book papers are manufactured in a 25-by-38-inch size, which just fits 16 pages (four 6’s out of 25 and four 9’s out of 38, with just enough left over for folding and trimming). If you’re printing a quarter-million copies of Harry Potter and the Dingbats of Doom, it makes an enormous difference whether your trim size is 6 or 6-1/2. But this ain’t Harry Potter, it’s poetry, and if the print run is more than 1000, I’ll eat my Haberule. (My Haberule)

    Poetry lives on the fringes of the publishing world, where you don’t enjoy economies of scale, but you’re not subject to the tyranny of scale, either. Random House can’t afford to let a designer suggest a different trim size or kind of paper, but most poetry publishers are small, independent operations who are more likely to engage in a dialogue.

    2009-07-21 22:56:04 • bowerbird

    I agree with bowerbird about the majority of poetry books being rather uninteresting in terms of typesetting.

    To me, careful and individual placement of poems is the most important aspect; each poem must relate to the page as a whole. This fails miserably in the case of short poems clinging to the upper left corner of the text block. The most pleasing – but also the most functional – placements, which allow the poems to breathe, are (visually) centered on the horizontal and have maybe a generous vertical indent, too.

    I’ve seen many books where the poems were set so that their last line was always at the bottom of the text block – what was changing was the vertical position of the first line. This doesn’t always work, though, and sometimes too much jumping around can also become distracting. You have to figure it out accordingly.

    There’s an example of a poetry book in Hochuli’s Desining Books with the title on the verso and the poem on the recto.

    Nothing fancy, no poetic cliches, but make sure that individual poems don’t look like they were falling down or out of the page. Don’t make them look ashamed of their existence, place them in a way that will underline their self-sustained aesthetic importance.

    Why must academic books be 6” x 9” instead of 5.5” x 8.5?

    Because publishers charge a fortune for them, since adopted texts are a captive audience, and the larger size is a rationale they can use for the higher fee. (Like a unit price of 4.27 for the smaller size, and a unit price of 4.59 for the larger price justified selling the book for $40 more).

    Computer books in the 90s were other books that would sell at any price, and thus were overpriced. They also tended to be a slightly larger format.

    ravel said:

    Running text is set in 9 pt with generous leading at 12 pt, and titles in 14.56 pt.

    i’d use 10-point, or even 11-point, (or 10.7-point), and still feel 12-point leading gives enough spacing.

    i also feel comfortable changing the leading of pieces. so i’d go with a generous leading for most of the book, and tighten it up only on the few pieces when needed. (oftentimes i’ll make sure the leading matches for the two pieces that compose a facing-pages spread, but i don’t think readers notice a change between pages. and even if they do, what difference does it make? i’ve known poets who set every poem in its own font, specifically chosen for it, and i liked that just fine, so i see no problem with doing intelligent copy-fitting.)

    i’d also size the titles down, and count on bolding and their separation/appearance to make their role clear… that would give you a touch more room, when needed.

    i also didn’t care for the horizontally-placed poems… but maybe that’s just me, which brings up the disclaim: this feedback is given solely because you asked for it. you’re the one doing the job; the decisions are yours.
