2009-11-18 16:07:41 • Arno Enslin

Glyph Scaling, perhaps?

Right. The roots of contemporary H&J systems go back to the work done by Zapf and others in the 1970s and 1980s, which were designed with text scaling in mind. Most fonts can handle 2–3 percent with no noticeable distortion. Some typefaces can handle more; Vialog was designed to distort 5% before it begins to show. Unfortunately many designers never use glyph scaling because they don’t read beyond the most basic books on typography which usually insist that scaling type is always criminal.

2009-11-18 19:41:07 • Nick Shinn

To a large extent, that 150% (or whatever) in the Max Word Space column does not have as much control as one would hope. When the H&J routine gives up and can’t solve your line justification within your parameters, then it’s fallback position will be to just throw in a bunch of extra space anyway. So don’t think that you’re limiting the spaces with that field.

Mostly it affects the threshold above which letter-spacing will be added (if you’ve allowed any). And it might slightly influence the calculations of a multi-line composition algorithm.

There’s a danger that having too narrow a range of parameters will actually flummox the algorithm more often and yield gappier spacing, despite your desires to control it.

2009-11-19 17:54:04 • Arno Enslin

Roy:

Dale Guild Typefoundry, has a nice account of their attempts to recast a full set of Gutenberg’s 42-line bible type.

They ended up with 245 sorts.

They wrote:

“The narrower letters were used to contract the length of a line and the wider ones to expand it. The space separating words remained constant.” And again, “…the narrower or wider alternate characters were skillfully deployed to yield lines of identical optical width…” (Kapr *) These are faulty conclusions. Either the wider or the narrower characters are alternates. As the textual use of the wider characters out-number the narrower ones by approximately 3 to 1, the latter must be considered the alternates. What usually makes these alternates narrower is the fact that they lack serifs on the left side. They are never used or omitted to shorten or lengthen the space required by a given number of characters within a line. Gutenberg had employed them in both his earlier founts, the Sibyllenbuch and the Donatus/Kalender, but none of the extant printed specimens used justified lines. Their inception and use is purely aesthetic. They do not represent consciously narrowed letterforms. These alternates were cut and cast to create tighter fitting of certain character combinations that produced too much white-space within words. The alternates (left side sans-serifs) always follow C, E, F, X, Y, c, e, f, g, r, t, x, and y.

For the full text: http://www.daleguild.com/B-42_Story_01.html

2009-11-21 16:39:48 • hrant

Like other typographic parameters, H&Js should relate to the job in hand. Variables such as measure (column width), leading, typeface, tracking, and word length all come into play.

Tolbert Lanston invented a system of justification by variable letterspacing, for the Monotype machine, in the early twentieth century. This produced an appearance akin to the default that Quark introduced in the late 1980s. However, while Quark’s method was accepted willy-nilly, foisted upon the industry as a fait accompli, Lanston’s innovation was roundly dismissed and never made it to production. People preferred the large variation in word space they were used to, rather than a general watering-down of colour. So the “perfect” mode of justification is also culturally informed, specific to the contingencies of time and place.

It might be noted that in the case of glyph scaling by 2 or 3 percent, the scaling, of course, affects the width of vertical strokes. When the individual characters of a typeface are modified to fit within the constraint of something like the Monotype 18-unit system, the width of the vertical strokes would stay the same - and so it is reasonable to suspect that the scaling of 5 or 6 percent that this might lead to (changing 9.5 units to 9 or 10 units) would also be hard to notice.

I agree with both of your points. But obviously the brain can recognize letters, set in different typefaces. Why? Because there is no fixed image of the letters stored in the brain, but the specific characteristics of the letters.

I assume, that there are two requirements for the non-attention of scaled glyphs:

  1. The difference between the scaled and the normal glyphs must not be so big, that it gets through the consciousness. This kind of difference would be a change of things, that are not characteristic for the specific shape of the letter (of the used typeface), the color for example and also the visible change, that is caused by glyph scaling. It is hard to explain, what I mean. There is unconscious reading on the one side and conscious viewing on the other side.

  2. The scaling must not change the specific characteristics of the letters: If a p is horizontally scaled too much, the descender would become unvisible. (In this context it is interesting, that the bottom serif would partly preserve the descender and therefore one of the specific characteristics of the letter p.)

There is a nice anecdote in While you are reading: A designer creates an unusual ligature and asks different persons, if they can see, what it means. BTW, Gutenberg didn’t do that because he had high ideals. He was simply imitating the scribes* (as Don says). He had to come as close as possible to appease the critics of printing.

This is also why ragged right is seldom used, except in publications with an avant-garde look, or where justification would require retyping the text, because of the ancient or inexpensive equipment used. The scribes Gutenberg imitated justified their pages on both sides, and if that was even good enough for Aldus Manutilus, it ought to be good enough for everyone else!

Eventually, with digital typography, someone is going to think of designing a new font format, to succeed TrueType and Type 1, wherein some portions of the letter can be defined as flexible… so that, for example, the letter H might be scaled horizontally by lengthening or shortening the bar in the middle without changing the width of the vertical strokes.

And word processors might be more advanced, so that one could specify not just limits, but weightings, and even curves… so that one could say that optical scaling below 0.5% has a low cost, but above that a high cost, and similarly with letterspacing - so that instead of one taking place only where the limit for the other is reached, both would be done gradually in the ratio specified by the text composer. Predefined glyph reshaping would come before optical scaling.
