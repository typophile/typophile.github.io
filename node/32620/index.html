2007-03-29 02:44:54 • hrant
I don't have any experience with art schools in particularly, but here are my thoughts as one who spent a lot of time at Universities, as a student and teacher, and also sent my three kids to college. I assume you talked to students and attended classes when you visited. This is essential to seeing whether you are a good match--and doing the same for 4-5 other places. If the students who love it are like you, great. If those who hate it are like you, not so good. I am a great believer in the importance of the distinctive cultural chemistry or atmosphere of an institution. And you only get it by physically being there and talking to students and teachers. It is very important because that's what you live with. The other thing, which is a totally different issue, is what kind of jobs the graduates go into. Does the Art Center actually have a cachet that gives you an advantage in the market place; and do they help graduates make the transition? Find out. The atmosphere and the job edge will help you judge about whether it's worth the money. There is also the question of great, inspiring teachers. Ask the students whether they have any of these. Even in the best institutions they are rare, and they also certainly exist at places that have less cachet. If you hear of some great teachers from the students, that is a very good sign.

A couple of years after graduation I had a job tracking carnivores - bobcat, mountain lion, wolverine, fisher, etc. - with the wildlife department of the US Forest Service. It is worth noting that you can tell the difference between a wild animal and a domestic animal in the register of their prints. When a bobcat lifts its front left foot, for example, the rear left foot comes in and prints precisely in the same spot, no matter what speed the animal is moving. When you look at the print in snow there are sharp, crisp ridges between each of the pads; it looks like a single print, but is actually two. Domestic animals, by contrast, have a much more slovenly gait and leave tracks that are all over the place. It is as though they have become imprecise on a biomechanical level. The rear foot will frequently miss the print of the front foot entirely.

2007-03-30 09:55:03 • WorkOslo
I teach Typography and Design in a Community College in the LA area, so I have some exposure to Art Center. Their program is very intense and can be completed in three years if one has the strength and $ to go continuously. Most of their "freshpersons" have several years of college already. I think that only a small percentage come directly from high school. Many local students go to Pasadena City College first to tighten up their admissions portfolio. The other posters are correct that there are some excellent programs at the local Cal State Universities including San Diego. CSU Long Beach has a national reputation in this area although it is "enrollment impacted" which means that it may be difficult to get classes. Admission into their upper division program is very competitive and available only to students who have been there for several semesters. As a teacher, I tell my students that the greatest determinating factor in their education is their own contribution and effort, but that being said, a competitive place like Art Center can bring out the best in a talented and dedicated student. Good luck with your education!
