The minutes/seconds and feet/inches typographical convention

October 12, 2011 • Seamus

I don’t know if this is the right forum for questions about the history of typographical conventions (if it isn’t, I doubt one exists) but here goes. You often see measurements in feet and inches represented like this: 3'5" meaning 3 feet, 5 inches. You also see the same thing for minutes and seconds: 12'34" meaning 12 minutes, 34 seconds. And even with minutes and seconds as subdivisions of degrees, rather than as measurements of time: 12°17'22" meaning 12 degrees, 17 minutes and 22 seconds. My question is: where did this convention originate, and when did it make the transition from one context to the other? (And are there other units that this convention is applied to?)
The Wiki page ([[http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_(symbol)]]) is skimpy as all get, but looking through the history of astronomy, mapmaking, mathematics and measurements will likely help.

If it can shed some light on the matter, in Italian, the scientific definition of minutes is “minuti primi”, and that of seconds is “minuti secondi”, where “primi” and “secondi” mean respectively “first” and “second”. So it most probably comes from a form of superscript ordinal Roman numerals (I and II).

A very nice find. Besides the unusual symbols for astrological aspects on that page, that primes sloped in reverse are used to indicate higher integer powers of 60 is something that was news to me.

This site: [[http://www.luigigobbi.com/EarliestUsesOfSymbolsOfOperation/]] notes that Cajori’s survey of the history of mathematical notation cited a book from 1571, [[http://books.google.ca/books?id=6NnUKv3eihEC|Prutenicae tabulae coelestium motuum]], by Erasmus Reinhold, as the earliest example of the use of this notation known - but a different notation for the same thing was used by Ptolemy in the Almagest. (Cajori is cited on this web page as disagreeing with an older view that credited Ptolemy as the first user of the prime notation, ignoring the difference in the appearance of the symbols used.)

Another very nice find, John. So the convention probably originates in 16th-century astronomy. (I also doubt the attribution to Ptolemy that Cajori mentions; I don’t think there are any ancient papyri of the Almagest, and the notations in medieval manuscripts vary quite a lot, but perhaps use of the keraia for fractions had some influence.)

So if this convention was transposed by to feet and inches, I suppose it must have been by analogy of duodecimal notation to sexagesimal.
