Tanya, the vast majority of Amerindian peoples north of Mexico had no writing systems at the time of contact, so I suggest you give up trying to find something that conveys 'First Peoples': it would be just one more imposition of artificial, non-Native preconceptions on Native culture. Also, consider that Native peoples do not consider themselves to be a homogenous mass: they belong to different ethnic groups, tribes, civilisations, and to what, in post-contact parlance, they came to identify as different nations. So you should consider which Native people are referred to in the exhibit, and consider with whom they were in contact. Then you might well find that using a contemporary European typeface might be the most appropriate choice: one of the significant achievements of many colonised peoples around the world has been to take the script and language of the colonial power and to redeploy it with their own messages. Here is a thought: in English language typography it is conventional to use italics for words in foreign languages. So you could play on the accepted conventions of the people attending the exhibit, and put the messages of Native peoples in roman type – i.e. make it the norm – and the European text in italics.

2003-10-26 13:58:26 • Dan Weaver
I've been involved with Native American publications since the late 80s. Since there is no true Native type free from European influence, you should concentrate on good design choices for any historical museum exhibit. The Native and European text should feel equal. Any self-conscious treatment of the Native languages will get you no closer to a true Native typography, and may appear to some to be condescending and racist. So, handwritten, distressed, or otherwise quirky fonts should be ruled out unless you are also using them for the French/English. I agree with John Hudson about trying italic & roman to differentiate between the cultures. The odd word or two needing to be "italicized" in blocks of italic text should be set in roman. If you weren't tied to using period faces, a carefully selected serif / sans serif combination would work well. Again, nothing quirky. There's such a wide variety of styles in Native American art. I wish we could see the exhibits you are designing for.

2003-10-28 16:51:36 • tsprowl
The problem with trying to find an authentic "Native style" is that there is none. Even if public opinion isn't a concern, any attempt to find one is likely to be contrived. It's not a matter of political correctness. Even if someone is overly sensitive to what Natives will think, it'll still end up "fake" if they try too hard to find something Native. So, the real problem for this museum exhibit is finding two clearly contrasting faces which go well together and fit the time period covered. The Native aspect is a bit of a red herring. I was overreacting a bit when I suggested staying away from handwritten forms. It's just that I've seen people choose primitive looking styles when trying to "be native". In general, the writing samples I've seen from literate Natives are in the handwriting style of their era. Cursives are not unauthentic. I have seen child-like writing from barely literate Natives, but it is no different than what you would see from poorly educated Euro-Americans from the same era.
