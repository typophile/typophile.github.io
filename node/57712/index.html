Style sheets - best practice?

May 6, 2009 • stephen_k

Is there a ‘correct’ way for the naming of style sheets in InDesign/Quark? I know that with css it’s more regimented h1, h2, h3 etc but have run into problems in print, receiving large documents with very unclear hierarchies of information.

Thoughts?

Hi Stephen,

everything that makes sense to you – ideally to others aswell –, now and later on. Name your styles by function, not by their appearance. If you name a style ‘Bembo all-caps green’, and then change the headlines to small-caps of a different font in a different color, the name will stop making sense. Better use abstract names that refer to the function or structure of the element within the document, e.g. ‘Headline’. Whatever system you use, make sure it is logical and consistent. And avoid special characters. I once ran into a problem with style names that contained colons. F

Largely as Florian says, but think about a self-imposed hierarchy. This hierarchy should make sense to you and to anyone else who may have to work with your files. & if you do lots of similar work, it could then be the basis for further style sheet naming.

I do only books, for instance. So my style sheets tend to look like this (the matter in brackets is not part of the style sheet name, but only to explain it here):

1 CN [chapter number 2 CT [chapter title] 3 epi [epigraph] 4 epi att [epigraph attribution]

5 tx 1 [since most often the 1st text ¶ is different than subsequent ones] 6 tx [text] 7 tx noi [text with no indent]

Then I jump to the extract style sheets: ex 1 [1st ¶ of multi-¶ extract] ex 2 [2nd ¶ and subsequent until ex3] ex 3 [final ¶] ex 4 is for a single-¶ extract

And so forth until the whole darn thing is styled: footnotes, end notes, shoulder notes, bibliography, appendices, tables, index, blah blah blah.

The addition of “a” or “b” or “x” after a style sheet name allows for variants.

I’ve been doing it this way for years. It makes it real easy to start a new book design; names is one thing I need not think about. Composition shops I work with know this system by now. When they find an instance I’ve overlooked, they add style sheets and follow my name system.

As far as those “unclear hierarchies of information,” remember: it is part of the designer’s job to sort out the hierarchies and impose clarity (with the editor’s help).

have fun.
