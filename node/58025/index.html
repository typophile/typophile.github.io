2009-05-15 04:28:46 • micah.min

As someone who just went from a career to art school, trust me, it’s not worth it. A degree from an independent art school is extremely expensive, and because they’re small schools, financial aid is especially limited. Entry-level design jobs are not easy to get and many don’t pay. And if you want to specialize in branding (or anything but web development and interactive design) you’re really limiting the number of job possibilities that you’ll have.

It also doesn’t help that at a private art college your study will be focused even more on art and design than it might be at a larger school, which makes the degree less valuable when job hunting. You might hear stories about how big companies want to pay lots of money to smart creative people with art degrees because they can solve problems, but it’s a myth. I’ve had interviewers tell me that they won’t hire me just because I went to a private art school; they don’t want arrogant creatives who only know how to think about big concepts around.

Go to a state school. Instead of taking a bunch of high-minded arty classes learn how to handle Flash, ActionScript, and web CMS systems like Drupal and Joomla. Your degree will be worth more because non-designers will have heard of the school and won’t think you spent four years in figure drawing. You also won’t have nearly as much debt after school, and not having that burden on your psyche will probably make your life easier and your work better.

2009-05-15 09:26:54 • Andreas Stötzner

I attended 3 art colleges totaling 7 years. The 3 years at the Royal College of Art in the 1960’s was a total waste of time as far as tuition went, but I had a grant from the government so I went my own way and used the facilities and ignored the biased, narrow minded and inflexible tuition. But the situation here is totally different as payment is involved. I spend 90% of my time carrying out commissioned work which includes graphics and calligraphics, and spend sometime, mainly in the USA, holding workshops in lettering, calligraphy, calligraphics and drawing as well as giving private tuition. In the past I have taught at 5 different arts schools, on a part time basis, but at no time for more than 2 days week but mostly just for 1 day a week. More time teaching would have destroyed my freelance work as the most important requirement to succeed as a freelance artist/designer/craftsperson, is not talent, creativity, reliability or how much tuition or time spent at art school or design colleges but being available. If we are not available then it does not matter one bit how many attributes or qualifications we have as we won’t be able to take on the work and the client will go elsewhere probably not to return, Don’t get me wrong, talent, creativity, individuality, reliability, the ability to communicate etc etc are all extremely important things but if we are not available what is the point. A client would rather have a second best piece of work done on time and within budget than an unfinished masterpiece.

So what advise would I give a young hopeful graphic designer ?

The most important requirement is not to be too blinkered in you approach. Be open to all possible avenues and constructive advise and base everything on drawing. To be versatile we must learn to OBSERVE before we can really SEE the things we look at. Without observing, questioning, reasoning and evaluating what we see then we can’t do justice to it. The purpose of drawing is not to produce pretty pictures but to learn something about the subject, be it proportion, movement, color, mood, structure, contrast, trick of light or optical illusions etc but most importantly learning and appreciate the importance of NEGATIVE SPACE. Too much emphasis is based on images rather than the background that reveals the images. To me its the balance of the negative shapes that takes up most of my time, get that right and the rest will fit into place. Too much of the wrong kind of diverse and conflicting opinions, especially in art schools and design studios, causes confusion and uncertainty. it is far better to start trusting your own judgement, difficult as that is, but if we question and observe more then the subject itself will be our best teacher. All design work have their own visual problems and our role is to identify those visual problems through observation and offer alternative solutions until the job in hand tells us that it is happy. No set of concrete rules will find all solutions but observing the work and communicating with it and respecting it and be versatile in suggesting possible solutions will eventually find an acceptable answer. There is no quick fix but rather the enjoyment of finding acceptable solutions through experimentation, failures, discoveries and faith in our own judgements. You don’t need colleges to help with these, we need to learn by DOING especially from the failures. Failures are never failures if we learn from them.

Don’t be tide down by non constructive ‘rules’ as they can stifle creativity and individuality. Constructive flexible advise- yes, concrete rules-no. I would like to think that my versatility comes from the lack of past conflicting rules as I have always been free to experiment without constrictions, so I don’t break rules as I have none. If a job does not look visually right then don’t look elsewhere for an answer or advise because the answer will be found in front of you as long as you identify the problem or problems and work with them. It may take some time but well worth it. Don’t forget also the importance of market research. What bugs me with many graphic designers is that their work may well be visually creative and often exciting but too often they do not clearly communicate. it is obvious that the so much work is done on the computer-nothind wrong with that- but when you see logos for example on moving vehicles they usually fail miserably as no market research had been done. Work on livery needs to be more spaced out and clearer due to foreshortening and movement. The artwork may well look acceptable on screen inches from the nose but in situ is something else.

I feel this is going on too long as there are so many things to consider, so I will stop and just conclude by saying that under current situations I would not waste my money by going to get help in an art schools or design college. Concentrate instead on having more faith in yourself, learn by questioning ‘failures’, seek advise but draw your own conclusion from them, be humble rather arrogant and don’t let ego in any form get in the way. Draw, draw, draw and OBSERVE, experiment and trust your instincts. At times you may need to learn to be a loner and not blindly follow the crowd. If you are interested you may have a look at my website where you can read more about my beliefs under the ‘About’ tab.

So much can be said on this subject.

I recommend you search for the answer to your question within your program. If there is justification for the debt you will owe, then by all means, accrue the debt. What bad is there to be had if you’re getting something from the fees you pay? If you feel like you will achieve nothing, however, then by all means either find another program, or enroll into another college. Really, only you know what is valuable to yourself. Other people can tell you what is important outside you, but within you, we can’t answer that.

I also must contest the idea by some others here that one cannot learn design at an art school. Granted they require different subtleties (pre-press and Web development classes will be scant), but the idea that design and art are two separate fields—in total isolation—is elitist at best. I know that both fields demand different things, but their mediums are virtually one in the same. Developing your artistic mind will keep certain faculties well-groomed and polished for your design mind. Obviously, it is 95% sure that exiting with a BFA in anything but design (or a BFA in design from a very artsy school) will not IMMEDIATELY prepare you for the field, but several years of practice, or an “Eh” job in design will temper you.

As a student in a Liberal Arts college right now, I’ve seen professors of Law who majored in Shakespeare studies and excellent Sociology professors whose original BAs are in Economics. You have a fair share of Business folk who all did God knows what prior to college, and the very common doctor and dentist who studies history or music in undergraduate. If a scholar can make the leap, a designer certainly can.

I agree with Nick Shinn; I don’t regret going to art school either. I had to borrow money from family to do it and tackle the education authorities head-on, but I got there. To study on a BA Graphic Design in London was a 1-in-5 proposition. However, we went to art school in another country and in a different era – before the explosion of ‘education as business’ and all that that entails.

I disagree with Nick on ‘your education should last a lifetime’ exactly for the reasons above, and in this I think James Puckett has the more relevant advice. Unless you’re a self-employed trustafarian there are no jobs-for-life anymore; therefore no education schema that will pre-fit an ongoing design career in the 21st century (where we are all supposed to change careers, vocations and jobs with increasing frequency). This leads us directly to the get-out clause of all these contemporary education hucksters; that we (the aspiring students) should embrace a ‘lifetime of learning’ – meaning they’re not to blame if, on graduation, you find you have to retrain immediately – and this is trumpeted by the universities as much as the state schools and private providers.

The last university BA programme I worked on routinely ignored the industry advice it received regarding graduate skillsets; as a result it outrightly failed (but not in the academic sense) many of its graduates. They thought they were going into design, and they ended up on the shop floor. Because NZ offers a student loan scheme like the one Simon Robertson describes, it’s only government money and the aspiring student’s time and career aspirations that get wasted in this process.

As a parting thought, did you consider job-release training from the security of the little family company you work for? Part-time, correspondence course or night class study?
