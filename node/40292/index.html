Regarding the paragraph composer to single line composer, as far as I know you should always use the paragraph composer. The paragraph composer is a nifty technique in which InDesign "looks" not only at the line it is justifying but also at neighboring lines to try to calculate the best possible justification settings. Single line looks at each line separately, which means you're more likely to get rivers and lakes in text. If you're fully justifying lines, hyphenation tends to be a good idea, especially if the column width is small, so that InDesign has small enough units to work with to keep the spacing consistent. But since rag left keeps the word spaces the same, hyphenation is far less necessary. Caveat: I'm a student whose knowledge may be more academic than practical, though I think it's valid.

I agree with Ricardo. Moreover, why in the world would you ask type designers about setting type? Here are a few observations from a compositor, with the caveat that I have 20+ years setting type with TeX, but less than a year with ID CS2. Paragraph composer versus single-line composer: In general, if you take the time to get the min-ideal-max spaceband settings optimized for a particular typeface, particular size, and particular measure, it will set better type. If you do not, then the single-line composer will set better type. If you think about it, you will see why, but for what it is worth, I've tested this notion on a limited basis and found it correct so far. As to hyphenation and all the rest . . . All typesetting involves compromises. Making the best compromises depends on the particular standards of an audience. That audience is ideally the readers, but in practice is apt to be the editors and/or the designers. There are formal standards, too. AP style is different than "Chicago" style, and both different from MLA style. Legal style has variations too -- the Harvard "Blue" being one style for legal works. If all this were cookbook, the people at Adobe are smart enough to have pre-programmed settings; if not just one, then "book" "magazine" "newspaper" etc. But setting type isn't a cookbook problem, and they're smart enough to recognize that, too.

Here are my thoughts on a few things about InDesign text setting for a magazine. First, a lot depends on your typical line lengths and the typeface you're using. Knowing that would help a fair bit. That being said.... Second, IMO the paragraph composer will almost always give results that are as good as or better than the single-line composer - usually better. However, extra work in adjusting line breaks is the cost of this, as noted. Third, rather than trying to *control* the paragraph composer, you should be thinking about how to *influence* it. It's much easier to stop it from doing something you don't way than to make it do something you do want. In particular, careful and limited use of "no break" formatting is usually the best way to get it to keep stuff together or otherwise influence its line breaking decisions. In a pinch, switch the entire paragraph to the single line composer and manually tweak the line breaks. Fourth, if you're using a well-made typeface, mostly use "metrics" kerning rather than "optical" kerning. BUT, the latter is still useful when mixing type sizes/styles or trying to correct for the omission of a specific kern pair in the text. Stick the cursor between the two letters and change the kerning to "optical" to just affect that combo. Finally, InDesign has that really handy slider (code name: Nigel) that lets you control the tradeoff between hyphenation and spacing. If you don't like hyphenation, don't turn it off! Instead, adjust the slider to the point where you are happy with that tradeoff. That allows InDesign to still hyphenate when the alternative is really bad. (My own opinion it that there's nothing wrong with hyphenation, and I'd much rather have a few hyphens than lousy spacing, so I put the slider pretty far the other way. But I recognize that there are diverse opinions on this issue.) Hope those tips help. Cheers, T Thomas Phinney Product Manager Fonts & Global Typography Adobe Systems
2007-12-25 00:45:27 • Thomas Phinney
I have a few more suggestions: 1) Use the paragraph composer. The paragraph composer should significantly reduce the number of lines that need manual adjustments to improve color. One reason some magazines don't use it is because they are concerned about the effects of late-stage edits (the entire paragraph could reflow). However, it doesn't affect more than 1 paragraph and the change is generally the best alternative. 2) Turn off the hyphenation zone (set it to zero) and experiment with real layouts with real text with different hyphenation-slider settings (like Thomas suggests). 3) For things like headlines or pull-quotes, you can turn off hyphenation and try using "Balance Ragged Lines" which does a first-pass at balancing the line lengths (it doesn't do it grammatically, just mathematically).

2008-01-07 05:23:57 • Thomas Phinney
My advice is to experiment with the Justification (H&J) settings, fine tuning them for the column width, typeface, size, and leading. For instance, in InDesign, minimum word space is set to less than 100% by default. (80%?). The same is true for Quark. But if you like the old-school look of letterpress, then set the minimum to 100%. And letter spacing should be zero!--anything else is desktop publishing. Look for the sweet spot of type size, leading, and line-length where everything falls into place. It may be something unusual like 8.3 on 9.7. Some typographers will even nuance tracking and horizontal scaling too, e.g. -5 tracking and 97.5% scaling. Bear in mind that there is no ideal size and leading, as the nominal size of x-height and extender length varies from face to face.
