2004-03-12 10:53:47 • gerald_giampa
Can I add my 2d worth as a constant user, not a designer, of books.

I find the typeface to be about the least important thing in whether a book is nice to read. How the type is set, and how it is printed, and on what paper, and how the book is bound -- these are far more important. Crispness and modernity are as likely to come from the design of the book, as from the design of the type. I have often seen two books set in the same type, and one is lovely to read, the other ghastly. This is down to the detailed typography, not the typeface.

I suspect that using a new typeface for a book is a leap in the dark. Very long text settings really stretch text types. Little tiny idiosyncracies which would be perfectly tolerable in, say, a magazine article, become infuriating.

I don't know how many books you have designed. But if the answer is "not many", it might be better to choose a typeface that is in common use so that you can look at lots of books that use it and learn its idiosyncracies. There is still surely plenty of scope for creativity in the many other (actually, more important) design choices to be made.
2004-03-12 11:12:41 • pstanley
I agree with Paul that there are many other factors as (or even more) important to the design of a book as the text typeface. If those other things have been dealt with competently, however, the choice of a typeface can play a key role in determining the "voice".

And, as Gerald implied, the choice of a text typeface is often a very personal one for a book designer.

It is tricky to work with a new typeface. I often play for a long time with a new face, before I feel comfortable spec'ing it. When time is tight, I fall back on old favorites because they are reliable in the clutch.

But someone has to be bold and put these new faces through their paces, so good for you for wanting to explore.

Hrant did not provide a link above, so allow me to do so. Whitman may be seen and purchased at the Font Bureau site: www.fontbureau.com/fonts/font_frames.tpl?fontname=Whitman.

However, online showings are not so helpful for book types. A more extensive PDF specimen is available from my site: www.kentlew.com/Type/. The Whitman PDF has several samples in text sizes (9-14 point) with two different leadings.

Even this is not as good as seeing some real-world uses. Unfortunately, the books that I'm aware of using Whitman are all from relatively small publishers here in the States. I don't know that any of them would be on a UK bookshelf. The most likely would be Extreme Simplicity by Christopher and Dolores Lynn Nyerges (Chelsea Green Publishing Co., 2002).

I'll go one better than that, though. If Whitman fits your criteria and you would like to seriously consider it, then send me 1000 words of your sample text in plain text format and give me some specs -- trim and margins, or line-length, leading, and number of lines -- and I'll set you some sample pages in a couple sizes and send back a PDF file.

Best of luck with your project.
