The modular scale is a system of ratios. You pick a key type size and the system generates a set of compatible sizes based on whichever ratio you pick. So in that slide, the key type size is 16pt. The modular scale shows two other sizes, 25.88 and 9.88 point, obtained by multiplying and dividing by the Golden Ratio, 1.618. Setting type at these sizes is said to be harmonious on the page/screen. For me there are so many variables (leading, line length, and of course the style of the font itself) that even with the modular scale you can still easily end up with a mess. Modern notions of science tend to focus on method rather than on 'arrangement of knowledge'. Knowledge can be arranged in any number of ways, many of them arbitrary, whimsical or willful. See, for example, the majority of typeface classification schemes, which certainly involve arrangement of knowledge but are anything but scientific. Science isn't something one has: it is something one does. So the question is not whether typography is or is not a science, but what it means to do science in the context of typography.

Christopher, I don't agree that operational definitions are necessary, and they can be misleading and harmful. I don't want to get too deep into the weeds here on philosophy of science, but Bridgeman's hope that operational definitions are a magic wand to clear up terminology in science turned out not to work. Such scientific variables as force, mass and time do not have operational definitions. They are defined implicitly as part of testable theories, and that, in my view is the best way to do it. Going round and round is the result of getting hung up on definitional matters, instead of trying to get good theories to understand and explain our experience. What are missing is not better definitions, but better theories. By the way, the idea of "construct validity" of a term as explained by Donald Campbell provides an alternative to operational definitions. It is widely accepted in social psychology, I believe. I am not totally happy with Campbell's approach, but it shows how you have to depart of operational definitions.

Typographic variables such as point size and leading are numerical, and categories such as serif and sans appear homogenous and distinct to non-typographers, giving the impression that typography is a good candidate for physical science where instrumental functions can be precisely measured, as with Bill's earlier example of the eyeball. But this is deceptive. Where would Karl Popper have drawn the line? I think he would have concluded that any theory that would be central to a science of typography, concerning such things as serifs vs. sans, line length, leading, or x-height, is not falsifiable, and therefore a science of typography is not possible. For example, a serif-superiority theory that attempted to disprove the null hypothesis, "Serif and sans serif fonts are equally readable" would run into serious statistical difficulties of acquiring a random sample meaningful enough to enable falsification, beginning with the subjective categorization of candidate fonts. Try the keywords "serif" and "sans" at MyFonts, almost 10,000 fonts, and see what comes up. At the moment, reading researchers don't even use randomized font samples. Without random sampling, if an experiment favors Times over Helvetica, that's all it's doing, not saying anything about serifs vs. sans. Formulating general theories about typography but only testing the usual suspects can't be good science. But it's not just the fonts—there are other typographic strata that affect readability: * layout * font size * leading * line length * paper stock * printing method * digital device * platform * browser * font format * hinting * reader demographic * typographer * text content Given the current narrowly defined methods used, it's a huge jump to extrapolate from laboratory to general usage, too big, in practice, for science. Bear in mind that the target is moving, too, with new technology. It would be possible to come up with a few standard settings, and index the readability of certain fonts in those settings, for specific output devices and reader demographics. Would that be science? Is my criticism—that falsifiability is in practice, impossible—valid?
