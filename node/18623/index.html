We seem to have this discussion a lot lately. My opinion is that a true PostScript printer is necessary if you are going to test anything typographic at smaller sizes. Unfortunately, PostScript printers seem exponentially more expensive that laster printers with PostScript emulators. But the output really shows a difference in the details!
2006-03-17 09:04:31 • dan_reynolds

I would rather have a printer without PostScript at all than one with PostScript emulation. If it doesn’t pretend to have PostScript, the computer (either Windows or Mac OS X) does all the rendering, so everything looks as it does on-screen, though it may print more slowly.

If it has fake PostScript, the printer gets a PostScript program, and whether it correctly executes and renders it is anyone’s guess.

2006-03-19 05:12:23 • canderson

Using a PostScript emulation may have downsides, e.g. HP’s had and may still have problems with interpreting hinting. (So I proof fonts unhinted.) But I would not put too much emphasis on the Adobe vs emulation PS. Say, you use an Epson laserprinter: This does have Adobe PS, but its text image too dark – not very useful either. There are many factors involved. In the end it is important that you can check if the design is consistent. Digital type has the disadvantage that the “real” design is imaginery, and no realization of it is “really” trustworthy.

2006-04-18 17:30:37 • mili

It really depends on what your priorites are. Inkjet printers will usually provide better and more consistent color reproduction (especially for contone images), while laser printers will usually provide sharper and truer type. But regardless of what your priorities are, you’ll need a true PostScript printer for professional work.

There are losts of great printers out there. Some cost less than $500. Others need to be leased for $6000/month.

If I were a one man shop, I would buy a cheap PostScript Black and White printer for examining type and an inkjet like the HP Designjet 30 with EFI RIP for color proofing.
