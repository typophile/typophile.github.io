Mongolian cursive

February 12, 2011 • John Hudson

One of the theses I have put forward in various contexts on Typophile over the years is that every mature scribal culture produces both formal (literary, static) and informal (chancery, cursive) styles. So I was delighted to discover this illustration in György Kara’s Books of the Mongolian nomads. Most reproductions of Mongolian script focus on the formal literary hands found in religious manuscripts, and although I have seen examples of cursive brush style, this is the first specimen of a chancery quill style that I have seen. Isn’t it splendid! [img:sites/default/files/old-images/mongcurs_6700.jpg]

2011-02-16 18:11:43 • Nick Shinn

The connection between Mongolian/Manchu writing and Aramaic, via Sogdian and Uighur, is well established. But the Uighurs (a Turkic people of Western China) were the first to rotate the Sogdian script and write/read it vertically, presumably in imitation of Chinese writing (but read columns left-to-write).* The Mongolians adopted and adapted the old Uighur script. There is some scant evidence of Mongolian being written in other Chinese-style scripts, such as Kitan, also vertically, prior to the adoption of the Uighur script.

*Later, after their conversion to Islam, the Uighurs adopted the nastaliq style of Perso-Arabic script, so their old script now survives primarily in the Mongolian context.
