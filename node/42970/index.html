«January is capital “J”, and I guess so is the location.»

All-lowercase seems to be a conscious stylistic choice here, in which case capitalisation becomes somewhat irrelevant.

The proper apostrophe is a must, though. So is using an en dash rather than a hyphen in the two cases on your poster, since they both indicate an interval. So, “11th october 2007 – 20th january 2008” and “10.00 – 18.00”, rather than “11th october 2007 - 20th january 2008” and “10.00 - 18.00”. And if you’re going to give the dash such wide spacing as you do between the dates, you need to do the same between the opening and closing hours. It would be preferable to do it the other way around, though, and reduce the space surrounding the dash between the two dates. Don’t remove it altogether, but rather than having a space character on each side of it, type it directly between “2007” and “20th” and add a bit of kerning (and then add similar kerning between the hours).

 

Did you consider filling the p, o, and a of “portraits” with the orange colour used for “pop”, rather than the green colour used for “art”? That would create a somewhat more even oppositional relationship between the words, I’d think.

And the thing that boggles my mind most of all: why are you designing a poster for an exhibition that apparently ended six weeks ago?

Curious, where did your color palette come from? I don’t see green in any of the “example” images.

Also, you might want to consider at least 4 different artists, the POP ART movement was pretty big, I’m sure you can find lots of great examples from a plethora of artists.

As for the layout, it seems rather stodgy and conformed. I’m assuming this is for a Design 1 class, Intro to Typography, or perhaps a Layout Techniques class. So, I’ll also assume you’re still learning value design skills such as hierarchy, balance and rhythm. Using these principles will help create focal points within your poster with clear movement for your eye to scan without hitting to many visual traps. As of now, the overall color of the poster isn’t inviting, and my eye gets trapped in your “negative” cross that’s formed between your images. I can’t focus on the text as the bottom because it’s formatted in a way that’s difficult to read, and your header text is tight, using only color to determine the breaks (which gets confusing with the negative letter forms filled with a secondary color.)

This is an honest critic of what I see in your original post. I think you have room for improvement. Don’t be discouraged, it’s good to make mistakes. You learn more from them.

The negative spaces between the images should be smaller than the negative space between the images and the edge of the design.

The woman in the upper right is staring right out of the composition. You may want to reconsider the arrangement of the images.

The colours of the images are rather cold, dower and uninviting in contrast to the text. As per the advertising dictum; “make the product the star,”, either use different images or tone down the rest of the design so the the images stand out more.
