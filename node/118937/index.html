1920's sheet music title face

August 23, 2014 • steve9753

This was used in the titles of sheet music printed by Forster Music Publisher, Chicago, in the mid- to late-1920s (on the first page of music, not the front cover). It is a little similar to Century Old Style, Bookman (and many others), but the distinctive shapes are the lower case h, m, n, c and e, and the upper case T. The lower case h and n are my main concern. The letters shown are taken from a few separate titles.
I have not seen this font except in music industry publications by Music Publisher and Edwin H. Morris Company. The distinctive bowed strokes on some letters and the unusual tilde serifs on them remind me of Invitation, by Morris Fuller Benton for ATF, created in 1917 or earlier. Invitation has bowed strokes on the same letters and usual serifs on them, but these are in the form of a reverse wedge rather than a tilde. I don’t know whether the sheet music font appeared before or after Invitation. I have not seen a digital version of either font. Do you have any more letters that you could post, particularly Q and &? Don

I have been trawling through PDF’s of out-of-copyright sheet music from 1900 to the early 1920’s and have discovered that many of the major sheet music publishers in the USA (Leo Feist, Forster, Irving Berlin), Witmark, Jerome H. Remick, Harry von Tilzer, Shapiro, etc.) used this font or variations of it in the titles of their sheet music. The font (or a close relative) was also used occasionally by some European publishers of instrumental music. Later in the 20th century there is a fancier version (also with many variations) which has differences in the serifs and overall shape of some letters, such as the upper and lower case U being shaped like the U in the Korinna font (the top left corner being bowed inwards with a tilde serif), and the upper case A having a crossbar at the top like a smaller version of an upper case T, different amount of slope on the legs of the upper case M, etc. It might actually be a font of a different name which bears similarities.

In the sample image I supplied, which was made up from screen shots of about three separate pieces of music, I think that I might have inadvertently mixed up characters from more than one version of the font. I shall have to spend some time working out exactly which one I am looking for. If I can manage to sort out the various versions, I will post back.

After some research, I’ve found several metal fonts with similar lowercase characteristics during the period from mid-1890 through 1920, but not this exact one – and none of the capitals look like this one. So now I am thinking this may have been a proprietary face commissioned by one of the presses during that period when there was a ready supply of punchcutters at hand. Probably more than a few of them needed the work, too. Even Monotype would do commission work back then.
