You’ve just hit one of my projects, so I’m going to go on a bit, and probably ramble. Hope the information is worth that.

We need to know if these are manuscripts or not, Your use of the term “double spaced” suggests the are. If so, the most important consideration is “Are all the needed characters available?” And that means “Charter” as exemplified by the SIL font family Charis. It may not be as pretty as Yoga or Guardian – though I can argue that, too. (In case you didn’t know it, Charter was designed by Matthew Carter, and it is hard not to gush about his work.)

Here is an analogy. If you’re a typesetter, you might do beautiful work. But if you make typos, you are unemployed. The first requirement is to get the words spelled right. If you can’t clear that hurdle, all other skills are meaningless. It is similar with a typeface: if it doesn’t have the character sets you need, dump it.

FF as a foundry now has a policy that you can’t modify their fonts, which includes adding characters. They want to do the work and charge you for it. SIL Charis is open source. We recommend it to university press editorial departments, where the editor works on the author’s files, preparing them for composition. To date (except for manuscripts having Greek), the only characters lacking were some vowels for Comanche.

It gets a little more complicated if these are finished pieces, as opposed to “manuscript files.” I’ve made up a Charis roman and italic for print, adding old-style numbers, a lot of kerning, redrawing ligatures, adding OT features, etc. Nice fonts, we’ve now used them for two books. Charis prints a bit better than Charter (which wasn’t designed when computer-to-plate offset was the norm). But the hinting is lacking, and I haven’t the skill to do that work. That means PDFs as a final, distributable product (one kind of ebook) may not show as well as with some other fonts. I’m trying to get somebody in the AAUP to volunteer to learn hinting. If we can get that, we’ll have a very good looking, free, open source font family.

I’d suggest you download Charis and see for yourself. If for some reason you want a copy of my version, that would be fine. Bear in mind that as a typesetter, I do work on fonts as needed, so “our” Charis is a work in progress.

When I design books in the social sciences, I often use Charter or Charis. Arnhem Blond would be another choice – Rich Hendel uses it. I can’t get it to work. That happens often to designers, they can get certain types to work for them, but not others. Presuming the typefaces are appropriate to the subject, a different designer can have just the opposite experience.

You will not like my thinking about typefaces for the social sciences. The stereotypical social sciences book is Times with Helvetica display. Stereotypical because circa 1970-80, that’s what was used. And for the reasons you gave. No, not all books of course. Moral: It became comfortable to that audience. Now Times/Helvetica have become such a stereotype that this has sunk in, even to an audience not accustomed to thinking in terms of type. So translate what was comfortable in 1970 to 2010, an you get your answer.

At the risk of offending about 90 percent of the people who read this, book interiors – academic papers – are not a matter of Graphic Design. They are a matter of presenting the author’s arguments to a readership. Go check Beatrice Warde. “Pretty” is not a factor, at worst, it is distracting, at best, neutral. Now if you are in the commercial world, getting someone to buy the book is a concern. But that isn’t you. You sound like a student. If you can borrow some authority for your arguments by selecting a typeface familiar to those in your field, that’s a good thing. And if you’re trying to sell academic monographs, remember they aren’t displayed at the bookstore. There are few graphic elements in these books – papers –that can affect sales. “Disgustingly ugly” would be one, but that judgment would have to come from an audience not particularly attuned to “graphic design” and the latest trendy typefaces.

Charles, my feeling is that no roman type at text sizes is really “pretty”, including Jensen. The beauty of individual glyphs are only really seen at larger sizes, generally over 12 point. What is seen at text sizes, though, is the texture on the page, and the look of words, and that has an aesthetic impact and carries a lot of feelings subliminally that I think you have given short shrift in your comment. Feelings such as of authority of the text are not a matter of familiarity alone, but also design.

Forrest, I would caution you against making decisions only on the basis of viewing type on line. The beauty of large type on the screen is a really deceptive guide to how it will look in text sizes on the page. You’d best look at books you admire to know what your setting will look like. When working on my own Caslon revival, I felt I had to constantly resist the temptation to make it look more elegant on the screen (by increasing contrast) because I think that would have affected both readability and the aesthetic I wanted at small sizes.

Hi Bill. OK, we disagree on this one. I’ll still recommend your Caslon to the AAUP community for the times a Caslon is right. In fact, I already have.

I view interior design similar to set design. Audience counts for a lot. But in the case of books, the presentation isn’t as crucial an element as in a theater set (you don’t have an actor searching vainly for the door the script tells him he is suppose to be exiting), and the designer should take less upon themselves, certainly if their is no author involvement.

As to Jensen in The Pacific, a second grumble is the quote marks. Frequently closer to the preceding word than to the word beginning the quote. I’ll allow it colored my judgment. On the other hand, I was having lunch with Rich Hendel and mentioned the book was set in Jenson. He is not a dramatic fellow; the water spewed on the table cloth was a genuine reaction. On reflection, it is best not to make such design comments when someone is eating or drinking.
