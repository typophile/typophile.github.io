2006-03-07 20:01:38 • BradB
Some major differenced explained here: http://www.ms-studio.com/articlesarialsid.html The squished appearance most likely has to do with how it is hinted. Hinting is a process that adds instructions and other data to help render a font at low resolutions. Generally, the effect is to distort the shape of the letters to better fit the pixel grid, retaining enough of the shape to keep it recognizable (as an "a" in this case), but not necessarily accurately displaying the real shape, which isn't possible at low resolution anyway. For what it's worth, Arial is probably one of the most intensively hinted fonts in existence. The problem is, the basic design (derived from Helvetica) is not well suited to on-screen display, especially at small sizes (also true of Helvetica, or any print-centric font). You can't say they haven't tried, though.

2006-03-08 18:20:17 • BradB
Photoshop's sharp/crisp/strong/smooth options ignore the font's hinting, being based on a "plain" rasterization of the font's outline. You can see this by doing manual kerning, and watching how the vertical stem changes. The "none" option shows the hinting. In fact, Arial can probably get away with having a heavy central crossbar because it is so extensively hinted, and designed more for PC monitor display than being used in Photoshop. Faces which have a markedly thinner central crossbar to the "e" and "a", such as Gill Sans, fare better in the kind of test you're doing.
