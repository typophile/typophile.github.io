SE, I think you're really right and would add only that, although they may not teach it in business school, a lot of these types of undertakings are the product of executive shoving matches over who's in charge and who's going to have the most visible legacy. Ambition and craft can make for some uncomfortable partnerships, and it's a lucky day when craft prevails. When you see relatively powerful entities tinkering with a successful brand it could be a lot of things — the need to stay fresh, the desire to expand, and so on — but it's also important to remember that for high-ranking people the very act of taking action and of making decisions is the single most important thing. You don't get recognized for doing nothing, and the tinkering is itself a theatrical device, an expression of power, a shaping of reality.

2011-01-08 19:48:11 • riccard0
It isn't a bad logo. Or, perhaps more accurately, it wasn't a bad logo. Originally, when Starbucks was one cafe in Seattle, a logo that looks like the sign outside, say, an English pub, was entirely appropriate - both to the business, and to the available budget for concerns such as branding. Over the years, I think they did an excellent job of modifying the logo so as to combine the two conflicting goals of having a brand symbol that has the elegance and stylishness befitting a major corporation - and a symbol with the quaintness and charm associated with a friendly neighborhood business devoted to quality. While the evolution has been away from (b) and towards (a), they've done an excellent job of keeping as much of (b) as possible while progressing towards (a). That mermaid with stripes is, in its own way, a great work of art, holding emotions and symbolism in perfect balance. Compared to the Mona Lisa, she's been minimalistic since 1987 and especially 1992, when the secondary circle motif was established.
