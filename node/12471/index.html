well, it sounds like you’re looking for a low cost way to get going (much like I was when I was in LA). here’s a few techniques: get to know local businesses, join an active chamber of commerce (eg City of Industry, Long Beach, LA), make contact with every agency and studio you can find, and pass out lots of business cards. oh yeah, and never stop making phone calls – you never know who you’re going to find.

and a few other thoughts: I found Craig’s list tends toward the low-ballers of the world. then again, I haven’t looked in a while. CreativeHotlist might be a good spot for getting in touch with other agencies or corporate contract jobs. and AIGA LA might prove to be a strong resource (I hate designer socials, so I didn’t try).

best of luck to you. your work is quite good, and the writing on your site should win over any sensible business person.

Hi Dan,

All I can suggest is:

  1. Look anywhere for clients. We have found that some of our best clients have come from the strangest places.

  2. I assume that you’re a designer first and a businessman second. Most of the people you deal with will probably be business people first. This means that:

100% of your clients will be honest, likeable individuals when you discuss their initial requirements.

40% of your clients will need two or three reminders before they remember to pay up.

25% of your clients will have a book keeper or accountant who goes on holiday for three weeks whenever you submit an invoice.

One or two of your clients will just refuse to pay. (If the legal system in the US is anything like it is here in the UK, don’t waste your time chasing debts. Either write the debts off, or hire someone big & scary)

Dan,

Congrads on going out on your own in a new city. big challenge.

All the advice is good - most of it can be summed up as make lots of noise and eventually momentum will begin to work in your favor.

Can I suggest that you take a little more strategic approach?

Sit down and figure out what your position in your marketplace should be - based on who you are, capabilities, where you succeed, what kinds of clients are you successful with and what do these clients look for in a design firm - that is for starters.

Anyway, figure out what you can deliver on and who is an ideal fit. That fit needs to include who is able to appreciate your value and is willing to pay for it. Once you have a profile of the ideal client start researching your market to identify actual companies that fit your target. Create a database of these potential clients - call them, ask for who buys your type of service there - get name, title and phone number. no need to talk to them yet, just get info at this point.

OK - now that you have an idea of your perfect client, a list of potential perfect clients and some free time on your hands … begin to work out what value these clients are looking for - NOT what you have to offer, but what value they want. (If this does not click, get some books out on value oriented marketing etc.) Figure out that value - it may be different for each one or all the same. You gotta figure it out. if it is different for each, you may have been too broad in identifying your target. Keep it tight for starters - you can always broaden once you have some success under your belt.

Now, you have your value identified, create a message around that value - tell them how you offer the value they want. this is your mantra. hammer it home. Any contact you have with them should communicate this value - phone calls, direct mail .etc.

You have a number of references here on self promotion and marketing, so I will not go through a step-by-step on that. The key here is:

Identify your target based on fit Identify the value your target wants Create messaging around that value

March out your marketing machine. Unleash your campaign - and don’t just rely on a one-off effort. Soften them up with a post card, make a phone call, get a portfolio review, send off a white paper that you wrote (make sure it is value oriented!) and book orders, baby!!!

Also, key - I am begging you not to sell on price. You live by price you die by price. No need to step on their neck and take advantage - that is wrong and bad business. but, charge a fair rate and get paid for the value you offer.

Gasp … one last thing, I do not agree regarding earlier comments about business people not wanting to pay and 25% of them screwing you over. That may be someone else’s experience, but it is like marriage and divorce - rarely is it just one side’s fault. I rarely have a client that does not pay - rarely. We work with great people and we do great work. We deliver and they pay. Everyone is happy. Out of a million in sales this year I am out about $1,200 and that is because the company went under. Not bad odds if you ask me.

Ok, enough outta me - all the best Dan!

– Mike

I started my company by purchasing 40 white pocket folders at Staples Office supply, printing different 5 samples of my work 40 times off my Commodore Amiga & b/w Canon bubblejet printer, putting a cover letter and price list into the folder with the samples, and then mailed it out to the 40 closest printshops in my area.

I had NO CLUE as to what I was doing.

After three months, I got a call from one printshop who picked out a line off my cover letter that read - we have (we meaning me, myself & I) over 70 (wow!) different typefaces. They had to replace a line of text in a job, and didn’t have the font Romic.

Neither did I, but that didn’t stop me! I bought the font from Letraset on a Mac disk, paid $40 to have it transfered to a PC disk, and used a PC to Amiga disk reading program to get it onto the Amiga. Then I converted it to an Amiga font with a PD utility (Amiga’s had a utility for ANYthing back then) and I typeset the line of text they needed.

That was 1992. Today, we do over $20k a year business with that same (my very first) client.

Moral of the story…

Getting business means solving people’s problems. You are never loved or needed more then when you are solving a problem for someone. All you need to do is let the world know what problems YOU solve. HOW you do this is the subject of many, many marketing and business books, but once people know you can solve their problems, they will come to you. The first step is letting them know you exist.
