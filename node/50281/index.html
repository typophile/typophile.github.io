2008-10-10 20:06:35 • Robby Woodard

Everything looks OK, except

  1. You should change fi and fl in your substitution to f_i and f_l, with no Unicode number. If you want to leave fi and fl in with a Unicode index that’s fine, but copy the glyphs, name them f_i and f_l, and switch those.

  2. You should switch the double-f’s first (or double anything. . .)

e.g.

feature liga { # Standard Ligatures

Latin

sub f f i by f_f_i;
sub f f j by f_f_j;
sub f f l by f_f_l;
sub f f m by f_f_m;
sub f f n by f_f_n;
sub f f r by f_f_r;
sub f f t by f_f_t;
sub f f u by f_f_u;
sub f f y by f_f_y;
sub f i by f_i;
sub f l by f_l;
sub f f by f_f;
  .
  .
  .

etc.

Also, you may want to exclude f_i and f_f_i for Turkish via a language tag.

Your oldstyle numbers aren’t named properly, IMO. Numbers should be named zero, one, etc., with variants using a period.

I use .op for “oldstyle proportional”, .ot for “oldstyle tabular” .lp for lining proportional, and .lt for lining tabular.

so @onum2=[zerooldstyle oneoldstyle twooldstyle threeoldstyle fouroldstyle fiveoldstyle sixoldstyle sevenoldstyle eightoldstyle nineoldstyle numbersignoldstyle dollaroldstyle percentoldstyle perthousandoldstyle centoldstyle sterlingoldstyle yenoldstyle florinoldstyle eurooldstyle];

goes to

@onum2=[zero.op one.op two.op three.op four.op five.op six.op seven.op eight.op nine.op numbersign.op dollar.op percent.op perthousand.op cent.op sterling.op yen.op florin.op euro.op];

and they need to be so named in the font – again, with no Unicode index.

2008-10-10 23:37:38 • Nick Shinn

BTW, if you set up oldstyle figures as default (as was done e.g. in the Microsoft Corbel and Constantia fonts), please remember to replace then with the lining figures not only in the “lnum” feature but also in the “case” and perhaps also “titl” feature. Microsoft have not done so, and therefore users who activate “all caps” in InDesign, end up with all-uppercase text with oldstyle figures, which is not optimal. They would need to explicitly switch on lining figures as well, which some of them may not be aware of, and there may be OpenType-aware applications that do expose an “all caps” feature but do not expose the different figure styles.

Regards, Adam
