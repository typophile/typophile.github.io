Sorry for the delay. A couple of people sent me e-mail with guesses, but no one got it quite right.

You are correct, William, in noting that the document is a palimsest: an earlier text has been erased and the new text written over it.

The language of the text is Hebrew, but written in with Arabic letters. This is the direct reverse of the normal interaction between language and script, e.g. Yiddish, Ladino and Judaeo-Arabic, in which Jewish communities develop a dialect of majority local language(s) but write it using Hebrew letters. This example is stranger still, because although the text is written with Arabic consonants, the vocalisation is written with Hebrew nikudot (vowel marks). The text is Biblical (Exodus 1:12-22) -- which makes the use of Arabic letters even more remarkable -- and also include te'amim (accents or cantillation marks). I wish I had a colour image: the letters are black, the nikudot are red, and the te'eamim are green. I'm not sure what colour the Arabic shadda marks are. The original manuscript is in the British Library, so I'll go look at it next time I am there.

The likely history of this document is fascinating. It dates from the 10th or 11th century, and is a product of Karaitic Judaism, the anti-Talmudic competitor to Rabbinic Judaism. Colette Sirat notes: 'Certain Karaites considered the original written text to be identical with the true tradition of reading: it is not reasonable to think that certain of the divine words were written down in a certain way but must be read in another, as the masorah maintains. So as to remove any further ambiguity, some people decided to write the Bible in Arabic characters without the masorah, thus giving the only text that they considered authentic.' It takes some doing to wrap one's head around this reasoning: only the original Hebrew consonantal text is authentic, and to better isolate this from latter readings we're going to write it in Arabic letters.


See also:

Sirat, Collette. Hebrew Manuscripts of the Middle Ages. Cambridge, 2002. pp.49-51.

Hoerning, R. Six Karaite Manuscripts of Portions of the Hebrew Bible in Arabic Characters. London, 1889. [Cited by Sirat.]

I certainly didn't intend to equate practice of religion simply with observance of ritual. It is indeed possible to be slavishly observant of ritual yet lack charity or develop spiritually or, as a friend put it, to know all the rubrics yet live like an animal. I maintain that what determines a religion is form and matter (which is a particularly Thomistic way of looking at it, I'll admit). The form and matter of Catholicism differs so completely from that of, for instance, Presbyterianism, that I think it is ridiculous to try to lump them together into a single Christian religion. The problem of this discussion may be, simply, that we lack a sufficient terminology to describe, at once, an historical set of shared beliefs and, in some cases, similar practices (what Adam calls religion) and also the very clearly distinct set of formal practices and material beliefs (what I call religion). I'm certainly not denying that both of these phenomena exist. Without the former, there wouldn't be any point in praying for Christian unity, but on the other hand how would you know such unity if you saw it? As agreement in form and matter. If, as Adam has, you apply the term religion to the shared beliefs, rather than to the distinct matter and form, you are left without a good term to label that which is distinct within this 'religion'. You can, as Adam has, fall back on the term 'churches', which is certainly a common currency in Protestantism, but this is a terminology that runs directly counter to the explicit teaching of at least one of the distinct entities to which you are trying to refer. One of the most important palimpsests, if not the most important is the one containing Archimedes Method. Here Archimedes explains how he made his great discoveries. The Archimedes was erased to write a prayer (!) and thus a precious historical record was almost lost. Someone saw the erasures at the end of the nineteenth century and realized what it was. Recently they have been able to recover more of it using infrared technology etc.

Archimedes is arguably the most original scientist in history, and his proof of the area under a parabola is just mind blowingly inventive. This was the first breakthrough to the calculus - done geometrically! In the same generation as Archimedes works were translated into latin in Europe, the scientific revolution took off. They, such as Galileo and Newton's teacher Isaac Barrow were his disciples, referring to him as the 'divine Archimedes'.
