Does hinting help for prints?

It depends. It definitely makes a visible difference on common laser printers.

With hints, stems that are different may look the same in the printout. Without hints, stems that are the same may look different.

I did test prints (laser printer, 1200dpi) without hinting, with FL autohinting, with AFDK autohinting and with my own manual hints. All hinted versions printed better than the no-hinted in text sizes (manual method is the best one). No difference with poster sizes.

If you draw the fonts as Postscript curves then you should test them as Postcript fonts. Converting to TrueType curves will introduce deformations in your curves that do not exist in your working files.

As for hinting, it’s best to not hint test fonts unless you’re working with a printer that has true Postscript support and you’re printing light weights at small sizes and find that you have serious problems with stem weights varying. Printers that use various PS emulation systems do not always interpret hints correctly and will sometimes produce deformed curves. Printers that do not even support Postscript do some really wacky stuff when converting the curves and should never be used to proof fonts. Never use the Fontlab autohinter as it produces bad hints that will deform curves in any laser printer. Even with hinting you will still have problems with light fonts at small sizes and should just proof these fonts at larger sizes.

Make an effort to learn what your laser printer has trouble with so you can think about it when you’re proofing. For example, most laser printers cannot print hairline fonts (~40 unit stems @ 1000 UPM) consistently at less than 24 points. If you know that your outlines are correct and it still prints poorly, you have to accept this limit of the technology and just work around it. It can be a lot more efficient to just run the FDK stem weight checking script and review that than to deal with screwy printouts.
