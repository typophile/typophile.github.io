One thing I’ve noticed that gets left out of the equation when comparing the sustainability of digital communication to print is transportation. With electronic media we just have to push a few electrons around. With printed media you have to transport raw materials to a mill, then from the mill to the printing plant (which also needs ink transported). Then the finished printed material is shipped to a distribution point before being sent to stores and/or readers. If that material is to be recycled then it is shipped back to a recycling center and the whole transport cycle is done over. That’s a lot of in-between steps versus skipping all that raw materials transport and just taking the same electronic communication and piping it directly to the reader using electrons. To me that seems like an exponentially more efficient way to communicate. We all know that the current ebook media is primative, and society in general isn’t used to reading on them. But kids learning to read today are more accustomed to reading electronic media and as they grow up the media is going to get far better than we can imagine. A very long time in the future, it may indeed be that digital books will almost completely replace paper books. The preconditions for that are, of course:
  • Digital books, while they may be protected by some form of DRM, will be freely movable between display/storage devices,
  • A digital book, since it doesn't consume paper which is heavy to move around, will be somewhat less expensive than its paper counterpart, and
  • Digital book readers will be comfortable for reading, suitable for almost all types of book, inexpensive and convenient.
We're not quite there yet. But if these conditions are met, then the great advantages of digital books - the ability to carry a library of thousands of volumes in your pocket, rather than building an edifice to house it - would take over. However, for a long time to come, many older books will be available secondhand in paper form for as little as a dollar. Even if the DRM for digital books is such as to let one keep one's library when one buys a new digital book reader, and eliminate any concern over losing one's library if something happens to that book reader, it is not likely to also permit a market in secondhand copies of digital books to exist. Since every secondhand book started life as a new book, and purchasers of new books will have their own utility in mind when buying a new book, this in itself will not inhibit the success of the digital book. But until the last paper book either falls into the public domain, or becomes an expensive collector's item, even long after new books have stopped being printed on paper, there will be a robust market in secondhand paper books.

2010-01-14 18:32:34 • Christian Robertson
Rich: But it seems I wasn't far off - at a UPM of 2048 fussing over moves of one-unit seems a bit nit-pickishly anal retentive. However, if I ever actually design a typeface (unlikely) or more probably edit an existing one, I will be a one unit mover. I've now worked on two projects in which we settled on a UPM of 4096, because the 2048 grid didn't provide for fine enough adjustments. These were both display types with fine features, so one would expect them to be used at larger sizes at which the construction of these details would be visible, but the other aspect of the design in which ‘fussing’ over single unit movements is important is in relative weight of thin strokes. Rather than thinking about the single unit movement relative to the UPM, you should think about the single unit movement relative to two thin strokes, one horizontal and one vertical: suddenly that single unit makes the difference between these strokes being optically balanced or not. I remember many years ago listening sympathetically to Luc(as) de Groot's complaints about the difficulty of designing a super-lightweight sans on a UPM of 1000. If the stems were on average 20 units wide, a shift of a single unit represented a 5% difference in stem weight, and that's much too big a jump when trying to optically balance horizontal and vertical stems. Again, in design what matters is not the distance of a move relative to the UPM size, but the distance relative to other distances. Type design is an excellent example of Marx's principle that cumulative quantitative changes result in qualitative change. :)
