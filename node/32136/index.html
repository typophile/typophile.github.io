Adobe OT PS Standard Names wrinkle?

March 10, 2007 • charles ellertson

In an earlier thread, Miguel Sousa (I believe stating Adobe’s current practices) indicated that the correct glyph name for, say, U+00B2 is /two.superior.

I’m a little confused. According to Unicode and Glyph Names [Document version 2.3, Last upated April 17, 2003], “The specification consists of the Adobe Glyph List (AGL), a mapping of specific names to Unicode values, and of rules for decomposing glyph names. . . . This specification is intended to be stable, i.e., never revised.”

Now this covers the rules for decomposing glyph names, not the naming practices that may change from time to time, so that’s all right.

But one of the rules is “drop all the characters from the glyph name starting with the first occurrence of a period (U+002E FULL STOP), if any.”

So, the glyph string

/one/zero/two.superior

becomes

/one/zero/two

or 102. Much different than 10^2

on the other hand, /one/zero/twosuperior

is perfectly clear.

The only reason we worry about glyph names occurs when we are trying to derive character information from a glyph list, right? And practically speaking, the only time this comes up is with Acrobat, right? And one of the features of Distiller is that a cmap is made a part of the PDF file, so the only time we’re going to have to construct character information from a glyph list from glyph names themselves is when that cmap is missing or damaged.

What am I missing here?

Mac and perhaps Unix systems still look at the glyph names.

Charles,

your analysis is correct. Mapping of “two.superior” to U+00B2 is semantically inconsistent. To be fully faithful to their principles, Adobe should have a glyph “twosuperior” or “uni00B2” mapped to U+00B2, and an identical duplicate, “two.superior” or “two.sups”, unencoded. Only the latter would be used in OpenType Layout features such as sups.
