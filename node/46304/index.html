2008-08-05 16:43:09 • Si_Daniels

Thanks to Thomas for finding the actual study report. Unfortunately the popular press report missed the point of the study. Here is my summary:

In their first study the participants read instructions about an exercise program. The instructions were either written in an easy to read font (Arial 12) or a difficult to read font (Brush 12). The participants who read the instructions in the easy to read font reported that the exercise program would take less time and they would be more likely to take part in the program than the participants who read the instructions in the difficult to read font.

In their second study the participants read instructions about how to prepare a Japanese roll. The instructions were either written in an easy to read font (Arial 12) or a difficult to read font (Mistral 12). The participants who read the instructions in the easy to read font reported that the recipe would take less time to prepare and they would be more likely to try it than the participants who read the instructions in the difficult to read font.

http://sitemaker.umich.edu/norbert.schwarz/files/08_song___schwarz_effort_psychscie_in-press_30apr08.pdf

To me this is a perfectly reasonable demonstration that typeface selection is important. There isn’t anything to feel threatened by here.

Does anybody actually READ these studies before criticizing them at great length based on bizarre snippets of media coverage? Have none of you been misquoted or had the media quote you oddly out of context?

To repeat what I posted on the ATypI list, with a couple of minor edits for clarity:

It turns out the actual studies (there were three related studies) are posted here: http://sitemaker.umich.edu/norbert.schwarz/files/08_song___schwarz_effort_psychscie_in-press_30apr08.pdf

The first study compared just Arial and Brush Script, used in restaurant menus. The second and third compared Arial and Mistral, used in recipes.

Yes, they found that Arial was more readable than Mistral or Brush Script, but the point was not to find the most readable font possible. They were trying to manipulate the readability as an independent variable, by using the font. That is, they needed a more readable font and a less readable font, in each study. The dependent variables were the conclusions people would draw about the content being changed by the ease of reading.

So, unless you think Mistral and/or Brush Script are in fact more readable than Arial, folks should not have problems with this study based on their beliefs about the legibility of different typefaces.

Having read the paper, I am reasonably convinced that they succeeded in measuring what they wanted to measure. The results of the first study would have been of dubious by itself, as one could argue that there were other plausible causes for the experimental outcome, which would be a result of the font but not based on simple difficulty of reading. But the second and third studies lead to a reasonably strong conclusion that their theory is correct.

What clearly dissatisfies some of us is how incremental and simple the results of these tests can be. We know the result before it’s revealed. But there also seem to be a number of tests with flawed questions at their root, such as “is my new font, designed specially for the blind, that I’m getting school credit for, more readable than an intricate, curlicued display font?” The result might have integrity (why yes, this simple font based on Comic Sans is more readable than that scratchy, ambiguous display font), but the test itself doesn’t; it’s a foregone conclusion, and the design of the test is manipulated to give the desired result. Those tests are specious because they aren’t objective, and they ask irrelevant questions.

However, Nick and David, while I know what you know, and we know it, and it apparently is going to take until 2168 to accumulate enough empiric data to confirm it scientifically, what Kevin has said holds true: if an experiment asks a very simple question, and I’m sure you’ll agree it’s difficult to design these tests, we can actually get meaningful results. But the meaning and the import of the results is likely to be much more satisfying and consequential to someone who doesn’t design type all day. It doesn’t make the test false, or the question wrong, or the whole thing a load of poppycock, if we find the result boring or predictable.

Kevin is not disputing that it may be more interesting or revealing if we test other variables, like size and contrast and all the niceties that go into reading. But we’re throwing the baby out with the bathwater if we dismiss every finding because it isn’t sophisticated enough for us. Once a number of these results are assembled, a more nuanced and specific test can be designed that includes those parameters and takes into account the results already discovered. That’s the scientific method. Reading is very complicated, and so many factors affect it, that of course it will take a long time for all those variables to be understood. Each one has to be isolated in a large testing situation and clear differences have to be demonstrated. Then there are the thousands of typefaces, the choice of which inevitably rubs people the wrong way.

2008-08-09 17:20:09 • billtroop

The methodological weakness of the Song & Schwarz study is a weakness that is very common in the ‘softer’ sciences such as psychology and social science. It is that the theory they are testing is not well specified. And as a result it is not clear what lesson to take from the results.

My old teacher Popper used to say that an experiment is only as good as the theory that it is testing. If the theory that is being tested is not clearly and precisely specified, then the chances are that you don’t learn a lot from the result.

The problem that typographers such as Nick Shinn and David Berlow are having here is that they know that there are so many more variables involved in readability than typeface. Since Song and Schwartz don’t discuss these, it is not very clear what you can conclude from the study.

Also note that they asked about the readability of the typeface after they had done the reading and got answers on how difficult they expected the task specified would be. This is a problem. I know from doing simple surveys of satisfaction with courses I have taught that if a person likes the course, they are likely to be positive about everything, and if they are negative, negative about everything. The respondents can’t necessarily separate out very well what they are responding to.

Also note that people may perceive ‘readability’ of the simplest sans forms as being most readable, but that perception may be based on impression of individual letters or words, and may not necessarily reflect actually readability of extended text as far as ease, speed and comprehension. The subject guessing is about readability, not giving an objective result.

So with these confounding factors, what they found is only this: post-hoc feelings about typefaces of these sizes, fonts and layout, and post-hoc feelings about ease of task described went together.

So is this a somewhat interesting confirmation that typography matters? Yes. Is it clear exactly what this study means? Not really. Not a great study, and not a dreadful one either. Just so-so.

…where do you draw the line between persuasion and coercion?

Song & Schwartz: “We propose that people draw on the ease with which information about … behavior can be processed in making effort estimates… We predict and find that people misread the difficulty of processing instructions as indicative of the difficulty of executing the behavior…”

This assumes that typeface choice acts coercively, in a directly physical way that the reader can’t avoid. So, you specify a particular typeface, and it’s hard to read, therefore the reader thinks that what it’s describing is hard to do.

A more persuasive explanation: the reader’s “effort estimate” is made by categorizing the genre of typeface (hence my reference to memory theory). Therefore, typeface choice acts to suggest attributes of the document (not the text), based on the reader’s cultural awareness, not their biological constitution.

Now admittedly, there is plenty of room for coercive cultural propaganda, but it can’t be tied to typeface choice pure and simple, given the mutability of typeface meaning.
