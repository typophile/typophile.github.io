Times New Roman Sans

March 26, 2006 • versterhaus

In G. Kevin Connolly’s thesis: “Legibility and Readability of Small Print” (http://www.psych.ucalgary.ca/PACE/VA-Lab/gkconnol/Abstract.html ) he lists one of the typefaces applied in the test as: “Times New Roman Sans”.

Does anyone know what typeface he might be referring to?

Perhaps a custom version of Times New Roman where the serifs where chopped off?

One thing is quite certain, calling a typeface Times New Roman Sans is basically the same thing as calling it Times New Serif Sans, which is nonsense. So, Times New Roman Sans is probably Times New Roman without serifs, created for testing purposes.

2006-03-26 23:18:29 • Miguel Sousa

Yes, Courier was the mainstay in legibility testing well into the 80s. The main reason is that every typewriter had it! :-/ Or at least something your average cognitive psychologist thought was close enough… And it’s not much different now, with Arial The Ubiquitous And Modern taking over.

Comparing Times to “Times Sans” to get an idea of whether/how_much serifs help readability is certainly much better than the usual buffoonery of comparing Times to “Arialica”. But it’s still hard to pull off, because the necessary spacing change when you remove the serifs is as critical as it is difficult to nail down. Also, their Times Sans probably looks odd, so extra special attention has to be paid to the Familiarity factor.

The thing is, since we’re essentially still babes in the woods when it comes to the PROPER testing of typeface readability, the serif/sans issue is probably too ambitious. A much better test, although one that would result in gleaning some deep mechanics instead of providing handy canned recommendation, would be to gauge the effect of letterspacing. And since Thessaloniki ‘04, Larson has been doing just that. Now if he could just get his subjects deeply immersed…
