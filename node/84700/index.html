I’m concerned about two things about that article.

One is that to many people, old-style figures look so strange and peculiar, that if you bring them up to people who doubt that good typography is important, you will end up being dismissed.

The other is that the other differences in the example you showed were much too subtle to make an impression on the general person - and while the article itself is written for an audience of typographers, it’s about how to make themselves clear to non-typographers.

To convince a layperson that typography matters, you need to do so in a simple manner that even the layperson can immediately grasp.

Thus, what I would suggest as a basis for comparison would be a page set in a modern typeface… of the thin and anemic kind favored in the late nineteenth century. Compare that with an example of readable fine typography, and you will begin to make the case that typography can matter, at least to a limited extent in extreme cases. That will be a start.

2011-08-19 18:26:04 • William Berkson

As a type designer, I don’t have to explain Why Typography Matters, but How Is it Possible to Make a Living Doing That?!

I explain that everyone with Word is now a typographer, and that despite bundling the number of font users (such as themselves) is so huge that the small segment of users (professional and otherwise) who would pay to licence something different is nonetheless large enough to support a digital font industry with hundreds of foundries producing new fonts, every day.

The next question is along the lines of How Do You Make it Different? —and that get us into Thomas' Three Fs, but I also like to discuss how the diversity of typographic form (even within a single writing system) makes sense from both a formalist and a structuralist perspective.

In other words, if you consider type shapes purely formally, there is an almost infinite number of ways a legible alphabet may be shaped. And if you consider type forms as representing meanings within the culture in which they occurred, then because cultures are ever-changing, then so too must type forms be ever-changing.

One is that to many people, old-style figures look so strange and peculiar, that if you bring them up to people who doubt that good typography is important, you will end up being dismissed.

Years ago, I was typesetting an educational document, and pointed out to the author the fi etc. ligatures that the font I had used provided (in an expert set). She was most put out, and said she thought that they looked weird and would distract readers. I pointed to the novel that she was halfway through reading, lying nearby, and asked her to look in it for words containing fi fl ffi or ffl. She did so and was amazed to find that the book was full of those distracting ligatures. That evening she phoned me from her home to exclaim ‘All my books have them!’.

Books are (mostly) your allies. They represent several hundred years of typographic practice and refinement, and can be used to demonstrate the norms of typographic communication. Even oldstyle figures.

2011-08-20 02:33:19 • John Hudson

Nice job, Thomas. I’ve been spending the last year in my masters of ed in arts & learning program exploring the communicative aspects of glyphs. Several of my research projects involve students creating then assessing typographic or lettering pieces on what they perceive in terms of message, emotion, association of imagery or concept, etc.

The projects use non-latin alphabets, illegible handlettering, and/or obscure languages in order to remove the “reading of words”. In other words, the pure communicative power of letters/glyphs themselves.

Many of the students were astounded how much they could communicate without “words” and as a result, many pay far closer attention to the minutia and subtleties of typography.

Now if we can only get the designers and creatives to “see” as opposed to “read”.

First, nice article, Thomas!

Non-designers typically get in arguments about form without considering function (like in the ligatures example that John Hudson mentioned), and to me that’s the reason why typography is so important and relevant for designers.

Typography is the study of typefaces and their relationship with design and communication. Typography, to be effective, must be the last thing the audience has to worry about about. The typefaces can be noticeable, legible, and possibly meaningful, however, they cannot be something too complex to decipher.

There’s a wonderful book by Jan Tschichold called “The Form of the Book”, it deals with many aspects that are relevant to designers regarding legibility, typefaces, etc., but from the editorial design perspective. I’ve found the principles in that book to be applicable to today’s digital design (web, apps, etc.). I highly recommend it :-)

@William Berkson: That’s because of a strong tradition of everyone being trained in hand written brush work. I hope that doesn’t disappear because of the computer!I’m afraid to agree with you because I feel that might be selfish of me.

Never mind the computer; script reform should cause that to disappear.

The reason it isn’t impossible for the Chinese people to learn to read is because they really only need to memorize a few hundred basic components of characters, with meanings like “man” and “heart” and “speech”.

These are combined by meaning to produce a few hundred more characters, such as “bright”, formed from “sun” and “moon”.

Once those characters are learned, to someone who knows how to speak Chinese, the rest of the 8,000 or so characters a person needs to know to be literate are now comprehensible; they’re formed from a basic character component identifying the general category of the word’s meaning, and another character with the same sound.

This is a big oversimplification of the Chinese writing system, since (for example) it doesn’t deal with the fact that most Chinese words aren’t only one syllable long, even though Chinese texts written in Classical style abbreviated words by only representing their first syllable by a character.

Anyways, though, this means that learning to read Chinese is not impossible, although more difficult than learning to read an alphabetic script: but since there are alternate possible ways to write most characters - the radical (meaning) is obvious, but the phonetic often could be several different characters, of which only one is “correct” - the Chinese writing system serves to make the less-educated easy to spot.

Like English spelling, only much worse.

Quaint traditions may be charming from a distance, but one has to spare a thought for the lives of those who actually have to live with them.
