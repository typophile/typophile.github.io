2007-03-28 05:43:53 • ChuckGroth

here’s the advice i give to a lot of students: look for a salary you’re comfortable with.

you have to weigh a lot of factors. how badly do you want that “first job”? what size organization do you want to work for? how creative is the position? what are your expenses, and how much will you require to meet them? of course, you don’t want to be underpaid, but think about these factors on sliding scales. for instance, you might say, “Primo are offering me 29,000. Acme down the street are offering 31,500. but Primo is a studio i think i’d feel comfortable, and the work is more in line with what i’m interested in.” in that case, the lower salary might be better overall, in terms of your chances to grow, do some interesting portfolio building and fit in.

in the end, if you’re getting a paycheck and you don’t FEEL like you’re being taken advantage of, then you’re doing okay.

“I don’t think that designers right out of school have quite so much power as that to dictate their starting pay, but maybe i’m out of touch.”

I hear that a lot from design managers. Years ago at a AIGA student portfolio event, I offered up a question to the panel about that and was basically brushed off with the same response. I think that’s bad advice. We all didn’t spend money and time going through college JUST because we love design. We aimed to make money doing it. g My first real job interview, after the portfolio review, the owner of the company came out and said ‘so, what were you expecting salary-wise’. Being a student I really had no idea so blurted out a number. He said “OK”. I, of course, said a number that was way low and it took me a year to get the courage to ask for a raise of 5k or so. Once again they said “OK”.

So, my point is that you really do need to seriously think about salary and absolutely do NOT be afraid or intimidated of asking for more than you think you are worth. If the company has gotten to the point where they are actually willing to talk salary with you, they’re not going to just say “that’s too much, get out” when you answer. They’ll either be happy to pay you what you want or will counter offer.
