2008-03-25 11:47:49 • guifa
These tables show the commonest v’jaz ligature patterns, but they don’t show what can happen if two ligature patterns are adjacent and the bordering letters are also candidates for ligation: in that case the whole unit may be ligated. At that point, one is looking at flexible layout that isn’t really best handled with ligature glyphs per se, since it might require thousands of them. The situation is akin to that for nas’taliq Arabic shaping. One needs to analyse the joins and to break down the ligatures into components that can be contextually built up into dynamic shapes.

Here are six links to illustrations. Both manuscripts are Spanish. The language is Latin. The text is visigothic minuscule, the display script an angular majuscule rather like vyaz. These examples show: letters side by side sharing a vertical line, one letter above another, sharing a vertical line, and one letter occupying a space inside another.

(1) BL Add MS 11695 c. 1,100 A.D. at the British Library

folio 008r http://www.bl.uk/manuscripts/Viewer.aspx?ref=add_ms_11695_f008r

(bottom right) prologus beati / iheronimi in libro / apocalipsin iohan- / nis apsli The prologue of the Blessed Jerome in the book of the Apocalypse of the Apostle John.

I H E (RO) (NI) (MI)

RO: “O” is partly inside “R” NIMI: “I” is below/inside “N” and “M”

(2) folio 021v http://www.bl.uk/manuscripts/Viewer.aspx?ref=add_ms_11695_f021v

explanatio supra scriptæ ystoriæ Explanation of the written history

E X P (LA) (NA) (TI) O

LA: “A” is above/inside “L”

(3) folio 042v (bottom line) http://www.bl.uk/manuscripts/Viewer.aspx?ref=add_ms_11695_f042v incipit prologus sinagogæ Here begins the prologue of the synagogue

INCI: left vertical of “N” overlaps “I”, right vertical of “N” overlaps vertical of “C”, “I” is inside “C”
