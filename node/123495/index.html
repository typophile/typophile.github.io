Cultural Imperialism

February 26, 2015 • grubstreet

Saw this Spiekermann piece on Medium: https://medium.com/@espiekermann/cultural-imperialism-stems-from-ignorance-7bb32f1de196

Apparently Spiekermann is writing to resonate with the medium team’s piece that self-praises how great and inclusive their typography is. He writes

US clients often wonder why they need to pay for all those obscure accents. A bit of cultural imperialism in play, or plain ignorance, which is not the same, but has the same outcome.

Not wrong at all. But it got me thinking: do you really need to buy a Pro version of a typeface that supports extended Latin, when your client has a super limited budget and they’re sure they will only deal with English-speaking regions? Big companies like Google will definitely need as much language coverage as possible, but “all US clients” seems to be overstating.

I’d tell the client what both versions cost and let them decide. If they pick the non-pro version and regret it later, it was their decision.

?? This almost sounds like pricing fonts by the glyph. Does anyone do that?

For what it is worth, language support is the same in all my fonts, “pro”, free or otherwise. It’s the OT features and general complexity that increase prices. Why would I charge someone who needs some of those “obscure foreign accents” more than I’d charge anyone else? Or put another way, how can I expect to sell any fonts in Turkey or Esperantia if I ignore language support?

From the client’s perspective, it also depends on the application. In two recent projects, I commissioned signage versions of two typefaces for my employer. I didn’t specifically say to the designers, “Don’t include all those obscure foreign accents.” but I did suggest that for at least one of the typefaces that the designer could limit language support to Latin 1 - But not because I thought I’d save anyone any money. Since our signs will only ever be set in English and French, by the time the projects passed all the hurdles of getting approved, the time to actually do the work was getting really tight and it seemed like a logical place to save a little bit of time – Although how much time, is a good question. 2015-02-26 21:55:03 • russellm

I would modify the comment “It’s the OT features and general complexity that increase prices” to read “It’s the OT features and general complexity that increase costs.” It takes relatively little time to churn out a full set of accents using a modern fontmaking program. It is time consuming to design ligatures and do correct OT coding. Time is cost.

However, if I retained anything from my economics degree of many years ago, it’s the concept that the price of a font – like everything else in a free market – is determined by the intersection of supply and demand. What this ends up meaning in the real world is that the price at which a font will actually sell bears no direct relationship to cost. Some fonts make a profit and others end up being “a labor of love” which will never pay for the cost of developing them.

This applies both to bespoke fonts and retail ones. For a bespoke font any pricing scheme – no matter how peculiar it may seem to the fontmaker – works if it is what the client wants.

Lawyers can get rich from “billable hours.” Any fontmakers who could get this deal would be in the lap of luxury.
