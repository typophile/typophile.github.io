2004-09-06 02:52:32 • alexander
In Tschichold's 1967 revision of Die Neue Typographie, he appologizes for the overly fascist tone that he took when originally writing it. Germany during the 1920s was a maelstrom of political activities, ideologies, and false righteousness. Tschichold was just as adamant about the new typography as the NSDAP was about their socio-political issues. In 1933, when Tschichold himself became a victim of government imposed backlashes again the avant garde (suspected of communist sympathy), he got a taste of doctrinal rigidity first hand.

Plus, while he was working in Britain (during the war), he had the chance to work with traditional subject matters, and I think he personally fell in love with the seeped-in tradition of Monotype typography that was popular among book-printers of the day there. Maybe it just felt safe.

After the war, Max Bill (Dessau Bauhaus, then Zürich, then the HfG Ulm) accused Tschichold of apostasy for breaking with modernism, and Tschichold accused Bill of being too rigid for holding on so tightly. Paul Rand covers this nicely in one of his books.

From Norman Potter's What is a designer

"As Walter Gropius often explained, the modern movement was not some matter of dogma, fashion, or taste, but a profoundly wide-ranging attempt to encompass the nature of the twentieth-century experience and to meet its physical demands with a constructive response." (p. 36)

Speaking of 'presentism', this is an interesting way to view a movement that's been sliced, diced, regurgitated and reinvented a million times, particularly by the Swiss-Modernist fetishism that hangs around some academic institutions in the US. There are many layers to examining modernist thought and work. And understanding some of the motives can lead to solutions and approaches that aren't set in Akzidenz Grotesk and bland as hell and don't seek to piggy-back on a pale echo of the modernist style.

Tschichold was a very talented man, and he did what's being suggested in the Breaking into the industry thread: he was being bold. I know much less about Saville, but I imagine there's a theme that could be explored along those lines.
