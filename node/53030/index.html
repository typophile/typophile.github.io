How to handle a Typedesigner ?

December 30, 2008 • Lukes

Situation: In summer 2007 I contacted a wellknown typedesigner to finish a font (a sans serif - medium weigth) that I had started designing in fontlab - the task was to finished the fontset (missing characters, kerning etc) and expand it to a light and bold version + italics (slanted)- at the time of agrement the guy was broke so I paid upfront so he didn’t had to worry about the rent. We agreed on a deadline october 2008 - final work.

In the months that followed I asked for material - to see how things was going but was told “im doing this for another client - very busy - bla bla” and time went - no font. Come summer 2008 I contacted the typedesigner and asked if we could make it for october deadline and suddenly I had to do some stuff myself- He said as he was not the designer and therefor would not design the missing charcters (we are talking east europe characters)?! - so I did the characters that I was asked to do in a few days (not to delay the process) - now december and I have just recieved a medium version that i will consider 90% done designwise (missing characters, optical adjustments)but no kerning and other technical stuff plus some of the things I requested had been totally ignored.

So we went from having a good relationship to really none - and I dont' know what I am getting or when (if ever) - Or what I did since the guy didn’t produce as agreed.

What I need is a good advice how to get the job done - A solution that i thought of is to get him to finish off the medium version and get somebody else to do the rest (the light and the bold) !? But with my luck I have no idea who to contact, so I can get the font done in the other weigths.

Basically I need somebody to proof check the work and somebody (maybe same person) to produce the other weigths.

The price he set and that we agreed on was 9000 €

Please note that the payment and price has been according to his charge - and I even gave an extra 1000 € this summer to speed up the process.

I have talked with a laywer - but they really don’t understand the situation and a laywer in my opinion is something that will only cost money not produce a font.

Any suggestions is highly appriciated - and please don’t ask for a name - I don’t want to badmouth anybody.

regards Lukes

Well, for starters, this can be a cautionary tale for others: it’s a bad idea to pay completely up front for work like this. Instead, it can be done based on agreed-upon milestones in the development. Plus, if you’re going to be giving somebody nine thousand euro (or ten thousand in the end), it is actually worth putting a contract in place. Not that this all helps you any now, of course, for which I’m sorry.

First, did you have communication by email or in writing such that you have a written record of what was agreed to?

Second, decide on what if anything you want from the existing type designer. Do you want him to do any more work? Do you want some or most of your money back? If there’s stuff you want, you could ask him to agree to arbitration (another element that one could have put in a contract, if you had one), and get somebody knowledgeable and respected in the community, who you both agree upon, to be the arbitrator. The alternative would be to pursue him legally, but you seem uninterested in that, and as you say, that is a good way to make sure that lawyers make money rather than either you or the type designer.

I expect that many of the folks here would be interested in doing the finishing work for you. as well.

Both of these roles sound interesting to me, actually, though it would probably be a conflict of interest to have one person do both.

Good luck with all this.

The price you agreed to was fair, if a bit low for the work.

The length of time for this project (more than a year) seems really suspicious to me. Generally it should take a professional no more than two weeks to create a new weight from an existing design. Two weeks for the light and two weeks for the heavy. Adding the additional Central European characters should be a fairly simple task. The kerning for the three fonts can be done in a week, and the slanted italics can be done in no time (if you just want digital obliques).

This is really routine work. It should have been completed in five or six weeks at the most. We have been doing this sort of thing for designers and art directors since 1990.
