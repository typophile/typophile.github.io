There needs to be this clause put in for GPL:

“As a special exception, if you create a document which uses this font, and embed this font or unaltered portions of this font into the document, this font does not by itself cause the resulting document to be covered by the GNU General Public License. This exception does not however invalidate any other reasons why the document might be covered by the GNU General Public License. If you modify this font, you may extend this exception to your version of the font, but you are not obligated to do so. If you do not wish to do so, delete this exception statement from your version.”

Please state other differences between GPL and OFL.

The OFL has other clauses having to do specifically with typefaces. There is a clause that allows redistribution freely, sale of the typeface bundled with other products, but not sale of the typeface by itself. There is also a clause that requires changing the name of any modified versions of the typeface, meant to protect the good name of the original typeface if someone makes poor modifications, I would guess. That’s my memory of the differences, aided by a quick scan of the license.

The two (the OFL and the GPL+font exception) are quite different, although both are valid free/libre and open source software licensing models for fonts. Although the GPL+font exception model is legally valid, we feel that it does not fit fonts well, and actually discourages the release of free/libre fonts, so we created the OFL. The philosophical differences are that the OFL does a better job of protecting designers in an understandable way and places less restrictions on the use of derivative works. That’s a gross oversimplification, but gives you the general idea.

For a lot more detail, read the [[http://scripts.sil.org/OFL_web|OFL itself]] carefully (it’s a lot less wordy than the GPL), and consult the newly-updated [[http://scripts.sil.org/OFL-FAQ_web|OFL-FAQ]].
