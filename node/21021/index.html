Floursihes–Criticism?

June 29, 2006 • design-ed

Just created this logotype for a client and It would be cool to get some advice/criticism on the flourishes that I've made on the Uppecase A and C. They were based on the flourishes in the typeface 'Odile' by village, but fused with the Caslon (two twenty four) style. I've never attempted a heavy-edit like this so would be good to know if there's anything wrong with it! See what you think, Ed
Hi there Ed, I really like the serif part of the logo, I know you asked if we could comment on the flourishes but I just wanted to say a quick thing about the Associates part of it. Using Helvetica thin makes the word almost dissappear, it feels very dominated by the seriffed word. It might be an idea to go up a weight. Just a visual thought thats all. As for the flourishes, I think they are working really well, I have just designed a logo for a swiss bank based around a 16th century script font and the one thing i couldnt put my finger on at the time was that I have not made the flourishes big enough. I realised that I should have been a lot bolder with them and really excentuate them. I think the C is working really nicely, but I think the A could do with a little more. Its the start of the logo and might be a nice way to lead in.

2006-06-30 14:18:19 • Matt Squire
I think you can afford to be more extravagant with the flourish on the C and extend it to the right past the serif to balance the letter. I looked at the decorative initials of Odile, I find them a bit cramped which is understandable since it might have to function with lines of copy above and below, for a logotype with an exclusion area you don't have the same problem. Extending the C's flourish will also help it to balance with the cap height of Associates. I would approach the A from a different direction either by extending the lefthand vertical downward and inserting a scroll (you could try curving it up to form the crossbar). The other approach I would look at is a horizontal flourish from the top going left and curving down. An alternative might be to really go over the top on the C, leaving the A as standard, if you make the focus of the flourish lead toward the centre of the logo it could balance nicely. Matt's idea for using a flourish from another font is one way of working alternatively if you type out the logo with no flourish and print out several copies and work over the top with a pencil, scan it and draw it. I would also increase the rn kerning, stop them crashing. Tim
