Brian,

Text, type, and information excess are all intimately related. Insofar as you would like to understand the impact of this excess on contemporary culture here are some resources that may help you understand more. You will find that they are of varying relevance and usefulness to your interests and efforts. Some speak about information glut, some about handling it, some are natural outgrowths of the fact that there is information excess.

(Please indulge me I work in a library so making bibliographies, related reading lists etc. is something that I seem to be predisposed to doing…)

Books The Gutenberg Galaxy, Marshall McLuhan The Rise of the Image the Fall of the Word, Mitchell Stephens Interface Culture, Steven Johnson Information Anxiety 2, Richard Saul Wurman Information Architects, Richard Saul Wurman Information Graphics, Peter Wildbur & Michael Burke Mastering the Information Age, Michael J. McCarthy Data Smog, David Shenk Visual Display of Quantitative Information (second edition)*, Edward Tufte Envisioning Information, Edward Tufte Visual Explanations, Edward Tufte The Cognitive Style of PowerPoint, Edward Tufte A Short History of the Printed Word, Warren Chappell & Robert Bringhurst

*First edition is still highly useful

Websites/Links http://www.visualcomplexity.com/vc/ (great visual overviews) http://www.typophile.com/node/18165 http://www.informationdesign.org/ (awesome links and articles designers and issues) http://depts.washington.edu/vcd/designs/02/sources_reading.shtml (a good reading list)

Okay I’ll stop now. It was said that John Milton was the last scholar to have read every book in the library. So information overload isn’t a new problem. The big picture of culture is not synthesized through dealing with original works, but through critical reviews. The Edinburgh Review, founded in 1802 (?) was a key publication in providing the big picture, although it did not reach the masses. I guess the salient point would be, what percentage of the literate public did it reach? In the era of mass literacy, look to publications like the Ladies Home Journal and the Saturday Evening Post in the US c.1900, and the role they played of making available democratically useful information in the “Progressive Era”. I don’t know what the comparable publications would be in NZ.
