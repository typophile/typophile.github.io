2006-06-22 15:45:12 • dezcom

Just signed up. Firefox on PC worked for me.

If you are wavering on whether to stay Sunday evening to see the Museum of Printing, I highly recommend it. I had the pleasure of visiting it in April. It has two collections that are fascinating if you are interested in type.

First, it has nearly all the kinds of presses and typesetting machines from 1850 to 1980–the era starting when power-driven presses started and ending with the eclipse of ‘relief’ printing and the takeover of offset printing.

What I had no idea of is that the ‘revolutions’ in printing technology over the past 40 years in fact go back another hundred. There is significant turn over in the technology almost every twenty years for the full time. And Gardner LePoer, the director of the museum tells a fascinating story of what drove this change. How power printing drove the search for machine composition, and how offset printing drove new changes in composition and in type production. And you can see it all before your eyes.

The unique treasure of the Printing Museum is the complete collection of Merganthaler Linotype drawings, over a quarter million production drawings, including Dwiggins' typefaces. Larry Oppenberg, who worked for Linotype in the photo type days, and knows the collection well, showed me some of the treasures.

It was thrilling to see these drawings, on which so much care has been lavished, and which have been reproduced in type millions of times all over the world.

On important thing also became clear. Although English Monotype, especially under Stanley Morrison, did the most talking about type, Linotype actually did most of the composition and printing–perhaps 70% until the advent of photo type. Also they had an amazing tradition of solid craftsmanship, evident in the drawings. Further, leading designers such as Matthew Carter, David Berlow and many others had worked in the Merganthaler Linotype drawing office, so its tradition lives on in their work, and those they influence.

Anyway it promises to be a rewarding visit!

The Boston Public Library William Addison Dwiggins Collection (Rare Books & Manuscripts, x4432)

Dwiggins (1901-1986) was a type designer, calligrapher, typographer, illustrator, marionette designer and performer, and writer from Hingham Massachusetts. The collection includes holograph manuscripts, type designs, correspondence, photographs, paintings, woodcuts, stencil prints, woodcarvings, architectural sketches and diagrams, and layouts and correspondence relating to his book and type designs as well as marionettes, a marionette theater and related material.

2006-07-20 14:48:49 • Si_Daniels

Through the generosity and kindness of others, I’m suddenly going to be heading to Boston for the typofestivities this year (woo hoo!). Hearing this, my friend who lived in Boston for a while sent me the following ‘Selected Highlights of the Area’ list. I hope it’s helpful:

For lunch, you must go to Chacarero. It’s right in Downtown Crossing. It’s very close to TypeCon: LINK

I would bet a LARGE sum of money that you will love it.

For dinner, you’ll be near Chinatown. Check out Suishaya if you like sushi. Actually, check it out even if you don’t; they’ve got good Korean food.

There’s a tiny hole in wall, with space for perhaps 15 people, at 74 Kneeland St in Chinatown; it also is very close to where you’ll be: LINK

Order the scallion pancakes; they’re a lot like so: http://www.cscs.umich.edu/~crshalizi/weblog/252.html

Also order the Shanghai noodles. Pancake + noodles will cost you something like $6.

There is a dearth of good coffee around there, though you should take a bit of a walk to here: LINK

They make a damned good cappuccino, and they’re probably the best bookstore in Boston. Not the best bookstore in greater Boston, mind you; to me, that honor belongs to the Harvard Book Store in Harvard Square, which is a 15-minute T ride from the Park Street T stop, which is the one nearest to TypeCon: LINK

The best pizza in Boston is probably The Upper Crust: http://www.theuppercrustpizzeria.com/

For great and cheap burritos, try Anna’s Taqueria. They were a staple for me when I was poor, and I would still chow down on them now if I lived there when not poor.

My favorite Irish pub in Boston is the Kinsale, in Government Center: LINK

They pour a damned fine Guinness.

If you go farther afield, you could check out The People’s Republik in Central Square in Cambridge (so called because Cambridge is known as “The People’s Republic of Cambridge), or The Field just up the street. Central Square is on the red line, one stop south of Harvard Square, so it’s maybe a 10-minute T ride from the Park Street T.

Now that I have money to buy expensive cheese, wine, bread, olives and so forth, I wish I lived near Formaggio: LINK

I’ve not actually been to that Formaggio; I’ve only been to the one near Harvard Square, which is actually quite a hike even from the T. But I assume the South End one (that I just linked to) is equally great.

The Boston Public Library is a work of art on its own, and I’m pretty sure it has a bunch of Sargent paintings. Architecturally it’s breathtaking. LINK

The Isabella Stewart Gardner Museum is filled with Sargent paintings: http://www.gardnermuseum.org/

It’s on the green line, which your T stop serves. To get T directions, go to http://trip.mbta.com/cgi-bin/itin_page_dhtml.pl

and get directions from 1 Avenue de Lafayette in Boston to 280 Fenway in Boston. It’s a 15-minute ride, with 5 or so minutes of walking on either end.

The whole neighborhood of Coolidge Corner – on the green line – is cool and fun. They have a great indie movie theatre, restored to its 1920’s grandeur: http://www.coolidge.org/

Boston is a film town. Boston is also an ice-cream town, and you can’t leave there without going to Toscanini’s: http://www.tosci.com/

Apparently the New York Times said they have the “best ice cream in the world.” I can’t testify to that, though I can tell you that it’s the best ice cream I’ve ever had. And mind-bendingly great milkshakes. I might recommend a Belgian-chocolate milkshake with a shot of espresso in it.

That should do for now.

I’m a big John Singer Sargent fan, hence the Sargent stuff. I’m sure he’d be happy to give more recommendations, so feel free to send me an IM and I’ll pass it along.
