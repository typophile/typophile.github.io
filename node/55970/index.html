Hi Stewf,

no, not really. I remember that somebody here on Typophile once argued in favour of ‘spurless’ instead of ‘stemless’, since it’s not the stem that is missing. Looking at bdpq in [[Gill Sans]] (or FF Signa, among others from your list), that makes sense to me: there is a full stem – it just doesn’t stick out.

In a narrow sense, ‘stemless’ sounds appropriate only with the uppercase ‘U’ – as distinguished from the ‘stemmed U’. [[Spur]] is traditionally (exclusively?) used with the ‘G’. However, Martin Pecina & David Březina also use it plausibly for details on other letters (b, e, q).

Both terms are used on Typophile, here’s a quick survey: ‘spurless’: Hrant, Erik S., Stewf ‘stemless’: Mike Y., Marc O., William B., Kris S., Stewf Maverick points for George T. and his proposal of ‘un-stemmed’.

Nick S. uses ‘spurred’ for the projecting terminals of [[Cheltenham]]’s ‘A’ and ‘G’ – but I haven’t found evidence for the usage of ‘unspurred’. Then again, that term would offer the advantage of being able to differentiate between ‘unspurred’ letters and ‘despurred’ ones, i.e. those that look like they used to have a spur once, before it got amputated (FF Sari?). Despurring? Don’t let [[Steve Jackaman]] hear about that!

You now may make up your mind. :D F

(It’s hard to tell from that pixelated sample, but I’d say that ‘wagewatch’ actually does have tiny spurs on its ‘a’ and ‘g’.)
