2005-11-07 10:53:48 • Forrest L Norvell
There is another piece in the Telegraph, with a different photograph of the inscription that is easier to read. I've tried to transcribe, but I don't recognise most of the words and some may be abbreviated. The first letter of the fifth line is confusing. I'm guessing it is sigma, simply because I can't imagine what else it would be (san = as), but everywhere else the lunate form has been used. It is difficult to be sure whether the first word in the second line ends with a double tau or a really wide pi. I'm guessing the latter, because double tau seems unlikely at the end of a word. ΠΡΟΣΗΝΙΚΕΝ ΑΚΕΠ ΤΟΥΣ ΗΦΙΛΟΘΕΟΣ ΤΗΝ ΤΡΑΠΕ ΣΑΝ ΘΩ ΙΥΧΩ ΜΝΗΜΟΣΥΝΟΝ I believe 'ΤΟΥΣ ΗΦΙΛΟΘΕΟΣ' refers to 'their love of God', unless Ηφιλοθεοσ it is a proper noun, e.g. the name of a deceased person. The last word is 'memorial'.

2005-11-07 22:00:40 • dezcom
The fifth letter of the third line must be a zeta -- the accusative form ΤΡΑΠΕΖΑΝ wrapped around the lines. I think ΑΚΕΠΤΟΥΣ must be a single word/name, the subject of the sentence, who is the (feminine) φιλοθεος in question. I can't explain why the NYT gives a plural subject, since the nominative nouns (assuming ΑΚΕΠΤΟΥΣ is a name) and the verb (which I can't make out, but is evidently a 3rd person singular form) are both singular. The verb looks like a relative of προσφέρω, in this context ”to make an offering.” Contra the Telegraph, the mosaic reads (unusually!) “[to] God Jesus Christ,” with the words abbreviated as nomina sacra.

I've been checking around to see what my colleagues make of the verb, and so far as I've been able to tell the consensus is that it must be an odd form of προσφέρω. Brandon Mason conjectures that the mosaic was broken and misrepaired; he also calls attention to the ΗΝ ligature in the first line. Phil Harland provides wonderful amplification on the matter of nomina sacra in the penultimate line. And National Geographic provides a photo of the mosaic in situ.
