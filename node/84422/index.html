What version of Garamond should I use?

August 9, 2011 • Eric Kelsey

There are many different versions of Garamound and I’m not sure which one to use. Any thoughts?

Consider the medium, and the client. Choose a version which you have seen in a particular medium, and liked (performance varies by medium), and one which has a suitable tone for your project—there are some which are bookish, others which are more corporate, still others which are more suitable for retail.

Bear in mind that not all Garamonds have Garamond in the name, e.g. Sabon, Verdigris.

For which purpose and which printing condition?

Garamonde Premiere Pro from Adobe is the most extensive Garamond interpretation and close to the original. Its outlines have subtitles that are best fit for good printing conditions. For general office use Adobe Garamond Pro might be the better choice, if you don’t need opticals (versions for small text and big headlines) or italic small caps.

However, if you print in a language with diacritical marks, then you might check if those characters collide with a previous lowercase /f/ of the two Adobe Garamonds and use a different version of Garamond. In that case I would advise you to resort to Sabon or to Stempel Garamond. A free version of Stempel Garamond, which has some flaws however, can be found on [[http://www.garamond.org|garamond.org]], which is an updated version of the original – and older version – in the [[http://www.artifex.com/downloads/|URW font package: Garamond Nr.8]]

Monotype Garamond and the pre-packed Garamond on Windows and Mac are actually “mistitled”, they are no Garamond, they are designed by Jannon, a historical confusion.

2011-08-09 23:04:49 • Eric Kelsey

If the company’s brand uses “Garamond” without being more specific, and you have all those variants at your disposal, this can only mean one thing — playtime!

When setting a headline or text, pit several variants against one another, tweaking each according to its needs to try and make it the best looking. For heads, play with kerning and tracking, for text, h&j’s.

You might even consider mixing them up in the same document.
