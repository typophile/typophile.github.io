By ">1 italic angle" do you mean (1) there is a font with an italic angle, and it is greater than one, or (2) there are multiple italic fonts, with different angles? Which ligatures do you consider basic, that "extra ligatures" are an addition to? By "hinting" do you mean that the alternative is an unhinted font? What the heck are "script-script numbers/letters"? That aside, here is a first take at some answers, from my personal POV as an end user of type, rather than as a type designer (nor in terms of Adobe's priorities): Features should be present in all weight and inclines unless they really make no sense in that font. Must have: - italic - bold - hinting Unlikely to buy without: - small caps Really nice, useful: - >2 weights - extra ligatures - super/subscript numbers - super/subscript letters Nice but won't use much: - upright italic - arrows - dingbats - math symbols - other symbols - multiple scripts Not much use to me personally: - language-specific glyphs Just plain silly: - reverse italic - double struck

2008-12-10 02:59:13 • Thomas Phinney
Oddly enough, the most important feature for me is that it be relatively easy to obtain permission to modify the font. Modifications I'll make would be (1) to add characters, esp. easy-to-create ones such as glyphs with diacriticals, extra ligatures, true-cut superiors, etc., and (2) to add or modify features, usually just to include the extra characters just made up -- such as "extra" (but functional) ligatures (fb, fk, fh, f-with-accented vowels, etc.), angle brackets to swap out the greater & less symbol when the text includes < http >, and on & on. I don't think it is possible for the font designer to include everything that will come up, and a text interior designer often doesn't take into account what a font has before specifying it. The comp gets stuck with setting the text, and it is just easier to make the changes in the font than to make them in the file. Most text designers I know (which includes me) don't purchase a font because of it's features, we select it because we like it, or sometimes just because it fills a need. We might, with reluctance, rule it out because it lacks needed features or characters. Big things that might rule out a purchase (rather than a use for a particular title), include the lack of an italic, roman small caps, and both old-style and lining numbers. Most of the many other things needed can be filled out by someone who has only workman-like skill.

I do often choose to add fonts to our type library based on the features they may include. That’s because I’m lazier than Charles and have never learned any of the font editing tools. So since modification is not an option for me, I must find types that have what I want. I have rejected some very nice faces because the font set lacked certain features. So my basic features list will include roman and italic small caps; true-drawn superior figures; a full range of f ligs (given all the work we do about Norway, I really need that fj lig for fjords); and good set of made fractions, along with all the elements to create piece fractions that match the made ones. I don’t want a bold, but I do want a medium, or a semibold or demibold, depending on what it is called and its color. I’d like the features listed above to be present in the medium also. I’m drawn toward the idea of a matching sans, but it is not of utter importance. I’ve never been interested in this critter known as the “caption” variant. I seem to do OK with italic or sans for captions. Old-school, I guess. True-drawn superior letters will be nice also. Given the nature of the work I have done, do now, and will likely do in the future, that about fills my needs. 2008-12-10 14:44:13 • will powers
Here are my thoughts, based on the sort of books I'm used to working on. Essential (I won't ordinarily pick a text face without them): - italic - small caps - basic math symbols (the ones in MacRoman at minimum) - semibold, or a bold that isn't too heavy Very important (a text face with them is much more likely to be considered): - multiple scripts (Polytonic Greek, Cyrillic): I regularly need polytonic Greek for the books I set; Cyrillic I don't really use as much as Hebrew or Arabic, but Greek and Cyrillic are different in that their designs typically have enough relation to the Latin that the visual dissonance when they aren't quite coordinated is unpleasant - hinting - super-/subscript numerals - language-specific glyphs: either lots of them or, as Charles suggests, easy permission to modify the font - multiple cuts (e.g. caption/text/display): most of the things I set have footnotes, and a page really does look better if the chapter head, the text, and the footnotes are not all generated from the same outlines - extra ligatures: it depends on the meaning of "extra"; I'm assuming you mean anything beyond f_i and f_l Nice and useful (will be considered but probably not the deciding factor): - super-/subscript letters - >2 weights - arrows - additional math symbols and other symbols The rest may be nice but not terribly useful to me; the only ones I would pick out as being really useless for me are reverse italic and upright italic. The multiple cuts need to be available for all the scripts; I've had Greek show up everywhere from footnotes to chapter heads. I can live without a regular-italic distinction for Greek, if the regular is fairly calligraphic.

Craig -- There are two different variants that "caption" might conjure in this context. The earlier one is the sort of alternative italic, like the one that accompanies the digital version of Fairfield (not part of the original design, BTW). This approach and nomenclature is patterned after what Matthew Carter did for some custom work for National Geographic, where he created a more open, simplified italic for their use in captions, and thus called "Caption." (This is many years back; not currently employed.) The other variant is the small optical size that Adobe calls "caption" in its typical suite of optical variations. This is what you're referring to, I presume. I don't know which Will is referring to. -- K.
