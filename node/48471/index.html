Stone Sans, Serif, and Informal

August 16, 2008 • Dan Gayle

I have to read a lot of information set in Stone Sans, Serif, and Informal. While I can handle Sans and Serif, the Informal cut hurts my eyes. (Especially when set as body text.)

What I need to figure out is, Why? What is wrong with Stone Informal as compared to the other more elegant and usable Stone family variants?

How I see it being used is for children’s material in a magazine. The “adult” material uses Serif for the majority of the body text and Sans for smaller articles and sidebars.

But the kids articles get Informal everything, and it hurts. I can’t quite figure it out, but it hurts.

Any thoughts?

I can’t find my copy (it’s possible it didn’t make it through my latest Book Purge), but if you feel like spending $2.60 (used) on Amazon you can pick up On Stone, which I’m sure covers his rationale.

There’s also a funny sample of the lowercase “r” from “Stone Extra Informal” that has a puppet face for an ear in the July 1988 issue of Fine Print, reprinted in Fine Print on Type, $9 used on Amazon. In that article Stone writes:

“I noticed I was frequently receiving letters and memos printed out in Times Roman on the laser printer. I had the feeling that these messages were probably not for me, or if they were, that they had come from the IRS. I realized that as laser printing technology became more commonplace, there would be an increasing need for typefaces suited to personal communications generated by a computer.

“There was no historical model for these Informal designs, but I had several basic ideas to start with. I thought first that they should be related in some way to handwriting and that they should have a more dynamic, rhythmical character than formal text typefaces. The branching used in the lower case letters abdgpqhmn is one such cursive characteristic.”
