De Groot works quite nicely, I think (but fails when the thin weight is very thin*).

Suppose one grade has a horizontal thickness of 20 units and the other grade has a horizontal thickness of 40 units, then your interpolation must have thickness √(20 x 40) = 28.284… (significantly less than 30 which is the mean).

That means if you are interpolating between 0 and 1000 your instance is at (28.284 - 20)/(40 - 20) = 414

This will start to get more complicated if you have an even number of grades (e.g. 4) and just thinking about how to calculate that makes my head hurt.

Most will tell you to trust your eye, the final arbiter on the matter.

  • Suppose your stem at the thin extreme is 1 unit thin (unlikely and not very practical I admit) and that your thick end is 100 units thick. De Groot will say that the optical halfway is √(1 x 100) = 10 which is still very thin so we may be looking for new maths in this instance.
2011-06-16 15:19:18 • Nick Job

De Groot’s formula is not quadratic and there seems to be no choice. Here is some Python code that returns the list of the n values to put in between the endpoints a and b; the list also returns the endpoints; from math import * def inter(a,b,n):   d=(log(b) - log(a))/(n+1)   l=[]   for i in range(n+2):     l=l+[exp(log(a)+i*d)]   return l

and here is some trace: >>> inter(2,6,1) [2.0, 3.4641016151377539, 6.0] >>> inter(2,6,2) [2.0, 2.8844991406148166, 4.1601676461038073, 6.0]

i.e. to interpolate one value between 2 and 6 you need to choose 3.46, and if you want two values, they are 2.88 and 4.16.

For opticals of small point sizes, Knuth seems to be using a completely different formula (with, this time, some parameters to choose).

2011-06-18 05:52:41 • dezcom

De Groot’s method is just a guideline. Just something to keep in the back of your mind and consider as an option.

Here’s a concrete example of where De Groot’s method will give you the wrong design answer.

Suppose you have a text face in a regular weight and a bold. You want to create a semibold. Some designers would just interpolate 50% and be done with it. Other designers would say “aha, I’ll use De Groot’s method” and calculate an interpolation less than 50%.

I propose that both these answers are incorrect. In actual use, both a semibold and a bold are meant to contrast with the regular. A bold is more useful if you need to emphasize an occasional word or phrase; a semibold is more useful if you’ll have to emphasize whole sentences.

But the semibold & bold don’t need to contrast with each other. For instance, would you have semibold running text with bold for emphasis? No, never.

So the ideal interpolation for a semibold is something more than 50% — that way, you can ensure that both semibold and bold are distinct from the regular weight. A semibold that’s closer to the regular than the bold — which is what you’ll get with the De Groot method — is not useful.

Which is a long way of saying (again) that the De Groot method — or any algorithmic method — can be useful as a guideline. But it’s not a substitute for conscious thought about how people will be using your fonts (a process also known as “design”)

Disregard my prior post, I didn’t have the information when I wrote it, this should make a more sense:

Interpolation Theory.

This has a reasoning in human perception vs the answer if a true engineering result were given. In choosing an intermediate weight of a typeface, according to de Groot it is not the mean (arithmetic mean) between the two weights but is instead a value slightly less than half way. Numerically, this can shown that if a typeface has a value of 40 and the next typeface has a value of 70, an intermediate type face, half way between the two, would have a value of 55 if a strict engineering result would be used – this would be in error. Human perception does not follow the laws of math and the science of engineering, perception is altered slightly. The true intermediate typeface would therefore, be slightly less than 55, say maybe 50 in value. The calculated value of the typeface then lies along a log curve between the starting and ending values, with “Extra Light” and “Black” being closer to the arithmetic mean than “Normal” which inflects the most from the arithmetic mean. The calculated typeface value then lies along this curve (a log curve vs a straight line) with leeway being given to the thickness of a front. Also of note, the vertical axis and horizontal axis of a font comes into play. A horizontal axis is more confined to expansion than is the vertical axis which can expand a lot more. In the end though, it is the type designer who’s good judgment should determine the look of the font.

Where else do we see this “optical phenomena”? We see it in the columns of the Parthenon in Greece. The columns are not dead straight (as an engineer would make them) but are slightly thicker in the center. The reason is, that at a distance human perception would view a straight column slightly thinner in the center but a column with a slightly fatter center is viewed as straight. This is the difference between the human arrived result and the mathematically engineered arrived at result.

http://www.math.nus.edu.sg/aslaksen/projects/perspective/parthenon.htm

His “Interpolation Theory” explanation is best explained here:

It means that according to Lu(cas) de Groot when designing a font, that all fonts weights should stray away from the arithmetic mean the closer you get to “Normal/Semi Bold” when you choose “Extra Light” and “Black” as starting points, this is the inverse of the optical illusion seen in the Parthenon columns!!!!

2011-11-09 18:50:05 • John Hudson

" The fact that it is possible to consider designs intended for e.g. subhead use that are not also size-specific designs just indicates that it is a bad idea to conflate the two things in naming."

Perhaps so. Either name is still much better than “optical” yet neither may be the better label to put on type that is specific to a functional set of variables not aptly captured in the current one-outline-fits-all digital type genre. I would like a term that stands for “multi-outline” that would combine both the usage-specific and the size-specific outlines rather than expect the hair-splitting nomenclature discussions to be replaced by an even more confusing concept for users to unravel. Users are often confused about when to use one or another “optical” size. Perhaps it would be best to use an unknown variable glyph as we do in mathematics (x, y, z). Even better, we could use a common Greek letter like π followed by either a sweet-spot size like “π-12” or usage designation like “π/subhead, or π/sh”. The π glyph would become a sign for the whole usage/size amalgam and would be very short to add to a font name. Bodoni Bold π/sh is using less space than Bodoni Bold Subhead 12. The problem I have with using numerical size in the name is that, as you say, users may choose a size differing from what the type designer chooses [even sometimes rightfully]. The marketing folks might even buy in on a name less unerwhelming than “optical” ;-)
