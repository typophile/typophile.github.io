Reading distance

April 13, 2010 • Nick Shinn

[img:sites/default/files/old-images/Uhland_6700.jpg] I recently came across this delightful 1817 silhouette of Ludwig Uhland by Luise Duttenhofer. At the time, Dr. Uhland was 30. He was a well-known Romantic poet, and there are many portraits of him on the Internet, several in old age; in none of them is he wearing eyeglasses. Was this the style for immersing oneself in romantic poetry? [img:sites/default/files/old-images/Friedrich_4690.jpg] In this painting by Friedrich (detail) from the same era, the lady is also holding her book quite close. This is anecdotal, so I wonder if any studies have been done on what normal reading distance is for various kinds of documents. And what does type size have to do with this? And offset vs. letterpress?
I'm guessing it may have been artistic license to make the subject appear more absorbed in the book. I'm sure studies have been done on reading distance as that info is important when making reading glasses and bifocals. Recently I was talking to my optometrist about my bifocals and he mentioned "arm's length or slightly less" as a typical reading distance, although when I was younger I typically held books a little closer than that because I was nearsighted.

Nice collection of images, Craig. Perhaps it was just a question of vision impairment? That would only apply to the 20% of the population who are sufficiently impacted by short sightedness and stigmatism to warrant reading closely, and many of those would use glasses. The lenses to fix simple short/long sightedness are quite simple, e.g. they can be bought off the rack at chemists/drug stores today. So AFAIK they were readily available in the 19th century and earlier. It would seem that reading distance is multivalent, related to individual physiology, reading position (chair/desk/standing), document scale (page/type size), text genre (difficulty of language), document genre (balance of text/image) and lighting conditions. After a quick search: Old, but of potential interest. Menozzi, M., Rehor, M. & Studeli, T. (2003). Font size and reading comfort (Proceedings paper, Annual Spring Conference of the GfA/17th Annual Conference of the International Society for Occupational Ergonomics and Safety) Munich, Germany. 135–138. Abstract An experiment was run, in which 23 subjects rated six text of different font size. Ratings were carried out using a presentation on paper as well as a presentation on a cathode ray tube display at a viewing distance of 50 cm. Most preferred font size was 14 pt in the paper presentation and 13 pt in the presentation on display. Preferred size convert to about a letter size of 23.9' and 22.6'.
