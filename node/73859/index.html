September 5, 2010 • Michael_Rowley

I am an enthusiatic user of optical fonts, i.e. font families intended for use at different ranges of size, e.g. text, caption (smaller), subheading (larger), and display (largest). But it seems to me that there are comparatively few of these; is my impression false? And it it isn’t, why should it be? Their use seems to me obvious.
There are very few optical fonts, mostly because few designers know or care what they are.

Part of the problem is that the different optical sizes of a typeface have to be sold as separate fonts.

One could perhaps use the programmability features of TrueType and other modern digital font standards, normally just used for hinting, to make a typeface wider and thicker as the size is reduced - but then there would be no way to turn that feature off, since word processing programs do not, in general - and by “in general”, I mean all the way down to WordPad - allow one to specify an “effective” point size in addition to the point size used for spacing.

Old type catalogues, or data from Monotype on the set widths of different type sizes, allow one to research what was considered a typical increase in width as a typeface was reduced in size, and looking at specimens under suitable magnification would help answer the question of thickness.
