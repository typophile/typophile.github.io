2008-01-09 12:42:27 • James Arboghast

While I completely agree with the principle of not supporting ‘derivative fonts’ (a.k.a.clones), it seems there is a huge gray area here when it comes to revivals/digitizations of old typefaces. Many, if not most, of us do not have the experience, or type libraries, to know when something we see being used is original, let alone whether it is a revival, or a derivative. I suppose if we knew when they were originally designed we could guess, but in most cases that would be it – just a guess. For example, the many fonts created by David Rakowski, Dieter Steffmann, or Dave Nalle of Scriptorium, that are taken from old specimen books. Often these guys changed the names, but as far as I know their digitizations were original. Even ‘grayer’ are the cases with the Brendel, Opti and FontBank collections, even though they are often the only sources we know of for digital versions of certain type families, or certain weights of familiar families (by other commercial names). More than once legitimate foundries have issued ‘new’ fonts, based on old work that had already been digitized by ‘amateurs’. That shouldn’t make the amateur version ‘derivative’. We all know many examples of legitimate foundries with virtually identical copies of popular styles, especially from the pre-digital era. Heck, it was standard practice for metal type foundries to offer their own versions of popular typefaces, just changing the name, and maybe one or two minor design details.

As IDers, often the best we can do is name whatever we know that looks like the sample that is posted for ID, regardless of its ‘pedigree’. The fact is that the people who created many of those samples used the fonts without knowing (or caring) where they came from originally. I suspect almost all of us have bought ‘bargain’ font collections more than once, or got them bundled with other software, like Corel products. Not every sample we have to ID comes from a squeaky clean design agency that has nothing but licensed fonts in their font libraries.

I guess the bottom line of my almost-rant is that we just do our best. If the font we know is ‘suspect’ for some reason, then the moderators, or the folks who know more type history, can set us straight, as Tiffany did this time. I didn’t intend this post as a criticism of Tiffany, because I think she did just right, but I wanted to make sure that IDers don’t feel they need to apologize if they don’t know the ‘real’ font name.
