Justification in old books

May 18, 2006 • redge

I have in front of me a book called The Art of Cookery Made Plain and Easy, published in 1805 by Cottom and Stewart, Alexandria, Virginia. In its day, it was a popular book.

The paragraphs are justified. While not annoying, there is quite noticeable variation in spacing between words and spacing in relation to punctuation.

It seems clear that the text would flow better had the paragraphs been set ragged right.

Does anyone know whether obvious variation in spacing is common to “everyday” books of the 18th/19th century?

If so, why were such books set justified rather than ragged?

Thanks.

I haven’t seen the book in question but if there’s a noticeable variation in word spacing causing rivers and white blobs, then it wasn’t set very well. Justified setting, if done well, should give the page a really nice “constructed” feel with even colour and I personally think it reads very well as there’s no jarring rags to disrupt your reading patterns. I think FL/RR sometimes has an unpleasant texture on the page but this depends largely on the typeface.

Also, most type set in books in the past five centuries have been justified text, even the scribes justified their handwriting in the past.

Others with experience in designing books should be able to answer your other questions!

The best of printing throughout the centuries had been painstakingly crafted by guild printer typographers whether justified, centered or flush left rag right.

Obviously, that’s not to say ALL printed matter received the same attention, nor was it warranted.

Here are two broadside samples from the National Library of Scotland (a great web site for examining period style of the time and in the area).

Here’s a background on the Scotish broadside archives: For almost 300 years until the mid-19th century, broadsides filled the place occupied today by the tabloid press.

Originally they were single sheets of paper, printed on one side only, designed to be read unfolded and posted up in public places.

Notice that spacing between sentences were generous, a stylistic tendency only, and not poor typography for the standards of that time.

Style varies over time. There are a number of things in the Homer page that would be done differently today. Such as no space before colon and semi-colon, less space before a new sentence, and a tighter Y-e kern. To my eye, the initial B hangs out too far, and the final period should fill out the measure: is this perception caused by a fault in the original, a difference in personal preference, or the way style changes over time? it’s impossible to know.

19th century modern types require ample word spacing, due to the large size of the serifs, and the lack of kerning. Words like New, and You, with a big space between the first two letters, need to be preceeded by plenty of wordspace. Compare Scotch with Times I don’t think this is a fault of the Scotch (although today one would kern and space it slightly tighter), it’s just that subsequently people have preferred a more even letterspacing over “full” lettershapes; the openness of the Times glyphs would have seemed improper to the Victorians, who found wide open apertures vulgar. In other words, Redge, they paid more attention to letter detail than “flow” – meticulous individual grooming being more important than social ease, for a socio-cultural interpretation.

2006-05-19 03:42:10 • Nick Shinn

No absolutes. The value of anything, including cultural diversity, depends on context. Do Armenians today gain anything by using a smaller x-height than the standard approach to non-Latin type design prescribes? Yes. Did 19th century Brits gain anything from that loose “Ne”? If they somehow did, it must have been microscopic, clearly less than what they lost: cohesive spacing, leading to greater readability. Remember that laymen don’t deliberate on letterforms at text sizes.

A designer can wilfully demote instances of good craft due to whatever other factors, but the overall will remain good craft only if the designer is aware of what he’s sacrificing. There are elements of good craft that don’t change from one time and people to another simply because of the physical realities of humans, which now evolve at a pace quite close to zero.
