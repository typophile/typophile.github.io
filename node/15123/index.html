Ragged-right on 8.5x11; to indent or leave extra leading?

Greetings, everyone. I have been lurking around Typophile for quite some time. A number of you seem enlightened when it comes to typography, not just font design. I've read some interesting (often quite heated) discussions. My question has to do with paragraphs that occur in essays and reports on an 8.5x11 page. My right and left margins are 1.55 inches, top and bottom are an inch. Font is 11 pt. Palatino (Adobe's Palatino LTstd.) Leading is fixed at 16 points. Indent is 18 points (should be 16, but I prefer a little more emphasis.) Line length is 12-13 words (less than 6 inches.) Text is ranged left, ragged right (I find justified text abhorrent.) Gentle hyphenation where necessary. There's nothing like a soft, soothing rag! So, would it be better to indent paragraphs, or simply leave a white space between paragraphs, calculated at half the leading (8 points or so)? I've always gone for the "block paragraph" look, but recently I've gotten the impression that it is poor form. I'm willing to change in the name of correct typography. I'd appreciate any advice.

According to Bringhurst, Elements of Typographic Style, the ideal line length (measure) is 66 lower case characters--2 1/2 lower case alphabets. The big problem with letter sized paper is that usually the measure is too long, as it sounds like it is in your case. A line over 75 or 80 characters is not good for continuous reading, even with additional leading, as you have done. I think this is an extremely important feature of readable typography. Check on this and fix it if need be.

There is no rule on indenting vs space between paragraphs. One way of dealing with the excessive measure is to have a wide left margin and put subheads in it. Another is to have two columns. Whatever you do will affect your decision on indents. The most important first question is who is the audience, and what do you want to accomplish. If this is school reports, then the main thing will be to relate to teacher's expectations or requirments. If it is published, it depends on the material and the readership. The goal of the work takes the lead in determining typography. If you haven't read Bringhurst, do get a copy. It will give you an immense amount of useful information.

If your measure is too long you can also step up your typesize a bit or use a wider font. If your measure is long you need to increase your leading to assist the reader in locating the beginning of the next line. For continuous formal text in a traditional vein, I think paragraph indents are better. Block paragraphs slow down the flow a bit because each one is more of a new beginning.

2005-09-15 22:00:12 • Glyn Adgie
>were only really necessary on manual typewriters. No. Indented paragraphs are very old, long before the typewriter. They are an excellent way to mark paragraphs, and in my opinion you should only depart from this standard when you have a good reason to. The standard indents are, according to Bringhurst, one en, one em and one 'lead' meaning that if the text is 10/12 the indent is 12 points. Bringhurst notes that spaces between paragraphs are standard in business letters, and can be OK in short business materials because they suggest "precision crispness and speed. ...In longer sequences, they may seem soulless and uninviting."
2005-09-15 22:17:53 • William Berkson
Yes, I've seen that indent rule: indent matches the leading. Jan Tschichold, Bringhurst, and I think even Eric Gill are all in favour of it. It's intersting that an indent of that length doesn't really stand out on the page. Sure, you know it's there, but it doesn't draw the eye to it. In fact, if the last line of the paragraph runs to nearly the full measure, you might miss the indent at first glance, until of course your eyes get there during the course of reading. It's a very unobtrusive way to signal a new paragraph. Perhaps some people simply prefer the clarity and crispness of block paragraphs. Eah paragraph is a separate, logical unit. I find that block paragraphs that are separated by half the leading and no more are more pleasing. Still, I'm beginning to feel that indenting is a warmer and more inviting method. This time, the margins and comfortable leading provide the breathing room, and not the white space between paragraphs. Although one usually ends up with some white space anyway, since the last line of a paragraph doesn't usually run the full measure.
