Logos as fonts

September 7, 2009 • nina

Here’s a confused question.

I’m finalizing a logo for a client, and suddenly started wondering if it wouldn’t be a nice extra service to give the client not just individual files, but maybe a “logo font” just with the different versions of the logo in it (so only maybe 5 glyphs). That way it might be easier for the client to use the logo (fewer different files/formats), and I wouldn’t have to worry a lot about the thing being scaled wrong or whathaveyou.

So if I have a logo that’s based on a (commercial) font which I’ve customized in Illustrator, and I make this into a font again, is this technically a legal problem?

Also, is there anything else I’m forgetting that might make this a bad idea to offer to the client?

I don’t know the legal ins and outs of it, but it’s a professional way to deliver a logo. You should specify minimum point size for usage probably.

[I]f I have a logo that’s based on a (commercial) font which I’ve customized in Illustrator, and I make this into a font again, is this technically a legal problem?

I assume you’d put every logo variation into a single glyph? It doesn’t sound anything like you distributing a custom version of that font – rather, you supply the image in the form of a font. I don’t see how that would differ from giving them an EPS or PDF version.

It would get slightly complicated if they asked for the customized font, to use for advertising or other graphic work.

The few fonts I made (for internal use only!!) use an OpenType feature to change “JW” into my own logo :-) as a sort of easter egg.

2009-09-08 00:14:26 • Paul Cutler
The only big technical downside is that some programs, including MS Word, will not display glyphs that are more than too wide (I can’t find documentation of the actual numbers). For some logos this will mean either shrinking it down so small that fidelity is lost or chopping it up across glyphs. So be careful before you make promises about what you can stuff into a font!
