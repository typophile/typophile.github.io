I’m partial to Miller. I’ve never had occasion to use it, but I’ve kept it in mind ever since taking note of its use in both New York and GQ magazines (if I remember correctly) because of its legibility and pleasant, rounded character. I tend towards san serifs for most of my work, and I’ve never really had to contend with many pages with lots of text, but I remember Miller specifically because I read the two aforementioned magazines and liked the way the body copy read, so I called the design departments to inquire what the font was.

2005-05-25 15:31:00 • William Berkson

Many of Fred Smeijers' (Arnhem, Quadraat, Renard) and Martin Majoor’s (Scala, Nexus, Seria; Majoor is a favorite designer of Abbott Miller) typefaces work well for magazine design. Their design philosophy of purposely designing idiosyncracies in individual letters makes them particularly suitable to display settings; it goes without saying that they work well in text settings.

Also recommended are Jonathan Hoefler’s Hoefler Text (used quite a bit by Robert Valentine) and Hoefler and Tobias Frere-Jones' Mercury (designed for Esquire Magazine; with hints of New Century Schoolbook and Fleischman).

Rather than “Delicato,” I would suggest Jeremy Tankard’s Enigma, which Delicato strongly references. (Details Magazine currently uses Enigma.) _ David Cabianca
