2011-10-17 03:59:56 • Richard Fink

Now that PDF is an open standard, electronic publications can go two directions: push HTML/CSS in the direction of PDF, or push PDF in the direction of HTML/CSS.

The web standardistas have had 15 years to make the case for HTML/CSS as the foundation. They have not made the case. Web development is still an infuriating process of debugging 15 platforms simultaneously (namely, the individual web browsers). Apparently “standards” are not as standard as they look. And seemingly simple tasks are still impossible: try putting two page elements in a web page side-by-side, or in adjacent columns. Ugh. Why is this so complicated?

“Because we want the web to be based on open standards.” Great, but open standards aren’t much use if they overly limit what creators can do with them. For instance, the idea of semantic markup is nice, but as a publisher, it’s not always what you need. Sometimes you need to say “THIS THING goes THERE and THAT’S THAT.”

Worse, though the standardistas have always claimed the high ground — “our way is better because no one company can hijack the standards process” — that process has still been repeatedly hijacked. For instance, the canvas element. That was something invented by Apple so its developers could avoid Flash, while ignoring the existing open standard for vector drawing, SVG. And then it somehow got shoehorned into HTML5.

As for ebooks in particular, the situation is even worse, because ebooks are built on a rudimentary subset of HTML/CSS. [[http://fontsinuse.com/typography-for-lawyers/|As I’ve said elsewhere]], today’s ebooks are at the sophistication level of a website circa 1993.

Respectfully to Håkon Lie et al., I’m not sure that electronic publishing needs another round of turd-polishing, i.e. taking HTML/CSS standards that are woefully inadequate and making them slightly less inadequate. That sort of plodding incrementalism has defined the last 15 years. I shudder to think of plodding along the same way for another 15 years.

Turning to PDF. Publishers who went onto the ipad with PDF versions of their publications were pilloried for being Luddites and idiots. But here’s the thing. New York Magazine in PDF is a much more satisfying reading experience than the New York Times app, which freezes every time it shows an ad. Though at least the NYT makes an effort — most of these news / magazine apps are a joke. Meanwhile, PDF works because a) it requires zero compromise and b) it’s intrinsically low-tech. After all, why is it we want companies like the New York Times to be in the app-building business? To annoy us?

How about we let print companies do what they do best — make print publications? I mean, the iPad and similar devices are at their best when they’re playing content made for other media — music, TV, movies. Why not just let print media be what it is, rather than forcing it to be what it isn’t? PDF captures this reality. Martin Scorsese did not have to develop an iPad app so you could watch his movies on the iPad.

PDF has many virtues. I, for one, would like to see more effort go toward making PDF a more interesting and capable publishing format, instead of primary attention going toward HTML/CSS. PDF is far from perfect, but in its current state, it’s a lot closer to the ideal publishing platform than HTML/CSS. That’s not an argument for abandoning HTML/CSS. It’s an argument for recognizing that just as digital music and digital video have their own specialized file formats, digital publishing also deserves a specialized file format.

Fixed Media versus Laxed Media. If one simply takes the stick out of one’s behind and accepts that onscreen, the designer has very limited control, that the designer has partners: the device manufacturer, the user, and who knows who else, then all is well. Learning to hang loose is hard.

Removing the stick is very, very difficult. We are all, because of when we were born - trained to think fixed. 2011-10-17 16:34:10 • aluminum

As a print designer I tend to agree with Darell (Aluminum). If a client approaches us for a “digital” version, there is nothing simpler than to tell him “well you already got a PDF”. On request, I can even pimp it up with bookmarks and the occasional hyperlink.

However, even fancy hyperlinking does not intrinsically add to the user experience; or, indeed, make any publication move “up” to a fully qualified e-publication. For that, we rebuild the document from scratch into The Best We Can Offer in HTML3. For the odd novel or something like that, the results are … adequate. But any serious publication, including tables and illustrations, HTML3 as displayed in iBooks on the iPad (to name the one platform I am acquainted with), is not good enough.

I don’t care for page-flipping. What I need is more typesetting control: proper hyphenation, justification the way I want (the iPad seems to object), Keep With Next and Keep Paragraphs Together options for headings, figures which automatically float to the top or bottom of a page. And it goes without saying, I do like reader friendly options such as adjustable Font and Font Size. Hell, if I’d object to that I’d might as well stick to PDFs.

What the industry needs is nothing like “more HTML controls”, or “better CSS support for print-type media”. What the industry needs to see is that an eBook reader is .. not a web page and neither a 1-to-1 translation of printed pages. It needs a new Reading paradigm. Something in between.
