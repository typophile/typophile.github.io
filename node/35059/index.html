2007-07-15 22:51:06 • HaleyFiege
Perhaps I understand the question better with more context, Paul. I was originally seeing it in a Type/Graphic design perspective, and more specifically, in a client/creative worker relationship. It is no more dangerous to look at contemporaries for inspiration, than to look at a specific moment in the past. In the now, there are rough diamonds that others bring to the surface, in the past are buried polished gems. Inspiration always comes from somewhere. A broad historical study is smart. Researching a specific moment has a novelty quality that is marketable, but otherwise is roughly equivalent to emulating contemporaries (which is not the same thing as being aware of them). If you only compete with yourself, you are guaranteed to succeed. And, to come in second every time. Not that that matters; I recommend exploring and challenging yourself, and just assume you are continually getting better if the new challenges are still interesting. If you work is to affect anyone, they they are your client. In entertainment, people claim are looking for risks and surprises, so you have a leeway that other jobs don't afford you. Even if there is no one else you want to reach or impact, then you are still your own audience. So, aside from habit or competence, what makes you listen to yourself? Every now and then, do something you totally shouldn't, aren't prepared for, people wouldn't know was you if they heard it, something that is possibly even just plain silly (or totally serious if you are generally lighthearted). Take it all the way, as if you were serious (or reverse). Projects like that sometimes teach things that you don't even realize for a while, and develop "muscles" that you can flex inside your usual repertoire.

2007-07-16 19:36:33 • Paul Cutler
(You mean surfeit, not dearth.) If, as you say, marketing directors want retro, they want it because it looks cool. The rationalization comes afterwards. Who makes it cool? Creatives. And that extends to the synergy between type designers and typographers who use their fonts. For instance, Veer bought Jukebox because their customers were buying retro scripts -- so they gave them what they wanted, and added Bluemlein as well. Most of the other foundries they represent, including mine, also have script offerings. The phenomenon is too widespread to attribute solely to the manipulation of marketing directors. Most of the people licensing retro fonts have no association with marketing directors. A retro font like Helvetica is a phenomenon for many reasons -- and this was apparent in the Emigre "Helvetica Again" document a few years ago. I'd be more inclined to look for explanations of the McLuhan variety -- technological determinism -- his law of Retrieval states that with new media, what was previously ground returns as figure. So for the first few years of the digital revolution, there was a lot of experimenting and new typography, but afterwards (now), everybody's attention shifts to the rear view mirror.
