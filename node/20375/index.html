Stencil type fonts for a sign

June 1, 2006 • Grant Dunlop

Dear All, I am an architect with a strong interest in all things type related and I am in the process of getting a sign made for my house/apartment here in Japan and for when we return to Australia. The sign will be made of laser cut steel plate (similar to the sign by Peter Verheul in the attached image http://www.farhill.nl/02_lettering/applettering.html). I would like to use a calligraphic such as FF Quadraat (please forgive my poor grasp of the lexicon!) as I love their letterforms and especially those of the italics (and I have an existing license of the family). The final sign may simply have our family name (Dunlop as you can probably guess!) or we may include an additional line above of 'Grant & Yoko' in a smaller point size. The obvious problem is with the counters in some of the characters (D, O, P, a, o, p) especially and the process of laser cutting them. There seems to be two obvious approaches to solving this problem: 1. Find an alternative 'stencil' type font which possesses the same calligraphic feel of FF Quadraat (or Fedra Serif for example) which would be suitable for a stencil type use. 2. Modify the problematic characters of an existing font to make it suitable for stenciling. A third less obvious solution is to design a character/font from scrarch which fits the purpose. This could be possible but I may then never end up with a sign! I have taken a look at some of the other 'stencil' threads (Wallflower:) http://typophile.com/node/4933 & http://typophile.com/node/15078 here and they have provided some inspiration to tackle the third option. Peter Verheul's letters and signs are also an inspiration and a spur. I would be grateful if anybody could provide some advice for the first two options: any fonts which fit the first options and what the correct process would be for obtaining permission to alter those problem characters to make them suitable for the purpose. I look forward to your responses. Regards, Grant Dunlop

2006-06-01 17:55:18 • ebensorkin
One piece of advice: the letters in a font (or a logotype) have to belong together. When you introduce cuts in letters like "D" and "o" out of necessity, consider also adding cuts in letters that don't absolutely need them, out of style. And this can affect where you put the cuts in the letters that necessarily need them. For example, in "Dunlop" it might look best to disjoin the stem and the arch in "u" and "n"... and as a result of that probably disjoin the "p" at the top instead of at the bottom... and as a result of that you might cut the "o" at the bottom, to have a low-high-low-high cut pattern in the whole. You could get a caligraphic 'practice book' from a used book store. It would show you several caligraphic forms & you could try to deconstruct the letters in Quadraat from there. Or you could - if you want to get into something with extra info or context get: Gerrit Noordzij The stroke: theory of writing published 2005 Translated by Peter Enneson http://www.hyphenpress.co.uk/titles/stroke/index.html It's a lovley object in addition to being very interesting & theory rich. I think that just the bit about the process that created blackletter/fraktur/bastarda is worth the price of the book. > Understanding the strokes and construction will hopefully help me in placing > the breaks themselves and how they could be designed. Either way, you won't get a recipe for cutting your letters. Just information about stroke stuctures in conventional calligraphy. I think it's a smart place to start looking. Talking about cuts: if you go that route - they are also going to have to have to support word & letter recognition, and avoid uneven color in the word form. That's why Hrant was talking about modification of more that the 'problem' letters too. The problem with cuts is that unless they are made in a clever way they jump out at you in a distracting way. Rather than thinking in terms of 'cuts' you could think instead of a modified stroke - one that creates a gap. You could write out the forms yourself allowing for the breaks. Or look for a way to simulate this in the forms of Quadraat. Again, you would want to apply this approach for an even looking visual effect. Or you could Drill out the forms of the letters in circles of various sizes, so that the letter appears to be 'made' from holes like random-ish bubbles of different sizes filling up the target letter form. 2006-06-05 18:41:54 • ebensorkin
I think a bigger issue than typeface choice is effectiveness of the finished sign in its intended location. Grant, consider the legibility of the third virtual example you posted. To most passersby, unless the sign is right at face level, near the viewer (which signs never are, to people driving past looking for your house), a gray metal sign on a grayish background with reflections and shadows is going to be "ambiguous" at best. I would concentrate on finding an appropriate material to contrast with the background, and find a way to make the sign that doesn't require connecting links to hold the counters in place. It could be a piece of flashed glass (thin colored layer on clear) sandblasted so the colors contrast, and it could be lit from behind. for that matter, since you're an architect, I don't know why you aren't considering just having the letters themselves cut out and mounted on steel pins as is done on buildings. Then, you can choose any typeface that is clear and strong. Is that too obvious? It's simpler...
