If one wants a decorative face with lots of swashes, and there are places where such a thing could be used tastefully, why not start from a good typeface as a basis?
My feeling is that it's more about the name and reputation of the typeface than it is about the design. "This typeface is very popular, but not very decorative. Why not add swashes?"

Making a new typeface or adding swashes to a lesser known typeface requires more work in marketing. I don’t think anyone is adding swashes to any typeface to improve the design, but rather to ride on the success of its name. Why else would Helvetica Flair exist? Certainly not because it’s exactly what we were looking for or because it has so much potential.

2014-12-20 16:18:00 • quadibloc

I agree it’s not awful, but definitely not great. But my point is that my expectations for the outcome for these practices are very low. I don’t consider it to be a good practice to attach swashes to typefaces that weren’t intended to have swashes. If this typeface was intended to have swashes, I think it would have been planned for the swashes better.

But also, I think the swashes itself in this case are often just not well designed. I find the swash on /y to be horrible, and I would love to modify the swashes in /a and /e as well. I feel the swashes are very much an afterthought, rather than part of the design of the typeface.

2014-12-20 16:38:41 • Martin Silvertant

The fact remains, whatever its merits or flaws, Endowed Jones never gained traction with graphic designers. I don’t think I ever saw in actual use. It was an attempt to exploit the popularity of Bookman Swash. In that it was not unique.* But it flopped in the marketplace, never made it to digital, and only old type geeks like me have even heard of it.
