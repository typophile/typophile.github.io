Bidding opportunity extended for Athena Ruby

April 13, 2010 • doaks books

Dumbarton Oaks Research Library and Collection Publications is EXTENDING our call for bids to create new scholarly inscription font. We are inviting professional type designers to submit bids for the creation of Athena Ruby, a font for the publication of inscribed coins, seals, and other objects from the Byzantine world. We are extending the bidding process through May 21, 2010.

We would really love to see more bids from designers interested in this unique opportunity. This typeface will be used by Byzantine scholars world wide in the study of ancient material culture. You can be a part of the proud history of this contribution to the scholarly community!

For complete specifications for the Athena Ruby typeface and the bidding process, please go to http://www.doaks.org/publications/athena_ruby_bids.html.

if you have any questions or concerns about the bid specifications, please contact us at AthenaRuby@doaks.org

2010-04-24 05:40:45 • ebensorkin

If I understand the RFP correctly, what they want is a simple way of dealing with a situation where glyph variants are semantically significant to their user community. From experience of working with very similar material, I’m sure they get text submitted to them using all sorts of random kludges - for instance, I worked for a while on a book that had a lot of Greek coins, where the author had typed Cyrillic che to represent the Hellenstic-Roman shape of Greek koppa, and the element symbol from set theory for lunate epsilon. It’s time-consuming to fix a book-length manuscript full of this kind of thing, and it requires knowing more about epigraphy than most editors/typesetters do. So I think they want to create an integrated system that channels people into doing things the “right” (i.e., standards-compatible) way.

One large problem, as John says, is that even if all the pieces of what they want are individually doable, making the whole thing work well may not be. If the workflow involves an author typing her/his text in a word-processing program, and then a publisher moving this into a page layout program and/or into a website management system - all the while maintaining control over minor variations in glyph forms that are of stylistic or chronological relevance - well, how do you manage all the variables? Is the author using Mellel on Mac or OpenOffice on Linux? Or do you create a workflow that only allows Word 2010 on Windows? And my limited knowledge of TEI does not suggest to me any easy way to make the whole thing compatible with TEI XML while retaining all glyph-variant information: perhaps if I actually read the spec I’d find something, but it seems at first glance like it would require a fair bit of scripting to get glyph-level information out of one system and into another.

What they need is a team, not a type designer, and I really wonder if they budgeted in accordance with what they’re asking for.

2010-04-24 15:34:01 • DTY

Eben, the point of having a transcription font like Athena Ruby is to transcribe text in a way that represents the physical appearance of that text. But at the same time, you want that text to be searchable based on the underlying character. Eleven different shapes of the letter Alpha are still all the letter Alpha. Ideally, you want users to be able to do two things: to search for text and to search for uses of particular variant letters, since both of these are of interest to students of numismatics and sigillography: what a text says and what a text looks like. A scholar wants to be able to say ‘Show me all the coins/seals that include the following word’ and also to say ‘Show me all the coins/seals that use this particular form of Alpha’.

And as I hope this makes clear, no, I am not talking about using MS Word. :)
