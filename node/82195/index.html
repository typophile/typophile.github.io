2011-05-16 17:03:51 • Maxim Zhukov

Sorry, but the fact is that slab serifs do not mix easily with either serif or sans faces, lacking sufficient differentiation from either.

However, if there is a large contrast in weight between the slab type and either the serif or sans face, then it might be OK.

In the past, typographers would often set the entire job in a slab — e.g. Cheltenham in the 1900s, or Beton/Stymie/Rockwell in the 1930s.

2011-05-16 20:21:21 • Nick Shinn

I think one does indeed need a weight difference to make a slab stand out from a serif (without resorting to a strong clash in style) but those huge serifs certainly make a slab stand apart from a sans! Especially when you consider that stroke contrast difference can also be leveraged (with both slab and sans capable of going either way).

BTW Cheltenham’s serifs (especially printed letterpress, like during the time period you mention) are too adnate for it to be considered a slab. I myself like calling it a “Doric serif” font. :-) And I don’t know about using Beton/Stymie/Rockwell for an entire job, but Cheltenham can certainly handle that with poise… not least because it’s not a slab.
