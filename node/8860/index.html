Morison wrote several good pieces in addition to the History Hrant mentioned, Printing the Times since 1785, 'The Times New Roman: cutting and designing the new type' in The Times house journal, and Memorandum on a Proposal to Revise the Typography of 'The Times'. See also Tracy's article in Letters of Credit and John Dreyfus, 'The evolution of Times New Roman' in Penrose annual 66. In short, The Times had used a Caslon until the late 1800s, when it switched to a modern called simply "Times Roman". (Richard, you are of course correct in the current usage of the term, more on which see Charles Bigelow's posting to comp.fonts.) Both the Linotype and Monotype versions were used, Monotype for the text and Linotype for headlines. I don't think the newest members of the Times lineage are commercially available. Just Times (New) Roman, and Times Europa. See also John Berry's article 'Times Classic: a new type for the old Times' in Contemporary newspaper design, and the notes on Briem Times, which is the "new" name for Times Millennium.
