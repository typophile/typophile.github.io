Comparing significant Grotesques/Neo-Grotesques

July 9, 2007 • blank

I am starting a research project—hopefully to become my thesis—regarding the design of grotesque typefaces. My goal is to identify the commonalities in grotesque type as well as the individual quirks, flaws, and standout features, then use the knowledge gained to develop a neo-grotesque of my own. I think that ten faces will be enough, and right now I am still a little unsure about which ones to choose. Following are fonts I am looking at, I would appreciate your feedback. The Current Contenders: 1. Akzidenz-Grotesk: A great starting point, as it is the first grotesque to make the jump from jobbing to serious work. Also important as it was the starting point for the design of Helvetica. 2. Franklin Gothic: This appears to be the first significant American sans and has remained popular since it was introduced. 3. Trade Gothic: I am unsure about including Trade Gothic. While it appears to have enjoyed decades of popularity, the more I look at it, and into its history, the less reason I see to include it. 4. DIN/FF DIN: Important because of the historical signficance of the DIN organization in design and because I think that the narrower, more vertical design than its predecessors which has influenced the design of the neo-grotesques. 5. Monotype Grotesk: Again, this one seems much less significant as I move along—its only real significance seems to be that Arial is pretty clearly rooted deeply to the design of Grotesk 215 as well as Helvetica. 6. Univers: I’m pretty sure this one requires no explanation. 7. Helvetica: Honestly, there’s so much to pick at here that I would love to be able to leave it out for the sake of balance. 8. Neue Helvetica: I think that this one should at least be combined with Hevetica, if not dropped entirely. While the design does an excellent job fixing the flaws in the design of Helvetica and making it consistent across weights, from what I can tell, the high cost of complete sets has made it somewhat unpopular. 9. Arial: Do I include this one for the sake of its popularity, or just dismiss it as a fluke? 10. Meta: Historically Meta seems especially important as the first really popular neo-grotesque. Because Meta is so well designed, an examination of the features Prof. Spiekermann incorporated reveals a lot about its predecessors. I am also considering adding Verdana to the list, this seems justified as Verdana seems to be the most significant well-designed grotesques meant for onscreen use. It certainly seems like a better choice than Arial.
Can't see how you can avoid Helvetica. I would only cover Arial and other metrically compatible variants (some of which, eg. Monotype Albany do interesting things on Arial/Helv metrics) as a subsection of the Helvetica story. Definitely include Verdana ;-) esp. if you're coming to TypeCon as we'll have some never-before-seen behind the scenes material on the font. Just got back from a trip to Vancouver where I interviewed Virginia Howlett for the exhibit.

2007-07-10 12:53:46 • tina
This seems to lack focus at the moment. Historically, there are a lot of variations of the grotesque, more than enough for learned library research; and there are huge lacunae surrounding the origins of the genre, and the origins of its lower case and italics. I suspect that one would have to dig deeper than specimen books to find the answers. At the other end of the scale, today there are many foundries doing "their" grot -- OurType and Process spring to mind -- and IMO these are more interesting extensions of the genre than widening it to include Meta etc. In fact, Meta has spawned its own genre. The Helvetica-ish category is presenterd in the recent FontShop promotion, and Stephen Coles amassed quite a few fonts for that. What do you mean by "significant"? certain forms of historical research could quantify that, eg the gross weight of foundry type sold, or the number of newspaper readers. In general I am extremely sceptical of that term, as it is used by authors to justify there own preferences, preconceptions, and prejudices. I've designed a couple of grots, with another on the way, all of which I consider extremely significant! -- and for quite different reasons.

Gill is sort of the grandaddy of humanist sans type, so it’s out. Metro and Futura are geometric sans, and even farther from what I want to get into than Helvetica/Univers. Gotham falls into some weird vernacular hybrid category (Along with Neutraface and other similar designs) that I’m not about to try categorizing. After thinking about this stuff some more, I see two options 1. Cut things off, stylistically, just before Helvetica/Univers. This way I get to focus more on grotesques with a more human quality than those with none. I also don’t have to worry about the grotesque/neo-grotesque categorization arguments. Design-wise, this sends me down the path of a traditional grotesque. 2. Ditch my earlier notion of neo-grotesque and treat Univers and Helvetica as the origin of the neo-grotesque (which after a little reviewing last night I think is correct) and follow those developments up with the newer categories that come with the innovations of Meta, Verdana, etc.. This one is a little more appealing as it gives me reason to explore the definitions of new categories, and to create a contemporary design of my own.

James, one of the most helpful insights I learned about design history was from an very old show on PBS about antique furniture. The guy would put one chair or something on a pedestal, and go on rhapsodizing about it for a half hour--or maybe it was an hour. It was great. One of the things he always said was that if you want to understand an object, look at the period just before it. Every craftsman is reacting to what came just before, what he has grown up with. It's true, and that's the key. So I think concentrating on the original grotesques, and then going forward to look at modifications is a great idea.
