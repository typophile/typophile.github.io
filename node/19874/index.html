Drop caps and quotation marks

May 11, 2006 • jnbixler

I’ve been looking around and can’t seem to find any clear information on this anywhere; If I use a drop capital in a sentence beginning with a quotation mark, what is the convention as far as the mark is concerned? Do I:

a) Include the mark, adjusted to the same size as the cap, or

b) leave it out?

Thanks in advance for any advice!

Bringhurst says put it in, the examples he shows has the mark at a larger size than the body text (definitely not the equivalent size of the drop cap), and overhanging the lefthand margin if necessary, an “A for example offers plenty of space to include it in the measure. He also says that the convention of dropping it is a result of the difficulty in setting it in metal and for the reader’s sake and with the ease of digital setting to keep it in. So I think the convention (insofar as one exists) is c) Include the mark, adjusted to suit the cap but not necessarily of the same size. Tim

2006-05-11 14:47:57 • Grot Esqué
You have to include it so people know it’s a quote, but it can/should be smaller than the drop cap, but bigger than the text size. You’ll probably have to play with kerning and baseline shift to make it look OK, it should align at top with the drop cap. Of course this also necessitates (in Quark) making your drop cap 2 characters.

I’ve seen it the other way, Norbert. You often see a quote of many paragraphs. There will be starting quotes at each paragraph, but only one ending quote. The idea is that the start quotes remind the reader that we are still in a quotation, and the only closing quote is needed to completely end it.

(But I know some editors that will make you throw in the additional close quotes.)

I’ve never seen open quotes omitted, but closed kept. To me that is like closing the barn door after the horse gets out.

I think Don is right - the convention is that each paragraph of a long quote should have opening quotes but there shouldn’t be end quotes until the very end of the quotation. 2006-05-11 19:50:08 • pattyfab

Just a reminder… normal style standards for opening quotations of text the same point size is not being debated.

Once a large lead-in cap was decided on, it’s became the typographer’s (back in the days when it was an actual profession) or designer’s obligation to make it work right, whether it was simply the first letter of the sentence or a quote.

My first inclination was and is always to seek one or more reliable references, then decide whether I break the rules or not.

You can’t use the same guidelines for every situation. If you are designing a broadside or poster with short text, or if you are laying out a five-hundred page book… different situations call for different solutions.

I follow conventions until they make no sense.
