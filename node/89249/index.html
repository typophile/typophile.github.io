2012-01-20 17:29:45 • JamesM

I have a Canon multifunction inkjet printer (I think it’s some kind of Pixma), and I’ve recently made two discoveries that might be common knowledge to those in the know, but may be helpful for the self-taught like myself:

Working in InDesign, I’d always chosen “Document” instead of “Proof,” in the printing dialog, because in my mind “proof,” equated with “draft,” as in something lower quality than I wanted. My recent brilliant discovery is that what “proof” actually means is “simulate printing press results.”

In the printing dialog (in InDesign), in the Advanced menu, there is a checkbox for “print as bitmap” which seems to be on by default. Turning that off seems to improve the quality of the printed type quite a bit.

Those two changes, plus going into the printer’s own print dialog to make sure it was set to plain paper/high quality, vastly improved the quality of my proofs. The color on the plain paper is still a little washed out, but proofing onto matte photo paper will pump up the colors closer to what you’ll get with a laser printer (and inkjet can deal with some subtleties better than laser printers, I think).

When I was in college I had a $50 hp printer that I bought at target in desperation. I printed a picture of a large etching onto 4 pieces of matte photo paper and taped them together on the back to make a whole. I brought it in to class and showed it to my printmaking teacher who, at first glance, mistook it for an actual etching. Even when it was clearly not an etching, no one believed that it was made by an inkjet till I turned it over and showed them the tape. That same printer pumped out the ugliest jaggedy black text you could ever imagine. So, I don’t know, printers and the layers and layers of software and drivers you have to slog through, remain pretty mysterious to me.

True Postscript vs. Postscript emulation also makes a dfference. I have a true Postscript monochrome Xerox Phaser 4510, and two Postscript-emulating HP color laserjets. Text on the HP printers always comes out heavy. Text on the Xerox is accurate (by accurate, I mean it is a very close approximation of the text color once it has been offset printed on coated paper). For font proofing, I rely on the Xerox for accuracy, but I rely on the HPs to show me what office users are likely to see.

I used to have a Lexmark color printer — it was Postscript-emulating but still had very sharp text output. Unfortunately it also consumed toner way faster than the specifications predicted, so it had to go.

The cheaper Phaser printers are not true Postscript.

Also, if you’re looking at color printers, Xerox sells both toner-based and solid-ink printers, which I’d expect to print text differently.

So you’ll want to look at the same documents coming through different printers.

The one flaw on my Phaser 4510 is that while it comes out of sleep mode quickly to print, the output is pale. Once the machine prints a few pages and warms up, the toner coverage gets deeper. So if you’re making test prints off a cold printer, make multiple copies.
