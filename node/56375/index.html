In the old days one would bill the client for the cost of typesetting, for the paper you‘d use for paste up, for the postage and packaging needed the send the lay outs to the printer, etc. Sundries would be included in the hourly rates. In these days the client pays for the software you use and all other costs of doing business. Be it in the form of a surcharge on your hourly rate (more convenient), or on a per item base. If I client wants files (including fonts), he pays extra (a lot extra, if I can get it) for receiving these. If I think the fonts I bought for a project will be needed again in future, but the client wants the fonts too, I will buy another license and transfer that to the client, billing the client for the license and the time I spent arranging everything. Or I tell the client that it‘s cheaper to buy a license all by himself (a URL may be provided by me, as a courtesy). We ARE in a service industry, with quite clearly defined boundaries of what these services include. The service provided is a product according to the original request, not a collection of tools. . . . Bert Vanderveen BNO

2009-04-01 03:29:43 • aluminum
so, are you telling me that vectorm could not “install and use” a font that was _legally_licensed_ in the name of his client to do work for that client? again, this is not a rhetorical question. It depends on the license. It's as simple as that. If you're asking if vectorm could make a copy for use on his own computer, most font licenses would not allow that unless vectorm were an employee, not a freelancer. Or he can license the font up front and later transfer the license (most font EULAs seem to allow this, though not all). It's my understanding that in the USA, a EULA that is agreed to by the licensor is legally binding, as long as the terms are not "unconscionable" (http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unconscionable) and do not require illegal actions. They *can* restrict people from doing things that they would be legally allowed to do if they didn't agree to the license. Of course, I'm not a lawyer, and like anybody who has had way too many dealings with the corporate world, I do recommend consulting a lawyer for legal advice. If people care, they can choose fonts based in part on the licensing terms. Interestingly, if users ignore the licensing terms anyway, there isn't much point in font vendors trying to make their license terms more appealing, is there? IMO, comparing this to the RIAA is more than a little misleading. Although there are plenty of other font use cases, it seems here we're talking specifically about people who are making money from their use of the fonts. Not personal use at all. If I were creating a font license myself (a situation I foresee within the next couple of years), I would certainly *consider* having a clause to allow usage-by-contractors and usage-by-output-providers, as long as everyone with simultaneous access was licensed. So you could let a contractor or output provider use the font, as long as you didn't exceed the number of simultaneous licensed users, and they did not keep a copy of the font when the work was done. But then again, while I lean towards liberalism as far as font license terms for my own fonts, I strongly support other creators' rights to set license terms they are comfortable with. I think they *ought* to set license terms their users can live with, too, but I don't think they have a moral obligation to do so, because any customer who actually cares about following the terms of their EULA has plenty of choices out there. Cheers, T
