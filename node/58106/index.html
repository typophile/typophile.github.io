sii said:

no one in the eBook world cares about type.

not entirely true.

i’m here specifically as an e-book programmer who is soliciting feedback on a wide variety of issues…

i can’t say you guys are using the opportunity wisely, not so far anyway, but i am nothing if not persistent.

as to the particular matter of type – that is fonts – the wired article misses the point in a substantial way…

one of the primary tenants of e-book philosophy is that the end-user should have control over the fonts used… so the investment of a lot of design considerations over that particular aspect of the typography would be wasted. the very same situation applies, of course, to web-pages.

this applies to some other typographical aspects as well… should the text be justified or ragged-right? user decides. how tight should the leading be? however the user likes it. how big are the margins? as big as the user wants ‘em to be.

the challenge to an e-book programmer, thus, becomes how to offer the end-user the options in an easy-to-use interface, and how to best present a display based on the user’s choices.

this requires a sensitivity to the practices of the typographer, an awareness that i am continuously attempting to cultivate…

URW’s hz program, which included paragraph level justification and linebreaking on which Adobe’s InDesign composers are based, was developed for use in static publishing (in which the text becomes a fixed state prior to delivery to the reader), but in many respects it is particularly appropriate to dynamic publishing (in which live text is delivered to the reader, who may e.g. change the size, change the typeface, etc.). This is what is missing from the software driving eBooks: a justification and linebreaking model that is smart enough to provide, on-the-fly, something close to the standards of a well-typeset print edition for dynamic text.

Dear Bowerbird:

Assuming that you are not simply being argumentative for your own amusement, your questions deserve honest answers; I’ll attempt to provide them.

Q: “what point-size is “optimal”, such that, if i were to adjust it, i would be causing myself “a substandard reading experience”?”

A: Obviously, it depends upon the size, resolution and contrast ratio of the device or page in question. The optimal point size is determined by the number of characters per line, including word spaces; a substantial amount of objective research, using double-blind methodology, has determined this to be between 45 and 70 characters per line; at these line lengths, both reading speed and comprehension improve for most readers. Moreover, these line lengths will prevent “rivers” from drawing the eye downwards rather than across the page. Rivers are excessive negative space between words; well-set type will appear as a series of clearly-defined strips when one squints at the text. The vertically-oriented iPhone illustration shows excessive rivers in the text.

Q: “what leading is “optimal”? what margins are “optimal”? and would changing those make “a substandard reading experience”?”

A: Leading will be sufficient, (and I will define “optimal leading” as sufficient leading) when the phenomenon of “bounce-back” does not occur. Bounce-back can be defined as the eye of the reader tending to return to the beginning of the line he has just read once he has reached the end, rather than naturally moving to the next line. Again, this is measurable, and has been demonstrated with double-blind studies; eye-movements of the participants were photographically measured. The horizontally-oriented phone shown in the last image I posted has leading that is slightly too tight; some readers may experience bounce back when reading that passage. Leading added after bounce-back has been eliminated would indeed be a wholly subjective decision.

Optimal margins are much more subjective; Jan Tschichold and others have written extensively on the subject; but it would be sufficient to say that the margins are acceptable when the reader is not distracted from reading by awareness of the physical medium. The intended design of this comments section has both optimal line length and leading, that is when it is not defeated by users physically entering returns, as you appear to have done.

Q: “what font is best? (and if you gave the job to 20 great designers, how many of them do you think would choose that same font?)”

A: There is no “best” font; however, legibility (defined by the OED as: “clear enough to read”; i.e., the letterforms cannot easily be confused with one another) is the prime consideration to be taken into account when choosing a font; that’s why almost no one except semi-literate gang members set text in Old English capitals or Script capitals (although I’ve met people who think both are “pretty”, or “classy”—shudder). Avant Garde Gothic has a slight legibility problem with the lowercase “a”, it can be easily mistaken for a lowercase “o”; Calypso is almost completely illegible. After taking legibility into account, the next choice would be voice, the feeling one wishes to convey; this is subjective, and other choices made after these two have been taken into consideration are progressively more subjective.

How do you define “great designer”? Phil Dusenberry would say a great designer “cuts through the clutter”; David Carson immediately comes to mind. I would define the term more conservatively; a great designer is one who can impart information while requiring the least amount of effort from the consumer of that information; Paul Rand would meet both Dusenberry’s and my definitions. If you asked twenty “great designers” to come up with a list of ten typefaces for consideration for a new edition of The Odyssey, though, many font names would likely be repeated.
