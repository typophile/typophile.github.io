2005-05-25 00:22:50 • jillo

FontAgent Pro!

Working with Suitcase / Font Doctor can be a nightmare (plugins crash Quark and InDesign, Suitcase corrupts fonts, Font Doctor gives the high sign to all kinds of corrupt fonts), and FontReserve is getting kind of creaky in its old age (it’s been many years since either it or Suitcase X saw a major revision). FontAgent Pro is the simplest overall, and it almost never does anything unexpected to me, although I do have some issues with it. It validates fonts as it imports them, and then it can validate them again prior to activating them. You can sequester fonts used for specific jobs or clients in their own libraries, which ensures you’re using the same version of the fonts as the client and / or the service bureau.

That said, there’s no good substitute for figuring out your own policy for managing fonts, and only keeping around the fonts you actually need or use on a regular basis. I used to have a gargantuan library (16,000+ – obviously not all of those were legit) on call, and it caused way more problems than it solved. Reducing my library down to the stuff I could establish ownership of, and only activating the small subset of those I use on a daily basis, totally improved my user experience.

2005-05-25 00:25:06 • Forrest L Norvell

The biggest difference between FontReserve and Suitcase X is that the former wants to hoover up all the fonts on your system into its database (although it’s possible to get it to leave them where they are).

FontReserve runs as a headless application and Suitcase X runs as a regular application, sitting in the Dock.

FontReserve tracks information about fonts in a relational database. It used to have problems with corrupting this database, but I ran it under Mac OS X between 2001 and 2004, placing pretty heavy demands on it, and it never corrupted my database.

FontReserve sells a server solution that’s pretty much the best / only game in town for service bureaus and design departments, although it has its own problems.

In practice, the two are pretty much the same, barring some UI differences (I think FontReserve has better font previewing capabilities than any of the others), but FontReserve crashes less.
