2010-07-23 13:00:15 • riccard0

There are many factor that I consider (or at least one of many). Most often, I need the typeface to be relevant to the subject of the text. For a book about the British Museum, I chose Caslon (an English typeface from about the time the Museum was founded), for a car magazine I went with an American typeface.

Other times, the capability of the typeface dictates the choice, as Riccard0 points out (weights, styles, etc).

Still on other projects I go more with the feel of a typeface. If it’s for a ‘quirky’ brand/subject, I’ll choose a ‘quirky’ typeface, one which I feel communicates the feel of the brand/subject. Of course, how it looks is a big factor.

Some typefaces were designed for specific purposes (signage, screen, etc) so that comes into play as well.

Those are the ones I can write down so far. If any others pop to mind, I’ll write some more.

Hope this helps.

2010-07-23 14:37:38 • TypoJunkie
I have a printed list of my top 20 fonts (evenly split between serif and sans-serif) and I choose which one is most appropriate to the job. I came up with the list, based on tried and true workhorse fonts. Often, just the most popular choices available. I keep things as simple as possible, and as conservative as possible (which is demanded in the work I do.) A few simple questions leads me to the choice: Do I want lighter or darker? Old fashioned or modern? Compact or extended? My list covers the options. Only on special projects do I move away from the list and let myself have more fun in choosing a font. Sometimes I see a font I like and keep it in mind in case the right job comes along. I developed this strategy because the speed at which I need to get projects finished is incompatible with the speed at which I make choices. Also, my taste is not the same as the aesthetic of the work I do. So- I keep the choices to a minimum.

I think BeauW is absolutely right.

I worked in a large advertising company and all art directors, graphic designers and web artists have their ‘bag-of-fonts’. Or their favorite, ‘tried-and-true’ selection of typefaces. Also, most agencies and companies have purchased a standard font library, in the past, and most have to work within that framework. When the occasional new job pitch or ‘high priority’ client comes along, or makes a special request, a requisition has to be submitted and approved before a new font family is added to the library. With all the different font licensing schemes around, companies tend to shy away from purchasing additional fonts, simply, because of the added cost. An art director really has to make a good case for wanting the font.

In any event, I would advise your students to just look through many different ‘high-end’ magazines, over a period od time, and see what they like. Put together a list of fonts and start from there. With experience, I’m sure they will experiment and try different solutions.

Selecting an appropriate ‘typeface’ is just like selecting and appropriate outfit when you go out. The occasion dictates the choice.

I hope this helps.

It’s like asking a contractor which power tool to use given that there are so many on the market.

Part of it is context…the right tool for the particular job. A nice serif text face is likely the right tool to set a novel’s copy in and a grungy block face is more likely useful in the context of a music magazine headline.

And then there’s preference. Some folks like x on this font and y on this font because, well, they just do.

And like a good contractor, the key is to not have TOO many typefaces otherwise you end up with a mess and you spend more time finding the tool than using it.

…there are too many … faced with the dilemma of choosing an appropriate typeface

A dilemma involves two options. Although a significant percentage of people follow the sloppy understanding that it means many options, there are enough hardliners (like myself) to warrant the strict usage.

I favour the “historical allusion” method (a term first used by DB Updike), whereby the historical style of a layout should match the historical era in which a typeface was designed. Also, if the content is historical, the layout and typeface should allude to the appropriate era.

2010-07-23 21:17:02 • Nick Shinn

I think I consider a host of characteristics for a potential typeface: Technical requirements (suitability for the medium, number of weights and widths, OpenType features), historical connotations or denotations (per Nick), temperament (when it can be divined), ability to pair with other typefaces used, and comments I’ve heard about it – many times from this forum. But the decision as to which criterion to use tends to be spontaneous and unpredictable. On most occasions, something about the project’s brief or the past design will trigger a thought of a genre or particular typeface. The key is to have a broad enough knowledge of the type world to connect the brief to a typeface.

As I’ve learned about and used more typefaces, I’ve also realized that, many times, there are numerous good options. While my tendency is to look through the online offerings of foundry after foundry, I’ve learned that eight or nine times out of ten, my first instinct ends up being what I stick with.

For example, a magazine I’m working on now differentiates itself from others by publishing fiction and poetry in addition to reportage. This led me to the idea of a suite, with a paired serif and sans. I had seen FF Yoga, as promoted by Fontshop, and that was my first thought. I looked through a bunch of other super-families (helped in part by Fontshop’s FontLists), but Yoga still seemed to fit the magazine best, as a bit literary but appropriately unusual. In the time since that initial decision, I’ve realized that fiction and poetry are not equal partners with reportage, but the choice still seems sound. As the direction has shifted to become more of a take-off on book design, I’ve now gone looking for a contrasty Garamond for accents. I need a face in the Garamond genre to cement the literary quality, and it has to be rather sharp and fine to contrast enough with the bluntness of Yoga.

2010-07-25 05:55:03 • jcrippen

I start with two assumptions: (1) not every character/feature I’ll need will be in the font, and (2) some of the kerning-spacing in the font won’t be to our taste. That means permission for us (the end user) to modify the font is important, either explicitly in the EULA, or by having an arrangement with the designer/seller.

I should note that “not every character” can mean something as simple as an extra lower-case “f” with a different terminal or right sidebearing (or both), so it can be controlled at the word boundry. Little details in the font can save hours of time with composition of a text, and of course, improve the appearance. “Type” has become complex enough that workflow issues are a factor.

This is in addition to other things noted above, of course.

Rich Hendel’s On Book Design spends some time on factors designers consider when selecting typefaces.

2010-07-26 18:39:57 • marcox

…the semiotic, semantic and rhetorical influences that we attribute to typefaces.

You’re focusing on the wrong user in your definitions. It is to the professional user – art directors, graphic designers and typographers – that types have most meaning.

For readers, it is the finished document that signifies. The tools with which it is constructed mean very little.

It’s difficult to draw analogies between type and other areas of design. Is a typeface a tool, a material, or an ingredient?

Fundamentally, the signification of a typeface is determined by the typographer, in varying the typographic parameters of size, style (U&lc, all caps, italic, etc), weight, leading, line length, spacing, alignment, colour, and rotation. Textual content and juxtaposition with pictures further alter the meaning of a typeface, as does the output medium (print, pixels, posters, etc).

This is not to deny that there may be some small cultural quality of a typeface which the lay reader responds to in a direct manner, but its importance is generally overblown.

2010-07-27 00:42:31 • charles ellertson

  1. I start with an analysis of requirements (non latin yes/no, special characters or symbols yes/no, one family / more families, number of weights, typographic features yes/no, oldstyle / lining / tabular figures etc.)

  2. Then I try to find a general type style(s) matching with my design concept. I use a 2D “space”: the horizontal axe describes the fonts from hand made (left) to geometric (right), the vertical axe is for extravagant fonts (top) and boring “invisible” typefaces (bottom). This chart helps me think what I really need for a particular project.

  3. The third step is to make a list of type families in the desired category matching with my technical requirements. I use my memory, MyFonts, printed catalogs and friends.

  4. Now I try to limit the long list to a couple of finalists. I consider the aesthetic values, consistency of letter shapes, quality of kerning, diacritical marks, overall color of the typeface, how the italic and bold work in the text, the economical aspect (sometimes I have to save paper, sometimes I am asked to make to book as thick as possible), price of a font.

  5. I usually end up with two or three favorites. I try to test them, I print samples. When working on a large projects (newspaper, magazine), real offset printing tests are necessary. In the case I don’t have selected fonts, I contact type foundries asking for a temporary licence; if they don’t offer this kind of service, I asked them to open my InDesign file, change to default font to requested font and send back as PDF. If they won’t provide it, they are out of the game.
