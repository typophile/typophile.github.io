Scotch Roman: Good Versus Bad

December 23, 2009 • quadibloc

As I’ve expressed the opinion that there are both good and bad Scotch Roman types, it makes sense to explain more clearly what I mean by that.

[img:sites/default/files/old-images/compare_5838.JPG]

On the left is what I consider to be a “bad” Scotch Roman, and on the right a “good” Scotch Roman. Both samples are from the same old type specimen book.

I had noticed that to find examples of “bad” Scotch Romans, it seemed as though I had to look at specimen books sufficiently old that the types were sold by the traditional named sizes instead of on the point system: another old specimen book, which had types of both kinds only had the “bad” Scotch Romans in the older sizes.

The example of a good Scotch has somewhat lower stroke contrast than the bad one. But the most obvious problem with the bad Scotch is how condensed it is, and there are types overly condensed from a viewpoint of readability in all type families - although generally, only in Scotch Roman had they been considered fit for text instead of for specialized display purposes.

There are both “good” and “bad” every-kind-of-Roman types. Also considered fit for text at various historical points in time.

Of course there are good and bad typefaces in every genre, oldstyle, sans-serif, script, and so on.

So, if I have a point to make, I suppose I had better state my point, whatever that point might be.

I hereby claim, aver, and assert the following:

  • That it is possible for a typeface of the Scotch Roman genre to be attractive and easily readable;
  • That, however, for a number of causes, Scotch Roman faces had a bad reputation; and
  • That this reputation was earned by the prevalence in 19th Century typography of Scotch Roman fonts with numerous faults, the most obvious of them being a tendency to be overly condensed.

The second point is non-controversial. The first point seems to be disputed to a limited extent. The third point seems to be particularly controversial.

From the combination of the third and first points, a relatively uncontroversial conclusion can be stated - that the problems of 19th Century typography stemmed more from pressure to economize on paper than from the aesthetic that happened to lead to a preference for Scotch Roman faces.

But I want to draw a much more controversial conclusion:

The prevalence of overly light and overly condensed Scotch Romans eventually led to people thinking that this was what a Scotch Roman should look like - and this led to faces like Modern No. 20, and Monotype’s Modern Series 7 carrying over, to some extent, the flaws of the lesser exemplars of the genre… so that a good Scotch Roman had been hard to find (say in the 1960s or the 1970s).

Now, of course, with Type 1 and Truetype fonts, instead of typefaces coming only from major foundries or typesetting machine makers, although not 100% true, it seems as though almost any conceivable type of typeface could not possibly be all that hard to find.

There. Have I at least said something now?

Secondly, I do not find much to debate in the three points you just made, nor necessarily in your bold conclusion.

I suppose what rankled me in your initial post (and which I did not take time to adequately engage or address) is the very last point that implies that Scotch Roman was the first style to foist condensed proportions in text upon the reading public.

I don’t think this is true at all. The Scotch Romans that you refer to probably inherited these proportions from the earlier Ionics and original Clarendons from which they ultimately derive. Although these were not strictly text faces, they were nonetheless employed and accepted as such in a variety of text settings. (I’m not claiming that these were any kind of exemplar of fine readability, per se; just that they were widely used and read and, at the time, deemed at least marginally fit for the purpose.)

In the previous century, precedents for these same sorts of proportions will be found in some of Fournier’s fonts, especially those he described as dans le goût Hollandois – which in turn take their direction from the incipient narrowness of some Dutch Baroque oldstyles, such as may be found in the catalogs of Enschedé or Rosart for example.

While these narrow oldstyles may not have achieved the same widespread deployment in their own day as the Scotch Romans to which you refer in the 19th century, these earlier examples were nonetheless designed, used, and accepted as “fit for text”.

2009-12-24 19:14:31 • William Berkson

I’ve expressed the opinion that there are both good and bad Scotch Roman types

I’m not going to quarrel with the opinion, but if you are going to express it I think it might be useful to be clear about what ‘Scotch Roman’ is.

In his Plain Printing Types T. L. De Vinne referred to the ‘Scotch-face’, a type ‘originated in 1837 by S. N. Dickinson of Boston’, who got punches from the foundry Alexander Wilson of Glasgow, originally ‘a small round letter, with long aacenders, and not noticeably condensed or compressed’. Later a condensed form was made, but when he wrote in 1900 he remarked that this was ‘now out of fashion’. Dickinson had put ‘Scotch cut’ in his specimens, and ‘Scotch face’ became a kind of generic term among founders in the USA.

In about 1900 – but I can’t be more precise than this – the Farmer typefoundry in New York made a type that they called ‘Scotch Roman’. De Vinne bought some sizes of it. It originates with Miller & Richard in Edinburgh, who had begun to recast it from matrices of the early 19th century dating from between about 1810 and 1820 for use in an edition of the works of John Dryden.

In their recasting Miller & Richard had introduced some letters that did not belong to the original types, notably one that De Vinne noticed as something peculiar and which I regret: a t with a flat top. There are some others too. These alien characters are all in Farmer’s type too, and they make it very clear where he got it from: maybe by agreement with Miller & Richard or maybe not, but pretty certainly by making new electrotyped matrices from imported Scotch type. (These new sorts most be why Dwiggins put a flat top on the t of his ‘Caledonia’, believing wrongly that this was authentic Scotch usage, and I suspect it was also why Benton put a flat top on the t of his Bodoni – something Bodoni rarely did, if ever.)

Then things began to happen. The type was used, set by hand I think, for the text of the journal Printing Art, launched in 1903. Then Mergenthaler Linotype and Lanston Monotype quickly made their own versions of ‘Scotch Roman’. ATF eventually had their ‘Scotch Roman’ too, maybe cast from the Farmer matrices (but they already had Will Bradley’s ‘Wayside’, a frank piracy that was probably made by electrotyping the Farmer type).

In Britain the type was sometimes called ‘Dryden’ (for obvious reasons), or just ‘Old Roman’. British Monotype made a competent version based on the distinctive 12-point size, their series 46 (1907) without giving it a special name at all and this is the model for their digitized version. Later, in 1921, their series 137 was a quite skilful copy of all the different sizes of the Miller & Richard type up to 14 point, made for a Scottish printer, and they failed to publicize it. It would have made a better model for the digital version. Eventually, maybe in the 1930s, they borrowed the US term ‘Scotch Roman’ for both these types.

I have written plenty about the history of this type in different places and I am not done yet, but this is quite enough pedantry for my purpose here – which is to point out that ‘Scotch-face’ was a US generic style, albeit one ultimately derived from some Scotch types of the 1830s, and ‘Scotch Roman’, so-called, was something quite different, a type reproducing the earlier type of one typefounder. It is a different design, originating a generation before Dickinson’s, and many people have preferred it. Maybe it makes life simpler to merge the two under a single name, but speaking personally I should prefer them to keep their separate identities.
