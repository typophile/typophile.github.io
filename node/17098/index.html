Sounds like a lot of people who make design decisions for children were never children themselves. I really don’t buy the patronizing, oversimplifying twaddle that is presumed to enable children to comprehend, to see, to sense. Simon actually has a point; though children aren’t born with high visual acuity, nor the ability to read, they are tremendously perceptive, curious, and absorbent of their visual surroundings. Their brains process and develop incredibly fast, and they move from one thing to another like ravenous caterpillars. Assuming that subtle colors and 2-storied a’s are wasted on them really sells them short. A lack of imagination on an adult’s part should not hinder a child’s experience. Children have no trouble learning 2 or 3 languages simultaneously. That’s not whistling Dixie!

I find I’m also reverting to the adult pattern of lumping all children of every age together. I bet developmental psychologists perceive dozens of brief distinct stages in development between birth and adolescence.

I think the concern with “infant forms” such as the a in Comic Sans is relevant for a VERY BRIEF PERIOD when children are learning the alphabet, and how to read. I suspect that once that skill has been mastered, suddenly, large print, infant forms and other silliness are completely irrelevant. But don’t tell mom and dad!

2006-01-15 06:01:56 • hrant
It should be a higher priority. Most people writing here will still have handwriting that is not legible, let alone readable. That’s the work, as Gerrit Noordzij argues, of teachers who believe that letters are not things but pictures of things most closely represented by the forms of Futura. In training in deliberative reading, 14 pt Gill Educational will do nicely. In training in immersive reading, any readable seriffed humanist-axis font at 11-12 point will do. But children should also have reproducible models for handwriting, perhaps offered at a stage between those two, or simultaneously with the second. ‘Confusion’, in practice, doesn’t apparently come about: as Noordzij says, children can even tell the difference between lunch and dinner; and it will create some awareness that different styles for letters are possible, and that there can be reasons for preferring one to another.
