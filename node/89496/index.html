2012-01-31 03:48:58 • hrant
A lot depends on scale. For instance, a “display” font will work well at small size in a small document viewed closely—the most stringent measurement of apparent size being arc of vision. By a different token, “display” fonts will work better and smaller on coated stocks, rather than on uncoated stock or pixel screens. Conversely, “text” fonts will work well at display size when fit is tightened, by decreasing tracking and adjusting kerning accordingly. Also, such ostensibly “text” fonts are to be preferred for headlines in hard-to-read media, such as billboards and signage. Again, their broad readability may be leveraged in deliberately low-contrast situations, such as color-on-colour, and surprinting the “noise” of patterns or images. Context is king, and the typographer the highest authority. This is not to say that the distinction between Text and Display is arbitrary, merely provisional; and to these categorical poles must be added the interim designation of Deck.

John — I suggest you use the term "typeface" or simply "face" to talk about individual designs. What a "typestyle" might mean is ambiguous. E.g., "Curlz is my favorite typeface (or face)!" "The Curlz family (or type family) is highly extensive, comprising one font." Interestingly, the term to refer to styles of calligraphy is "hand." I've always thought it's suggestive that type comes in "faces" but calligraphy comes in "hands." Traditionally, the "three Fs" worked like this: A type family (e.g., Times) comprised all the versions of that design, and would have been stored in several cabinets in a letterpress shop. A type face (e.g., Times italic) was one version of that design, and would have been stored in a single cabinet. A type font (e.g., 10pt Times italic) was one size of one face, and lived in a single case (drawer) in the type cabinet. Nowadays, the terms can kind of mean anything, with "font" doing duty for all of them. But "typeface" is usually the way to refer to a specific design.
