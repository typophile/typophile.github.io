urls and line breaks

October 11, 2007 • pattyfab

I’m typesetting a book that contains urls - some of them are really long. Both Quark and InDesign default not to break the names, which results in some mighty bad word spacing. Does anybody know the protocol with this? It would look much better to break them but would that confuse people - make them think there’s a hyphen in the url? I just ran into this problem while reading the NYTimes - they don’t break the url, but they also are not known for their typesetting finesse either (lots of widows & orphans). The problem is particularly glaring in justified type.

I would break the URL and NOT use a dash or hyphen, then surround it in some sort of bracket. I believe WIRED used to use <>. So, you’d have:

visit the long crazy URL company at < www.thisisourcrazylongurl.com/keep/ going/this/is/really/long.php?crazyQuerystring=939283849392837474&923930 3939498382930949483 >

All that said, you may want to suggest they don’t bother with this. A few arguments:

  • Long URLs in print will never be typed in by hand. People are lazy
  • they are ugly and hard to typeset
  • the longer the URL, the more likely it’s going to a very specific page/bit of data on the site and the more likely it’s going to
    rot than a shorter url.

For instance, this URL:

www.ourcompany.com/products/fancy-yellow-hammer

is more likely to rot faster than:

www.ourcompany.com/products/

which is more likely to rot faster than:

www.ourcompany.com

So, if linking to fancy yellow hammers, in print, I’d suggest they use:

visit the products section of our web site at < www.ourcompany.com > to find our selection of hammers.

The rules I think make sense (and I believe this is close to CMS) for breaking long URLs:

  1. Never use a hyphen to indicate a break
  2. Never break directly after a hyphen as it creates ambiguity
  3. Send anything that could be construed as terminal punctuation to the next line (. ! ?)
  4. Break directly after all other analphabetics
2007-10-11 16:08:21 • Ehague

I totally agree with Darrel and Eric. Break up a long url but do not use dashes. You may want to enclose the url or set the url in italics.

Two other ideas:

  • Leave off the http:// to save yourself 7 characters, or even the www. as in Google.com.
  • Try using SnipUrl.com, NotLong.com, or TinyUrl.com to shorten monsterously long addresses. SnipUrl also gives registered users the option of selecting their own keyword instead of the default alpha-numeric value. E.g., these two take you to the same place: http://snurl.com/parsi http://www.tomdispatch.com/post/174838/peter_galbraith_the_iranian_conundrum

I do a newsletter and run into this all the time. I prefer to break after logical groupings or split words where they would normally be hyphenated. I also think it’s best not to split after periods, hyphens, or underscores (as Eric said) so readers don’t think that’s the end of a sentence or part of the URL.

2007-10-12 21:28:20 • ChuckGroth

A lot of this advice really isn’t correct.

  • Do not attempt to use delimiters, especially less-than or greater-than, except in the easily avoided circumstance where readers cannot tell if ending or surrounding punctuation is part of the URL. A subtle font change (does not have to be monospaced; I don't see how italic really works) is adequate in most cases. People do not understand that delimiters are for E-mail programs so that URLs are not broken and become clickable. Even if you had to use delimiters, they wouldn’t be actual less-than/greater-than characters but set delimiters from math (easily available in the Symbol font).
  • Test every URL without www. If it works (some servers are misconfigured and it won’t), then do not use http://www in the printed URL.
  • Do not use a TinyURL-like service. Do not. You are entrusting the validity of the URL to a third-party service. When TinyURL (or equivalent) goes tits-up, there will never be a way for the reader to locate the original URL.
  • Experience has shown – ask Jeremy Keith about this – that the right way to go is to either maintain a list of URLs on your site or maintain a list of URL aliases on your site that you can update if the destination URL changes. Of course the destination is not your responsibility, but for the presumed lifetime of the book you might want to make that effort. For example, http://typophile.com/node/37994 could be listed as mybook.org/howtolink which, when typed into a browser, sends you straight to http://typophile.com/node/37994 by a Redirect 301 command.
  • Break before a dot, not after. Yes, this puts a dot at the beginning of a line. It also eliminates ambiguity. Certainly break after slashes. Those locations are perfect for optional-return characters, available since WordPerfect 5.1, present in DTP programs, and almost completely unused.
  • Use a character style for URLs. I would do an onscreen check of every paragraph containing that style before going to press. You never know what kind of linebreaks are gonna happen, and the wrong kind are always gonna happen at the last minute.
  • Try to avoid hyphens at all; if you look around you’ll find they're rare. A bigger issue, undiscussed here, is shitty Microsoft or CMS URLs that are a mile long and contain a dozen parameters and arguments separated by ampersands. Do you really think those URLs are going to last? If so, should you even publish them?

“People do not understand that delimiters are for E-mail programs so that URLs are not broken and become clickable.”

I understood that…merely pointing out that WIRED, IIRC, started that technique in print eons ago. Not that that makes it right, but WIRED is one of the first publications to have web savvy style guides.

The main reason to have a delimiter in print is to avoid the punctuation at the end of a sentence. The alternative would be to never end a sentence with a URL.

Personally, though, the more I think about this, the more I think I’d prefer footnoting the URLs if at all possible.

“Try to avoid hyphens at all; if you look around you’ll find they’re rare.”

They’re increasingly common in URLs as people jump on the SEO bandwagon. Wordpress, for instance, uses hyphens in most of its URLs.

“Hypertext transport, usually — but not always. Do not omit the http:// prefix; it is an indispensable part of the URL, and not at all superfluous.”

while true, I’m not aware of any web browser that doesn’t, by default, assume an HTTP protocol.
