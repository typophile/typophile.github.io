I did not find Times NR Seven MT at Adobe or Myfonts, but Times New Roman® Seven [TimesNRSevenMTStd.otf] is available from Fonts.com http://www.fonts.com/font/monotype/times-new-roman/seven There is also Times New Roman® Seven Bold [TimesNRSevenMTStd-Bold.otf] http://www.fonts.com/font/monotype/times-new-roman/seven-bold Both designed by Victor Lardent I observed some differences between the letter-forms in the regular and bold versions. Note for example that the bold has a spike at the bottom of the vertical stroke for b. Also that the contrast is more than the book title. Using the last title as a test: It seems to me that the book title letters were made by applying a parallel line tool to make a thickened version of the regular version of Times NR Seven MT Voilà, no significant difference between this and the book title. All that is needed is to adjust spacing. Don

2014-04-16 13:45:51 • takashikato78
No problem, if you don’t ask questions you won’t get useful answers. I use a program called Fontlab to make and modify fonts. The parallel line tool is part of this program. I have not done this with Indesign so I can’t say if they work the same. The Fontlab parallel line tool looks at an existing outline and creates a new outline parallel to it with a distance from the existing outline that you specify when deploying the tool. The new outline can be on the outside of the existing outline, making a new larger outline and producing a glyph that looks thicker – as I did for this font. Or it can be on the inside and produce a glyph that looks thinner. You can use parallel lines that are both inside and outside to produce rimmed fonts, like the ones you see on college athletic letters and numbers. You can also make outlines with a different distance for the horizontal and vertical strokes. Unfortunately Fontlab has a steep and long learning curve. I expect that you could do something similar with other font editors, such as FontForge – a free open source program. It has extensive online help resources. Google will produce all the appropriate links for information on Fontlab and FontForge. For discussions about font making and font modifications you might find it helpful to browse through the Typophile Build forum http://typophile.com/forum/6 I trust that persons active there could give you advice on these issues. Don
