How to do anything good on A4?

June 10, 2006 • DB

I’m trying to come up with a layout for academic papers on A4 format (210×297 mm). The papers will be made available online as PDFs to be downloaded and printed.

Question: should I treat the single A4 page as the right half of a spread, i.e. small left, larger top, even larger right and bottom margins?

Problem: the font is 11 pt Adobe Garamond. From Bringhurst’s ‘Average character count per line’ table (Elements, p. 29) I get an optimum line length of 24 pica (103 mm), which is not even half the width of the (indeed, much too wide) A4 format. Result: very spacious margins, which may be suitable for some luxury edition, but not for this kind of writing (home printed on cheap paper). How to fill the width of the paper? I see the following possibilities:

Put the footnotes in a separate, narrow bottom aligned column on the right side of the text block. However, the reader might not be used to seeing notes there.

Or trade in the default portrait for landscape format and divide the A4 page horizontally in two columns. But the end user might have problems printing this; he might not like the format when storing the print-out in binders and the like.

I guess there are other typophiles who have fought battles against the A4 standard. What solutions have you come up with?

If it’s not a codex why would you treat it as such? If you’re setting up single pages, your margins should (conventionally) be symmetrical (eg. L&R=1.5 : T=2 : B=3).

As for the text measure, the obvious answer is up the type size to 12, but if you’re anything like me 12pt type makes you feel ill. Aside from that, remember that Bringhurst’s suggestion is the “optimum” line length, and your format doesn’t particularly suit that “optimum,” therefore you’ll likely end up extending it another few picas.

The other option, as you mention, is to shift the content as though you were, indeed, working with a recto page and use the right margin for side-notes. This seems like a lot of work, however, for a downloadable, but if you have the time and inclination, you certainly have the page size to accomplish the task.

If you were working with a document that you felt would be mainly read on screen the landscape idea is a better solution, but as you say it would be printed and bound (presumably at the printer’s choice of binding), you would need a generous top margin (the binding edge). For a portrait format you should probably budget for at least a 30mm inside margin to accommodate any form of binding, combined with your text measure, would give you a good 50mm column for footnotes, instead of having them aligned on the bottom, you could also align them with the reference in the text (although that becomes tricky when you have many footnotes to fit in). But even if the sidenotes are moved slightly down while still sitting on the same page as their references, the reader’s eye has to scan a larger area (namely the side column) to find the note he’s looking for. If the notes were on the bottom of the page, where they would have more a more expansive line length, they would need less lines, i.e. the note numbers are closer together.

Tschichold says (Ausgewählte Aufsätze über Fragen der Gestalt des Buches und der Typographie, p. 148): “Fußnoten sind die späteste und höchstentwickelte Form der Noten. Marginalnoten (…) sind eine längst veraltete Form.” (My translation: “Footnotes are the oldest and most developed form of notes. Marginal notes are a form that has since long become obsolete.")

For some contrast, see Bringhurst Elements, p. 68: “Sidenotes give more life and variety to the page and are the easiest of all to find and read.”

Apart from those few statements I’ve not found a systematic account of sidenotes and these being put to (good and bad) use. Does anyone know of such a work?

2006-06-13 11:45:42 • DB
The trouble with footnotes in this case are that you are trying to make better use of a page, so rather than take up space at the bottom of the page that could be used for text and leaving large unused areas, sidenotes will use otherwise unused space; when the reader has located a sidenote once they will surely not have any trouble finding the next one, they will not be difficult to spot after all. Footnotes present the problem that the optimum measure is often a lot shorter, given the point size is smaller, and you have to consider whether you should stick to the text measure or use the optimum measure, equally sidenotes containing long urls will also present problems in linebreaks or even having to break the addresses over more than one line. The best way to make this decision is to look at your provided copy and see if the footnotes might present either problem, if they are really heavy you might consider doing them as endnotes (either end of chapter or end of document) but they are less user-friendly. As a personal preference and with all other things considered I prefer a sidenote to a footnote. Tim

One other way to deal with a wide page/narrow measure is to have a significant left indent. In other words, the headings might start as much as six picas to the left of where the text starts. This should work well as long as you have one or two headings per page, and for academic work that shouldn’t be a problem.
