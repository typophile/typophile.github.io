2010-07-05 22:56:28 • Arno Enslin

These variables all interact as to how comfortable a read the text is: x-height, length of extenders, how wide the set it (how long a lower case alphabet is), the leading, the measure (width of column). And also how light or dark the text is. And margins.

Judging by what people actually do, when they have a little more space they go for the purportedly ideal measure for a single column–2 1/2 lc alphabets, and adjust the leading according to the typeface. That’s for books.

In magazines, and more so newspapers, they tend to go not for the narrowest typeface, but rather for a large x-height, short extenders, and relatively little leading in narrow columns. That seems to be the way to stuff a lot of text on the page.

Of course there’s a lot more to a choice, but you get the idea: you can gain in one department and lose in another, so it’s a balancing act on economy, readability, and getting the look you want.
