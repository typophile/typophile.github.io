George Williams asks:

“English has lots of bigrams which sound nothing like the letters which make them up: ch, ph, sh, th, wh, qu, kn, gn, wr, gh and at least one trigram: sch (I’m not including things like “st”, a consonantal combination which is common but where the distinct sounds of the letter are evident)

I treat these as single phonemes. I presume these happen in other languages. “qu” is common throughout Romance languages. French also has “ch”. Spanish (and Welsh) have “ll”.

Can anyone provide me with more combinations?”

I don’t see “ng” on that list. And there’s the Italian “zz” which is in a number of English loan words like “pizza”.

The relations between written letters and pronounced sounds are much more complicated.

Here are a few examples for “th”:

  1. “th” in “Thai”, “Thames”, etc.
  2. “th” in “thank”, “thatch”, etc.
  3. “th” in “than”, “that”, etc.
  4. “th in “cathouse” etc.

All four “th” are different phonetically. It is strange, but there are English native speakers, who think that “th” in “that” and “th” in “thank” represent the same sound.
