2008-03-04 10:18:37 • Florian Hardwig
> Hard to believe that huge news companies don’t have composition systems that include basic typography elements. Not all that hard for me to believe, actually. Consider: Through much of the 20th century, the vast majority of newspapers used Linotype systems for setting their newspapers. As we all know, Linotype metal technology did not allow for kerning. Most faces included f-ligatures, but they were time-consuming to implement, so they weren't really used in newspapers. (Imagine if you had to actually type shift-option-5 every time you wanted an ﬁ ligature, instead of today's software handling it for you.) But since Linotype f's were designed not to crash, it wasn't really an issue. I can imagine a pretty straight line from that predominant newspaper technology through the development of digital pagination systems for newspapers, with the result that today there are still many such systems without the kinds of typographic niceties that we take for granted in other applications. I'm not defending, just trying to reason out an explanation. -- K.

A lot of the systems actually descended from pagination software that was never intended for newspapers or anything that remotely resembles fine typography. They mostly descended from the pagination software designed to layout phone books. Think about how awful it would be to paginate a phone book by hand? I get nightmares even thinking about it. So they developed the software to do it for them. That software then was picked up by newspapers originally to do classifieds. Before you know it, the entire works was dominated by the software, primarily because of a newspaper's primary source of revenue, the ads. These systems are designed for the placement of ads, keeping track of ads, and billing for ads. Typography is far, far down that list.
2008-03-05 06:28:16 • Dan Gayle
[img:sites/default/files/old-images/firefly_3544.jpg] Here are some of the "no lig" types I've done for newspapers--Chicago Tribune, Globe and Mail, Daily Express (Worldwide), and Nicholas (not for a specific newspaper). The arm of the f has to work with the shape and position of the i (and j) and l (b, h, etc.) It's actually not as difficult as it would be for a text type, as those have a lot of width to "f", and large features. So it's easier to tuck the ball of "f" and the dot together in display type. Nonetheless, it can sometimes be a bit of a trade-off, and I think the ball of the Chicago "f" and the ear of the Nicholas Bold "f" are a bit twee, but not hopelessly so, and they do combine neatly. Ligatures may not really be appropriate for headline type, in as much as they are too fussy for news. So they have the opposite effect to their original function at text size. Is it possible that for ordinary readers the typophile-offending example Chris posted is fine, and a ligature would be problematic?! IMO the ideal solution is a "ligature" glyph (or contextual alternates), but with non-ligating forms nuanced for good fit, but of course, that won't work with CCI just yet.
