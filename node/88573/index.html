The undertie is used in IPA phonetic transcription to indicate the “absence of a break”. That means that there isn’t the usual tiny pause between words that would otherwise be expected. Aert Kuipers and a few of his European students used it to indicate clitics in Salishan languages, whereas nowadays most people use the equals sign instead: s‿qał versus s=qał, both pronounced like [sqaɬ] and not [s qaɬ]. (I made this example up for illustrative purposes.) Clitics are elements that are word-like but not entirely independent words, usually because they can’t be said alone. English examples are the ’s for possessives like Phillip and Edwin and Alfred’s apartment and the n’t for negation like don’t ‘do not’ and couldn’t ‘could not’. There’s also the ’d for the ‘would’ clitic and ’ve for the ‘have’ clitic, e.g. I’dn’t’ve ‘I would not have’. All of these could be represented with the undertie in transcription, so /… ʔænd ˈʔæl.fɹɛd‿z …/, /ˈkʊd‿n̩t/, and /ˈʔai̯‿d‿n̩t‿əv/.

I don’t know much about Latin poetry, but I suspect that the IPA symbol probably derives from the Latin academic tradition. Hence it’d be appropriate to return it to its Latin-usage roots.

Off topic: Does the possibility exist of Georgia ever expanding beyond its somewhat limited character range? I like it a lot, but it’s too limited even for my everyday needs.

I’ve spoken to Harry Parker (Mike’s son) at Font Bureau. Their official position is the end user may modify fonts for their own use, AFTER Font Bureau has both seen and approved the modification. Approval is the key here. Some font manufacturers – H&FJ comes immediately to mind – take the same official position, but will not even reply to a request.

Harry also indicated that in keeping with the old Linotype position, expanded character sets may already exist. The old Linotype policy was that the original customer paid for the modifications. Anyone could then purchase them, but they were not advertised. This gave the person who paid for them a slight edge. Of course, this came about in days when type was not available on a personal computer; it makes rather less sense today.

I don’t know who actually owns Georgia. Mathew Carter drew it of course, and may retain the right to allow modification. But I suspect FB to be far more accommodating than H&FJ. It wouldn’t hurt to ask them.

Your choice, I suspect, would be to ask for permission to modify if you are willing to limit use of the fonts to yourself, or pay for the extra character sets if you want fonts which can be distributed.

By the way, for Greek poetry, the long breve is also needed. In a way, this discussion rivals the one on character sets for linguists. IIRC (and I may not) the needed characters only exist in Unicode in the combining diacriticals. Of course, they are not diacriticals, but if your desire is simply to put ink on paper or pixels on the screen, no one will know – always assuming a device that will actually use your fonts.

2012-01-01 22:40:22 • charles ellertson

hrant,

This is a long & involved topic. What my far from exhaustive research showed was that regardless of what the EULA says, the publisher may give permission to modify. IIRC, Adobe’s EULA forbids modifications, but the same group of lawyers responsible for the EULA OKd the FAQs where permission is explicitly given.

Christobal Henestrosa’s EULA does give permission, if he is notified. I believe a few others, – perhaps in the Village group? – have something similar.

As a type designer, you have to consider markets. There are about 100 presses in the Association of American University Presses. Some are small, 3-4 book per year. Some, like Oxford, Cambridge, Yale, Harvard, Chicago, etc. publish a lot of books. Or at least we think so. Maybe not, when considering publshing as a whole.

Secondly, 90% of the characters needed are accented letters. Modifying the font to take care of these rids one of a nuisance. It is not needed, just a convenience. As an oddity, we can set Arabic or any of the Indic scripts in the original language, but setting the words romanized can be a nuisance. The window you have is there are enough words you don’t want to do the work by hand, but not enough to write a script to take care of it. Small window.

It’s when you get into needing characters like an eng that you can’t (yet) do the work within a layout program such as InDesign. As you know, you can legally modify the hell out of a font in the layout program, and they are getting more tools all the time. Well, it isn’t hard to make up a supplemental font, which you might need anyway to preserve syntactic integrity with some accented characters – the accent characters like dot below, not the alpha/numeric glyphs. Again, nuisance value.

Etc. etc.

Basically, what I advise AAUP presses to do is to investigate carefully what the font publisher will allow before purchasing a font. Secondly, there are getting to be enough open source fonts that we as a group can combine our skills and make what we need. I’ve set a couple books with extensively modified open source fonts that got in he book show – they are not all bad. This, coupled with Adobe’s policy, and the smaller studios that allow modifications, will allow any needed work to get done.

Now as I said, it is a small market, and probably its needs are not something any of you need to take into consideration for your livelihoods. Your only issue is whether or not there may be enough other such markets.

BTW, over 50% of the work I do when “modifying” fonts is to work on the kerning. Again, to work in the font is a convenience, not a necessity.

From my perspective “no modifications” is an attempt to stop piracy (which I abhor) that will backfire.

2012-01-01 23:54:53 • charles ellertson

> I don’t know who actually owns Georgia.

Just a clarification on this. The Georgia/Verdana Pro projects were a joint effort by Font Bureau and Ascender, with the blessings of Microsoft. And in fact, all of the Georgia expansion was done by the Ascender team, led by Steve Matteson.

I believe that Microsoft still owns the rights and retains some control over both families.

I don’t think that FB can grant permission for modifications to Georgia unilaterally — and Ascender has since been absorbed by Monotype Imaging. So getting official permission for mods could be a sticky wicket.

Also, the “Pro” designation was Ascender’s initiative. FB doesn’t use this designation itself.

The Georgia/Verdana Pro fonts cover the WGL4 character set (a Microsoft standard, basically), which purports to be “Pan-European,” but this obviously does not extend to more specialized linguistic needs.
