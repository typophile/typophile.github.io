2007-08-18 20:14:18 • blank

… they sure look significantly different to me

Since the legal notion of “considered identical” (as used in the Segoe decision) ignores minutiae, it is useless, from the legal point of view, to point to such details as e.g. that the Myriad-i has a round dot and that the Frutiger-i has as square dot.

The only legally relevant criterion is the “show-through effect”.

Typophilers are so very much specialized and so very much devoted to minutiae that they are usually not able to experience the show-through effect. Therefore it is necessary to explain the show-through effect to typophilers with reference to other intellectual works, for instance with reference to poetical works.

Have a look at the following poem:

Lion Lion, burning bright, In the deserts of the night; What immortal arm or eye, Could make thy dreadful symmetry?

If you are familiar with English poetry, you will almost instantaneously experience the show-through effect while you are reading the above poem. What does this effect mean?

Of course the words “Lion Lion” not only “look significantly different”, but ARE “significantly different” as compared with the two words in the original poem, and the words “deserts”, “arm”, “make”, and “dreadful” are also “significantly different”.

Nevertheless, the original poem shows through, when you read the above forgery. Therefore it is not necessary that I quote the original poem for comparison, because you instantaneously know the original poem which shows through here. This is called the show-through effect.

Exactly the same show-through effect is experienced by non-typophilers, if they are accustomed to read texts typeset in Frutiger and suddenly see a text typeset in Myriad. Of course, non-typophilers do not know the name Frutiger and do not know the name Myriad, but if they have read many texts typeset in Frutiger, they will spontaneously think that it is the same Frutiger typeface, if they are presented with a text typeset in Myriad. This means that Frutiger shows through in texts typeset in Myriad, and hence Myriad from the legal point of view is “considered identical” with Frutiger, even though several letters of the alphabet are not fully identical in design.

(Note: The term “show-through” is also used in the paper industry denoting that a text shows through from the reverse side of thin printing papers.)

What is the value of the opinion of the person who ‘spontaneously thinks that Myriad is Frutiger’, given that the value put on type is entirely determined by ability to distinguish between different typefaces? What is the control in this experiment? What if the same person spontaneously thinks that Frutiger is Univers, or Myriad is Gill Sans? The question of value suggests a different test: whether an experienced typographer finds it worthwhile to purchase both typefaces, instead of just one and, even more importantly, whether he uses them in distinctive ways, i.e. whether he finds value in their distinctive features. To say that the details are unimportant in anything related to design is to risk undermining the whole. As Charles Eames famously said ‘The details are not the details. They make the product.’ And that is as true of type design as it is of furniture design, another field beset by unauthorised copying. The problem with relying on the opinion of people who can’t tell the difference between an Eames chair manufactured to the exacting standards of the original design and a knock-off version is that the same person is going to buy the knock-off because it is cheaper. Effectively, you’ve created a legal environment in which educating people about design and quality becomes contrary to the interests of designers and manufacturers, because they are going to rely on people who are ignorant and are not going to pay for quality to be unable to tell the difference between two products in a legal contest, rather than relying on a knowledgeable, educated person to accurately describe the ways in which the two products are similar or distinct. This means that the legal interests of designers and manufacturers now run contrary to their normal commercial interests, which are to educate the market and increase the number of people who value the quality of the originals. That’s a crazy situation.

Mr. Hudson, as often, made some valuable contributions to this thread which are worth a more detailed discussion. In order not to digress too much from the topic, I will select here only his statement about who is to decide whether two fonts are to be “considered identical” or not. In this connection, he mentions

“experienced typographer”, “knowledgeable, educated person” “designers and manufacturers”

As far as Europe is concerned, the situation is a follows: Six years ago, in December 2001, a “Council Regulation” was published, the legal text of which is reprinted here

http://www.sanskritweb.net/forgers/segoe4.pdf (see page 2, section 14)

For your convenience, I quote here this section 14:

“The assessment as to whether a design has individual character should be based on whether the overall impression produced on an informed user viewing the design clearly differs from that produced on him by the existing design corpus, taking into consideration the nature of the product to which the design is applied or in which it is incorporated, and in particular the industrial sector to which it belongs and the degree of freedom of the designer in developing the design.”

The two important legal notions are “overall impression” and “informed user”. In the legal discussion preceding the above final “Council Regulation”, three types of persons were discussed:

  1. “ordinary consumer”
  2. “informed user”
  3. “design expert”

The Council selected the middle term “informed user”. For instance, the “informed user” of typefaces would be for example the production man in a school book publishing house who selects “Myriad” for the chapter titles and “Minion” for the body text of a new schoolbook. This means that the “informed user” is neither a “bloody layman” (e.g. his grandma), nor a “design expert”, for instance a font designer (who may or may not be found at the Typophile website).

If I remember correctly, it was Luc Devroye who estimated that there are only about 300 type designers worldwide, and if this estimate is correct then the group of “design experts” (in the sense of “font design experts”) is much too small as compared with the large group of the “informed users” working in printing plants, publishing houses, advertising agencies, etc.

The other important legal notion is “overall impression”. This means, as far as fonts are concerned, that you must abstract from (minor) details of single letters and must view the “overall impression” instead. The “overall impression”, in my personal view, is very hazy, and I do not like it very much, but it must be accepted as the law in most European countries. The term “show-through” (in German the term “Durchscheinen” coined by a famous German copyright expert named Ulmer) is connected with the term “overall impression”. Here again we may explain the term with reference to the Blake poem:

Such words as “lion” instead of “tyger” etc. are (minor) details, because the “overall impression” of the above mutilated poem still makes it possible that the original poem “shows through”.

Concerning Myriad vs. Frutiger and Segoe vs. Frutiger this means: Does the “informed user” come to the conclusion that Frutiger “shows through”, if he or she abstracts from the (minor) details and only compares the “overall impression” of the two fonts? For Segoe vs. Frutiger, this was confirmed by the legal decision quoted in this thread. For Myriad vs. Frutiger, I am of opinion that the same decision would be made by the Invalidity Division, if Adobe tried to register “Myriad” as “community design”. But you cannot be sure, because the decision (“yes” or “no”) depends upon the court and upon the judge and upon whether the judge had a delicious or an awful breakfast in the morning of the judgement day :-)

This thread nicely illustrates that the decision whether a typeface is regarded as belonging to the same genre of typefaces, or as a mere imitation of another typeface, is a matter of perception. Which changes. As long as some typeface is novel in a way or another, and one or two other typefaces imitate these novel features (be that construction, serifs, stem endings), these will be regarded as imitations. As soon as many typefaces imitate these novel features, it turns out that there is a genre – by looking at typefaces similar in one respect (the one that unites them and makes them belong to one genre) we teach the eye to see more minute details and learn to differentiate within the tight design space of this genre.
