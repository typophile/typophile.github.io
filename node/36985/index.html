I followed the link you posted, and it didn't do much for me. It instantly reminded of "How to burn off 20 pounds of ugly fat in just 5 days." -- In other words, a waste of money. The phrase "tips the pros use" is one tip-off. If it were a matter of "tips," we'd all be pros. Rich, too. Or the repeated phrase "keep your readers riveted to the page." You keep your readers riveted to the page by the copy, not the design. Design can help organize material for a clearer presentation, but as for riveting, no, you have to go to the shipyard. More seriously, If your newspaper/newsletter company is a smallish company, I can well understand the boss not wanting to pay for such a course, hands-on or not. If you are going to be with the company a number of years, they might be willing to help defray the cost of some design education, but realistically, unless you are in the advertising department (and it is more of a newspaper than a newsletter), there isn't much in it for them. None of which should stop you from learning, at first through books, and later, if the interest is there, through formal training if you want. Depends on what you want. I've always believed that school gets you your first job; how you do in that first job gets you your second job. But that's just jobs; education is a different matter. Charles Ellertson

Hi, Amber, Here are some books you may want to check out: On newspaper design: Contemporary Newspaper Design Editing by Design (This is a 2003 edition... There are also older editions, as far back as 1974's, going for very little money.) Elements of Newspaper Design (Don't know this one... Just found it on Amazon.) On grids: Making and Breaking the Grid Grids: The Structure of Graphic Design (a little pricey on the U.S. Amazon, I admit -- maybe it's out of print) As to your own work, I like the new logo and the use of a serif face for the body text. However, beware of setting body copy across the entire width of a page... The usual advice given is that a column of text becomes uncomfortable to read if you go beyond 10-12 words per line.
