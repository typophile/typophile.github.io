Peter,

Futurist and Dadaist poetry come to mind as examples of pre-digital sub-culture typographic expressiveness….Marinetti, Apollinaire, etc. Johanna Drucker’s book “The Visible Word: Experimental Typography and Modern Art, 1909-1923” (University of Chicago Press, 1994) goes into great detail about their work…the punk fanzine aesthetic of the late 70s - early 90s established its own visual language (via typographic expressiveness) as a way of sub-culture identification…

Have you checked out Saki Mafundikwa’s book Afrikan Alphabets? He covers the writing systems of several African language, many of which were established during and post-colonial rule as a way of establishing and maintaining cultural heritage.

This is a paper I wrote in grad school this year about non-standard orthography and non-standard dialects that you might find interesting…i cited Haitian Kreyol and Rastafarian Jamaican as examples where nonstandard orthography is used to express national/ social identity.

Sounds like a good topic, good luck!
