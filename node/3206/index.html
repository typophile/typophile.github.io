Hi Chris, It seems like an interesting project, but it seems a bit removed from typography. Looking at the process the artists are asked to go through, they seem to be designing 36 bits of square clip-art, which someone else then arranges in visually interesting way. The clip-art may be mapped to a particular letter, but there doesn't seem to be a link between a letter and the particular image it's mapped to. You could draw comparisons to the FUSE project, but in that project practicing type designers (mostly) were given a theme, and they designed a font around that theme. With your project you've asked people with regular jobs (architect or dancer) to design a font somehow related to their job. Your project is interesting; it's just somewhat less typographic than FUSE, which kind of ran it's course anyway. With FUSE most of the fonts had some typographic qualities, an A was supposed to be an A (eve when it didn't look like an A) - this doesn't seem to be the case with your project - you refer to your template as a 'landscape' not a character map, you only allow one case and no punctuation. The person drawing the glyphs has to be reminded not to use color - seems like art is being forced into a font file. That's not a bad thing, but perhaps it explains the lack of discussion. Cheers, Si
