How many nodes make a happy font?

November 15, 2006 • jason

As mentioned in this thread, I've begun working on digitizing some calligraphic letterforms to build my first attempt at an OpenType font (for the moment this is just an experiment). So far I'm still roughing in the letterforms by tracing scans in Illustrator, cleaning up the drawings, drawing new/missing glyphs from scratch (that is, literally drawing them and then tracing in Illustrator), then bringing the vectors into FontLab, but this process has produced a wide range of node totals. In one glyph, for example, I might have 200 nodes (remember, these are calligraphic forms), while another might have 600. This is producing far too much variation in the consistency of the vectors, not simply in terms of the amount of information, but the "edges" of the forms are, in some cases, far too "rough/detailed." So, I've been cleaning them up by hand to get the balance I want from one glyph to the next. That said, what I'm wondering is how many is too many nodes-per-glyph? At what point is the font going to get sluggish with too much information? For example, should I shoot for an average of 100, 200, 300 nodes?

2006-11-15 20:11:16 • hrant
If you look at P22 Zaner Script by Paul Hunt, you will get a good idea. As usual, the ideal is to have as few nodes as are needed to draw the curve you wish. A swashy script will need far more than a geometric sans of course. I would rethink your "draw in Illustrator first" strategy. You would be beter off starting your tracing in FontLab and getting fewer bad and un wanted nodes. If you are starting off witha scan, maybe a first cut with ScanFont then reshaped in FontLab might be a better solution. Illustrator does some things to curves that may work fine as an illustration but can cause trouble in a font. http://fontlab.com/Font-tools/ScanFont/ ChrisL

2006-11-16 17:51:55 • Goran Soderstrom
Hi Jason, Let me appologize in advance for the long post with hijack potential. I wanted to encourage you to keep going with your original process of drawing, scanning, and refining. This is a process that I have used and come to love. What follows is a journal entry of sorts, documenting my thinking on the font I'm currently working on ... eventually I hope to pair entries with samples in a *making of* format (but not in your thread :-). (my nodes average around 25-75 per glyph) ... While speed was the catalyst that sent me down this road, I've grown to love the process. Most *distressed* typefaces, by definition, start of as something clean. Some process is applied, and the result is a rough interpretation. The results tend to be static and lack an organic quality that I enjoy in a lively typeface. My process works in reverse, starting dirty and getting cleaner. I start with letters drawn as tightly as possible at about 1 cm cap height. The small scale allows me to work quickly, taking in character relationships at a glance. There's so much freedom when you can draw a complete alphabet in minutes. It also introduces a lot of distressing. You can only draw so cleanly at that size because of the ink, paper, and hand shakes. From here the work is refining the typically rough results scanned and imported into Fontlab. Proportions are generally good, but often need some adjustment. I find that organic quality not just in the letter shapes, but also in color variation within the set. With Olduvai, a serif type, there was very little color correction required. For my new sans though, there was a much lower tolerance for variation before the design just looked nasty. As I adjust the letters I'm reminded of the expressionist letters of Pressig. There is an approximation of curve, where the final curve is drawn in the eye of the user. I'm not saying that this is how one actually reads text set in the font, but that you need only suggest form instead of defining it. While he chose to use straight line segments, I enjoy even more flexibility using distorted bezier curves. It is a fantastic medium to work in, unconstrained by the ever present desire to define a precise shape with the fewest number of nodes. The freedom dawned on me fully when I tried to make a *clean* version of Olduvai. It was nearly impossible to nail down the suggested shapes in a consistent fashion. One letter isn't a problem, but capturing the spirit across a full set proved frustrating. I empathized with designers who've tried to revive a metal face from rough letterpress samples. It surprised me though, because I had drawn the originals in the first place! There's something elusive about it, and I think that's the beauty of designing in the rough ... GO FOR IT!
