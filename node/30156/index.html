December 19, 2006 • Manlio Napoli

Few months ago I moved from Italy to Bosnia-Herzegovina and, since I could carry with myself only few design books and a Bible, I ended up reading Bringhurst and the Four Gospels from dawn till dusk. Now I’m going to spend Christmas holidays in Italy so I can take some other book from my library and buy new ones. My suitcase isn’t huge and I won’t be back in Italy again before next summer so I must select carefully. What would you choose?

Without a doubt, Underworld by Don Delillo, I feel a strange kind of regret everytime I go to a bookshop and see it on the shelf because I’ve read it and own it and have no need to buy it.

Dostovesky’s Crime and Punishment, possibly the greatest novel ever written, the writing is so good and immersive the divide between Raskolnikov’s life and mine began to blur a little as i read it. I’ve heard the Borthers Karamoazov is as good if not better.

Continuing the Biblical theme Pilgrim’s progress by John Bunyan, Screwtape letters by CS Lewis for two very different, entertaining, helpful and wise takes on what it is to live as a Christian.

2006-12-20 03:20:40 • Linda Cunningham

Read House of Leaves by Mark Danielewski. It’s a great example of stories-within-stories, American gothic horror, hardcore erotica, erudite writing, and it’s got great typography set by the author, with words that go forward, backward, into shapes, etc. Literary critics describe it with respectful comparisons to Nabokov and Borges; Poynor included it in Graphic Design and Postmodernism. And getting funny looks when you have to keep rotating a book when you’re on a plane is always great.

If you want to read something tremendously fun, contemporary and erudite without delving into the design world, read World War Z by Max Brooks. On the surface it looks like a kitschy zombie book, but it’s just as engrossing as Harry Potter, and far more intelligent.

2006-12-20 11:23:53 • timd

Manilo,

Yeah you could compare Delillo to pynchon, perhaps in intent more in style; I think both are interested to capture and comment on American consumer culture/hyper-capitalism in all its manifestations, (especially Underworld and White noise) Delillo is much less scatty/absurd then pynchon, much more formal and dramatic, I’ve only read Vineland so that’s what I’m going off. I own gravity’s rainbow and have managed a total of about 6 pages; I know Patty would disagree, but I found Delillo much more accessible to read than Pynchon.

I reckon the trick with Dellilo is to accept early on that the characters don’t speak quite like people speak but, though you seem to lose a level of identification (and ability to suspend your disbelief), you gain so much insight into reality and relationships and people through the actual content of the dialogue that it doesn’t matter. White noise is fun and a lot less intimidating in size than Underworld, I’d read that first and see if you dig what’s going on, cheers, have fun in BH
