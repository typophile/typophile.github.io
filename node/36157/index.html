Eben -- You should have grabbed Mike Parker while he was at TypeCon. He probably could have given you insight into the Linotype ligatures/logotypes. (Though you might have gotten a lot more tangents than you bargained for.) A more practical contact for you might be Larry Oppenberg. He's head of Galápagos Design, and on the board of the Printing History Museum (which is in North Andover, not Boston, btw), and worked in the drawing office at Mergenthaler Linotype toward the end. You can still find drawings in the museum with Larry's name on them, I think. IIRC, these logotypes were only made for a handful of fonts, and you would have had to insert them manually, since there wasn't a place for them in the magazine. I'm not sure I'm understanding your first questions about the shapes. As far as which combinations, I'd be willing to bet that this would have been decided on a font-by-font basis. And the decision would likely have fallen to the head of typographic development -- which would have been C.H. Griffith (until 1949), then Jackson Burke (until sometime in the 60s), then Mike Parker (until the early 80s). For the ligatures, obviously there will be separate drawings. The telling thing would be whether there were separate drawings for the logotypes or whether there were just alternate spacing specs and directions for cutting the logotype matrices from the constituent parts. I hope to be making a research trip to the museum within the next few months. If I still remember this question then, I'll try to see if I can dig up any more concrete information for you. -- K.

2007-08-20 00:51:19 • ebensorkin
Eben -- I believe, as Bill says, that the full logotype treatment was typically done with faces that were targeted for bookwork. Further, the extensive f ligatures/logotypes would have been applied primarily to faces that were adaptations of older or competing typefaces, where the overhanging f was an integral part of the design (and a customer's expectations). For original designs, you usually find that the f has been crafted to disguise the Linotype's mechanical limitations, and so, extensive f-combo logotypes were not necessary to achieve the proper effect. -- K.
2007-08-20 14:34:46 • kentlew
These glyphs were called typographic refinements. The punches in combination (double hit) were used in order to make one matrix. Both Linotype and Intertype manufactured these matrices. Because there were no overhangs allowed in linecastng machines special "true cut" matrices were made but the tracking was not esthetically pleasing. Of course Monotype composition never had these problems. Yes, it is true that most of the type families that contained the typographic refinements were for book composition. Ludlow display mats were also punched with overlapping characters.

I have looked at the Linotype drawings at the Museum of printing now. And what I found was that 98% of the time what looks a little like variation is in fact differences in inking, pressure & so on. So Bill, you were bang on in general as I think we all knew you were. However! I did find some nice examples of honest to goodness for sure, no doubt, contextual alteration. Part of what 's fun with the drawings is seeing the original line erased. Mind you - there might be more - but I only had a day. I wouldn't expect to see very many more if there are any at all. What I wish I had time to do is to compare across pt sizes. This might not be possible in some cases because the special logotypes were not always available in all pt sizes. It is even possible that thye only exist in the point size in which I found them. So, here is what I found. Janson 8pt. The far right serif of the W is wider on the Wa logotype than on the W. Baskerville. 10pt - rf vs sf - the rf combo forces the crossbar to be shorter on the left side ( next to the r ). The sf logotype uses a consistant s & the basic special f found it in the logotypes. Caslon italic 10pt - fs vs fr ( also ffr & ffs) - the fr combo requires a shorter bar again but this time on the right of the f not the left. Garamond no.3 10pt. fa vs fy ( also ffy) - slightly shorter bar on the fy similar to the example above. 'fa' has a normal crossbar.
