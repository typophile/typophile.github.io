Characters from old contractions

December 4, 2011 • Matteo Ferla

Hello all,

This is my first post here — I thought I might as well register and ask, instead of holding my questions or asking friends and getting a “what are you on about?” reply. I used to have a graphic design job before uni a long time ago and ever since I am greatly interested in typography, calligraphy and paleography as hobby interests. Hence, my question is a just curiosity that is bugging me.

On old signs and old correspondence letters, several contractions are seen and they are written with the last letter with a dot or line underneath, such as st. for street, mr. for mister, yr. for your etc. (e.g. road sign in the Art Deco city of Napier, NZ) As to print these combinations is not straightforward, they have all but disappeared.

I do not know what are these dotted superscript letters are called.

What is included in Unicode always seems arbitrary to me. I, for one, would have a few English language specific combined letters, such a overlined w (for with as many still write) and these. To make things worse, Office Word seems to hate unicode and the unicode superscript t cannot be used, so cannot be used overstriked over a dot —one can always use the underlined superscript bug. In Illustrator, though, it becomes possible. However, interestingly, the letter height becomes an interesting dilemma of size and height… (Attached is S with unicode superscript t; S with underlined faux superscript; S plus a dot with a t (same size) shifted so its median line is on the cap line of the S)

Consequently, I would love to hear other people’s opinion on these.

Thanks
