Establishing a commercially viable foundry

October 27, 2008 • phattexta

Hey everyone,

I’m not sure how many people here have experience with this side of the type design scene but I’m considering establish a foundry. I can get my hands on some good capital but the issue of a skill base is the most pressing. I myself am not skilled enough to take designs from type and graphic designers in half or almost finished states and turn them into desirable commercial typefaces. I am however, confident in my business capabilities as well as my ability to curate the collection wisely. I have never worked at a type foundry but I am a budding type designer and my background is in graphic design.

Does anyone have any advice for me about aspects to keep an eye out for or put heavy consideration into? I would not be planning that this consume 100% of my time because I still want to design type and be a graphic designer but I can dedicate a good amount of time to the initial rollout and sustaining the company.

Cheers, Dave

In reading this post I can’t help but think of countless episodes of “Kitchen Nightmares” where someone, successful in another field, or blessed with an inheritance or rich parents, decides to open a restaurant, despite having no experience in the trade. As you can imagine it all goes horribly wrong – and it’s not just the food that ends up getting burnt.

Putting this image aside for a bit, my other thought is what’s your value proposition? What can you offer that established, proven foundries can’t? What will convince type designers to throw their hat in with your new company? Perhaps it’s as simple as offering large advances on royalties?

David, I should add that there isn’t a lot of money in retail fonts unless you are a large vendor like Fonts.com, Myfonts, Ascender Corp or Veer, with sizable collections of trendy commercial fonts from numerous sources. Some font buyers grumble about the price of retail fonts, but in reality vendors sell them at an absurdly low price and still have trouble turning in the kind of sizable revenue required to sustain a going business. Small vendors usually are not successful and rapidly go out of business.

The real money in type design is in commissioned works

2008-10-30 05:19:39 • innovati

I’m not sure how quickly you want to grow, but I don’t think you need a lot of money to start a foundry, just time. For $20 a month or so, you can host a site that will allow more traffic than you will ever get. Maybe a few hundred or thousand dollars extra for ads here and there, but I don’t think you need the level of capital that would require getting investors or bank loans or anything (and good luck making that happen for the next year or two!)

If your typefaces are really good, there’s plenty of small foundry pools out there like vllg.com and Type Trust that might publish your fonts, and they give full credit to your foundry. Even “big time” places like Veer give complete credit to the designer and/or foundry. Once you get a few type designs published, you could think more about developing your own foundry into a go-to shop. But when you have only a few fonts under your belt it would make more sense to me to put them out there on established sites as your primary point of sales. You might take a look at Underware.nl as a case study, they only have a few fonts for sale, but each one is really unique and has an incredible level of thought and skill apparent in its design. They sell their fonts on other sites, too.

Hope that helps, I would like to start a foundry myself, too, but I plan on taking it slow. If you want something that’s quickly profitable, typeface design is not the route to go – not as difficult as saying “I’m going to be a rock star” with no musical training and never being in a band or performing in front of an audience, but it’s close.

This made me laugh out loud. Will that $20 cover your shopping cart, your payment gateway or your merchant services fees?

In the beginning, it is certainly possible to sell typefaces using very little cash, particularly if you are using a service like PayPal to process your transactions. Because the service handles just about every part of the transaction, then things like a merchant account, payment gateway or SSL certificate are unnecessary. The processor takes care of that for you. Money that might be spent on those items comes directly out of your sales in the form of a percent to the payment processor. And, if you know how to wrangle a little code, you can easily set up a shopping cart yourself.

This can be a great way to start (and is in fact how we started) so you have very little cash investment up front. The only time you pay for services related to taking in money is when, in fact, you take in money.
