You're thinking of Germany: http://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/FE-Schrift There *was* a neat Flash movie out there demonstrating how it foiled any attempts at glyph modification, but it appears to be gone now. The Netherlands also modified their character set recently: http://www.dafont.com/en/font.php?file=kenteken LineTo do a line of number plate fonts as well: http://www.lineto.com/The+Fonts/Categories/Headline+Fonts/Numberplate/ In the US, of course, different jurisdictions use different die sets. Pennsylvania, for example, uses numerals slightly taller than letters. Most places just choose to avoid the problem altogether: combinations with 'I', 'O', and 'Q' are skipped, even if the dies are reasonably easy to differentiate. Texas just said 'the heck with it' and chucked all vowels on passenger-car plates. Florida used to skip 'I', 'O', and 'Q' in potentially confusing parts of their numbering system, but now uses them indiscriminately. This causes a raft of potential problems, especially because (a) especially with their die set, those glyphs are pretty tough to tell apart at a distance, and (b) their numbering system has devolved into chaos, so you don't really know for sure where the letters and numbers 'should' be anymore. (I'm exaggerating, but not much.) I've collected plates for twenty years now, so I can be [even more] exquisitely boring on the subject. Drop me a line and I can point you to more resources.
