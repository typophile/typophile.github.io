I recently stumbled upon Jeremy Tankard's Kingfisher type (http://www.typography.net/type/kingfisher.htm), and am curious as to what the general opinion is on this face. It seems like a nice, sturdy workhorse and is fully equipped with opentype features, though it is a bit pricey. The PDF is particularly extensive. Any thoughts?
It is a sweet face for sure. "...though it is a bit pricey..." How do you view that in relation to this: " a nice, sturdy workhorse, [] fully equipped with opentype features, {...] The PDF is particularly extensive. " More, precisely, I'm wondering how much a customer might value a product like this over another "nice, sturdy workhorse" with only the usual glyphs, no OT advantage outside of format conversion, and a single page pdf... i.e. no guide to the product's use in composition? Cheers!

    2007-09-21 12:02:18 • SuperUltraFabulous
    I used Kingfisher for a book in 2005, and it worked very well. The book was Cultural Breaks: A Collection of Short Fictions, by Brian Aldiss, published by Tachyon Publications in San Francisco. (I only did the interior; the dust jacket was designed by someone else.) Since Aldiss is a literary science-fiction author and lives in Oxford, it seemed appropriate to use a typeface with English roots; but the real point wasn't where it was designed, but what effect it gave on the page while you were reading. I wasn't sure at first that Jeremy's italic would work here; it's nearly a slanted roman, with just a few cursive touches, and one of the stories had extensive passages in italic. But in fact the Kingfisher italic proved very readable, yet still different enough in structure to stand out in the occasional italicized word in running text. The strong color made the pages easily read, even while the letterforms had a certain refined elegance; just what I wanted for this particular book. I appreciated details like the italic small caps -- and the regular small caps are strong enough to stand out in short acronyms in the middle of text. Having Kingfisher as an OpenType font made all the difference in quick, efficient book production; I could, for instance, automate all those small caps by using a character style in InDesign. Very good book typeface. And the display version looked very nice at Really Big Size, too. John Berry
