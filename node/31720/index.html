Learning from a master

February 23, 2007 • kosal

In my own efforts to better “get” beautiful typography and to develop a better eye for crafting logotypes, I have undertaken a simple type-kern-compare experiment with some of Joe Finocchiaro’s logos. Keeping that in mind, I am trying to answer these questions based on the idea that we are designing a logotype based on the letters of a font originally designed for text:

  1. What what choices were made, and why?
  2. Kerning characters can only go so far. What glyph alterations can improve spacing/color?
  3. What are the common differences between type designed for text and display type of the same family?

Here are the results from my first breakdown: When set to default in ITC Franklin Gothic Demi Cond., “Bank of America” has many holes. It looks like small type trying to be big. The comparison (font in red, logo in black) reveals a bolder weight to the logo, a wider apex to the A, extended ear of the r, raised dot of the i, extended ascender of the k, and an altered e.

Concluded reasoning: Generally, thickening the letterstroke connections helps when the logo is used at small scale. The widening of the A had to happen since the weights are heavier. Same with the r. The redesign of the e helps visually distance it from the r without physically moving it, thereby evening the spacing of the mer. The raised dot of the i eliminates the line formed by the negative space in the font. Not sure why the k was heightened. In the end, the logo is far more robust, confident, and than just setting the type in Franklin Gothic.

2007-02-24 06:24:15 • Nick Shinn
You hit on some great observations here koleslaw. Yes in fact you are right…type designed for smaller sizes has less contrast between thick and thin. Large display type can have high contrast, be tightly spaced and still be quite legible but at smaller point sizes the spacing and letter weight have to be adjusted to compensate for the small size. But the goal is that when comparing the two at different sizes, they will appear to be identical. Like an optical illusion. This probably also has something to do (I would guess) with why the “k” is higher on the one. It reminds me of how the ancient Greeks used to carve the columns on their temples so that the sides actually bowed outwards slighty. This made the sides appear perfectly straight…a truly straight column would look concave. (or so they say).
