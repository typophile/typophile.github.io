In old time, a typeface never got a name such we use now. We call Garamond, Bodoni, Baskerville to refer to a particular style ow their creators/designers/cutters.

Typefaces used name following their sizes, cut, but it was a generic name not related to the style.

So, in a print shop, around 1789 for example in France, the Mignone size can be a Fournier (how we call it now) when the Cicero can be a Garamond (how we call it now).

And if you cross the street, and went to another print shop, well, perhaps this one is more up to date, and their you will found for same sizes, some earlier cut of a Didot…

Rod, in short, I don't know the answer to your question.

But, the Didot family cut many typefaces (most of which were probably named by their size instead of their styles, as JFP said above). If you went to a Didot press to have your stuff printed, you got it printed in "their" typefaces. If you went somewhere else (esp. I guess, in Holland), you would get the typefaces that that printer used. I don't think that you could just walk into a printing office in the 1700s and say, "I really dig John Baskerville's types, can you print my book in that instead of Caslon's?"

Bodoni in Parma used his own typefaces as well, which appear similar (relatively) to Didot family typefaces. But Giambattista Bodoni's typefaces were not Didot typefaces, and Didot family typefaces were not made by G. Bodoni, even though later historians grouped them all under the heading "Didones." Confusing enough?

What we know as Bodoni, historically followed what the Didot Family did. From what I remember from that lecture (given by James Mosley) Giambattista Bodoni was hired in Parma to create something similar to what was happening in Paris at the time. Some people had left Paris but still wanted to feel a part of what was happening. Even then people were aware of what we call today, trends.
