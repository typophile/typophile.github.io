Hi Rene -

There’s no one way to successfully apply grunge effects to type. The best way to go about it is to experiment; this way you’re likely to come across a method that works well for you personally. Firstly, remember that effects like this are based on the idea that the type has been applied to a surface. This means that the only way you’re going to get the autheticity you want is by using images of REAL surfaces. If you want to create the impression that the type’s been painted onto a wall, go and take some quality images of walls (or source them from a royalty-free stock library) and create your own textures by manipulating contrast and saturation.

Textures have two primary uses in this instance:

  1. Adding to the shape of the text (e.g. for overspray). For this, I’d recommend desaturating the image completely, then increasing the contrast to create harder patterns and shapes. The less you increase the contrast, the softer and more textured the image will be. You can then play around with simple brushes, painting out areas that you wish to discard, dodging and burning, sharpening and softening. You can then apply layer styles to the images to either punch out areas of the text, or add details to the edges. It appears that, on Bruce’s album, the grunge elements are in separate layers to the text itself, and they have been given individual textures in various hues and transparencies.

  2. Lending texture to the shape itself. This should be done later, when you have the first step sorted. Try applying a layer mask to the texture you wish to apply to the text/grunge element and pasting the text/grunge elements into the mask.

Ultimately you just have to try things out and feel your way along. Tutorials are only so useful; time and work will produce the gold.

-NJ
