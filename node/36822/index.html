Here is a nice example of a mathematically perfect type area according to the golden section as used in old books:

http://www.sanskritweb.net/temporary/golden.jpg

Example: If the vertical height (or depth) of the type area on the right-hand page is measured from the first top-right word “autem” to the bottom-right catchword “mente”, and if the resulting measured height is divided by 1.618, you will get the mathematically exact line width. Contrariwise, if you measure the line-width of e.g. the first line on the left-hand type-area (i.e. “ptis … iudica-") and multiply the resulting measured width by 1.618, you will get the mathematically exact height (or depth) of type-area (from top-right “iudica-” down to bottom-right catchword “In-"). Note that the catchword is included into the type-area of old books.

Keep in mind that if you’re designing a book that will be perfect bound and won’t open flat, you’ll lose quite a bit of page space in the gutter. That has to be taken into account in designing a layout and grid. Also, in a book with a reasonable page count, the pages “feather” on the outside, making it look as though the outside margins are wider.
2007-09-06 16:32:03 • Gary Long

I was lucky enough to learn how to design books before the computer (and equally lucky to have the computer come along when it did!) and back in the day we actually drew up the grid and moved the elements around by hand before we settled on a look we liked. I learned to try to “see” the page before I started.

For text-only books it’s often driven in part by the cast-off. Once I choose my font and leading, then I do a cast-off (number of words in the document divided by page count) to determine how many words per page. Then I set up my margins in order to achieve that. Whether you give it more space on the top or on the sides is a matter of choice.

Art/illustrated books present more of a challenge. Some books call for (or have room for) a more open design, some call for a flexible design where you can accommodate lots of different page-layout options. I’m working right now on a photography book where the pictures are very formal and have a strong horizon (landscape photos). I chose to lay out the text pages with a low sink so that the page has a “horizon” as well. I want the text design to take a back seat to the pix so I’m keeping it really clean.

Sometimes you want a varied, dynamic, asymmetrical page. I’ve done cookbooks and interior design books that have sidebars, notes, different types of text, large and small pix where I would do a two or three column design that can work these various elements in.

But again, it helps to try to visualize the look of the book before you start or your head can start to spin with all the different options. I have never even considered the “golden section” in any of my book designs.

2007-09-06 17:27:27 • pattyfab

As Patty and others have indicated, the Golden Section is not always that useful for today’s books. I’ve never found the oft-cited formula, à la Tschichold et al., applicable. Publishers find those margins excessive and wasteful these days. Plus, there are too many other parameters either predetermined or otherwise constrained.

That said, I have on occasion made use of the 1.618 proportion. It’s pleasing, and when it works, great.

More important is the concept of Proportion in general (of which the Tschichold diagram is but one example). Deliberate and pleasing relationships between trim size, margins & text block, type size & leading, location of running heads & folios, etc. These are all important and work together to create a whole.

One important thing that you need to keep in mind is that a book is a three-dimensional thing. Gary mentions one aspect, which is that there is a very real consideration for the inside gutter depending upon binding method (and even different binderies). Whenever possible, take your sample pages, trim them up and stick them inside a dummy book. If you can’t get a blank bound dummy with the same dimensions, page length, and stock as your final will be, then try to find a published book with specs as close as you can, and stick your pages in that.

Something unique happens to a design when it curves into a binding and you perceive it in the context of a book’s dimensional form in space with thickness and volume. I frequently mock up several pages and put them into a dummy in sequence, so I can get a proper perception of pacing. This is particularly useful with the front matter, since pacing is most evident in the beginning sequence.

Hope this is useful.

– K.

2007-09-06 18:43:21 • pattyfab
When photos or maps span a spread in a perfect bound book, layout can be particularly tricky to avoid having part of the graphic hidden in the gutter. What I do in cases like this is create left and right “halves” of the graphic, actually more than halves, because I make some overlap in the middle, sufficient that when the book is bound, the graphic seems visually complete. It’s not a perfect adjustment, but if you get it right, it comes close to appearing a single uninterrupted image. As Kent mentions, nothing beats experimenting with a bound book (dummy or real), taking into account the location in the book (front, middle, end).
