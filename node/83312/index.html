2011-06-30 05:49:14 • apankrat

It’s the web designer’s responsibility to make sure that the fonts chosen for a web site work well in the site design, on different browsers.

Many typefaces originally designed for print, and properly hinted with alignment zones and stem width values, nonetheless look like crap on screen.

However, others only look like crap some of the time.

And there are others, where only certain weights look like crap, and other weights are just fine.

So web font distributors generally operate under the “caveat emptor” principle, giving their customers every option, but also giving them the ability to see how each font will be rendered in different environments.

The situation can be compared with print design, where it’s the typographer’s responsibility to, for instance, not use a hairline weight sans serif at small size.

As for the notion of “websafe” — this has nothing to do with how a site opens and loads. Don’t be scared to beat your own path, despite the extra effort required.

With great power comes great responsibility :-)

2011-07-04 01:25:17 • hrant

Staying away, for the moment, from some of the other assertions on this thread:

Nick Shinn said: “The situation can be compared with print design, where it’s the typographer’s responsibility to, for instance, not use a hairline weight sans serif at small size”

Uh, nope. On the web, in a browser onscreen, the size at which the font will be viewed is unpredictable and cannot be forced upon the reader by the typographer. The size will depend upon the size of the device’s screen, the screen resolution, the pixel depth, and the zoom level. All of which are variables that you can’t know beforehand.

It is a near-certainty that that hairline weight sans-serif will appear at a small size for some readers. It may be adjusted by them, it might not. They might find the look of that font when it first appears small - with the hairlines disappearing - enough of a turn-off to just click off and read something else. You can’t know. Restricting their use to what you think of as a “larger” size means nothing - it’s all relative.

This is a fundamental difference between fonts aimed at print and fonts aimed at the web. It’s more fruitful to think of a web font as being a collection of fonts designed to appear one way or the other at every pixel size setting. With web fonts, “point size” as it’s commonly thought of in print media, is a meaningless distinction.

What does it take to make a font look acceptable? (Look, the web-safe fonts don’t look all that great in GDI either, so some degradation in comparison to DirectWrite and perhaps Mac or Freetype rendering is unavoidable and perfectly acceptable, within limits.) Should it take an hour? Ten hours? Forty hours? What’s practical? What’s the state of the auto hinters that are available? Good, bad, what? An em unit up or down doesn’t mean much. But when you ask a separate program - an auto-hinter - to interpret the same points, an em unit up or down can mean a great deal.

Bottom line on this is: if there was a lucrative market in web fonts awaiting that those in the font business felt they could control to the extent that they do the market for print fonts, this problem would be solved already, guaranteed.
