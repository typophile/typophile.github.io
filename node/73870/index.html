Hi,

as its name implies Schulbuch/Schoolbook typefaces have been made particularly for school / educational books or are modified versions of existing typefaces – e.g. AG Schulbuch is a modified version of Akzidenz Grotesk Buch.

It’s no coincidence that this specific /I/-form reminds of Italics and/or handwriting.

In Germany, pupils were/are taught a standardised form script/handwriting (one of them is called Lateinische Ausgangsschrift, but there are several revisions). It was assumed that seizing the letter forms pupils were used to, e.g. the one storey /a/ and the /I/ like the one you mentioned would ease the recognition of particular letters and therefore improve the overall readability and legibility for reading rookies. Quite likely, the ‘abstract’ look of a Grotesk /I/ – a simple vertical line – was an issue, too.

The one-storey /a/ and /g/, the hooked lc /l/, the spurless G and the peculiar /I/ could also be considered an atavistic parallel to German blackletter letterforms. Even though blackletter wasn’t taught anymore in school at that time it was still way more present than it is today. And in blackletter (and therefore in Germany until WW2), the differentiation between /I/ and /J/ has not been popular till the late 19th century / early 20th century. This potential parallel might be a reason why a single-storey /a/ and /g/ and a hooked lc /l/ are still looked upon as being ‘German’ by some. Until then, it was most common to have one glyph for both /I/ and /J/ which either sits on the baseline or drops it, but always with some kind of crossbar and in the shape of what (nowadays) most people would read as a /J/.

And, being German, I doubt that a lot of people in Germany would misread this specific one as an /I/. In a custom font I made two years ago (a Geometric Grotesk), I used a /J/ with a crossbar similar to the /I/ in Schulbuch Süd, the /I/ being a simple vertical stroke. I didn’t hear of complaints about ambiguity or the danger of confusion so far…

I grew up in Germany, and I clearly remember that I was annoyed by the J-style I’s used in the children’s books by a particular publisher. I later understood the reason (which aarhaus explained very well), but I still dislike them. They were and are confusing, and they don’t help in learning to read at all, especially when you’re surrounded by Grotesk I’s in real life.*

My point, though, is that I’ve seen Schulbuch-I’s looking like J’s without a crossbar. In fact, I now think they sometimes used the J glyph from the font to “make our life easier” (how belittling – need we really be protected from the cold, mean world with all its scary Grotesk I’s?)

So, to answer your question: it doesn’t matter whether you give the J a crossbar or not. But please, everyone, leave the I as it is. Let’s work together to stop this “Schulbuch” madness :)

(btw, one could argue German doesn’t need the letter J at all …)

*I also don’t remember having difficulties deciphering double-story a’s and g’s.
