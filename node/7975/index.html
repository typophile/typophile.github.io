David - The only thing worse than a potential client who does not value the efforts of a professional graphic designer is a designer who doesn't appreciate the value of their own time and work. While I enjoy designing logos, and get a great deal of personal satisfaction from my work, I'm running a business and need to make a living. If I want to do some good for a nonprofit organization in whose cause I strongly believe I will produce some pro bono work and then promote the hell out of it. In many cases such organizations can't afford the services of a professional designer and will greatly appreciate what is done for them. There is tremendous value in working with nonprofits in this manner. The problem with the so-called "contests" for for-profit ventures is that the "client" will not appreciate the value of one's work when they are basically getting it for free. That is often what it is worth to them - nothing. These for-profit business are simply trying to avoid paying market value for the work of designers - and getting a lot of speculative designs in the process. They are basically taking advantage of naive designers. The value of my work is determined by 27 years of past experience, my talents and my skills. Potential clients need to base their decision to hire me on my portfolio of past work. They don't get to "test drive" me, by having me produce designs without a contract or deposit check, to see if what I do may fit their needs. Here's a great piece about speculative presentations. Design contests or spec work are a harmful practice Design contests amounts to providing design work for free. Many major private and public sector clients think it is good business practice to hold a design contest or require spec work as part of their search and selection process when recruiting the services of a graphic designer or design firm. In fact, requests for design contests or spec work: - are exploitative, as they demand design work for free and without guarantee of compensation, - offer no future potential, - can lead to copyright infringement, - devalue the profession of graphic design and lead to negative competitive practices, - threaten the integrity and work ethic of the graphic design profession, - are discouraged by allied professions such as the advertising industry. As a Professional Member of the Graphic Designers of Canada, I subscribe to our Society's Code of Ethics, which prohibits members from undertaking speculative (I design something; if you like mine, I get paid; if you like someone else's better, I don't get paid) design. I have found that people and organizations seeking quality design are sometimes not well- served by design competitions. A professional designer's job includes research and the establishment (with the client) of an effective marketing plan which should be the driving force behind any design. The risk with requests for speculative design can be that designs submitted might not be founded on sound marketing and long- term planning principles, and therefore not be a strong tool in the development of the organization. I'm not trying to be contentious, but… If you needed legal work done, would you ask a group of lawyers to submit work and the winner would get paid? Or dental work? Or film-making or copy- writing? If not, why is it appropriate for graphic design work? My guess is that it's because you have seen contests like this advertised before. But there are alternatives that are respectful of both a client's finances and a designer's work. I understand an organization's wish to achieve goals in the most economical way. All of my clients want their work done economically. If you are ever in need of design again, there are several ways that designers can be approached, solid working relationships established, and design goals reached, without the 'speculative work request' trap. I'd be happy to discuss this with a representative of your organization, should you want to know more.
