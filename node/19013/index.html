April 4, 2006 • George Horton

In 1516, at the impressive age of 66, Griffo made his last type for his own press in Bologna. This tiny italic, in which four line increments vertically occupy almost exactly 1 cm, has an enormous x-height and fat counters, making these 32º books surprisingly readable. Is it the first such type made? Does anyone know about other early experiments with monster x-height? George

2006-04-04 22:38:01 • Charles Leonard
No, Griffo didn’t use counterpunches. No counter is repeated perfectly in a font - even the counters on ligatured versions of letters are different from the standard forms, and the two halves of an m likewise. Part of Griffo’s interest is that, like the makers of Venetians, he believed in divergence, but his proportions and modelling are typographic rather than calligraphic - much more sophisticatedly so, indeed, than Garamond’s.

I’ve come to believe that there is: it’s much more harmonious with the “spirit” of the Latin lowercase as a whole; the conventional (closed) form is really too complex; especially when it’s doubled it can cause a distracting spike in the texture. 2006-05-07 16:17:05 • George Horton

A tangential note to George’s comment earlier in the thread about Griffo’s 1516 italic beating Pierre Haultin for the “niche-interest record” of making nonpareil-size type: Although Fred Smeijers writes in Counterpunch that Haultin was the first to make such small type, H.D.L. Vervliet in his 2000 paper “Printing Types of Pierre Haultin (ca. 1510-87). Part I: Roman Types” (Quaerendo 30/2) and in the revised version in The Paleotypography of the French Renaissance seems to indicate that his was not the first (unless by a hair) but was, more importantly, the first to be widely used.

“The Haultin Nonpareil roman and italic found their way into the main stock of the major European typefoundries. They were available until the end of the eighteenth century and, in that type size, for most printers the only choice on the market.” (From the 2000 paper, page 90)

“A Nonpareil type size was a rarity in early sixteenth-century typography and only owned by the better equiped printers. In France, I noted early uses by Estienne (1534) and Colines (1538). Haultin’s type is a bit smaller (though its capitals are bigger) than the second 42 mm Nonpareil which Robert I Estienne used from about 1543 on in his octavo bibles.” (page 97)
