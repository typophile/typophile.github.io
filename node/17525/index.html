Frutiger and similar typefaces in Bringhurst's classification system

January 25, 2006 • gabrielhl

In the type classif. system published by [[Robert Bringhurst|Bringhurst]] in [[elements_of_typographic_style|Elements]] and his article in Serif #1, "[[Grotesque]]" faces like [[Helvetica]], [[Univers]] and [[Akzidenz Grotesque|Akzidenz]] fall under "Realist Roman". It seems one of the characteristics of Realists is small aperture (don't know if that's the correct english term, sorry), but faces such [[Frutiger]] have a very large aperture, and while they are [[monoline]] [[sans serif|sans]] I feel they are different design concept than "grotesques". I'd like to know how other people feel about this. Are Helvetica and Frutiger to be in the same "package" (as has been in other type classification systems) or does the latter need another "compartment", along with other similarly open/legible/signage-oriented sans?
Stanley Hess, in his form-based classification system (The Modification of Letterforms, 1971), describes faces such as Frutiger as having "unfolded" curves -- this is the opposite of having a small aperture, and is defined in relation to stroke shape, not the counter or negative space. By his terminology, Futura and Gill Sans are in the same "unfolded" category as Frutiger. And yet, the unfoldedness in Frutiger seems more to do with having a compact letter form, and this design rationale continues in the Meta genre of semi-condensed, 4-letter name, sans serifs. So I think that we/Robert Bringhurst need a new "crossover" term to describe this contemporary type genre. As Bringhurst's method is to ally type genres with dominant art-historical movements of the type's era, "Post-modernist" would seem to be the obvious choice. The main problem with that is we tend to think of grunge and deconstructed typefaces as being the quintessentially Post-modern forms. Will the true PoMo please step forward?!

So it would be hard to categorize types as prosaic as Frutiger as Postmodern.
