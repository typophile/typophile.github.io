Tiffany –

I'm still trying to get my thoughts into bullet points. Here's what I have so far:

Education (a): I love the plaintive cries of the people sued by the RIAA:"I paid for Kazaa, how can using it be illegal?" Do people actually understand that "sharing" fonts is illegal?

Education (b): If most people can't (or don't bother to) make their tenses agree in their writing, how can they be expected to care about proper typography? "What's wrong with Ariel?" Getting people to care about the essential role of good typography in written communication is necessary before you can get them to care about the value (therefor ownership) of fonts.

Carrot: It'd be really nice if people would stop stealing fonts, but what do we offer as an incentive? Warm fuzzies are great in Church or around a camp-fire, but I don't know that you can use them to motivate people in the real world. Teaching ethics is essential, but I also think there needs to be a tangible reward for complying w/ IP laws (i.e. actually purchasing fonts vs. "borrowing" them).

Stick: Let's face it, if you know you aren't going to get caught, are you going to steal candy from the unguarded candy jar? Especially if it's a magic candy jar, that automatically re-fills each time a piece of candy is removed? I think the RIAA went way over the top … but if they hadn't gone that far, would they have gotten so much ink? There needs to be some realistic possibility of getting caught if IP laws are going to have any effect.

-/-/-

I keep going back to Bringhurst's comment in his AtypI keynote speech:"…software in its natural state is invisible." The task is to convince font users that something invisible has value. If something has no value, how can it be stolen?

[group] "Hi, Tiffany"

The reason I feel that there needs to be a carrot is that the problem is so pandemic. If you find someone who has fallen down the stairs, your first action is to stop the bleeding. Then you teach them how to hold on to the hand-rail. Reversing the sequence won't work.

If we don't give people a reason to stop stealing fonts, they won't stop. If the only reason to stop is a negative ("If you don't stop we will sue you"), it might stop a few people, but it will also engender a lot of ill-will and drive the practice underground. (Something I wonder about, but have no info: how many people have read the news articles about the actions of the RIAA and said:"Kazaa? What's that? Cool! Where can I find it?")

Stopping the bleeding won't be easy: there are too many fonts out there, and they can't be recalled or disabled. Most students, and most start-up designers, exist on a bare-bones budget. Encouraging them to spend money for fonts ("Hmmm, Meta or Beer? Wow, that's a really tough choice!") is going to be very difficult.

The carrot doesn't have to be expensive. This is not a fully thought out action-plan, but here's a scenario: Provide design teachers w/ a CD full of exploded fonts (discussed above). Enclose on the CD bios of the font designers, descriptions of the fonts & suggestions for proper usage, & a discount coupon for unexploded versions of the font. Font designers get visibility (for marketing purposes, and to put a face on the victim of font piracy); students get new, interesting fonts to work with, teachers get tools & opportunity to talk about piracy & why it's a Bad Thing.
