There are no laws regarding what does and does not go on a CD cover in the USA. In fact, unless the corporation has some specific policies regarding what must go on a cover (and many record companies don’t) the designer can just not put anything extra on it, and anything else can be placed on the outer wrapper as a sticker. A great example is Prince’s “black” album, which had nothing more than black paper inserts. Tool’s last two album covers also went this route.

ISBNs are specifically for books to be published (this can include electronic versions such as CD-ROMS and on-line) and software.

http://www.isbn-international.org/en/index.html

Music (and recorded sound) CDs are treated differently, they use the ISRC system.

http://www.ifpi.org/content/section_resources/isrc.html

There are copyright issues to consider with both even an in-company product. Barcodes can perform tasks other than carrying the ISBN, they might be used for stock control or searches.

There are some national variations in requirements.

A bookstore will not sell any product without an ISBN number. If your client wants the product sold in a bookstore it must have an ISBN.

A friend of mine has a company that designs and sells reading lights and magnifiers. The bookstores never sold batteries, because of the lack of ISBN. So he packaged a line of batteries with an ISBN number on them and now he sells the batteries next to the lamps and magnifiers.
