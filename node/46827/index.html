Sort of.

When upgrading to CS3 (I had CS and Macromedia’s MX 2004 Suite) I ordered a stand-alone upgrade for Fireworks, under the mistaken assumption that it would upgrade my Macromedia MX 2004 Suite version of Fireworks. The website and documentation I saw didn’t suggest there would be any issues. After installing everything and proving that CS3 Design worked as advertised but Fireworks didn’t, I called Adobe to find out why my Fireworks CS3 wasn’t working.

Turns out that the Fireworks CS3 upgrade only updates a stand-alone Fireworks license, not the MX 2004 suite license. Adobe said that I would need to purchase a new, full version of Fireworks. I didn’t like that and I really didn’t “need” Fireworks, so instead I went through the “Adobe refunds me while I destroy my Fireworks CS 3 copy” process. I discovered soon thereafter that several other people were effected by the same lack of information on the Adobe User-to-User forums. And shortly after that, Adobe put up additional information on the website to clarify that you couldn’t upgrade to Fireworks CS3 using a Macromedia suite license. And they eventually did a special deal where if you bought the CS3 upgrade they would reduce the price on the full version of Fireworks.

In Adobe’s favor, the service reps were very apologetic and professional. But the thing that still bugs me is that they didn’t think creatively about how to solve my problem (I wanted an upgrade, not to buy a full new version!) and they could have completely avoided the entire issue if they had the right information online!

And I don’t want to even get started on the Adobe Acrobat “Yahoo Search” tool and FedEx Kinkos “print” button. Proof that marketing is taking over the engineers at Adobe…
