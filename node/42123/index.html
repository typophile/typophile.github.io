2008-02-15 16:49:57 • Bert Vanderveen

You could try the codebreakers at the NSA. No, seriously – [[http://www.nsa.gov/public/publi00004.cfm|this NSA website]] lists a number of declassified printed documents you might be able to request regarding letter or digraph frequencies in Russian, French, Polish, and Japanese.

[[http://codepad.clanhosts.com/index.php?art=frq|This site]] has a letter frequency list for English, French, German, and Spanish, and [[http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Letter_frequencies|Wikipedia]] adds Esperanto. And Appendix A of Army field manual 34-40-2, Basic Cryptanalysis, has a list of English digraph frequencies. The whole manual is available as a zipped collection of PDFs [[http://www.und.nodak.edu/org/crypto/crypto/army.field.manual/fm34402.zip|here]].

(Later edit: I also found [[http://www.nsa.gov/public/Mil_Crypt_I.pdf|this NSA document]] (PDF), an introduction to cryptanalysis, that has some interesting information on pages 11 through 17. Unfortunately, it’s a book that was scanned into PDF, and dates back to 1938, so it’s not likely to be Unicode friendly – but it does have a claim to being authoritative.)

2008-02-16 05:35:47 • Linda Cunningham
I have done some extensive analysis in this field. The texts generated by [[http://justanotherfoundry.com/tools/generator|my test text generator]] are synthesized on the basis of triplets frequency lists, which I obtained by a very thorough analysis of texts. For example, for English, as an input I used several texts, 5 literature, 5 scientific and 2 economic, each 5-10 MB in size. In the end, I did not take the arithmetic average but something similar to the median so as to make sure subject-specific key words in a certain text do not spoil the overall result. As you can see, I have data for 22 languages but some of them are only based on 4-5 texts. I could convert my frequency lists to character frequencies or pair frequencies if there is a general interest. Btw, why were you interested in the first place, Paul? What would you use them for?
