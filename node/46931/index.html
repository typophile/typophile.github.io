Help me refine this logo (for high end caterer).

June 30, 2008 • showandtell

I’ve gone through dozens of concepts and after presentation this is the one that came out on top for the client – almost unanimously.

The client is a high end caterer that mostly caters high-end weddings held in private and public mansions. They wanted to upgrade their identity to reflect the perfect, polished, and remarkable events they cater. They have a lot of name recognition in their geographic area and are also very proud of the personal attention that each event gets from their staff.

I’m happy with the main “Russell Morin” type – I slightly modified a very beautiful typeface and added some elements of the owners signature into it. Though, if you have comments on that, I’d love to hear them (I’m certainly not a type expert). I’m having the most trouble with the treatment/typeface/size of the “fine catering” tagline. I just can’t get it to seem to work in a sophisticated enough fashion. Please help me out!

Thanks a million!

[img:sites/default/files/old-images/RM_logo_6140.jpg]

The signature script is nice. However, the R seems a little out of place. Alone, the R doesn’t resemble an R, you might want to play with the curve from the top of the R to the bottom of the leg as it extends under the u.

For the tag… I’m assuming you’re using Copperplate Gothic, which is a nice choice. I’d move the tag below the signature, centered and shrink the size to almost half the width of the signature. The space between the signature and the tag should be equal to about 1.5 the tag’s size. (So, if the tag was 12 points, the space between the tag and the signature would be 18 points.) Centering it, will also give the signature a nice balancing point.

The color seems very light. If you were to convert that to grey scale, it would probably be equal to 10-15% black, that’s too light. Consider bumping up the saturation.

It has a nice look to it, it’s really at the tweak stage. I’d be interested to see your other iterations before they picked this one.

While the R might be a bit too strong I believe the effort holds together reasonably well. We tend to overwork - logos especially - when it really is not overtly called for. Call it graphic narcissism. That said, a few tweaks and IMHO I think you are there. (I am basing my comments on the original).

As for the rule it should be a hairline or just a bit heavier adding to the classy effect you seek.
