Reprinting ATF specimen pages, copyright, and fair use

October 1, 2008 • blank

I’m trying to figure out some details regarding getting permission to an image of a page from the 1934 ATF specimen in a revival of a typeface. First, is there even anyone to get permission from? Second, has anyone ever checked to see if ATF even bothered to renew the copyrights for it’s books? Last, I feel that if I reprint a single page for the purpose of comparing letters on it with a revival I’m covered under fair use. Does anyone disagree?

I think this is a perfect case of fair use. You’ll be copying one page, the copying will not affect the sales of the remnants of ATF. If this is a noncommercial, educational work, it’s even better.

Go [[http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fair_use|here]] for details.

Considering how unpredictable the courts are in judging cases of copyright infringement, many publishers ignore the fair use option and just don’t print things for which permission was not granted even if they perfectly meet fair use criteria.

For works created before Jan 1978, the maximum duration for U.S. copyright protection is 75 years from date of publication, assuming all renewals were filed in a timely fashion.

So, for the 1934 ATF specimen, if you wait a couple more months to publish your comparison, you’re home free. Fair use or not. ;-)

The copyright on the 1934 work is long expired and cannot be renewed.

When ATF was auctioned off, the name and all rights associated with it went up for bid. Despite all the tear shedding one reads about this auction, it is my understanding that NO ONE bid on the rights. Kingsley’s digital font realm dissolved some time before the auction of the physical plant itself. There may be trademark rights etc still valid for licensing purposes but you are free and clear to reproduce whatever you want from the 1934 specimen catalog. My god, the BB&S catalog is completely digitized and available on ebay for $25.
