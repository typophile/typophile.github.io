2011-06-25 17:06:12 • JamesM

An easy way to separate the amateurs from the professionals* is to gauge their reaction to criticism. You admit you are amateur, so I don’t think we can fault you for that reaction to James' comment. But note that it wasn’t particular mean. Just blunt. As professionals, most of us** much prefer honest and blunt critique over sugar coated half-assed praises disguised as critique.

So, point being, welcome to your first lesson on receiving critiques: Don’t take them personally…it’s a comment on the work, not you as a person.

Also, IMHO, I find the best design solution isn’t always apparent in a vacuum. Often just one little idea from a critique can take the project into a whole other (and better) direction.

That out of the way, I have to second JamesM…no need to post 36 copies of one logo. Post one, and let us focus on that.

To offer some hopefully useful feedback, the first thing that jumps out at me on all your logos is the double-outline. It’s very jarring and adds nothing but complexity to the forms. I suggest as a start that you strip out all of the outlines.

Secondly, explore a bit more. Do some sketching. Play with some type. A big part of logo design is brainstorming ideas and exploring them to see where they take you. Figure for every hour of drawing the logo there are 5-10 hours of sketches and brainstorms.

  • Granted, in this industry, even some of our celebrity designers can be thin skinned Prima Donnas.

** OK, maybe I’m wishful thinking there. I hope most of us, as professionals, prefer the honest, even if blunt, critiques over the “well, that’s pretty good…” fluff.
