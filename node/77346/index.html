Scanning damaged, dirty or otherwise poor quality text and line art.

December 18, 2010 • Galane

A simple trick I resurrected from Graphic Arts class I took a couple decades (circa 1984-85) or so ago to reconstruct the partially obscured Indian head on the Porto-Power logo. ‘Course back then it was a big process camera, lenses, photo paper etc. instead of a scanner and computer, but the Sharpie’s still the same. ;)

I scanned the original at 600 DPI and cropped it down to just the head. Next I printed it on a US Letter sheet, filling most of the page. Since my Mark One Eyeball could see most of it, where no Photoshop tool could pick it out of the dirt and other noise, I used a black Sharpie to color in all the black outline. The final step was scanning that page at 600 DPI, making a much larger image that was relatively easy to magic wand.

I set the tolerance as high as it could go and just get what I wanted. Even so I had to expand then contract the selection by 1 pixel to pinch out all the little holes PS was calling the same shade of black as the brown/grey crud. Minor touch-ups were needed all around but a whole lot of labor and time was saved and it looks great when scaled down to the size of the original scanned as 600 DPI.

The same trick should work well on any other text, line art or solid graphics, or even stuff that’s not solid but you need to create a shape or mask from an image that’s too noisy/busy or doesn’t have enough contrast.
