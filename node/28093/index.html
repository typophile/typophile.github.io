Client "legibility" advice

September 7, 2006 • basicframework

I'm looking for some advice for dealing with a difficult client! There's this dry brochure we're doing that contains tons and tons of text. In an effort to mix things up, we've done some funky titles, using the three or four words of the title at different sizes. These are short and very obvious titles. (I've attached one.) Anyway, one of staff for the client always cites "legibility" concerns (mostly when I think he really just doesn't like something). And in a presentation, I'm suddenly left facing a group of non-design-savvy people who respond, "oh, do you think this will be legible?" I guess I feel like any design suggestions I'm making are getting trumped by this legibility card. (In one example, the block of very bold text was crossed by a light grey hairline. It was incredibly clear what the text said, but there it was again -- "do you think that line over the text makes it hard to read? Is it legible?" Honestly...!) Basically, if the text isn't level, the same size, or being read directly from upper left to lower right, I can be certain that someone will cite "legibility" concerns. I'd rather not throw in the towel and just create a boring, conservative piece, because it's not right for the overall brand. Any suggestions on how I can counter-argue the legibility stonewall? I've considered pulling out my old David Carson books and saying "No, THIS is hard to read. What I' doing is easy..." Help!
If someone asks 'is it legible' ask them 'can you read it?'

2006-09-07 19:08:17 • poms
A better way of phrasing the question back is, "Which words can't you read?" The responsent will typically read the words aloud making it clear that he read them just fine. A better tact is showing them the bottom line advantage in better product recognition or some sales advantage that comes with your treatment of the text. The truth is, you just may have a client who is too conservative for anything but the most basic design. Often times, it is not worth the extra time it takes to show numerous revisions to an unwilling client. Some people just like their Pizza plain. ChrisL

2006-09-10 19:55:37 • greengeorge
If you use hackneyed typefaces you will run into this kind of trouble. In order to put the stamp of creativity onto the work, you are obliged to come up with some fancy treatment - dropshadow being the most egregious, but here it's a Patrick Mitchell 1998 (Fast Company) thing -- this is where the designer adds value. An alternative would be to put the interest in a more discriminating choice of typeface (after all, there are 100,000 available) perhaps even, dare it be said, something contemporary? If Futura is the mandated corporate face, that's sad. It was, as Herr Renner noted, the typeface of his time. But perhaps 2007 spring shoes are gonna be retro 1927... sometimes for me,text that is a bit quirky to read makes it that much more interesting to try and read and in the end, it packs extra punch to leave an impression. That's a great argument. Legibility is more a text thing, but for heads, which are more left brain than right, you want to create just enough "difficultas" to catch the readers' attention, slow them down, and interest them in the content.
