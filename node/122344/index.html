There aren’t single words for most of those things. Most of the words you found are used, but have slightly different meanings depending on the context in which they’re used and the preference of the person or business using them. In English these kinds of industry-specific terms that vary from place to place are generally referred to as jargon.

In general, I would call a hand-written sketch a sketch, a rough or a rough sketch.

I’ve never heard a term for a partially finished InDesign file, and I would just call it, unimaginatively enough, a partially finished InDesign file. If it is a file with the basic page layout that serves as a starting point to build the pages of a magazine, newspaper or other publication, it would typically be called a template. A template might contain all the standard basics associated with that publication, like page numbers, sample headlines, sample table of contents, etc., that would then be modified by the layout artist/designer as needed.

At the newspaper where I worked as the design director, the processing of composing the finished text on pages was called pagination. This is not a universal term, however, and the process is referred to in different ways at different publications. I can think of no widely used term that means exactly that. Instead, the processes is a series of steps — each of which might have different terms in different places and contexts.

A finished, high-resolution PDF that’s been prepared for commercial printing is usually referred to as a press-ready PDF.

As I mentioned these terms are quite fluid in the sense that other terms are often used by other people in different situations.

Of the online definitions you found, some of them mean very precise things in specific situations. Several of the terms are outdated in the sense that they refer to processes and techniques that are no longer used. If you’re interested, I’d be happy to give you an explanation for any of those terms and whether or not they’re appropriate for what you have in mind.

Some of these processes and work flows are new enough and vary enough from one place or business to another than no widely accepted words exist to describe them. There are also lots of terms that are left over from pre-digital publishing days that have been borrowed, but are no longer entirely accurate.

A typical situation might be something like the following:

A designer or art director does a series of roughs, which enables him to quickly explore a number of ideas. The designer might then develop mockups or comps of these ideas to show clients or employers. If the client or employer suggests changes, a set of revisions are made. If the client accepts the revisions, the project moves into actual layout and production, which varies greatly depending on the project and the established workflows at the agency or publishing house.

At a magazine, for example, an art director might oversee the design and production of the publication. This would involve a coordinated effort between the editors and the art director. The art director might develop a basic layout of the magazine based on the general style of the magazine and using the pre-prepared templates. Stories and advertisements would be flowed into position to see how everything fits.

At this point, the magazine moves into production where a production or layout artist puts it all together into what would, in pre-digital times, have been called a mechanical that consisted of paste-up on mechanical boards and cut film overlays. However, this has all been replaced by digital or desktop publishing where the entire process is done on computers.

Depending on the situation a senior designer might design and lay out the more important pages while a production artist is tasked with making everything fit. The art director or a senior designer might personally design some of the more important feature layouts and the cover. Again, these more important pages might originate as rough sketches and proceed through the comp approval process before production begins.

Once the layout and production of the magazine is completed, it goes through a series of proofs, where it’s meticulously reviewed by the editors, publisher and art director. When finished, a press-ready PDF might be made to send to the printer. The prepress department at the printer oversees whatever needs to be done to get the files to RIP (processed through the image or plate setting machine) where negatives or printing plates are produced. These prepress tasks might include fixing problems with the files, determining imposition, etc. Using this same RIP, the publisher will produce a final proof, which the client must sign before the job goes to press.

Anyway, there are endless variations of this typical workflow that I’ve described. I’ve used a lot of the jargon that might commonly be used to describe these processes, so I hope that helps. A lot of the times, though, there isn’t an exact or universally understood word, so the relevant step in the process is simply described in a sentence or two instead of being named.

nikve wrote: “I would like to know about the verbs to compose and to impose."

I can see why you’re confused by these two words. They have very different meanings and refer to completely different things.

First, compose has many different meanings both in print production and in everyday life. In general, it means assembling something into an ideal configuration.

In print production, however, its something of an antiquated term describing a task that, for the most part, no longer exists. Composing referred to the task of assembling the metal type into words, sentences and entire pages.

Depending on the technology involved, the compositor (the person assigned to with the task of composing) would have been the person working directly with individual metal letters or the person running the machines that composed the metal strips of type for the printing press. Of course this process has been replaced by more modern methods in commercial printing, so the term “compose” no longer has the specific meaning in printing that it once did. Today, it’s word that might be best avoided in print production since its meaning is either obsolete or potentially confusing or confused with other processes.

Second, impose has a very different meaning. In printing imposing or imposition refers to the process by which pages are arranged on a sheet of paper that is about to be printed. When printing publications, the pages are never printed one at a time. Instead, multiple pages are printed on big sheets or webs of paper. These printed sheets of paper are then folded and trimmed. Imposition refers to the process of determining exactly how the pages need to be positioned and arranged on the sheets of paper to ensure that they will all be in the right order when the big sheet of paper is folded and trimmed to it’s final size. A folded and trimmed sheet of paper made up of many different pages is called a signature. In a book, for example, signatures consisting of multiple pages are bound together to create the book. Imposition isn’t something the designer or production artist needs to figure out. Instead it’s handled by the printer. The designer does, however, need to be aware of the process and produce artwork that takes into consideration the number of pages in the signatures. In a magazine, for example, the total number of pages in the magazine ideally corresponds to the most efficient number of pages in each signature.

It’s a difficult program to learn, and I’d strongly recommend taking a class if you can. And don’t forget there’s more than type. You need to learn how to set up pages, place photos, run type around photos, handle colors, and many other things.

paginating the text

If you’re referring to putting the pages in the proper order for printing, a professional print shop will usually do that for you. It’s part of the pre-press process as they prepare the file for printing. You’d prepare it as “reader spreads”, which means it looks the way it’ll look when printed and assembled. The printer will re-arrange the pages. (If you’re using a print shop for the first time, ask to make sure they’ll do this for you.)
