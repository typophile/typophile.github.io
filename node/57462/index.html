Alright, so I thought this might be an interesting place to ask this question, and im sure the answers will very but what the hell. I'm a filmmaker and type-nut (though im not where as good as most of you). Since I'm such a nerd I always like to make sure my type is historically accurate, at least as of late. Problem is it's tough to know exactly what are the best fonts to use from each time period. So I thought why not create a short list of the quintessential font(s) of the last few decades? That way if someone wants to make sure their type is historically accurate but doesn't want to do a tonne of research they can quickly glance at the list and use one of the suggestions. So what would you say? What are the quintessential fonts of certain decades? Obviously this is a matter of opinion and no easy question but it still might be fun to get a ballpark idea. Also we're not necessarily looking for fonts that were simply DESIGNED during that decade, but ones that were around and preferably in use/fairly common for the era. So while Helvetica was around in the 50's, it didn't really make its big breakout until the late 60s/Early 70s (if my memory of "Helvetica" the documentary is correct... which it probably isn't). That means we'd probably list it in 60s/70s rather than 50s. I dont know, maybe this is only something myself and Mark Simonson give a crap about. All I know is these titles for this pseudo documentary that takes place in 2006 but tells this history of a school dating back to 1956 is FRUSTRATING. I dont know which way to go... argh.
ummmm... This is a big and complicated question. It might help if you narrowed your search down to a certain decade or period, like the 60s or 90s. It's very difficult to define a decade by a typeface. Helvetica probably fits everything from the 60s until today. Then there are typefaces that fit to a certain art/music history period (like art-deco, pop art, psychedelic music) and will mostly be used only when referring to this specific period. As I'm a kid of the 80s and 90s, it's pretty hard to judge anything before that time, so I'll stick to what I know (well, sort of): 'Typefaces' of the 80's (in no particular order) - LCD - Squared typefaces (like Machine) - [[http://www.racketboy.com/images/asteroids-box.jpg|Rounded Atari typeface]] - [[http://www.mantripping.com/images/stories/back-to-the-future.jpg|Back to the Future]] - Helveticanisms for everything serious and grown up... ;-) 'Typefaces' of the 90's (in no particular order) - Comic Sans/Arial - Impact - Pixel typefaces - Techno typefaces - Everything stretched and bent, blurred and embossed beyond recognition cheerio Queneau

Hi Adam... I've found that it is much easier to point out when a typeface is used anachronistically in a film than it is to say what would be appropriate. That said, I would suggest doing some research at a library or somewhere you can look at magazines, advertising or books from the period in question. Another good resource for this kind of thing is the Taschen "All-American Ads" book series (assuming you're looking for North American examples). Once you've got some examples of period typography that you like or would like to emulate, it's just a matter of identifying the typefaces. If you need help identifying typefaces, resources like Typophile's Type ID Board can come be handy.

For set decoration and costumes, you might use an old Eatons or Simpsons catalog. So for type, look to ads in Macleans or Chatelaine from the appropriate era, or Art Directors Club annuals. But if your film is more niche, check out niche publications. Also have a strategy for temporal span: not every car on the street in any given year is a late model, but you have to decide how aggressive the mise-en-scène is in locating the action at a particular point in time.
