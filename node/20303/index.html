They frame their numbers by put little serifs on top and bottom. Is that to signify that they are numbers and not alphabet?

Yes, although not all the numbers are directly based on syllables (the writing system is syllabic, not alphabetic). In manuscript, numbers are traditionally written with a line above and below the full number, not the little disconnected strokes above and below individual numbers as shown in these photos. This has not been possible to replicate in typography, until now…

In the Nyala Ethiopic font I made for Microsoft, I use OpenType contextual substitutions to create a connecting line above and below numbers.

All the numbers include “IbP” even the 1706, does that make sense?

Presuming the European numbers are years in the Gregorian calendar, I suspect the Ethiopic numbers record corresponding dates in one of the Ethiopic calendars, so there is not a direct correspondence between the two sets of numbers.

The b-like shape ፮ is the digit 6. The I-like ፲ is the number 10. The P-like ፻ is a partitioning character indicates that the two numbers to the left of it are hundreds, so this sequence ፲፮፻ indicates 1600.

I read the Ethiopic numbers above 1682-1706 as ‘1674-1698’.

I read the Ethiopic numbers above 1632-1667 as ‘16?4-1660’ (the number in the tens position might be 20, but it seems to be missing a stroke).
