"Schoolbook" Alternates

April 19, 2006 • paul d hunt

I’m curious to find out what other typophiles think of Bembo Schoolbook with it’s altered a, g & y. I know that some people believe that these letterforms are easier for children to read. I am not among that number. But is there any good reason to make these alternates available? are there better-looking versions that what we see in Bembo Schoolbook?
I wonder if any writing teachers start their pupils out with binocular “g"s. Surely the single storey g and q are too similar – a fact attested by the addition of a check-mark to the q, which has to be later unlearned, a pedagogic kludge if ever there was one. What can be so hard about drawing two circles and connecting them with a short line?

BTW, I write single-storey ‘g’s, (surely they’re easier for cursive writing, and therefore for speed), doesn’t mean you have to abandon the double storey ‘g’ for typefaces derived from designs created in the metal days…

Having said that, we were all taught to write those single-storey ‘a’s as well, and at about the age of twelve I spontaneously switched to doing double-storey ‘a’s just because I felt they looked better. I still know they’re slower for me to write than single-storey ones would be but what the heck it’s a habit now…

2006-04-19 17:03:44 • paul d hunt

I think such “primary” glyphs are useful (although certainly not in such a bookish face as Bembo) but only for absolute beginner readers (and only because they form the -sad- standard of current alphabet instruction). Once they’ve been learned, and the child (or foreigner) can read them quickly, they should be replaced by full print forms.

the addition of a check-mark to the q, which has to be later unlearned

Well, I wish it didn’t. (And does it really? I’d say it depends on us a bit.) To me the best “q” for reading is the one with a single-sided bottom serif.

these alternate glyphs give a juvenile feel to the design.

Which however is lost on the target readership, so that’s not so bad.
