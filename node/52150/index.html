2008-12-01 18:15:02 • Nick Shinn
Usages of Comic Sans abound but what is your thesis statement? You could gather some anecdotal information of funny or hideous uses of the typeface (tee hee, look at this example kind of stuff,) but what are you trying to define? Finding examples will only ratify its usage in almost any context you want to refer to. Some of its existence and prolific usage is because; It is owned by Microsoft, It is a default typeface on a couple of Microsoft programs, It is readable at small sizes for dialog on computer screens, It is free. On the other hand, why is a typeface like Coop Forged by House Industries [[http://www.houseind.com/index.php?page=showfont&id=12&subpage=viewfontspecimens|view here,]] used so little? It is similar as maybe a dozen other comic-looking fonts. The argument my boil down to…it's not a default, not common and not free? A defined thesis statement will focus your intention, or what you are really trying to discover.

First of all, thanks for all the reactions. second, I shall explain the sentence quoted a couple of times above: 'offcourse i've never used comic sans'. I said that as a graphic design student who never dared to use it because it's such a difficult typeface to use in an art-environment. I can understand that comic sans is appropriate for applied graphic design, for example a hairdresser's shop (i picked this one because i've seen one with CS in the logo). At school during practice, it's easier to use typefaces that are allready proven to be good ones like Didot, Futura, Baskerville, Garamond, Univers, all the big names in typeworld. I think the only way to use comic sans (and in a design approved by my teachers :)) is in a design that refers to the commercial world and uses it's elements but without just copying them. There has to be an additional value. And layered meanings. Irony is not necessarily a part of it. This way of adopting elements of the 'low culture' can also be seen with other artists: Arne Sierens (http://www.arnesierens.be/)is a playwright who's stories are situated in for example fastfood restaurants, but his plays are considdered art because of the layers and the additional value. Also designer Walter Van Beirendonck (http://www.waltervanbeirendonck.com/) adopts elements of low culture and gives them a new meaning. It's in this way i would like to find some famous graphic designers who used comic sans, but i haven't found one yet. Maybe at the end of this year i myself can design something good with comic sans. And about my thesis statement: i'm still just researching and i'm following different paths at the same time. But i think i will mostly investigate the social aspects of the type: which people use it and for which purposes? which are the motives of the persons who use it? the things that are said by skinny_tires are indeed some of the important reasons of the popularity of comic sans, but even if we just look at the fonts delivered with every computer, comic sans is still the most popular. Is it, as some organisations who researched dyslectia say, the best readable type? is it good for children who are just learning to read? I hope you all get a nicer image of me and my thesis :) I haven't got the intention of turning my thesis in a freakshow to tell everyone how bad and ugly comic sans is. I want to investigate comic sans properly and if the solution is that comic sans a bad font is, then be it. But up to now, 'i believe in comic sans' (this is also my working titel of my masterproject)
