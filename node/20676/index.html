Mark Simonson created one of the most popular handwriting fonts ever made: Felt Tip Roman. It's a close digitization of his own writing. He told me once he knew he had arrived as a type designer when he received one of those direct mail pieces in which they try to fake real handwriting to make it seem more personal. They used Felt Tip Roman. It was like getting a postcard from himself.

You believe Benguiat? Okay (well, Ivan Chermayeff used to do the same thing). And I think you've all already heard the Chase logo story--I posted it some years back. Okay, I've got two I love (sorry so long): 1. In the pre-desktop days, I used to give Photolettering a lot of business and Sid Haber would take me to lunch sometimes. One time, he told me that he started as a young man as a linotyper, and one night, he had a blind date. It was love at first sight! He knew he was going to marry this girl. He said he was so excited, that he had to set some type. So he took her up to the shop and and set "I love you," and put it in her hand. And it burned her! And they got married and she still had the scar on her hand. 2. Right out of school, I had the great privilege of interviewing with Herb Lubalin to be his assistant on U&lc. Well, it turned out that I interviewed him. I spent all day with him and he showed me and reminisced about all his favorite work. When we got to the PBS logo (the "P" as a head), he was infurated . . . he said that PBS used it for a while, everything was fine and then they decided it wasn't good that the face was facing LEFT. So they gave it to Chermayeff & Geismar to rework and Lubalin said they just basically stole his logo and just turned it around. Well, 20 years later, I'm at Chermayeff & Geismar and told Tom Geismar the story and Tom loved it and said Herb was crazy and PBS approached him to rework the logo to show they were a corporation of many public stations--not just one. And it worked better facing right. I believe them both. (p.s. I got the job! But Lubalin died a week later ;( )

There's this story about Alexandre Wollner visiting Herb Lubalin in New York. He says:" H.Lubalin had this three-floor office in N.Y. The main room was as big as Alexandre Wollner's house with tables covered with sweets and chocolates. Wollner asked him what the room was for and how he managed all the luxury when he (Wollner) back in Sao Paulo couln't even dream of having such an adequate room. He replied:" I'm going to tell you a secret: on the first floor there's a room full of people working, but they only do retail down there, which I don't show to anyone and which doesn't appear in any book. We make a lot of money there. On the 2nd floor we produce pubblications of a slightly higher level:some adverts, but mostly magazine page spreads, medical pamphletes, and these make reasonable money. I don't publish this stuff anywhere either. Then there's is this room here where I do the things you see; the Avant garde alphabet, Eros magazine etc. These don't bring in a dime, they just cost money. It's the 1st floor that sustains the other 2. I don't know if this is the sort of anecdotes you are looking for but I've found it cool because now, when I visit some super high profile studio (not that I do this a lot...) I always think about the secret floors. The story is from Alexandre Wollner published by CosacNaify in Brazil (a project by Andre Stolarski)

Enjoying these anecdotes, and thought to add one in keeping with the general tone of the responses. Recently I finished reading a very well written book, "Book Typography" by Ari Rafaeli. There are many historical examples of types attributed to the wrong original designer. Much of the drama of early type is determining who did what. My favorite from the book is the story of Monotype Ehrhardt. Seems the original designer, a Nicholas Kis from the Holland, left town and ended up in Poland (c1680). He tried to sell his matrices to Edling Type Foundry. Edling thought Kis' work amaturish and declined to purchase the matrices. Kis left the matrices with Edling while he scoured the area for a buyer. Unfortunately for Kis, he was arrested by the king. He was later released on the condition he promise to never bring invading armies into Poland again. Seems the King blamed Kis for his recent political troubles. Kis left town and abandoned the matrices. They stayed with the Edling Foundry, passed on as companies were sold and acquired. Ultimately the matrices ended up with Stempel who issued a version from the original. The kicker for Kis is they attributed the design to Janson (Edling's mentor/master) who was not there when Kis first arrived at Edling's Foundry. Cheers! LTL, Xtian
