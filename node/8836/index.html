Casey -

I think some of the tourism related organizations have done a good job of using fonts that convey a sense of the Pacific Northwest mystique:

http://www.traveloregon.com/index.cfm

The Oregon tourism ads have been successful nationwide in promoting the state's image. They are posted here in PDF form:

http://otc.traveloregon.com/adcampaigns.cfm

In the past, when establishing the image for the Governor Hotel, I have used elements of writing from the actual journals of Lewis and Clark, typefaces from old event poster images found in the archives of the Oregon Historical Society and images from the 1905 Lewis & Clark Exposition.

The fonts used by the Doug Fir Lounge ( http://dougfirlounge.com/ ) also convey a Northwest lumber feel.

Some of the House Industries ( http://www.houseindustries.com/ ) might work for you as well.

One thing you can do to convey a rugged/rustic flavor without being *too* obvious is to contrast a narrow grotesque with heavy slab serif, in all caps. A typical combo is Trade Gothic Condensed with Clarendon (I see a lot of Portland design firms using them together). It works well with a little bit of 50's kitsch – I'm assuming that's what you had in mind when you linked to the Rainier can. If you want retro-looking body copy, take a look at Mark Simonson's Metallophile.
