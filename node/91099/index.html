How to keep my type mine if I work at a studio/agency

March 15, 2012 • Exploriment

(I ran a search but didn’t find anything. If it has been covered, apologies in advance, please direct me to the relevant thread and ditch this one.)

Not sure I sufficiently summed up my question in the subject line…

I’ve been noodling around with type for a long time. Took a long break. Now I’m getting back into it again.

I have no interest in selling my type commercially - as if it’s even good enough. I just want to use it in my own work, and hopefully it’s something of a selling point if I look for a job with someone. Unique type that no one else has. I figure it has more worth that way.

Now if I work at an agency/studio/etc., I don’t mind loading them on the computers to use in projects, but when I go, I want to take them off the computers. I thought of a legal document to state that up front. That when I go, my typefaces remain my intellectual property, and you can no longer use them.

Am I nuts for even thinking this? Would a potential employer laugh at me and tell me to run along? Would a lawyer tell me I have no legal basis? Does such a document exist? Or should I just be nice guy, leave them on the computer and quite possibly find them being disseminated on bit torrent sites in the future?

You could create a license which allowed your employer to use your fonts for their client work, but not for projects where you weren’t the designer. Is it realistic to expect that you are the only designer to work on a given client? What if you are sick or on vacation, or have moved on?

But you might have difficulties if you created an identity or campaign, and your employer’s client needed to do some work using it, in house, and would be unable to licence the fonts commercially to do that.

2012-03-15 18:18:27 • Nick Shinn

As Nick said, you could create a license. But I doubt if any agency would agree to such a license, for the reasons that he mentioned.

Even if you’re the only designer assigned to a project, it’s still the agency’s client and the agency’s project, and they will want to have access to the assets used in case work needs to be done in your absence. For example, suppose a year after you leave the client asks them to update the job?

Probably best just to use those fonts on your freelance projects.

If your fonts aren’t good enough to sell commercially, as you say, then you shouldn’t be using them for professional work. If they are, and you created them on your own time, then your employer should license them on the same terms that anyone else would.

If licensing fonts is your call as a designer, they don’t even have to know the foundry is you. But if they don’t have a budget for licensing fonts, then you should take to heart the reality that your job is not meant to entail a level of design that transcends choosing from the available system/bundled fonts. Your decision of whether to give your work away to impress people should probably start with the assumption that you can’t take it back later.

If you’re paranoid about people misappropriating your work, don’t even consider a career—fill- or part-time—in type design. If your work’s any good, people will steal it if they can; even if your work’s mediocre, people will steal it if they can. Unless you wish to produce your work as real-live foundry type—i.e., a real, physical product cast in metal, which cannot be emailed or downloaded—people will steal it if they can.

That’s the curse—and the blessing—of digital existence.
