2010-11-01 09:56:07 • JoergGustafs

Evan,

Pictures of real Ye Olde Worlde maps are by definition bitmaps (as said above, photographs of actual old maps). They didn’t think in the 15th century of supplying vector files :-) However, the samples you point to are all vector files, and since it seems this is for a corporate identity, that would be preferable over a bitmap.

Using data from GSSHS, I can produce high-quality Illustrator files in just about any map projection you can think of (Mercator – deprecated, actually –, Mollweide, Robinson, Werner, and loads more), and even throw in country borders. But there are two huge “howevers”:

  1. The data is contemporary. I don’t think Dubai’s Palm Islands are incorporated (I’d have to check), but the Netherlands for example have all of their polders filled in. .. Come to think of it, I don’t think that’s visible on a global map …

  2. The country borders are contemporary as well! In your link of examples, the Illustrator file ‘with borders’ clearly shows Yugoslavia as one big country (but not the Soviet Union). Whether or not that makes it “ye olde worlde” depends on your age …

A third ‘however’ is that it’d require you to manually make it old style. Sepia? Blurry lines? It mainly depends on the intended target size & usage.

2010-11-01 10:48:53 • Theunis de Jong

since it seems this is for a corporate identity, [vector] would be preferable over a bitmap

That’s a good point; logos are generally vector art.

Another thing to keep in mind is that while the map itself would be in the public domain due to its age, the actual photograph of it might be copyrighted by the photographer. And this would make sense as the photographer may have gone to some time and expense to find, photograph, and retouch it. And in some cases the photographer might be able to easily tell you’re using his photo due to a digital watermark, or some specific retouching he’s done to it, etc.

So be sure that both the map AND the photo of it are in the public domain (or use a licensed photo, like from a stock photo company, and be sure the license agreement permits use in logos). Or use a vector image made specifically for the identity.
