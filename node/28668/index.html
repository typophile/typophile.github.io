Pen angle in Greek lowercase letters

October 8, 2006 • Jongseong

I have been adding [[Greek]] letters to a text typeface I am developing called Gorani. Now, I was highly impressed with the Greek in Adobe Garamond Premier Pro, and decided to emulate the steep implied pen angle in its Greek lowercase. As you can see in this sample, the ductus in Adobe Garamond Premier Pro is such that the horizontal lines are thicker than the verticals. [img:sites/default/files/old-images/Garamond-Premier-Pro-Greek_6673.gif] None of the other non-monolinear Greek typefaces I have seen has this feature, as far as I recall, so I figured I would do something original. Here is my attempt so far for the Greek in Gorani (no bold or italic yet, obviously): [img:sites/default/files/old-images/Gorani-Serif-Greek_6208.gif] Now, after I drew the Greek lowercase letters for Gorani, I took another look at the AGPP samples online and noticed that I didn't use the same pen angle after all. In case it's hard to see in the samples, this is what I mean: [img:sites/default/files/old-images/AGPP-Gorani-Comparison_4096.gif] My question is this: is the angle I have right now for Gorani a wrong angle for use in a serious text face? Before, when I thought I was using AGPP's pen angle, I thought it at least had a reputable precedent, but now I am asking myself whether I should redraw the whole alphabet. My intention for Gorani is for it to be a readable, no-frills serif face for setting long continuous text at 8-10pt; I am not a native reader of Greek, so I don't have a precise idea of what is permissible in this case. And since I am asking, just how unusual is AGPP to modern Greek readers--not at all, a little, or a lot? Are there other examples of unusual pen angles in Greek typefaces? Also, are there sample images of the sort of calligraphy that Garamond's Greek is based on? The typeface exudes calligraphic heritage, but I'm just not familiar with any calligraphy that would have inspired it. Most Greek manuscripts I remember seem to use pretty monolinear letters.
Have a look at Greek Font Society's typefaces for other examples of unusual pen angles (and especially in GFS Didot). http://www.greekfontsociety.org/pages/en_typefaces.html Have a look at the greek fonts designed by students in Reading university as well. http://www.personal.reading.ac.uk/~lts98gl/ And (one of my favourites): Omonia CF by Karlopoulos - Kortesi - Freisz http://www.fonts.gr/tryfonts/sample.asp?familyID=329&cat=0 I wouldn't say that AGPP strikes me as unusual. I could say "oldfashioned". And that might made it hard to use in several contexts. George Triantafyllakos - backpacker.gr
