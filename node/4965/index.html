2004-05-24 09:29:19 • as8
I don't think there is really a dichotomy here.

I doubt whether we do need more than a handful of functional typefaces (for book typography, at least). But the fact is, we have more than a handful. Precisely because no one typeface stands head and shoulders above the others on any functional grounds (many work pretty well), we will continue to have a choice.

In these circumstances, to insist on using just one typeface for everything is itself a choice.

Among the many choices a designer makes, choice of typeface may well be relatively unimportant. But there is no reason in principle why a designer may not use a variety of different typefaces and use them all with sensitivity and care. Admittedly, excessive promiscuity may be a bad idea (because it often takes a while to get the "hang" of a particular typeface); but within reason choice may be good. If it helps the designer to keep fresh and enthusiastic, that may be good too.

How then to choose? For a whole range of reasons. Some technical (lots of information, little space; narrow columns; need oldstyle figures or not; need bold or not; need small caps or not); some personal (I like this type, not that). I see no reason why allusiveness and resonance to subject or period should not form part of these considerations, if you want them to.

If there are no such resonances then, at worst, those who believe there are will be selecting usable type for the wrong reasons. They are deluding themselves in supposing that their careful allusiveness adds anything, but it is a harmless delusion. Rot only sets in if it leads to the choice of faces which are functionally poor; but I see no reason to suppose, given the range of acceptable faces available, why that should happen.

2004-05-24 17:16:35 • Chris Rugen
In the realm of display type, few would doubt that "expression" is valuable in a font, which means we can never have enough of them. In the text realm there is indeed reason to doubt the usefulness of more than a handful of fonts, because immersive reading precludes conscious aesthetic impressions. In fact it might seem that the only good reason to make a new text fonts is to solve emerging technical issues.

But when you take into account that the subconscious is also open to being "impressed" (albeit in a very different way than the consciousness), you realize that it's really mostly a matter of subtlety, not exclusion, of features. This is why everything has to be toned down in a text font: first it has to "go below the radar" of the consciousness, for it to have any hope of marking its effect in immersive reading.

In the subsoncious, more "primal" things matter. For example, sharp corners say something and soft coners say something else, no matter that the reader can't verbalize -or even realize- the difference. This is what makes text design such a black art.

So choosing Garamond for a SciFi novel isn't bad because it will ruin readability (it won't), it's bad because it wastes the opportunity to gently amplify the content. Quality is in the details.

2004-05-25 19:38:57 • hrant
The origins of Italic type:

Conventional wisdom is represented by Meggs in his History: Saves space, copied from fast, informal scribe style.

That's much too utilitarian. Here's another way of looking at it:

Poggio invented the humanist script c.1400. It has been described by Donald M. Anderson as "plodding" and "sober". This is the writing style that Jenson's definitive Roman face interprets.

During the Incunabula, in the later 1400s, the work of the Italian writing masters (eg Sanvito, Sinibaldi) had become more stylishly cursive, no doubt to distinguish itself from printed text.

The era saw a swan song for the manuscript writer, before the profession morphed into a smaller number of Writing Masters (Instructors), the best of whom published how-to manuals.

Sinibaldi designed pages that were predominantly his cursive (ie Italic), mixed with "small caps".

It is this look which Aldus captured in type.

So, the origin of the Italic type style is caused by the swan song virtuosity of the manuscript hand, itself a reaction to the plainness of the original Roman typefaces.

Lots of interplay between old and new media -- exploited by artists looking for new forms, supported by a progressive marketplace.
