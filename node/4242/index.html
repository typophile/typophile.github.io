Although Fournier is known to have been inspired by the RdR in making his own fonts, his stuff was much more conventional in nature. The RdR style is very distinctive: it is a "constructed" letter, with vertical stress, high contrast, a smallish x-height, and most notably double serifs on the ascenders. It also exhibits a short line on the lc "el" (which apparently Grandjean introduced because the constructivism was leading to confusable forms), but to me that's not critical to its nature.

Addison, you're right - I was just wondering if you saw something in Arnhem that alluded to the RdR somehow - not because I'd get on your case :-) but because your eyes are different and you might see things I don't.

Tuleu was the bastard son of Deberny, and he ran the foundry in the last quarter of the 19th century. He took the RdR style to its pinnacle*, only to be undercut by a double whammy: the Arts & Crafts revival of Morris; and Gustave Peignot's obsession with display typography: the Deberny and Peignot houses merged in 1923, and Gustave's charisma and business sense caused all of the wonderful old Deberny text faces to get tossed out.

* The italic is staggering in its purity.
