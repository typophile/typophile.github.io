Pro same width: character combinations can be replaced by ligs without reflowing the text Contra same width: ligs appear to be too wide sometimes

Adobe seems to go the way to make ligs narrower than single chars. What about other foundries?

And if you did want a universal rule, ligatures should be a little narrower, because they were created to compensate for the extra space the single letters left, in lead.
2006-01-28 16:55:36 • julianne
The more enclosed a white space, the larger it appears to be, so ligatures should be actually narrower for optical equality (if that’s what you want, which you probably should if you’re representing two or more characters, rather than joining two or more glyphs to represent one character). Van Krimpen sometimes went further, to no very good end.
2006-01-28 17:20:07 • George Horton

As Julianene said, the norm is that ligs take up less space than the single letters. This may also have been a function of fitting characters on a line in setting lead type. Today, we have no hard boundaries with digital type and kerning and contextual alts have made the job easier for typesetting pros. Perhaps then today, the width of ligs is more about better matching of letter spaces than fitting to a line. I don’t know what value there would be in an exact match to the separated glyphs, what reads best and then looks best seems more important.

2006-01-29 00:08:53 • Sebastian Nagel

Typically, the ligatures take up less space. One of the things that might be a goal in the design of the ligatures is to maintain the visual rhythm of vertical stems in text. Once you’ve finished spacing the typeface, come back to the ligatures and see how they look in text. Also check what the spacing is of the lowercase el or i. One possible rule of thumb is that the space between the two stems in the fi or fl ligature should be equivalent to the space between two els or two i’s.

Of course, the eye is the ultimate arbiter. That rule of thumb is most likely to lead one astray in the heaviest weights of a monoline sans serif….

I’m throwing my lot in with the “depends” crowd.

I have made fonts where ligatures were desirable or simply sexy, and I have also made fonts where the design not only doesn’t require the fi and fl ligatures, but they look odd, so I simply make glyphs which are the two glyphs side-by-side, and therefor the same width as the two glyphs together.

There are two kinds of ligatures in the digital era: necessary and discretionary. The first are a solution to a problem posed by the design of the glyphs; a long-teardrop-terminal “f” for example. The latter are ligatures created by the designer to amuse themselves, recreate a historical effect, answer a request, etc.
