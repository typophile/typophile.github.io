FontMarketPlace.com

October 5, 2007 • William Berkson

At [[http://fontmarketplace.com/|FontMarketPlace]], a new web storefront of Ascender Corporation, there are lots of fonts which say that the names are copyright Monotype, but they are priced at a fraction of the price they are on the Monotype storefront, Fonts.com. Why would Monotype compete with itself in this way? Can anybody explain what is going on here?

I have no information at all, but that doesn’t stop me from offering an opinion!

I think it’s just a different market segment: the fonts are all offered with a single user EULA*, in TrueType only, and most (but not all) don’t include the small caps and osfs.

There are also some fonts that I suspect were never big sellers for Montotype, like Pastonchi.

It is interesting to note that clause 10 of the EULA offers editable embedding if used “in a “Personal or Internal Business Use” document (that is, a document other than a “Commercial Product” as defined herein)”.

In a EULA Internal and Business use, usually refers the the normal print work that fonts are used for in the corporate, design, advertising, and branding worlds. It would also allow graphics to be created for use on a web site. Commercial Product usually means something like a piece of software, a web book, or magazine distributed via pdf. That is created as a product for sale. Use of a font in tis realm would require a different license. Actually most EULAs contain this sort of language or something similar. With respect to FontMarketplace, the answer is no. The EULA language “personal and internal business use” describes how the font can be used (installed on personal computers, and not on web servers).

There is nothing to stop creative professionals from using the site, and if they increase the workstation license quantity to 5 then the price comes is similar to what they are paying today on our AscenderFonts.com site and many other creative professional font websites.

As others have described above, and as we have stated in our press release, our intent is to appeal to Microsoft Word users (and all the other software applications used at the home and office) by presenting fonts in a consumer-friendly manner, around themes and occasions, and to make them affordable with a single user license. Our believe is that there is a chasm between free font websites and creative professional font websites, and that’s where FontMarketplace is focused.

And if we can entice people to move from Comic Sans to one of the many other FontMarketplace fonts, then I think many of you will believe that we’ve done our part to make the world a more beautiful place :)

Here’s the deal:

The license to the Font Marketplace fonts are almost identical to Ascender- the difference is the amount of machines the software can be installed on; in this case, one user.

You can use the fonts for “for-profit” graphic designs just like “any other font”. The commercial license only applies to the digital software embedded in a product for sale.

Also if the PDFs you designed that are edited by a client/associate/ Ascender/FontMarketplace would like that user to purchase his or her own license (which happens to be very affordable).
