Design of an astrological font

February 4, 2010 • AxelHarvey

Is anyone interested in collaborating on a set of astrological glyphs? I was directed to this forum by a successful young designer who did not want to undertake the project and thought I should roll my own (maybe a 30-something dynamo is not aware of how daunting learning curves can be to a 70-year-old).

Although I have loved typography all my life, have several reference works on my shelves, and used to handset poetry for a small press, the discussions here have left me shorn of all confidence. At the very least 62 characters would be involved - most likely more because it would be good to provide alternatives in a few cases, particularly the tiny number in which I would insist on (I hope easily justified) departures from convention. But I am aware that the choice of glyph forms might be the smallest problem, compared to such questions as portability, which glyphs to design as both fixed- and variable-width characters, etc.

There was a discussion on astrological and astronomical symbols here a while ago. The participants seemed as lost in the astro world as I am in the typographic; but I think I can be a sound bridge between the two cultures.

I should add that my purpose is to have a set of characters that would marry harmoniously and modestly with a solid old-style or transitional font, or a modern text font based thereon. Almost all available astrological characters are either ruler-and-compass stick figures, clumsy glyphs drawn with no feeling for type forms, or self-indulgent fancy inventions. Those that are truly elegant lack many necessary characters.

2010-02-05 22:06:47 • AxelHarvey

The general point of Unicode is to assign a number to every written mark used in human language. These numbers are useful in developing fonts among many other applications. The specific design in the code charts is not prescriptive, but the meaning generally is, e.g.: the snowman at U+2603 could be drawn differently but still recognizably be a snowman; you’re not supposed to put just any random symbol in that spot.

You can submit a request to add glyphs that you feel necessary to complete the astrological/astronomical symbol group to the Unicode consortium, but that can be a complicated process. [[http://www.unicode.org/pending/proposals.html]]

Of course, that doesn’t get you the font that you need in short order, a very different question indeed.

Unicode is a standard for which character has what code, just like ASCII. ASCII just says that the number 64 stands for the uppercase letter A, not whether that A is in Courier or Times Roman. And, in fact, the Unicode document warns people against stealing the typefaces they were graciously allowed to use to print the thing as a way to create a Unicode typeface.

ISO 8858-1, the 8-bit code that added accented characters to the “high bit” range, was “official”, but not compulsory. One could put Japanese katakana in the high bit range instead, or different accented letters to support Hungarian and certain other languages. Even ASCII had “national use” positions.

To be Unicode-compliant, you would not replace the hammer and sickle with the biquintile… instead, you would put the biquintile ‘way up in the “private use area”. To be usable on some older systems, you might prefer to make an 8-bit font, not a Unicode font, and replace the accented letters by the aspects instead.

2010-02-10 09:05:24 • Andreas Stötzner

Sorry for my absence - it’s been hectic (including a furnace breakdown now fixed).

As for the Andron astrological characters there are two types of consideration. First, there are what I would call “substantive form” issues which have to do with preferences and taste, so there one’s decisions are as good as anyone else’s. For example I would prefer a Z shape for Jupiter rather than a curve for historical and symbolic reasons, but that is a personal matter.

Then there are “execution” issues. For example, I find there are too many serifs, and they are too strong. Here I think one’s inspiration should be numerals. The Andron digits 0 to 9 - which I like - have serifs on the 1, what might be called half-serifs on the 2, 4, 5, and 7 (only one on each), and a discreet finishing tick at the top of 3 (if I am seeing correctly). The same restraint should apply to astrological symbols. As it is there are right-angled full serifs on the Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Virgo, Scorpio, Sagittarius, and Capricorn glyphs, and I find them annoying. The angled half-serifs, as on the Libra glyph and the lower end of the outer vertical strokes of Uranus, are fine. The Neptune glyph has three harpoon barbs instead of serifs. I know this is a reference to Neptune’s trident and it is often done that way, but to me it is sliding from typography towards illustration.

All the above is “sous toute réserve” since I feel like a snail criticizing a football team. I can’t comment on the weight and shading because I have not seen the astro characters blended with a block of text.

The new 116-character Unicode block is good news.
