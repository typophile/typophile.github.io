Metric kerning relies on a typeface’s internal metric data, as determined by the typeface designer, to space letters. Optical kerning adjusts spacing based on general letter shapes. Here’s a good discussion of the two with examples, plus links to references: http://type-ed.com/2013/12/08/metrics-vs-optical/ Also, I found Jan Tschichold’s Treasury of Alphabets and Lettering (1932) to be a useful resource for understanding how kerning should work; his examples of uppercase and lowercase kerning are fairly straightforward. The basic principles still apply to digital type.
