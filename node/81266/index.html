Typophilers frequently mocked Hollywood's erstwhiile reliance on Trajan, and for the last year or so I've noticed that movie advertising has moved on from that generic era, becoming more sophisticated (like many areas of commercial design). It is indeed good times for contemporary typography. And using "historically correct" types is part of that picture. ** How do YOU do it? Consult mass media from the era and region. Matt McGrew's "American Metal Typefaces of the Twentieth Century" (Oak Knoll) will help identify the fonts used, at least in North America, so for e.g. Canadian WWI, Saturday Evening Post would be most promising. Sure, it was a US publication, not Canadian, but it had a huge Canadian distribution, and it's easy to get hold of old copies.

2011-04-16 22:46:05 • Frode Bo Helland
nskyers, first keep in mind that historical accuracy of fonts is not exactly the highest priority for a movie director, just as they might not care if a character's costume, hairstyle, language -- or even the movie as a whole -- isn't historically accurate in every detail. They're going for a look that communicates the general time and place to modern audiences, and if the details aren't right only a tiny percentage of people will know or care. Graphics used in the movie itself (a store sign, for example) are normally prepared under the supervision of the movie's production designer. As reference they may assemble historical photos from various sources like the internet, museums, library books, field trips, etc. Graphics used in marketing materials (like a movie poster) are often prepared by a different group -- the studio's art department (or farmed out to an outside design firm); sometimes with the director having some input and sometimes not. My guess is that historical accuracy in marketing materials is a fairly low priority.

Following regional origins for Canadian stuff means you're pretty much stuck with Cartier, unfortunately. It's been said (by Bob Stacey) that JEH Macdonald, who was an accomplished lettering artist and had a stellar career in commercial art in the early years of the 20th century, as well as being founder of the Group of Seven, would have designed very Goudy-ish typefaces—had there been a Canadian type foundry producing original designs at the time. http://www.penumbrapress.com/book.php?id=86 Sure, Cartier was the "first" Canadian type design, but the prolific Les Usherwood was not long after — his faces absolutely nail the look of 1970s and 1980s Canada. From 1990 there have been plenty of Canadian digital fonts, for instance Mambo and Fontesque were massive in the mid 1990s and typical of the era.
