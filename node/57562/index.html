But they look pretty." or "The Scotch Modern didn't originally have proportional oldstyle figures, but I figured, 'what the hey, I like them, so why not'"?
Scotch Modern is a close revival of a "Modern" (Didone) face of the 19th century. Such faces never had oldstyle figures, then or until now, AFAIK. Therefore it is anachronistic, not to mention ironic, to include oldstyle figures in a modern face! However, Adobe made me do it. By including "Proportional Oldstyle" and "Tabular Oldstyle" as non-bracketed options in the InDesign OpenType menu, it appears that there is an error in OpenType fonts that don't have oldstyle figures. (This is strange, not to mention ironic, as Adobe's own Trajan is without OSF.) Therefore, to avoid looking like there is a mistake in the typeface, I decided to produce oldstyle modern figures for Scotch Modern. Or perhaps they are modern oldstyle figures. Other than massaging the proportions, the only structural change from the lining figures is the removal of the foot serifs on 4. At any rate, they work surprisingly well, and are quite interesting. In fact, Michaël Amzalag uses them pointedly, and copiously, in his Interview redesign, e.g.-- [img:sites/default/files/old-images/Interview_contents_3572.jpg]

The problem with high contrast types is not the contrast per se, but rather it's the too slight image of fine details, in particular serifs, in offset printing. Caslon was first revived in 1844 with "Lady Willoughby's Diary", with the original matrices, I think. It caused something of a stir because it was so much more readable than the 'Moderns' that were then fashionable :) Updike, who admittedly was a Caslon fanatic, said the revival was "The typographic event of the mid-nineteenth century". Following this, foundries gradually issued a lot of typefaces called 'old style' based on Caslon, but with larger x-heights and I believe also less contrast. According to Mac McGrew's book, Bruce Old Style originated in 1869, Binny Old Style around 1863, and Bookman Old Style originates in 1860. Caslon itself was revived in the US supposedly from English electroplates at about the same time, also under the name 'Old Style'. Later in the century they put the name Caslon back on it, and with hot metal type every foundry had versions of both Caslon and "Old Style", often with shortened descenders. One of the most refined of the higher x-height revivals was Imprint, which I think you can still see used occasionally. I don't know, but I would guess from the 1860s on you will see a lot of 'old styles' in print. The peak of the craze for Caslon was probably 1900 to 1925. After the Bembo and Garamond were revived, it lost some of its mystique as there were other good and refined old style revivals available, as well as the new stuff from Goudy, which was very popular also in the US. Today I sense we are about due for a new Caslon craze :) By the way, I think the Scotch style, such as Nick has revived, in my eyes was never as problematic in text as the real Didones, which have more starved horizontals.

2009-05-07 05:00:24 • Sye
Nick, here's my take on your great comparative scans of Didot, Scotch, and Old Style. The way the arches are handled is, I think, as I said. There is a lot of variability because of ink varying quite a lot from letter to letter, but if you look at the lighter branches, I think you can see that in general the weight is carried farthest through the arch in old style, less in Scotch, and still less in Didot. This is more obvious in the digital revivals of these, which are mainly based on the metal. However, I have learned from your scans something I didn't realize before. A lot in the difference in look is not only how thin the thins are, and the transitions, but also just in the weight of the thick vertical strokes. The contrast is the ratio of the thicks to the thins and a lot of that here is that in Didot the thicks are thicker, say in relation to the x-height of the letters. The Scotch is lighter, and the Old Style a lot lighter. Thus a lot of the reduction in contrast from Didot to Scotch to OldStyle is lightening the stems. The net result is a striking difference in typographic 'color' in the three faces, with the heavy verticals punching you in the face, so to speak, in Didot. As you point out, interestingly, the heavier serifs in the Scotch does help relieve this, as well as lighter stems. The OldStyle dramatically changes it, giving a more open, lacy effect, but perhaps a bit too grey.
