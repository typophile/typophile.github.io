Ticket typography rant

September 13, 2006 • kosal

I have major problems with typography seen on tickets (movie tickets, boarding passes, etc) Maybe someone can enlighten me on the reasons why it’s usually so bad.

Is the current technology for ticket printing so outdated that it can only do off-register dot matrix patterns? In addition to figuring out the distorted letters, numbers, and cryptic abbreviations, the information organization on airline tickets could drastically be improved. The logical me wants to believe there’s a reason for it all. Is there maybe a digital-based foundation to the design of these things, like the MICR font?

Is the current technology for ticket printing so outdated that it can only do off-register dot matrix patterns?

My guess is: yes. For ultra fast, high volume printing, dot matrix is still the way to go. Why the letterforms are so poor, I do not know.

2006-09-13 18:56:26 • Stephen Coles

I would presume the continued use of dot matrix is still used because “it will do”. I guess airlines in particular have bigger issues to deal with. Maybe the letterforms are poor because no-one has given these types of typeface any serious consideration (there’s been plenty of parodies) since the technology that used them became antiquated.

On the subject of the presentation of information on the tickets, it’s probably down to who ever set up the computer systems behind them. As a programmer,their interest would be in what was quickest and simplest for them to set up, rather than what would be simplest for the user. A similar thing happens with utility bills, and in some cases, websites. I know Erik Spiekermann recently did design work on the tickets for (I assume) DB German Railways.
