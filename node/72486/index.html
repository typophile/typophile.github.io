2010-07-22 02:17:32 • William Berkson
That's like asking: Do books really matter. If not for the invention of the printing press and the artisans who followed how many books would be around today. The English statesman and philosopher Francis Bacon wrote that typographical printing has "changed the whole face and state of things throughout the world". Unfortunately, even though innovation and mass production has improved all of our lives, it has also created the impression of uniformity. We live in a constantly changing and more complex world. As a result, we use the technology but few of us really understand it. Unless you are 'in the business', so to speak, it's difficult for many to appreciate the work that goes into creating a 'beautiful piece of furniture', a 'fine painting', an 'emotional piece of music', or for that matter, a 'typeface'. That's why only type designers and graphic artists get excited about this wonderful craft of ours. Good typography is a labor of love. Just like a musician fine tunes his music, we love to deal with the intricacies of our craft. I remember trying to explain to my wife what I did for a living. I remember her saying, "but, all '26 letters' of the alphabet are already made. They exist. What does a type designer do"? And, that my friends, is what we all initially have to deal with. Oh, after I explained to her the whole picture, she finally got it. She now is my biggest fan. And, that's what we have to do with the rest of the world. It's our job to educate them. With time, I'm sure that many will come to understand and appreciate what we do, and how we all have contributed to this wonderful medium we play in. So, keep those great new fonts coming. I'm already working on the next one :)

2010-07-22 17:37:25 • jacobsievers
When I taught a course in book design, I told my students that typography is like music. You don't have to know music theory to be moved by music. You may not know one note from another, or what syncopation means, but the music still makes you react: One tune makes you want to cry, another makes you want to dance. Typography works the same way. You may not know Bodoni from baloney, but the choices a designer made cause you to see a page of text as easy or rigorous, old-fashioned or modern. That's why typography matters. Readers may not respond to type the same way professional designers do, but they do respond.

2010-07-24 20:07:36 • Robert Trogman
The word "subliminal" is prejudicial. Too many connotations of subliminal advertising, tricking people into buying stuff. But really, the "subliminality" of type is no different than, say, the music in a movie soundtrack, which is a far more manipulative way to work on people's emotions. How about hair styling? That's designed to send subconscious messages too. But no one calls it subliminal. All areas of design involve subconscious communication. And if the advertising in a publication targets readers with clever manipulation of graphic imagery, how is that different than the editorial (bastion of democracy and civilization), the typography of which targets its demographic in exactly the same manner?
2010-07-24 21:24:52 • Nick Shinn
The word ‘subconscious’ is prejudicial. Simpler to say that there are aspects of design that involve experiential associations that do not need to be formulated as thoughts and generally are not. This is what we mean when we say that a design evokes a sense of something. If we bothered, we could probably formulate that sense as a thought, which effectively means giving it linguistic expression. That we generally don't bother doesn't require a concept of the subliminal or the subconscious, it just means that it isn't necessary to the experience.
