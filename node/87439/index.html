This cannot be answered in a short forum thread. There are some many variables. You may be able to find a purely technical checklist, but then again this list may not be applicable to all cases. Sometimes even discarding the technical standards may be the best and only reasonable choice. You have to consider the medium and the task at hand: a typeface suitable for H Melville’s Moby Dick won’t necessarily be a “good” choice for a newspaper headline or a website. And, you cannot forget that the stylistic trends change all the time. So, what makes a “good” or “bad” typeface? The answer is: “it depends”.
2011-11-16 16:29:26 • Frode Bo Helland

What makes a bad typeface? What makes a good typeface?

That is not a very productive question. It will lead into semantics, which is not really what you should be learning about typefaces. I suggest you qualify it with respect to a design piece you have produced, discussing why you chose the typeface you went with, as opposed to others, and what perceived qualities you exploited in it, and its strengths and weaknesses you discovered in experimenting with it. And/or do the same with the work of another designer—either a peer, or a practising professional.

Design is practical and experimental, not academic and theoretical. Nonetheless, in an academic context, practical work may be analyzed to reveal important principles.

A story:

Many years ago, in a galaxy far away called New York City, I took a course in sound recording through the New School. We met at a small recording studio in the Village that did a lot of jazz recording.

The engineer there said this: “There’s no such thing as a ‘bad’ microphone. Just a microphone that you haven’t found a good use for yet. Each has it’s strengths and weaknesses and it’s just a question of what sound quality you want.”

With that, he turned and opened up a big cabinet filled with microphones - some of them dating back a lot of years. He never threw out a working microphone.

Same with fonts, IMHO.

2011-11-17 22:55:59 • Richard Fink

Bad in what way?

I recently used a (free) typeface which had received quite a few favorable comments here on Typophile,. It worked well aesthetically for the intended purpose, but, being curious and able to do so I took a look at in FontLab, where I found that the way it had been drawn was simply appalling. Nodes on top of nodes, radii that resemble rough-cut circular saw blades and worse.

That’s one way of being bad.

For me, that’s the only real way a typeface can be bad, aside from one for which the designer makes ridiculous, unreasonable and unsupportable “scientific” claims of superior performance in some way similar to Tiresias, that dyslexia font, or that “eco” one that saves ink. And, in the end, my objections to those fonts is moral and not aesthetic. :o)

Paraphrasing Richard, There’s no such thing as a ‘bad’ font. Just a font that you haven’t found a good use for yet...
