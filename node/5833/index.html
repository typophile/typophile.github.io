2004-08-05 02:09:55 • Joe Pemberton
Synchronicity.

There are many type design "ideas" kicking around my head, themes which interest me. I recognize these themes in typography I come across, explored to varying degrees. This can point in new directions. I also recognize other themes which can interact with the ones I have. Believing in synchronicity, I pursue obscure, eclectic lines of research in the expectation that this will increase the likelihood of serendipity occuring. I shake up my circumstances from time to time, For instance...

I'm moving at the moment, and am packing up a lot of stuff and throwing other stuff out. I was going to throw out a 1994 Bazaar, but the type caught my eye. It was set in HTF Didot, and the art director had combined different sizes of the face in the same (department heading) word, with the appropriate optical scaling. That reminded me of the way that the ultra thin weights of Luc de Groot's "Taz" work -- a similar kind of optical scaling (I had been talking with Luc about that at TypeCon). It was an idea I had explored a little with the ultra thin weights of Bodoni Egyptian. A few months ago I went to see "M", Fritz Lang's 1929 film, at a movie theatre, and was very taken with the title lettering. Of course I couldn't see it clearly, as the image was scrolling and somewhat distressed, so I was perhaps imagining things that weren't there. Anyway, I later tried to draw it from memory as an alphabet, but couldn't decide on the x-height. So I'm thinking maybe I can do it with just one weight, but in a family that has many members with different x-heights, from absurdly tiny to what looks practically like a unicase, with vestigial extenders. So that could be mixed and matched too, as the Bazaar art director did. New principles to structure a typeface family (other than roman/italic, weight, and horizontal scaling) -- that's another theme in my work.

This may turn out to be a typeface that is "the answer to a question that nobody asked", but what the heck.

Why do particular design themes inspire me? I don't know if they inspire me, it's more like I find them "useful" (in the way that soccer commentators describe a pass) for setting up the kind of design puzzle that I will enjoy tackling, with an eventual sense of accomplishment when it's finished.

As for "from what source do you derive the passion", well, the "why" question, especially with such purple phrasing, is going to get the heavy answers, ain't it? I like the St. Augustine rationale: I believe in order to understand. I've answered the question before, similarly, with "for me, type design is an epistemological pursuit ", and been accused of pretentiousness. Yes, it is a fancy word, and I always have to look it up in the dictionary to check the spelling and make sure it means what I think it does. But how else does one respond to "why do you exist?"

Yeah, lighten up, Paul, Chris. People want to take a break from work for a few minutes and blather about whatever is on their minds. So long as they avoid personal insults or threats, it seems pretty harmless to me.

Back to inspiration. Creativity in every field, whether in science or philosophy or the arts is a matter of problem-solving.

The inspiration comes partly in seeing and choosing new problems. Here a lot of the inspiration comes from looking at past work. The trick is to be both appreciative and critical - understanding the past without being bound by it. When you appreciate, you understand what the person was up to. When you are critical, you can see how they could have chosen another problem, or pulled off the solution better. More broadly, there is an historical understanding of traditions - how one effort influenced another where the turning points were. All this gives you an insight into what new paths you might explore.

An often ignored part of this in education is the need to keep fresh the goals that led you into the field in the first place. I think that spark should be nurtured always, for it is what makes you see things differently from others, and leads you to make a distinctive contribution.

An example of problem shoice is Nick's choice of the problem of producing a unicase, monowidth face; and he did multiple related solutions. In revivals, many people have tried to see what should stay the same and what should change for new ways of producing fonts and new way of printing.

Paul, you've seen some pleasures in an old type that others hadn't seen the the joy in. And Chris went for modernism and greater variety in letter forms. Hrant is obviously inspired by the problem of improving readability of text fonts.

All this is in seeing and choosing problems. I found in studying history of science the greatest originality is at this stage.

The other aspects are your imagination and talent in creating solutions. Imagination is the ability to come up with fresh solutions. Again an appreciative and critical eye is a help, but also a free spirit, seeing broad connections - even in religion! - and a treasuring of your own individuality.

Talent relates most specifically to the field of endeavor. In type, there is more than one talent at work, but certainly one is artistic, visual talent of doing beautiful curves. Another is in being able to visualize that complex geometry of how shapes will harmonize one with the other. Here talent help you come up with not just new and varied solutions, but good ones. Guys like Garamond, Bodoni, Zapf, Frutiger- they have the gift.
