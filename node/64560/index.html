Most languages seem to be okay with the use of weight (eg. bold) to call out specific words or passages within body text. Italic seems to be more problematic for languages like Japanese and for say Arabic. Anyone aware of any other language-specific conventions? For example is color commonly used in some languages, or switching style (switching to sans-equivalent within serif-style body text), perhaps underlining, size changes, reversing-out, yellow highlighter etc., etc.,? Maybe related: As Fraktur typefaces rarely have a bold companion (and hardly ever an italic), the preferred practice of emphasis was tracking. Also common: style-switching (e.g. Fraktur with Bastarda). In Textura typefaces, tracking was rather unusual. A bigger point size was used for emphasized passages. All-caps was a no-no, for obvious reasons. But capitalizing the first two letters was an accepted method – not only in ‘GOtt’.

C.f. Friedrich Forssman: Satzregeln für gebrochene Schrift [Blackletter typesetting rules], in Forssman/de Jong: Detailtypografie (also to be found in J. Schalansky: Fraktur Mon Amour)

2009-11-22 05:18:17 • Si_Daniels

As Chris Harvey said, in Korean you can use dots above the letters. However, this is rather old-fashioned, and you hardly ever see it nowadays.

There is a rather specialized way of emphasis where a different style is used for foreign terms. This is similar to how foreign terms are italicized in English, except that in Korean, foreign proper names are also given this treatment.

The style of the basic Korean text face, the equivalent of a serif roman, is a brushy serif known as ‘myeongjo’ or ‘batang’. In my earliest elementary school textbooks, I remember that foreign terms were set apart by the use of the ‘sunmyeongjo’ style, which uses the Chinese ‘Song’ style serifs. In many contemporary Bibles, foreign terms are set in sans serif, like this:

[img:sites/default/files/old-images/proverbssample_6216.gif]

In older bibles, where the text is set vertically, you may see single or double lines to the right of the terms, in the vertical analogue of the underline.

However, the practice of setting foreign terms apart stylistically is very rare and I don’t recall seeing it outside of those two examples. Korean uses so many foreign terms nowadays that it would be really impractical to mark every single instance.

I haven’t made a statistical analysis, but I’d say using bold (most often mechanical bolding) is the most common way of emphasizing nowadays. Other schemes are seen, such as switching to sans serif, changing colour, underlining, even mechanical slanting (shudder). I suspect this mostly has to do with the options provided by the word processing software, and no universally accepted standard has emerged. In practice, in many if not most serious books, you would hardly see any instance of visual emphasis of specific words or passages at all.

2009-11-23 08:52:51 • Andreas Stötzner

Good question! This might not be the best place for an elaboration but I’ll answer it here because it might be useful for other scripts. (Plus I don’t know where else I’d do it…)

I try to balance authenticity and functionality, and the furthest I’ve taken this is in the “multilateral” Nour&Patria* system: [img:sites/default/files/old-images/NP-System_5097.gif]

  • http://themicrofoundry.com/ad/CR.pdf

Modernists hate the complexity, but I maintain that the “dialog” between scripts is necessarily complex (and you guys know what I think of Modernists :-). So basically the Armenian is slanted (because that’s a good thing), the Latin’s Italic is slanted the same degree, and each script has a subordinate for the other script. The Latin’s subordinate Armenian is upright; it has too many serifs and the x-height is too big*. The Armenian’s subordinate Latin is slanted, but it’s not Italic; I actually love it, and I have a feeling it can be used to set quite long text (not too small though) but I also have to admit that it’s idiosyncratic. The subordinates are “flawed”, but it’s actually in a way that helps them serve their master design well. This system of both parallels and hierarchies handles a very wide range of typesetting challenges. Fonts that have one cut for Latin and one cut for Armenian are almost always culturally inauthentic for one of the scripts, plus they’re one-dimensional in terms of solving typographic problems.

  • Sadly the crushing majority of Armenian fonts are like this.

Now, it would be nice to have an “Italic” for the Armenian. But people shouldn’t see that as remotely equivalent to the need for an Italic in Latin.

The main thing I think is not to overly damage authenticity by forcing a script to adopt the typographic conventions of another script.

The tricky thing is emphasizing more than one word at a time. Historically people have used a different font for this purpose (certainly not very nice) but one reason is that besides missing italics Armenian fonts rarely had more than one weight. And that brings me to how I think text should generally be emphasized, even in Latin: a demi weight! Not dark enough to wrest attention from the reader, but dark enough to be noticed one saccade away. Typo magazine does this (still, I hope) to great effect. So I make my fonts with finely gradated weights (and my Bolds tend to be pretty light).

The Tai Viet script of SE Asia (used for the Tai Dam language and others) has an interesting typographic tradition. Instead of bold or italic, emphasized words are written larger, with an increase in ‘x-height’ and more compressed. This is reasonably common practice when writing the script. it occurs even within a paragraph, but here’s a good sample of a heading.

[img:sites/default/files/old-images/TDHead_6005.png]

When creating a ‘bold’ variant of our font, we considered creating a different ‘emphasis’ style altogether instead. The problem is that once the B and I buttons appeared on our word processors, the world got this idea that every font had to have a heavier-weight bold, a slanted italic, and (shudder) a sometimes terrible bold italic. We had limited time to choose what the ‘bold’ would look like, so we took the faster, easier, and more accepted modern choice - we added weight to the regular.

So while there are some interesting and useful written traditions for emphasis using style variations, I’m afraid they are dying out rapidly.

If you don’t want to subordinate one script to another, clearly you don’t have to. If Chinese or Arabic are quoted in an English text, one doesn’t seek to find a Chinese or Arabic font that somehow matches with a Roman font.

This is usually true even in the case of, say, Gujarati, or Ethiopic, where one could, if so inclined, try with reasonable effort to make a matching font.

So one could take an Armenian font designed without reference to Latin, a Roman font designed without reference to Armenian, line up their baselines and the tops of their ascenders - and not worry even about the descenders being different, let alone trying to match “x-heights” (in the case of Armenian, the term is really a misnomer, and not just because it should be “t-height” - the visual function of that space is fundamentally different in that script).

[[http://typophile.com/user/50554|quadibloc]] wrote: If you don’t want to subordinate one script to another, clearly you don’t have to. If Chinese or Arabic are quoted in an English text, one doesn’t seek to find a Chinese or Arabic font that somehow matches with a Roman font.

This statement is patently false if we are speaking not of necessity but of expediency. The fundamental problem of combining type (Which Roman with which Italic? Which Serif with which Sans-Serif? Which display face with which book type?) does not change when we are also combining languages on the same page and especially on the same line (as in the case of a “quote”).

In the case of Chinese and Japanese, for example, quite evidently a Roman Sans-serif/Grotesk typeface can be justly combined with a Gothic[黑體] face, and a Serifed face should best be combined with a either a Ming [明體] or standard script [楷書] typeface.

The solution however it not always so easy, and requires that one know how to set type in both languages independently before attempting to combine them together!

2009-11-25 20:50:32 • hrant

Ideally, yes, one designs with the right frame of mind. This requires, as a most basic precondition, familiarity with the typographic conventions and fashions of both languages involved.

My emphasis was on not trying to do too much if one is not starting from this position. If one is mixing two languages, and one is not familiar with what is acceptable or readable in one of those languages, then it is reasonable to say that one shouldn’t do violence to the font used for that language in pursuit of a visual harmony between the two languages.

Readability of both of the texts should take precedence over artistic goals.

Of course, the balance involved is very different depending on the application. One can distort fonts that are appearing in 48 point in an advertisement to be striking and noticeable - and so that two different languages will match - and thus creating, say, an Arabic version of Kabel or Stop or Neon is entirely reasonable there - whereas if one is typesetting an academic paper with foreign-language quotations, then of course one just takes the routine standard fonts for each language involved, with no attempt to engage in any funny business.

All official documents of the United Nations are routinely published in six languages: Arabic, Chinese, English, French, Russian and Spanish (German is the seventh, unofficial, language). Their formatting is cross-language-consistent, although different typographic features are used to interpret the structure and the hierarchy of the texts in different language versions of the same documents. Interesting that you bring up the angled brackets. The single and double angled brackets are something of a remnant from vertical typography that is finding new life in a more specialized use.

The angled brackets 『…』 and 「…」 are identical to the forms that were used for vertical typography, except rotated 90 degrees counterclockwise. (Korean was traditionally written vertically, but in the last century has switched to writing horizontally, with the last holdouts—newspapers—making the transition in the last couple of decades.) In addition, there are also the forms 《…》 and 〈…〉 that are becoming quite common in horizontal typography (the Korean Wikipedia uses these).

These used to be just quotation marks, interchangeable in horizontal typography with “…” and ‘…’. But since the different forms are available, it is only natural that they have adopted different functions. Increasingly, “…” and ‘…’ are used to mark speech and quotation, while 『…』 and 「…」 or 《…》 and 〈…〉 are used to mark titles of works, in the same way italicization and quotation marks are used in English (e.g., Macbeth, “Hey Jude”). In the past, the same set of quotation marks were used for both quotations and titles of works, but the availability of different forms of quotation marks has made the new distinction possible.

In the link Chris Harvey provided above, the angled brackets are all used for titles of works. These days, it is very rare to see angled brackets used for anything other than titles of works, which wasn’t necessarily the case even a couple of decades ago. You would probably never see angled brackets to mark quotations in a Korean text produced today in South Korea.

In Japanese novels and magazines, those dots are indeed the most common way of adding emphasis. In manga and comic strips, katakana (the syllabary with the more angular-shaped characters) is more likely to be used as emphasis. In fact, the use of katakana parallels italics in English in many ways — emphasis, foreign words, scientific plant and animal names, etc.

On websites, brochures, signs, and other informal materials, Japanese quotation marks are often added for emphasis. The translation agencies I work for often want to carry these over into translated materials, and I have to explain that English quotation marks are purely quotational rather than emphatic and give the text an ironic effect if used unnecessarily.

On the topic of making one language look like another…

I remember reading that our present lower-case alphabet is the result of a mistake made by (those working on behalf of) Charlemagne. They assumed that the uncial writing in current use was a faithful representation of a Roman hand.

We now have authentic specimens of Roman uncial, in which a few additional letters (like N) looked like their upper-case counterparts. Given all the bold and strange things type designers do nowadays, a typeface in which “Charlemagne’s mistake” is undone would not seem particularly bizarre.

But to go even farther… as we know, lower-case, with descenders and ascenders, is more legible than all upper-case.

The original Phoenician alphabet - or the early forms of Hebrew writing that closely resembled it - was written with parts of the letters functioning as ascenders and descenders, so it could serve as a lower-case script in partnership with the existing square script as the upper case.
