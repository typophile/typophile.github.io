2009-11-17 14:40:48 • jonathanhughes

It’s worth trying out other styles than the one you’ve started with. Free yourself for one moment from the preocupation that pictograms have to look “like pictograms”.

Very helpful is to [[http://images.google.de/images?q=sport+pictograms&oe=utf-8&rls=org.mozilla:de:official&client=firefox-a&um=1&ie=UTF-8&ei=nhsFS8HJMKbwmwOHwMyyCg&sa=X&oi=image_result_group&ct=title&resnum=1&ved=0CBQQsAQwAA|review sports signage]]. The “typical” pictogram style mainly goes back to Otl Aicher, who in the 1970ies declared them being “culture neutral” and “global”. But they are not. They are just the Otl-Aicher-style of 70ies German graphic design (though that style has been very polular since).

As you can see in the sports signage samples, pictograms can be much more vivid and beautiful than in the Aicher fashion. Think of them being written signs, like letters. Take a brush or pen and draw them. If you hesitate with that, beginn with utmost simple pictographic signs like a tree, a hut, a tent … Then return to “design” them.

I did a [[http://new.myfonts.com/fonts/andreas-stotzner/andron-corpus-publix/|series of pictograms]] myself, a series which actually belongs to a Venetian Renaissance style (!) typeface family. For my own part, it works far more better than the Aicherian frozen bockwuerstls.
