2014-11-26 20:23:43 • Mark Simonson
Stanley Morison (as Hrant tells us), back before we became sensitive to cultural diversity, went and said: The Roman alphabet is not merely in possession, but it is in possession by right of conquest. The conquest was not made possible, or even expedited, by external authority; the victory of the Roman letter was due to its inherent flexibility and rationalism. Well, it's hard to argue that the Chinese or Japanese writing systems, never mind Akkadian cuneiform or Egyptian hieroglyphics, would be a better way to write. However, beyond that, it is hard to defend such a sentiment. It's true that when it comes to mechanical simplicity of typesetting, the Latin alphabet has some advantages over the Ethiopic scripts, the Indic scripts (Devanagari, Thai, Burmese, Cambodian, Tibetan, and so on), the Korean script, or the Arabic script. However, the obstacles to handling these scripts on typewriters, let alone mechanical typesetters, were far from insuperable. And when it comes to Greek, Cyrillic, Armenian, Georgian, or Hebrew - there is no inherent superiority for the Latin script; for all practical purposes, there is an equivalency. Well, one could note the ink smudging problem with Hebrew's writing direction for right-handed writers, to say the equivalence isn't absolutely exact. One can praise the victory of the ancient Phoenician writing system, having most of the scripts listed above as its descendants, excluding the Chinese, Japanese, and Korean ones, without being too chauvinistic. Alphabets did help bring about mass literacy, and that was a good and progressive thing.
