2004-01-10 00:39:05 • Bald Condensed
If you look at this page in the Sans Serif Font ID Guide, you can see other styles with similar shape characteristics to Trebuchet: Agenda (Font Bureau), Faceplate Sans (Psy/Ops), Mahsuri Sans (Agfa), Today Sans (Spiece), Parisine Bold(Agfa), and Projekt (Linotype). Of course details will not match in each font, like the serif on the 'i', or the curved 'l', or the open lower bowl on the 'g'. However, finding an equivalent seems like an odd exercise for such a ubiquitous font. Doesn't Microsoft give it away?

Sorry for the delay, Yves. Well, MS wants people to pay, not for the fonts per se, but for the program(s) that bundle the fonts. That was the main point put to me by an upper level MS Typography rep who actually replied to my e-mailed inquiry (boy, was I surprised). Outside opinion, based partly on the timing, was that Linux users were being pointed to those fonts by many Linux resource sites and were grabbing them in very large numbers. Certainly it's MS' right to do with the fonts what they choose, but increasingly they have been choosing to slap their customers upside the head. The user of a properly purchased MS application who has a font that has become corrupted cannot simply get a replacement off the installation CD. The fonts don't exist as indi- vidual files. Personally, I feel that, after making sure that Arial, TNR and, to a lesser extent, Verdana are specified in millions of HTML pages around the world, MS ought to make these fonts available to all. Of course, as Justin showed, the fonts are available elsewhere.
