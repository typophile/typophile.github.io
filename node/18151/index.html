Font prices, like everything else, are determined by market forces. When Adobe began selling digital fonts in the late eighties, a two-font package (the smallest package they offered) was $145. This is $72.50 per font, or about $113 per font in 2005 dollars. Since then, font prices have dropped considerably. Today, most single fonts are sold for $20-$40 each. Purchased in multi-font packages, the price per font is even lower. Incidentally, although it does take a lot of work to create a font, that's not what determines the price. A movie costs millions of dollars to make, but you only pay $8 to see it. The reason is that millions of other people will pay to see it, too. The font market is much smaller in comparison, probably in the tens of thousands of potential customers. Everyone else just uses fonts they get for free or bundled with software or whatever happens to be installed on their computers. Everyone who has a computer uses fonts, but only a small percentage actually buy fonts. These are mainly professionals, who understand the value a unique font can add to their work and consider the cost of a font just part of doing business, like using FedEx or buying equipment. For these people, font prices are not high at all. Finally, the reason you see fonts everywhere is because, by nature, they are designed to be reproduced. There is no limit to how often a single font installed on a single computer may be seen on books, magazines, tv, movies, packages, websites, video games, etc. It just depends on the kind of work being produced on that computer. You just never know.

> He doesn’t see why, when fonts are everywhere, why one should have to pay $15+ for one. This is an unfortunate mind-set that is pervasive throughout this new age of digital distribution. It affects all forms of intellectual property that has been made readily accessible on the Internet; music, literature, motion pictures, photography, illustrations, typefaces, and the list goes on. I will make no attempt at giving an explanation to give to your boss or clients. But to put it simply AND mildly -- readily accessible DOES NOT EQUAL available for free! The price of a font and the value of a typeface are two different things. What unfortunately has happened within the type industry itself, is that well-known reputable font development companies joined up with hardware/software development companies twenty years ago and began bundling libraries of type with PCs, Macs, printers, software packages, etc., resulting in the proliferation of the "fonts for free" notion and devaluation of font software. This all happened BEFORE type development programs became available to run on simple PC/Mac platforms... so the excuse that cheap fonts and free and pirated fonts were the original cause of the problem -- it wasn't so. The "bigger" foundries initiated the proliferation of "free fonts" and the smaller type developers of the past ten years are almost crippled by it. The current habit of novice and hobbiest type designers releasing their designs at little to no cost keeps exacerbating the problem. I have no solution to what is already out there, but just because one particular typeface may cost $15 doesn't determine the value of ALL typefaces. Just like a pair of classic men's Gucci loafers will cost anywhere from 20 to 50 times more than loafers from Payless.
2006-02-23 20:59:49 • Norbert Florendo
Added Value is the name of the game. Firstly, there is perceived value: when everyone else is using the same fonts and your work stands out with special typography, that's worth something extra in the marketplace. To you, to your employer, to the client. Having a library of non-standard fonts, and knowing how to use them, is a value that a designer/design firm brings to any client relationship. Note that Mr Cheese advertises his font library on his web site; it is a benefit of doing business with him. Fonts that come bundled with applications or operating systems don't add value, because they are over-exposed. Secondly, working with fonts acquired for a specific design project helps create design synergy, which is more productive than wringing your brain for the big idea that will add life to otherwise hackneyed-looking typography. Fonts have formal qualities which can be leveraged to drive design concepts. "Free" fonts don't add value because they lack typographic sophistication: it's difficult to do good work with poor tools.
