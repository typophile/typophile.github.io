Darrel wrote:
Bad knock-offs, I think, are probably the most damaging, as they appear legit, and, technically, may be, but they harm the the integrity of the original work.

Yes, that's very true. When students see a free font that has 'Univers' in its name, they automatically assume that it is indeed Univers, but what they're most likely to get is a hotchpotch of different sanserifs, including a Gill Sans g! (That actually happened before). Knockoffs are already problematic in the commercial font business, it is even harder to pin down in the public domain.

Joseph D. Treacy wrote:
For instance, a large number of the users of pirate font software copies are people who work in the graphic arts. It's often art directors and also production workers at service bureaus. That's been well known since the dawn of desktop publishing.

That's a sad fact. While it's not uncommon to find designers complain about their designs being knocked off or copied by other designers, the majority of desingners are the ones who don't show any respect to intellectual properties when it comes to type designs. Typeface design is such a thankless job. Graphic designers rarely even give credit to the typeface used, never mind the type designer. Why would they even reveal the name of the typeface they used when they didn't even pay for it?!

Honestly, I don't see any cut-and-dry way to solve the font piracy problem. Only education can solve it, I think. I really shouldn't tell you this, but I've had an instructor who made a Zip disk full of fonts available for us to use/copy. Now that's really unethical, and it is terribly damaging to the type business. It makes it normal for designers to copy and pass around fonts. Most people here (I assume) won't even dream of pirating fonts. Why? We are just conscientious? We simply appreciate the beauty of typefaces, respect the talents of those who designed them, and understand how much work it takes to design and produce a great typeface. Only when the these values and artistic appreciation abilities are imparted to the students would they stop pirating fonts. These are acquired skills, and can be learnt. Students have to understand that they don't need every single typeface in the world at their fingertips to do great designs. They have to understand a good type library has to be built gradually, over time.

We are at a transitional period right now, with OpenType, PostScript and TrueType all coexisting and for many, the anxiety over Quark vs InDesign and OSX vs OS9. In the fall, my school will have InDesign 2 on all the Macs over in the Design Department. But, we don't have a single OpenType font! As much as I want to use InDesign with OpenType fonts exclusively for the class, it's going to take a while for the students to make the switch over. So I still have to cover Type 1 vs TrueType, expert sets and the like, and at least give them a taste of OpenType features. A lot of students will be working at home, some will be using PCs. Where on earth could they find decent typefaces to work with? As much as I want them all to go get a copy of Type Classics for Learning, I really can't make them do that. Already they'll have to get a couple of required texts, storage media, pay for printing, supplies and presentation material, how can I convince them to spend another $100 on type that may or may not work with the software they'll be working with? I'm trying to get the school to buy a site license for Type Classics for Learning, but that's going to take at least a semester, or even a year.

David Earls wrote:
Far better, I think, to put your philosophies into practice. Work on opensource or freeware lines and prove to people that there is room for more than one philosophical standpoint on property. Create a typeface of worth and use that will stand the test of time, and give it away, or dare to think of alternative licensing schemes.

Yes, alternative licensing schemes. I personally think that we should have access to each and every font available commercially at any given time (perhaps over the web). So we could test them on our own layouts and to show clients. Once we've decided that we want to use it for a particular project, we would then pay the foundry. That would be a very attractive licensing scheme. But I guess it would only encourage more pirates, unless we have a tracking system in place.

Type design is not a multi-million business. A lot of foundries are one-person operations. The economic return is quite minimal compared to say advertising, or even editorial design work. Yet the effort one has to put into a type design is enormous. We do it, because we love it. There's no question about it. Kudos to people like Victor Gaultney, who's making such an exquisite, extensive and useful text face available to the public. They always have my greatest respect.

"Classics for Learning"
400 fonts for $99

This is an example of the retail practice known as "Dumping", i.e. a company with deep pockets flooding the market with cheap product to boost market share and drive out competitors.

Colleges and design schools should beware of thinking that buying the Adobe package fits the bill. 400 fonts from one foundry is bad education.

To provide a well-rounded education, schools should expose students to the rich variety of contemporary type design. Sure, include some of Adobe's brilliant typefaces, to represent the mainstream, but students should work with fonts from other important type "movements/subcultures/genres", that I won't attempt to categorize, but how about: FontFont; House; Emigre; FontBureau. You get the picture.

> studio overhead

Nobody can deny that a studio -especially one in a prime location like James's- has a lot of overhead. But the question is, how valuable is a studio in the first place, especially these days? Sure, there's nothing like physical human contact, but it's not indispensable. Type design in particular has extremely low production and iteration costs. My biggest font-related client right now is an institution I've never even visited, via a contact I've never seen or even heard on the phone. And most significantly, I could be doing that project -and many of the other projects I've done over the past 3 years- while living anywhere in the world with a decent internet connection. And in many such places $100/hr is an astronomical rate - you could live like a king! For example, monthly rent in a very nice 2-bedroom apartment in Yerevan is about $200. Plus if you need to physically visit a client once in a while, about ten hours of work at $100 will get you there and back via Heathrow no problem!

So when you think of rates, don't limit the equation to working in midtown Manhattan, or even anywhere in the West. Why do you think Bangalore is exploding with employment right now? This ain't Kansas anymore.

--

> you have to join the real world!

Different things are real to different people - to me there's nothing real about working like a slave most of your waking like and wasting your weekends resting like a vegetable. And it depends what you want. If your involvement in type design revolves around simply making money (to spend on whatever, or to retire in physical comfort) then you invest what you need to establish the facade some old-fashioned, old-money clients might require. But if you want to be remembered by future generations (the way Gutenberg and Garamond are, even though they died paupers), a studio and a library of 70s throwbacks won't do jackshit for you. Most of what you need to be remembered (as opposed to die rich) is totally free - although certainly not easy to get.
