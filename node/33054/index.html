2007-04-15 02:38:57 • charles ellertson
Having recently read Much Ado, I thought I'd chime in. The play centers on two couples, Benedick and Beatrice and Claudio and Hero. Benedick and Beatrice spar verbally throughout the play for a great deal of comedy, and as a result of their friends' trickery, they acknowledge their love for each other by the end. While Claudio and Hero also marry at the end, there is a sinister element to the plot, as Don John (sort of like a prototype for Iago in Othello) schemes to drive them apart. He manages to convince Claudio that Hero has been unfaithful, causing him to leave her at the altar. Until Don John is found out, the play skirts death and duelling. The most disturbing element to the play (if it is emphasized) is the idea that you cannot believe your senses: the constant deceptions make what the characters hear and see falsely ("I have deceived even your very eyes"). So, to try to connect this to typography, maybe what would speak to the specific character of the play would be a typeface that is pleasant and fun but that has, if not an insidious touch, a bit of imperfection or mystery. Enigma and Delicato both have some aspects that draw the eye's attention (just enough), and Proforma in light weights seems to almost shake as if hiding something behind it. Finally, Quimera, because of its odd weight distribution, looks odd , but you can't quite tell what's weird about it.

2007-04-19 13:43:01 • dezcom
I should probably let this thread die, but as part of my research on Shakespeare's handwriting I came upon this thread once again (through James' reference above to humanist handwriting being more appropriate for the poster than blackletter). Although my study so far is very preliminary, I have learned that Shakespeare as far as I know wrote in a "secretary hand" (digital examples here and here), which is related to the Bastarda blackletter. While at the time people were using the italic style that would soon predominate (as seen in the Queen Elizabeth sample), it was not what Will used -- although he did often employ an italic long s in his signature. James was perfectly correct in suggesting that the humanist handwriting might be appropriate for the Much Ado poster (it's certainly easier to read than secretary hand!), but I just wanted to correct this for posterity's sake.
