jpad: no, that's too simplistic a read. A much more likely interpretation is that the visual system slices the image into spatial frequency bands, with subtly different processing depending on the frequencies involved. There's a study cited by Peter Enneson saying that 90% of the legibility of text is contained in a single spatial frequency octave. Relevant threads include: TYPO 13 readerability / readability Also read Enneson's TYPO 13 article that spawned much of that discussion.

with subtly different processing depending on the frequencies involved. Researcher Oliva calls the process of differentiation "perceptual grouping", allowing the brain to perceive two distinct images (Einstein/Marilyn, groan) in the same place. In reading, I would assume it allows the reader to process word-images (which extend into the blurry parafoveal region) first, and distinct from sharp letters. This model calls into question letter-based reading theories.
