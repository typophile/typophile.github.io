New terms for print-based typographers and designers

November 12, 2011 • Chris Dean

Treeware: Paper-based printed material, typically as contrasted with media that store or convey information electronically: We have to ask whether online publishing will ever replace treeware.

P-book: Abbreviation for ‘paper book’, often contrasted with ‘e-book’ (= electronic book). Close the gap between the price of p-books and e-books, and increase the profit margin.

From the [[http://www.catb.org/jargon/|Jargon File]]:

dead-tree version [common] A paper version of an on-line document; one printed on dead trees. In this context, “dead trees” always refers to paper. See also tree-killer.

tree-killer [Sun] n. 1. A printer. 2. A person who wastes paper. This should be interpreted in a broad sense; ‘wasting paper’ includes the production of spiffy but content-free documents. Thus, most suits are tree-killers. It is likely that both senses derive their flavor from the epithet ‘tree-killer’ applied by Treebeard the Ent to the Orcs in J.R.R. Tolkien’s “Lord of the Rings”. See also elvish, elder days, and especially dead-tree version.

treeware /tree�weir/, n. Printouts, books, and other information media made from pulped dead trees. Compare tree-killer, see documentation.

2011-12-09 17:28:37 • BlueStreak

Not necessarily denial, but heightened awareness. McLuhan noted that new media retrieves what has been obsolesced. Professionally, new media throws a lot of “manual” workers of the old media out of work, but creates an elite of transitional managers/directors who work in the new media managing its automation of the old jobs.

That was the case with photo-typositors, which put a lot of small-town lettering artists out of work, but created a new class of job for a select few to create fonts for the new technology. At the same time, niches emerged at either end of the market: upscale, for super-high quality work in one-off luxury craft, and down-market, also deliberately anti-automated, for reasons of extreme cheapness, amateurishness, or counter-cultural aesthetic.
