Political campaign logo - please comment, thanks!

January 2, 2009 • dinazina

Hi, I’m designing a campaign logo for a colleague, his first campaign. He has low name recognition and an unusual name.

I posted a bunch of tongue-in-cheek awful logos with inappropriate fonts for amusement, before getting serious. The candidate and his friends discussed them as serious proposals, which astonished me. So I had to make a disclaimer. Here’s the list: Campaign logo proposals

Actually, the logo design was quite a challenge. I wanted to avoid the usual clichés and butt-ugly campaign graphics such as these.

What do you think of number 11, which is the last design I came up with?

I wanted a image to increase the candidate’s name recognition, obviously. (Luckily, Seattle is a plant-loving city.) I wanted a font that was distinctive, strong and “masculine,” yet had some organic qualities, to harmonize with the “plant” theme. The font is Breughel (designed by Frutinger). I had bought it awhile back, but ended up never using this odd hybrid of angled slab serifs and curved stems. I think it works here, though. The subtext is Raleigh, which to me has that same semi-organic look.

It will be in light and dark green.

Please comment - THANKS very much in advance!

Number 11 seems to be the closest to an actual appropriate logo for a candidate. It needs a bit of refining, but the others have too much of a gimmick.

As for no. 11, I would suggest moving the plant (shouldn’t there be more than one plant?) to the right of his name. Moving the placement around makes the logo more about the name and less about plants. Having the plant on the left, and as tall as the complete name and the objective makes the logo more about that graphic and less about who should be for the city council. “Dorsol” also suffers from a lack of balance. Right now, “Dorsol” is floating as a separate element, and there is no tie to “Plants”, which is your second most prominent element. You may want to make “Dorsol” the width of the “Pl” in “Plants” and have it sit on top. Take a look at your objective text, it actually works very well. Seattle is balanced above the capital “C"s as well as the lowercase ascenders.

If you are intent on making a reference/enforcing the plants to make it more recognizable, you may want to experiment with incorporating plants into the word Plants (i.e. taking your existing graphic and using it to tie “Plants” to the black area or “Soil”). This may be a bit more cliché then you would like to make it.

I apologize, it was late and I wasn’t clear. I’m not against the visual representation of the candidate’s name, I wish more people running for office would be more open to breaking out of the boring yellowpages style. I think it makes them more human when they do something different, and less of just some office robot. What I meant is that the other samples didn’t really focus on Dorsol Plants for Seattle City Council, but more on the feeling of something. For example, sample 2 doesn’t invoke a feeling of comfort, but more of the plastic feel gotten from drive-in hamburger joints from the 50s, and at a glance it wasn’t about getting Mr. Plants elected.

When I am working on balancing my typography, I try to imagine what would happen if the words above suddenly fell on the words below, and determine where they would break up. For example, if Dorsol was to fall on Plants, you would have three different break points. Your D would stay, ors would fall between the l and the t, and ol would then fall to the right of the ts. If you apply the same exercise to for Seattle City Council, Seattle would only have one break, but it would continue to be recognizable as Seattle. Actually, logo 10 has much better typographical harmony, but at first glance it’s a graphic about Seattle, and not Mr. Plants.

Campaign graphics are a hard subject to design for, because of the nature of campaigns. Often it’s about name recognition and making it a memorable recognition at the time of voting.

Ha! I wondered when someone would say: “Well now it sucks, it was better before”.

In response to your comments:

1. Color
For some reason the greens do look light here. The dark green should be deeper. 

I thought about the issue of the green sign blending into a green Seattle lawn or hillside, but I think it has enough white to stand out. Besides, with the name "Plants" how could it be anything BUT green? In the summer a lot of the grass turns yellow or brown, anyway. And it'll stand out on a button, since few people wear green clothing, or on a bumper sticker.  

On the website, I'm using shades of brown along with greens for a pleasantly earthy look, with a few accents of blue on things I want to focus on (like the "DONATE" link!).

2. Plant images: The previous overlapping plant images really bothered me, as that seemed to blur and confuse the strong plant shape. Obviously this is subjective, since you think the opposite. But I have showed it to others.

I'm not trying to create realistic depth of field, as the plants are the same size. That makes them iconic rather than illustrative (to me). 

I tried using a larger plant below, but (to me) the more realistic perspective was actually distracting and put the focus on the plants, not the name. 

I think the fact that the lower plant ends above the margin keeps it from leading the eye off the page. But maybe it could be MORE above the margin. I'll try that. 

The way I see it, "Dorsol Plants" is the first focus, then below that "Seattle City Council," then right to the lower plant leading directly UP to the top plant, which points back to Dorsol Plants.

If several more people say they don’t care for this layout, I MIGHT rethink it. On the other hand, the average person is not a designer.

I have to remind myself of this when I see passionate Typophile disagreements over the angle of an upper serif or a descender terminal (“The ‘y’ looks all wrong!") To most people, all Roman fonts are Times. All sans fonts are Arial. The only fonts they recognize as pleasantly novel are Comic Sans or Papyrus or a script.

So I have to ask a number of regular folks to comment as well.
