The ~66 character line recommendation is for continuous reading at average text sizes (10-12 points) with reasonable leading. It’s related to saccades, the tiny eye movements across the line as one reads. Too many saccades on a single line causes discomfort and head turning, which distracts the reader, too few and the reader gets annoyed at dropping to the next line so quickly. IIRC Unger covers this in While You Are Reading which has a monster bibliography. Anyway, I would hope that good poets wouldn’t be composing in a word processor to begin with. If Neal Stephenson can write with a Mont Blanc than poets can.

I heard Bill Hill of Microsoft at last year's TypeCon say that the reason the 66 character limit works is that beyond that you have to turn your head, not just your eyes. And that slows you down--or maybe because it more likely you will have to look twice for the next line, but I don't remember exactly. >can it really be blamed for long line lengths being the standard? Well somewhat. Microsoft chose a condensed face--Times New Roman--as its default, knowing most people are going to be printing on letter sized paper or A4. With typewriters, Courier was designed so it would give the 66 character length on letter sized paper; Times New Roman at 12 point is way more. Given that these paper sizes are standard, I'm not sure that there is a general best solution, but the choice of Times New Roman--a great typeface in other settings--exacerbated the problem.

2008-08-15 21:20:41 • Nick Shinn
Two potential issues with continuous text reading are what I call "track-back" and "drift". Track-back refers to the activity of moving from the end of one line to the beginning of the next line. Ideally this happens smoothly, effortlessly, accurately, without distraction, without losing your place, without needing to look back up to recall the end of the previous line, etc. Long line lengths can undermine track-back -- all the eye-turning/head-turning sorts of things mentioned. Drift refers to the phenomenon of accidentally slipping from one line to the next, mid-line. One of the most common culprits in drift is excessive word-spacing. When the word space approaches the distance between lines, then drift becomes more likely. Short line lengths (justified) generally lead to greater extremes of word spacing and can become susceptible to drift. All of this is in the context of continuous text reading -- where the text/content generally has narrative linearity, momentum, forward motion. Poetry happens in a different mental time/space. It is a different typographic animal. > Poets determine [poetry] line length, not typographers. Precisely. -- K.
2008-08-16 15:05:11 • kentlew
To continue with the Lorca observation, it seems to me that poets (not just of the concrete variety) have long been aware of the effect of layout on printed verse, and follow the general maxim that the longer a poem, the longer the line lengths, and the more the look of prose. It's a question of pleasant proportion in the document, as much as any purely literary concern. Blake's Songs and his Prophecies are an extreme example. But even Eliot follows the principle: The Hollow Men, for instance, has shorter line lengths than The Waste Land. Another factor may be whether the verse is meant for silent visual reading, or to be listened to, the difference being that within the three-second span of conscious apprehension, many more words can be held by eye than ear. The relationship between temporal duration and speech/verse is discussed by Ernst Pöppel (originator of the 3-second theory) in Mindworks.
