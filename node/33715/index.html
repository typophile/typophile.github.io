2007-05-12 06:19:18 • Nick Shinn

Tiffany: If the text is such that the meaning, the phrasing, is most important then you should be able to break it as such.

Which is, of course, one of the ways in which verse is broken into lines, although not the only way.

Thai is always broken by phrasing, but in that case the breaking is not by lines but using spaces. Individual words within phrases are not spaced. This means that Thai would lend itself very easily to the kind of arrangement shown in the VSTF method, since the analysis by phrase has already been done, and you would only need to replace the space characters with line breaks. I can imagine that editing an English text for this method might be an interesting task. It seems to me that what the VSTF method is doing a lot of is extra-visualising punctuation, i.e. they are using linebreaks to emphasise things that are already explicit in a well-punctuated text. Of course, a lot of punctuation is stylistically discretionary, so the linebreaks can also be compensatory for absent punctuation. This means someone is making editorial decisions about possibly ambiguous phrasing.

Of course, the whole thing rests on the assumption that the text in question has a singular meaning that can be determined by breakdown of phrases. Recently I’ve been talking with Fiona Ross and Robert Bringhurst about Sanskrit examples in which entire sentences are written without punctuation or word breaks in order to be deliberately ambiguous. Fiona cited examples by the writer Bana, in which a sentence might have three different meanings, depending how you divided it up.

2007-05-12 19:06:04 • enne_son

Hrant: Are you sure? I don’t read Thai but all the text I’ve seen in it seems to have words and blank spaces just like English.

The spaces are dividing phrases, not words. Thai words are not visually distinguished: you have to know the language in order to be able to determine where one word ends and another begins within a phrase. So, for example, this

ผมไปสักห้าวัน

in text might look like one word to a non-Thai reader, but it is actually five words

phǒm pay sàk hâa wan

which form a phrase meaning ‘I am going for about five days’.

I regularly use this approach to setting type. I like the idea of breaking a sentence into its constituent phrases, and I feel the message in such a setting is easier to grasp by the reader. I started looking for natural breaks in sentences when setting introductory (usually centered) type. I then moved to asymmetrical settings because of my dislike for centered type.

The example below is taken from a client’s 1997 annual report. The bodycopy (ITC Officina Sans) is set justified on two columns. The asymmetrical type treatment shown was used to introduce each of six sections throughout the book (always on a right-hand page).

(I apologize for the use of Trajan)

I would never consider setting an entire book with this asymmetrical treatment. But I feel it offers a good counterpoint to more regular body settings. It doesn’t need to look like poetry or haiku. That can be controlled by the typesetter.
