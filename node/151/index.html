On a PC, every font is a member of a family that consists of 1-4 fonts but no more. The typical family consists of regular (roman), italic, bold and bold italic. Of course, this is just a technical family, and fonts may also belong to much larger meta-families including additional weights, condensed or expanded, etc. However many fonts are in the overall family, they must be sorted into discreet families of 1-4 fonts, and this controls the relationship of font styles so that, when you press the italic button in Word for example, you get the font that is associated with your roman font in a particular family. Does that make sense?

In your graphic, the large name at the top of the window is the font name, which includes the style extension (e.g. ITC Officina Sans Bold) and which corresponds to the 'Full Name' in the font file. The smaller name below, labelled 'Typeface name:' is actually the name of the discreet font family (e.g. ITC Officina Sans Book). So in this case you probably have a family that shows up in menus as

ITC Officina Sans Book

and which contains these four fonts

ITC Officina Sans*
ITC Officina Sans Italic
ITC Officina Sans Bold
ITC Officina Sans Bold Italic

*This font may or may not include the style extension Regular.

Of course, the overall ITC Officina family contains many more than four fonts, so these are organised into additional 1-4 style families.

In OpenType fonts, there are name table fields that allow you to maintain the functionality of the 1-4 style families in Windows while also grouping larger number of fonts together. Adobe takes advantage of these in their split menus, and I suspect future versions of MS software will also.
