This is a question of spacing, not kerning. The left margin includes the left sidebearing of the glyph in question. In InDesign and Illustrator, you can get around this to some degree by choosing optical margin alignment. If the font had OpenType optical bounds info, and an application used that, it could also help. However, there isn't a reasonable way around this in terms of the actual spacing of the font. Regards, T
2004-10-28 22:54:28 • Thomas Phinney
Left sidebearings are important for good spacing. Sorry. :-/ Fonts that don't have this "problem" are simply not well spaced. And of course the larger the point size the larger a sidebearing comes out. But I see where you're coming from, and I can think of one way of addressing it, although it's peculiar: a font can have an invisible dummy character, and kerning pairs for every single combination of it and another character, where the pair value "removes" the left sidebearing. It's not as bad as it sounds, actually - in fact I guess you could even do it with Quark's pair-adding facility or something, although you'd first have to open the font and record all the left sidebearings (and you still have to account for overshoots, like you want the "O" to be pulled back more than its "raw" sidebearing, otherwise it won't align visually). hhp
