Contrast Ratio & Legibility for Printed Type

August 7, 2008 • jeremykanter

I work for a small human factors consulting firm and we’re currently working with a client on a new labeling system for their products. Legibility is critical for this client’s domain, and their current designs use different color combinations in certain places to differentiate product variants.

Some of the color combinations they chose worried us, and so we set out to find an objective measure for legibility on which we could then base our recommendations. The best we have found thus far have been the [[http://www.paciellogroup.com/resources/contrast-analyser.html|color difference and contrast ratio measures]] put forth by the [[http://www.w3.org/TR/2006/WD-WCAG20-20060427/appendixA.html#luminosity-contrastdef|W3C]] and [[http://www.hp.com/hpinfo/abouthp/accessibility/webaccessibility/color_tool.html|Hewlett-Packard]]. However, it worries us that these measures were created to evaluate colors on screen, not on print. After a bunch of snooping around online and looking through some of the usability books we have around the office, we’ve been unable to find any equivalent rules for printed text.

If anyone knows any rules or resources that would help us with this problem, please chime in. Thanks so much.

…an objective measure for legibility…

There is no such thing. In print, graphic designers and typographers often work with Pantone colour samples. I sometimes use the Pantone Color & Black Selector, which shows what type looks like printed in a selection of colors on white, or knocked out. But of course a sample book which showed every possible colour combination on different stocks would have millions of pages. So the best strategy is to rely on a graphic artist with aptitude and plenty of experience in working with colors in print.

I don’t believe typography is susceptible to human factors management. At the general level, any guidelines are platitudes no better than common sense.

However, I do believe that it is possible to measure and predict performance within narrow ranges of variables, but this is really “product development”, and can’t be extrapolated into valid general principles.

There is always one active thread or another at Typophile going on about “readability” tests, and I have read most of these. So far, I have not come across any reference to experiments with colour and legibility. I get the impression that most test situations are typographically naive to the point of banality (e.g. http://www.typophile.com/node/46304 ), so of little practical use.

2008-08-08 16:19:43 • Nick Shinn

Tinker & Patterson conducted a reading speed study on print that looked at a variety of ink and paper colors. Black text on white paper performed the fastest and all other combinations are reported as percent slower than black on white:

Black text on white paper 0 Green on white -3.0 Blue on white -3.4 Black on yellow -3.8 Red on yellow -4.8 Red on white -8.9 Green on red -10.6 Orange on black -13.5 Orange on white -20.9 Red on green -39.5 Black on purple -51.5

(data from Tinker’s 1962 Legibility of print book)

But I don’t think contrast ratio actually has anything to do with hue color, but with lightness. It’s possible to make legible text with very dark blue letters on a very light blue background. Contrast differences should be measured differences between foreground and background luminance. This Legge paper plots reading rate by contrast differences as measured in luminance differences. http://gandalf.psych.umn.edu/users/legge/read5.pdf

Cheers, Kevin

Without more precise information on the chroma, value, and hue of the colours tested, the Tinker & Patterson data isn’t much use.

Why was red on green so much harder to read than green on red? Well, the green paper was probably a light tonal value, so should have been tested against a similar tone of red, i.e. pink.

If black on white was best, why was red on yellow better than red on white? That suggests it’s not something which can be just reduced to tonal contrast.

**

It would be logical for black on white to perform best, as that is what most type designs are optimized for! However, there is another genre, news text, where typefaces are optimized for grey-on-grey.

**

Results from prose-reading tests may not transfer to labelling requirements.

Without more precise information on the chroma, value, and hue of the colours tested, the Tinker & Patterson data isn’t much use.

The details of the ink and paper used can be found here: Tinker, M.A. & Patterson, D.G. (1931). Studies of Typographical Factors Influencing Speed of Reading: VII. Variations in Color of Print and Background,” Journal of Applied Psychology, 15, 471-479.

Why was red on green so much harder to read than green on red?

Because the ink color and paper color are interacting when the ink is being absorbed into the paper. Tinker says “Another factor that must be considered by the editor or printer is the change in visual appearance of a color when printed on another color. This becomes obvious by noting the observed effect of color combinations in [the above data].”

what I’m looking for is a set of rules or guidelines that establish a threshold for legibility.

The problem is that with legibility (like most aspects of typography) you have a huge number of variables which can interact.

That isn’t to say that we can’t research the effects of various variables on legibility, and come to reasonable conclusions such as “in general using lower contrast between text and background reduces legibility.” But formulae, or exact numbers for the effects of a single variable, which translate to your specific situation, are impossible to get from pre-existing research.
