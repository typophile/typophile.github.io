Extended unicode font for publication

November 11, 2008 • boyarin

I am looking for some publication quality fonts to suggest to my publisher. The text includes Hebrew (unpointed), polytonic Greek, and the usual diacritics used for Hebrew (underdots, hamza, ayin etc.) These are all available in the extended unicode (such as, for instance, TNR on Vista machines–not XP). Can anyone suggest either a single font or a combination of fonts (faces) that i could suggest to the designers of the book. Thanks Prof. Daniel Boyarin

Although I cannot recommend specific typefaces, I would like to put forth the recommendation that you abandon the search for a single font for this purpuse, and instead explore a combination of fonts. When dealing with unrelated scripts, my experience is that they are best implemented through the use of separate fonts, as opposed to an amalgamated font. Simpler things tend to work better, and as expected. And, from a pure design perspective, separate fonts will give you better results for each script.

Dr. Ken Lunde Senior Computer Scientist, CJKV Type Development Adobe Systems Incorporated lunde@adobe.com

I think Prof. Boyarin is asking about eg. the underdot h, to indicate a chet sound, etc. for latin text, and compatible fonts for other scripts.

First Adobe’s Garamond Premier is a rare (unique?) font in having matching Polytonic Greek.

For a project I have done, I needed to use the underdot h for Hebrew, and it was not present in an otherwise very complete Adobe font. At John Hudson’s recommendation I made a separate modified font, with the underdot h in it, and substituted that where needed as I input the text. The glottal stops for the ayin and alef (not called hamza, that’s Arabic) are often indicated with single quotes one way around and the other. Personally I better like transliteration rules where these are put in only when absolutely necessary to indicate pronunciation, and not routinely. But this gets into the Hebrew transliteration issue, which is a can of worms.

I might add that a lot of success in harmonizing different scripts lies in the way the page is designed, and not only the fonts.

A few other good text faces that include polytonic Greek integrated with the Latin design are Adobe Arno Pro, Adobe Minion Pro, and ShinnType Scotch Modern. Gentium and Palatino Linotype also have polytonic Greek, but aren’t quite as suitable for use as book faces. Scotch Modern and Gentium also contain the underdot diacritics and glottals for Semitic transliteration.
