2006-10-10 13:35:04 • timd

Faz, I think your premise is wrong. There was no type in the time of the ancient civilizations (which I presume is what you mean by Egypt, Mesopotamia, etc.)

Your first step should be to:

Identify the major historical genres of type*

and then try and correlate them with other historical movements. AFAIK, Robert Bringhurst is the only person to make a real good go of this, in his Style Guide otherwise known as “Bringhurst” - sort of a typographer’s Bible). His little history only takes a dozen or so pages in the book, but is IMHO, absolutely crucial to your study project.

*Perhaps the best book to do this is Geoffrey Dowding’s “An introduction to the histroy of printing types” – it is short and accessible to the non-specialist, and organizes by both phenotype (physical characteristics of type) and historical era – so is very useful in associating those two different means of classification.

If you want to make it more about the evolution of alphabets in different cultures (which would perhaps be more inclusive and politically correct), that is a completely different story, but it is not the timeline of type.

2006-10-12 12:02:36 • timd
For European type the start date is the mid fifteenth century with Gutenberg, so your study should probably begin there. As Tom Christensen says in this thread and his article Koreans had a printing technology which may have informed Gutenberg. So, although the earliest European types were formed based on the contemporary and local writing styles, as the technology spread through Italy and Europe the style changed, to what is familiar to the modern lay reader. It is technology rather than artistic developments that drove type evolution, although one cannot completely separate art movements from type design. Tim
