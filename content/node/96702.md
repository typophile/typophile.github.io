  body: "Nick, I <cite>am</cite> sure.\r\n\r\nRussian/Soviet typographic nomenclature
    was a calque from German. So the next weight up from the Regular was always <em>poluzhirnyi</em>
    (<em>halbfett</em>), not <em>zhirnyi</em> (<em>fett</em>). The <em>zhirnyi</em>
    was kept for what the English-speaking typographers would call \u2018extrabold\u2019.
    To add to the confusion, the Regular weight was called <em>svetlyi</em>, \u2018light\u2019.
    \r\n\r\nTake a look at the official description of <a href=\"http://base.gostedu.ru/424/42409/3.gif\">Severnaya</a>
    typeface, dated 1971. It was available in three versions: <em>\u04211: \u041F\u0440\u044F\u043C\u043E\u0435
    \u043D\u043E\u0440\u043C\u0430\u043B\u044C\u043D\u043E\u0435 \u0441\u0432\u0435\u0442\u043B\u043E\u0435</em>
    (\u2018Upright Regular Light\u2019), <em>\u0421/1: \u041A\u0443\u0440\u0441\u0438\u0432\u043D\u043E\u0435
    \u043D\u043E\u0440\u043C\u0430\u043B\u044C\u043D\u043E\u0435 \u0441\u0432\u0435\u0442\u043B\u043E\u0435</em>
    (\u2018Italic Regular light\u2019), and <em>\u04213: \u041F\u0440\u044F\u043C\u043E\u0435
    \u043D\u043E\u0440\u043C\u0430\u043B\u044C\u043D\u043E\u0435 \u043F\u043E\u043B\u0443\u0436\u0438\u0440\u043D\u043E\u0435</em>
    (\u2018Upright Regular Demibold\u2019); the latter was, in fact, the Bold.\r\n\r\nNow
    compare it with a relatively recent announcement of a new digital typeface, <a
    href=\"http://paratype.ru/cinfo/news.asp?NewsID=76\">Susan</a>, by Manvel Shmavonian
    (ParaType, 2007). It is offered in 4 versions: <em>\u041D\u043E\u0440\u043C\u0430\u043B\u044C\u043D\u043E\u0435</em>,
    <em>\u041D\u0430\u043A\u043B\u043E\u043D\u043D\u043E\u0435</em>, <em>\u0416\u0438\u0440\u043D\u043E\u0435</em>,
    and <em>\u0416\u0438\u0440\u043D\u043E\u0435 \u043D\u0430\u043A\u043B\u043E\u043D\u043D\u043E\u0435</em>
    (Regular, Oblique, Bold, and Bold Oblique).\r\n \r\nI remember the German and
    the Hungarian compositors who worked for my company confused by the typographic
    mark-up done by our copy-preparers who always called Monotype Series 334 <em>\u0422\u0430\u0439\u043C\u0441
    \u043F\u043E\u043B\u0443\u0436\u0438\u0440\u043D\u044B\u0439</em> (Times Demi-bold),
    and the typesetters thought they wanted Series 421 (Times Semi-bold) which was
    not, and is still not, available in Cyrillic\u2026\r\n\r\nTo answer your last
    question. Lebedev uses the old Soviet weight designations, slightly updated (the
    Regular is no longer called <em>svetloye</em> but <em>normalnoye</em>). Both he
    and <a href=\"http://paratype.ru/cinfo/news.asp?NewsID=76\">ParaType</a> use the
    neuter, not masculine, form.\r\n\r\nAs to the traditional German weight nomenclature,
    I vaguely remember that in one of the older threads Erik Spiekermann provided

    you use the masculine form, you're saying that it is the \"shrift\" that is bold
    or italic - here's a bold Arial, there's an italic Arial. If you use the neuter
    form, you're saying that it's the weight of the typeface to which the attribute
    applies: here's Arial in a bold weight, here's Arial in an italic weight.\r\n\r\nSo,
    even if masculine is more popular in this age of Microsoft Word, the neuter may
    be more typographically correct - which would explain ParaType et al bucking the
    trend.\r\n\r\nSo now you can make an intelligent choice of which gender to use,
    based on what you want to say."
