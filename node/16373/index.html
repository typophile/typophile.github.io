2005-11-21 23:03:22 • alldaveallen
If those letters are part of the building, it is almost certainly not a font but a product of the sign-making trade as it existed then. It was a parallel but separate industry to type founding, just as handlettering was. There was certainly some cross-polinization going on, but it was not like nowadays when you can go down to FastSigns and have them make a sign with any old font.

2005-11-22 11:48:42 • magnus_gaarde
"not a font but a product of the sign-making trade as it existed then" It is hard for today's younger designers to realize what a different world it was back then. Most things were done by hand. As Mark says, there was a "Sign Making" trade which was not the same as the "Hand Lettering Artist" or the "Typesetting trade". It was quicker and cheaper to get someone from the proper trade to just do it by hand from scratch than to try and port one set of letters from craft A to either B or C. Our world of the quick scan-font ID-online purchase-Postscript output-CadCam is quite incredible when compared to how things were done pre tech. People actually wrote hand written letters to eachother then too. ChrisL

2005-11-22 18:23:52 • Norbert Florendo
While we’re on the subject, was there a suite of off-the-rack letterforms that signmakers/engravers would use as templates or stencils back then? Did foundries make any such thing? Not foundries, but there were books and manuals on how to make the letters. There were general principles, but each author tended to have his own style. There were often references to popular type styles, maybe showing how to do lettering that resembled "Caslon" or "Futura" or whatever. There were also trade schools and apprenticeships, so the knowledge and techniques were passed on from generation to generation.
