Some thoughts:

A problem I see with likening type to the motor vehicle or fashion industry is that the perceived differences in quality is very subjective.

‘Quality’ type is given its distinction for an almost limitless array of subjective reasons. Whereas the workmanship, physical materials and overall workings of a motor vehicle or fashion item is more easily distinguished. Distinguishing a Mercedes to a Kia, and Prada to a Target is very easy. The higher prices of a Mercedes and Prada are justified, to the reasonable ‘average’ person.

Focussing on these subjective qualities, likening type pricing to artistic works, such as paintings also has downfalls. Most obviously paintings are unique, and type is a ‘software’ - make once sell many.

And continuing on, likening type pricing to software perhaps has its problems. I dont think the ‘average’ person respects font EULAs as highly as software packages.

So perhaps the answer is technology. Improving type technology to enable subscription based font usage (the way software is going these days), and forcing user registrations and respect of EULAs.

Perhaps the currently limited type technologies have left us with this interesting issue of pricing type.

2006-05-26 06:13:55 • noftus

Nathan,

Type may be perceived to have differences in quality, but where I disagree with you is where you seem to be saying that there are no differences:

“‘Quality’ type is given its distinction for an almost limitless array of subjective reasons. Whereas the workmanship, physical materials and overall workings of a motor vehicle or fashion item is more easily distinguished.”

On the contrary, there are a number of very clear differences in the actual quality (usefulness, versatility, reliability) of different typefaces. Only one of those is design quality. Other measurable ingredients in a font’s quality are performance on screen, completeness or usefulness of character set, outline quality (which affects output performance), thoroughness of design, spacing, and kerning. There are fonts out there worth nothing, and there are fonts out there worth hundreds of dollars. Ask very experienced graphic designers whether they would prefer to rely on Adobe Garamond, for example, or an unkerned freeware student font with no numerals and no punctuation, caps only. One can be used for thousands of uses, and is therefore more valuable to the user. The other may never prove to be useful for anything.

Redge, my initial thought about your thread topic is that due to the radical difference in the market for type, from any previous type market, we’re all making it up as we go along. It would be difficult to model font pricing on any other model, since delivery, storage, generation, design, use, and distribution are all utterly new for us.

2006-05-26 06:28:52 • crossgrove

I want to take the Ford/Mercedes analogy a bit further.

Some companies want type that is utilitarian and useful, and don’t mind if everyone else driving down the road is using the same type. These are the Ford users, who get their type from Adobe or (shudder, free sites off the net).

But other companies want a more exclusive image, and choose type that is less common. It may not be better built than Adobe’s but it will have lower exposure. This is the Mercedes buyer, who is willing to pay a bit more for some semi-exclusivity.

(You also have the Lamborgini buyer, who commissions a face and refuses to let the artist sell to anyone else.)

Of course, because the Mercedes type will not be sold as often as the Ford type, it needs to be priced at a higher level.

Well, this is called perceived value, if you position your product to the market effectively to the point it is viewed as “prestige” and more “luxurious” like the TEFF and DTL fonts. They’ve succeeded this to some extent that you’re not likely to find fonts of the same quality*. But this isn’t always the case, you could price your fonts higher to create a perceived value. This is the problem, experienced graphic designers and typographers can usually pick out the wannabes, others fall to this trap.

I think Paul’s analogy of the type industry to the fashion industry is fairly true, sure you’re bound to find some fluctuation in type, font and/or design quality relative to pricing but reputation and perceived market value is often always established upon quality work. Like fashion, if someone comes out with staggering work regularly or even once or twice, they’ve built up a reputation quickly and perceived value comes along with that. The better your reputation, the higher you can price your fonts, this is good business, who wouldn’t take advantage of that? But like any business, you need to retain that value by maintaining the competitive advantage (quality or unique fonts etc.) or you will lose money.

Really, I don’t see anything complicated or unusual about font pricing. Like fashion, prestige brands are expensive, reliable brands are priced above average and the rest set the standard pricing. If you don’t agree with the pricing, buy something similar that is cheaper, just like you’d buy a cheaper shirt. Free fonts are perceived, often rightly, to be of bad quality. Just like free shirts are crunchy and uncomfortable. ;^)

One way I look at it is that once you sell a given typeface to a given customer, that’s it for that typeface and customer. (This is assuming a static format, but I’m figuring OpenType is good for 15-20 years which is close enough to forever for the software industry.)

So, with pricing, you’re not only looking at how many people will buy a given font, but also at the fact that once they’ve bought it, they won’t buy it again. Would lower prices grow the market enough to compensate for that? I don’t know.

I have, however, noted that some of the companies who seem to be doing very well in retail type appear to have raised their prices rather than lowered them.
