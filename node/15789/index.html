Estimated sign

October 22, 2005 • Nick Job

European Union Directive 71/316/EEC gives official specifications for the estimated sign, that is U+212E (℮). Can someone tell me about the estimated sign and whether or not people use the precise EU specifications for its design (some designers clearly ignore it), or is it open to interpretation like the Euro sign, which no-one seems to design according to the EU specs.

The brief entry on wikipedia here gives the ‘exact’ specifications for the sign: http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%22Estimated%22_sign

Are there any more sensible specifications that the EU ones which are based on a 1 × 0.9 ellipse and give decimals (to 3 decimal places) for the other measurements. I tried to work it out but there are a couple of measurements that seem to be completely arbitrary. Anyone know how the design came about or who the designer was?

Finally what is the approach to spacing the character both vertically and horizontally?

Currency symbols are used in-line, often need to be on the figure width, and there’s a history of making currency symbols match the style and weight of the base font. There’s no one-size fits all solution.

Use and placement of the estimated symbol seem to be dictated by law, and it’s not used in-line in continuous text as far as I’m aware, just placed next to the weight on the product.

I made an estimated sign glyph a few years ago that I have used in all projects since. It is based on the EU directive, but I very slightly tweaked it to make some optical compensations. Since the sign is pretty generic, I’m happy for people to use my glyph in their own fonts. TT and PS font files containing this glyph can be downloaded here. I usually scale the glyph to match the height of the lining numeral zero.

At Adobe we briefly flirted with customized estimated signs in pre-release fonts, where the design matched the typeface and weight, but we decided that these would not meet the legal needs of the EU, and went with the generic design, resized to match the numeral height of the typeface (like John’s).
