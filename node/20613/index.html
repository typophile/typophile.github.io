2006-06-12 14:59:42 • Goran Soderstrom

Having separate accents is also handy for putting accents over characters arbitrarily, for example, in the name Spinal Tap, which gets an dieresis over the n. (There is no such character in Unicode.)

Probably the most common use is by type designers. FontLab will automatically create accented characters for you by combining components taken from upper and lowercase characters and the floating accents. Even better, by adding anchors, you can control exactly how the accents are positioned.

I take it from your earlier posts that you are using Fontographer, though. In this case, you can do a similar thing, albeit more manually. Select each of the upper or lowercase character and copy it using “Copy Reference” and then paste into the appropriate accented character slots. (It’s possible to do them all at once by selecting them all before pasting.) Do the same for the accents. When you are finished, all your accented characters will be composed of references to the source characters (or components, as FontLab calls them) instead of the actual outlines. The advantage of this is that if you later modify the source characters, the accented characters are also modified automatically.

2006-06-12 15:15:37 • Goran Soderstrom

Sure, you could do that, but where would you stop? In the realm of non-standard accented characters, there are no standards (literally). In other words, Spinal Tap are on their own.

The normal thing to do is leave the standalone accents unkerned–and don’t use negative sidebearings, either. In cases when people will use them in some non-standard way (e.g., Spinal Tap), your font will behave as expected.

2006-06-12 19:38:56 • Miguel Sousa

The ‘stand-alone accents’ you find in typical 8-bit fonts and those derived from them, e.g. Adobe’s OT fonts, are spacing accents, and the only good reason for them to be included in fonts is because they were included in a pile of standard 8-bit character sets and codepages. Whether there was ever a good reason to include them in those character sets seems lost in the proverbial mists of time.

They are not combining marks, which are accents with zero widths and which are separately encoded in the 0300-range of Unicode. Combining mark characters are much more useful than these odd spacing accents, and there are also many more of them. Unicode does not encode any more spacing accents, so if you want a spacing version of a combining mark you – ta-da! – combine it with a space character.

You can’t kern either spacing accents or combining marks to sit over or under base glyphs, because such kerning affects subsequent glyphs in the text string. Combining marks must be handled either by mapping to precomposed base+mark glyphs in an OT ccmp feature, or by using GPOS mark positioning. The latter is my preferred method, since it is so much more flexible and extensible.
