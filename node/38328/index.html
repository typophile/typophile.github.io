2007-10-22 20:17:57 • blank

I turn the grid lines on & turn on snap to grid. Make sure each seam point falls directly on a grid intersection. Keep an eye on the X & Y coordinates in the transform palette so you know it’s snapping to the grid like it should (you should have values such as 14 or -6, not something like 14.002 or -6.175).

To me it’s easiest to check how it will tile by duplicating your working copy, dragging it down and aligning it where it will tile. It sometimes takes a few times to get it right.

I have noticed some patterns will still show tiny hairlines, even after I go through and inspect the seam points and everything checks out. They print fine though.

When adding a pattern to a shape you may find that you want to move the fill itself without moving the shape. Make sure nothing is selected, then hold down the tilde (~) key, then click on the pattern within the shape to move it.

I hope this helps. I must apologize if any of this is difficult to follow, I didn’t get much sleep last night to make a deadline so I’m going through the motions today. :-)

2007-10-22 21:54:15 • mjpatrick

Step 1: Define the rough pattern in a square

Step 2: Make a copy of the square, aligning it with the first; this will show any weird overlaps (see red arrows)

Step 3: Select any element you want to move AND it’s “clone”, and move it til it’s in a better position.

Step 4: Keep doing this until your “original” square is completely surrounded by copies. If you need to tweak an element, make sure all its copies are selected at the same time (so any changes “update” in all nine squares).

Step 5: Select EVERYTHING that overlaps your original square (any object going over the “edge” will be repeated on an opposite side, and anything on a “corner” will be on all four corners) and copy it to a new area/file/whatever. I find this easier to do in outline view. Select it all and define a pattern (rect should be in BACK, and probably not stroked).

Step 6: test it and go!
