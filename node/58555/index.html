Truly tiny printed text

May 30, 2009 • Paul Cutler

I have a project where I have to strip in an obscene amount of legalese onto a ticket. Think 3-4 pts. It will be printed on an offset press. I know that I am asking the impossible but is there any font out there that has any chance of printed cleanly at this size? If it could be read with really strong reading glasses (or by Superman) it would be a major victory.

I am thinking condensed sans of some type. I will need a book and bold weight in both standard and italic.

The character of the font is not important other than it’s ability to be read smaller than anything should ever be printed.

I recently used microprinting for a check-security feature; the text appears to be a hairline rule, but when viewed at 8x magnification it can be read clearly. I used .75 pt. Helvetica Neue 55, and ran plates at 4,000 dpi.

By the way, I used to work for Siegel+Gale, who specialized in both corporate identity and legal-language simplification; the law in some states (here in the U.S.) requires that legal language must be above a certain point size for some consumer contracts (a ticket might be an example) to be binding.

2009-09-05 21:50:05 • eliason
I have a growing appreciation for ITC Weidemann. It takes the narrow route and is pretty mono-linear in its construction. It uses its otherwise neutral frame so as to promote the potency of the ‘thorn’ like serif structures which are cleverly used for the lowercase letters r and f. The basic innovation for Weidemann is that consideration has gone into pre-empting cases of resemblance and subtle design decisions were taken to lesson the effects of misreading text when small. Optical concerns have not followed to the Bold, where the design just gets fatter. I guess the text weight is where its at for this font.

Re: [[http://typophile.com/node/56103|Pseudo Formula]] vs [[http://typophile.com/node/18643?|M-Formula]]

Thanks @eliason, so young girls vs more chiselled men? @clauses. You’re right this wasn’t what I was thinking at the time. This may not be one of the criterion to appreciate legibility, though a good analogy of optical shifts. I do believe that the text needs to be comfortable at the desired size. A feminine aspect might be good for this? Consider though… unlike sharp edges… The rounded features may not actually go further out of focus. The fact that the shape is malleable means that the design can be manipulated to counteract optical effects. This is the concept of the ‘air traps’ otherwise called ‘gauges’ and ‘scrapings’. additional to the fact that those counters will gain 5% in size because of the manipulation (before any adjustments to extenders or horizontal scaling). Don’t discard the concept yet. It might just need someone to try a variant or for me to do a fine tune on the e and g which I will concede might need a little more thought.

@clauses. I should mention that what you saw was actually a text weight variant which I was trying out my experiments. I have since worked on a rough version of the Footnote variant which is further optimised for small scale applications. It has been particularly fascinating problem solving the Bold weight of the Footnote which itself has a similar density to a Text weight Bold. A Bold of a Bold if you will?
