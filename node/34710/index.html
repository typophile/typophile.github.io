2007-06-25 15:07:02 • pattyfab
The question of the appropriate side bearings of the em dash has been discussed here several times. Traditionally, at least in the US I believe it was as near to a full em as the metal type would allow, and was set with no additional spacing. Nowadays I believe it is usually designed with say 5% space on either side. Just checking one face, Minion has a em dash that is totals 922 units, including side bearings 50 either side--this is on a 1000 unit em. Thus the dash is about 90% of the advance width, with 5% side bearings on either side. Kent Lew has explained that Matthew Carter favors an em dash that is 75% of the em, with the side bearings taking up the rest of the em. Kent has followed this practice, also negatively kerning the em dash with itself, so that a line of em dashes will form a continuous line. Note that Minion's em dash is actually 822. Carter's practice makes a compromise between the British and American practices for parenthetical dashes. The British practice is to use en dashes with thin spaces either side. The American practice has been a full em with no extra spacing. Bringhurst rails against the American practice, decrying it as a relic of bad Victorian typography. Whatever the merit of this complaint, the current American practice moves toward the European style.

2007-06-26 00:24:50 • kentlew
As Bill pointed out, for an em dash, the tradition in metal was a full em wide with the dash generally extending to the edges (i.e., zero sidebearings). Typesetters had different conventions (in conjunction with Editorial standards) of spacing these dashes. In the U.S., the most popular style is "closed" -- that is, without space on either side. However, careful compositors would often insert a thin space to hold the dash away from rounds. Nowadays approaches vary among type designers. I've outlined the thinking behind my approach in the notes of the Whitman PDF specimen at my site. If you choose to outfit your em dash with positive sidebearings, then I recommend the kern that Bill mentioned above (at least for any serious text face). In bibliographies, it is common practice to use a 3-to-em dash to signify a repeated author entry, and this is usually set (not surprisingly) as 3 consecutive em dashes. If an emdash-emdash kern pair is not present, your unfortunate designer will find herself with a "dotted" 3-to-em dash (and will likely incur the wrath of the editors). -- K.

2007-06-26 03:10:39 • Ricardo Cordoba
I've been using a 3/4 em dash on an em body (or dash 75% of the em width, with the rest of the space as sebearings) as long as Fontographer existed. I also make up a "twoemdash" and a "threeemdash" character with no sidebearings, so that I can get any length dash (2,3,4,5 ... n). In Type 1 fonts, you had to be able to write a custom encoding vector to use them, but if you could, it was a character assignment well spent. With OpenType, you simply add them to your liga feature. sub emdash emdash emdash by threeemdash sub emdash emdash by twoemdash When you think about it, the tradition of no sidebearings came about, at least in part, so you could set a two- or three-em dash. The large "case" of OpenType has changed that. With the endash, you might want to give it the same sidebearings as the hyphen. While endashes are used more with numbers, both the hyphen and endash are used with both letters and numbers, and your kerning work is eased if they have the same sidebearings. In truth, you can make the sidebearings on the en about 5 to 10 units greater than the hyphen & still use the same kerning values; I think the overall fit with this is a bit better. As for hyphen sidebearings, I make them to work with n m h etc. and use kerning for the fit with other letters. If your overall spacing of letters makes words with a certain characteristic (lose, tight), you will want that same characteristic with the hyphen. FWIW

If you do have side bearings on em-dash than it’s far more easy and probably more right to kern the dash after a previous one to get rid of the space between. Well, that's why they race horses, I guess. But to me, they are different characters. Consider that a 3-em dash in a bibliography starts flush left. But you're not going to kern an em-dash with a .125 em sidebearing flush left, at least, in InDesign. I'd rather use a different dash that starts without a left sidebearing, measures a full three ems, and, since it is usually followed by a period or comma, doesn't have a .125 em right sidebearing. Bet on the gray if you want. The two- and three-em dashes have various uses, such as to replace a persons name (e.g., Mrs.----), but all the uses I know of are different than the use of the em-dash. Usually, the em-dash both separates and joins two somewhat related thoughts. Whether you think of it as "separating" or "joining" is at some level a word game, but graphically, the British word-spaced en dash follows the "separating" notion; the American closed-up em dash is in line with the "joining." Some British designers who have worked in the States for a while, like Richard Eckersley, began to favor the 3/4-em dash in the full em space, and from this thread, it seems that Matthew Carter also signed on. Another way of looking at an em-dash is that it replaces a pair of parentheses for expressing a thought that is related, but not grammatically connected to a sentence.
