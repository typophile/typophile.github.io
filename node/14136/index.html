how typography is included in graphic design courses,

I’ve taught at the B.Des course at York University, Toronto. Typography is well integrated, from letterpress to type design. In fact, it was a special requirement when the faculty moved into a new high-tech building in 2003, replacing studios with computer labs, that provision also be made for all the old metal type presses and their volatile chemicals. Design professor David Scadding is the man most responsible for York’s typographic thoroughness. Students are impressed: http://www.ratemyprofessors.ca/ShowRatings.jsp?tid=101521

There’s really a fine line. There seemed to be a few types of students to me. One is a student who accepts whatever he/she is told because they feel that the professor has more knowledge in the subject and should be always listened to. Another student questions what the professor says and asks why and challenges both the professor’s viewpoint and his/her own. I fell in the later category. While I feel I was respectful in my questioning of ideas (wisdom should never be underestimated) road number two often gets you less respect.

In the later example I feel the student learns less because they question nothing, they learn less. Anyone can regurgitate an idea, but it takes thought and questioning to produce something original. At my recent alma mater The Pratt Institute (Manhattan) I felt that the students that mirrored the “cool” design in annuals and made the slickest looking projects got the most recognition. Sorry, I disagree. Of course, learning about the history of letterforms does not help you immediately to design a better ‘d’ or ‘s’. The ability to form well shaped letters is not so much a matter of controlling the movements of your hand (or your mouse?) as it is a matter of learning how to see, how to look at letters. So the most important thing is to train your eye, and this is best done by trying - and probably failing (failure is an important part of the learning process, probably more important than the all too easily achieved success) - to design letter forms, not by reading about them. But type design is also embedded in a broader cultural context, and this is where theory becomes important again. For me, design is nothing but the visual representation of a series of (hopefully well-informed) decisions about about how to structure and organize content. People who are just applying the last photoshop-filter are not much more than decorators. The same applies by the way also to typographers who always use the trendiest type (sometimes the might have even enough taste to choose a really good typeface), but who do not take the pains to analyze the actual requirement of a specific job… This work is actually bound to be an ‘arrangement of parts made by other people into something static.’

2005-07-28 23:22:15 • fredo

You imply that theory must be learned from a professor Also reading books might help ;)

I do not think that theoretical understanding can only be obtained by formal education. The most important prerequisite is the curiosity of the student. It is more important to ask intelligent questions than to find too fast and ready made answers. Academic or formal (theoretical) education might only help you to formulate your questions more stringent. Probably your curiosity will be more focussed and therefore more ‘fertile’. And if you are lucky, the place where you study is providing an atmospere which encourages curiosity. But that’s about it. If you are not curious enough, if you think you already know it all, then the best formal education won’t help you.

There is no guarantee that theoretical understanding will have any direct positive influence on the (aesthetic) quality of your design. As far as the formal or aestetic side of design is concerned, the arbiter is the eye, and the eye needs to get educated by training, by doing. Theoretical explanations do not help much at this point. But design is embedded in a wider field (there is such a thing as society) and has also functional aspects. To get this aspect of design right, you can not only rely on your eye. You have to be able to ask the right questions and come along with conclusions.

The third important part (apart from the training of the eye and of the theoretical understanding) in design education is in my view to teach the students design methods. This should help students to develop ideas further and to not just to add one potential solution to another. It is important that the student learns how to develop ideas ans turn a potential design solution to an actual solution. For this reason I mentioned Basle as an extraordinary example of the teaching of design methods, especially for typographic design. Without the teaching of structure, the danger of design being not more than superficial decoration is always at hand. But on the other hand, the teaching of methods is also a quite tricky point, because there is always the danger that after a while people tend to get lazy and the methods might turn into dogmas or into all to easy recipies. A school might produce ony clones of a specific style.

By the way, some of the best designers I know had no formal qualification at all.

I just think that the notion that all talent is so by nature, birth or destiny and that thinking only drives away your muses is simply bullshit. It just cuts you from the opportunity to learn and is an excuse for intellectual lazyness.

You can be pretty sure that Miles Davis was quite curious and eager to learn.

Yes, the trumpet is always the same, but there is more than one way to how play it.

The promised tangent:

Daniel, the main reason chirography is flawed for text face design is that it creates the black bodies through “movements” (GN’s “moving front”, with the elaborations of translation, rotation and pressure), and this has nothing to do with the act of reading (the main point of typography, no?) which deals with black-white relationships. Basically, making type by thinking primarily about the border between the black and white is the ideal, and chirography confuses this.
