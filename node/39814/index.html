Futura Oblique - wtf?

December 6, 2007 • amv

I’ve been researching obliques in preparation for the execution of the style in my own typefaces, and have found that Futura’s oblique is literally nothing more than a 9 degree slant applied to the corresponding non-oblique weight. Now, while obliques are certainly simpler than true italics, they’re still supposed to be more than just mechanical slanting. I might expect this from a cheaper face, but in the case of a designer-favorite like Futura, I was rather surprised.

Is this more common than I apparently think? Futura’s Oblique pays no attention to the changes that need be made to stroke width, diagonals and curves.

BTW - For the experiment, I created two text boxes in Illustrator. Both were filled with A-Z, a-z and 0-9. One was in set in Futura Light, the other was in Light Oblique. I then converted them both to outlines and sheared the first text box (Futura Light) by 9 degrees. I then overlaid the two textboxes to find that EVERY glyph lines up EXACTLY.

Strange stuff.

This is quite common with sans serifs. Adrian Frutiger used to use it with his typefaces—I’m told—because back in the day when type foundry drawing offices still employed 100s of letter drawers, it would save the company so much time and money.

But if you look at Univers and Frutiger, as well as many other typefaces (like Helvetica, too) you will see that this is the case. Note that for new designs, this comes up way less. Good new families are expected to have true Italics. It seems to just be want the people want nowadays.
