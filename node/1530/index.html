Gill Sans and Joanna works but that is really no wonder since they have been designed both by eric gill! but actually this is also a good startingpoint - to check how typefaces of the same designer can be combined. a lot do well because proportions are similar. good way to start analysing what makes it work. I just combined Franklin Gothic Condensed with theSans Mono for a Arts-Event and Catalog seems to work fine. generally contrasts should be high - also contrasts in style - and usually no more than three typefaces on the same page - except logos etc. for combining sans and serif families on a level of same size they should have about the same x-height/proportions of heights. though it still is hard to match then. i found just a very few where it works ok. jan tschichold wrote some general rules in his book "die neue typographie" - the new typography or in his collection of writings i dont remember but i know that there is also a chapter in paul renners classic the art of typography. there is a nice basic examples online at http://www.ideabook.com/typepale.htm go there and analyse. so far t.

I'd start with making "The Elements of Typographic Style" by Robert Bringhurst the required book. He talks about using letter shapes and the timeframe the type was designed as criteria in determining what fonts marry well. The book also addresses a great many other issues germane to your class.
