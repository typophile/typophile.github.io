I think you have a few issues to fix with the look of the logo. I don’t know if the “sparkling fruit remedy” should be kerned across the entire length of the word. It just seems too open. I’d almost like to see you still explore more with the U and the “bubbles.” I think there’s a better solution out there for that. Perhaps try using the circles inside the U – containing the fizz as the bottle is not yet open when it’s on the shelf. I would also think about the placement of the pear, as I find that is the strongest solution of the naming conventions displayed. Remember, at eye level you’re reading “cure” at the top of the bottle, you want to quickly find the flavor and not search for it at the bottom. Perhaps it’s right above or below the wordmark. I hope this helps – best of luck.

2007-04-09 05:38:23 • wolfattack

Trying out all these suggestions has been a great exercise in exploration and comparison, but to be honest, I’m not convinced it has necessarily improved upon your original preferred workmark.

In your first post, your gut was telling you that the option on the right was perhaps better than your first. I think your gut was right, as many here have said. However, I’m of the opinion that flattening the bottom of the “U” was unnecessary and has failed to make it a recognizable drinking glass. The (questionable) benefit of seeking out this resemblance is outweighed by how awkward this new “U” now looks beside the other letterforms. Looking at it, I was left wondering: Wouldn’t a convincing test tube be better than an unconvincing drinking glass? Haven’t we just inadvertantly turned it into a beaker? http://img.alibaba.com/photo/51184814/Beaker.jpg

In the end, we get a sort of drinking glass/test tube hybrid. A compromise. In my mind, the test tube connotation was much bolder, more intriguing and conceptually richer. Your original preferred design achieves everything the latest version does, and is, in my opinion, the simpler and more elegant of the two.

As for identifying the flavour, I think you’re on the right track using a combination of colour and icon. Keep Brian’s suggestion in mind, use text creatively and avoid being redundant.
