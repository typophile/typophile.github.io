@AenTan Thank you for creating an article about signage typefaces. When it comes to designing signs using type you must make a difference between its surroundings in which the signs/wayfinding systems will be placed. Typefaces like ClearView and Interstate can work beautifully for road signs but will not work so good at for instance hospital wayfinding. Therefore it is difficult to compile a list of typefaces that will work under all conditions.

From my findings, these are (some of) the characteristics of a good signage typeface:

  • Usage in a clear and straightforward design
  • Easy recognizable letterforms
  • Large x-height
  • Positive letter spacing to enhance the visual appearance
  • The typeface includes a package of many different weights

2009-04-14 19:16:55 • mlvr

in general I think the problems and solutions are pretty similar.

Ralf, I’m surprised you say that. The problem of reading road signs at night is a pretty special problem, not shared by hospital corridors, which are generally well lit, at least in richer countries.

Reading a sign at night, whether it is reflective or not, is an added problem because of low light. The eye just doesn’t work as well at low light, particularly for older drivers. As I remember this was the main ‘brief’ for James M’s Clearview: it had to work better than alternatives at night for older drivers.

Also in a hospital you can just move closer to a sign. You don’t have to worry about missing an exit, so there isn’t the same degree of demand for clarity at a distance.

I would think that for hospital signage there would be much more leeway on readability under adverse conditions.

Ralf,

When we were first confronted with this terminology we were quite confused. As far as I can tell, from what traffic researchers tell me, is that it has something to do with the number one gets when calculating the reflection of the image. A positive contrast yields a positive number, negative contrast yields a negative number.

When we were naming the different ClearviewHwy fonts, Phil Garvey, one of the researchers suggested that we use W to indicate White letters, and B to indicate black letters, rather than use Positive or Negative in the actual name.
