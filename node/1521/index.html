Nonono, not a cheap Clarendon copy, please don't say that!

Egiziano is the digital version of an 1815 (!) typeface
by Vincent Figgins (b. 1766, d. 29|2|1844), an English
type founder. He trained at Joseph Jackson's type foundry
in London, and founded in 1972 a type foundry which existed
until 1908. It's in 1815 that he published a book of type
specimens, the first to depict typefaces (labeled "Antiques")
which later became known as slab-serif typefaces.

So you could argue it actually is the other way around, as
Egiziano predates Clarendon by 138 years!

If you're planning to use Egiziano: the best digital
reinterpretation of a Figgins slab-serif is David Berlow's Giza,
which is very similar to Egiziano.

The Craw Clarendon family of types was a huge success when made available to North American printers in the mid fifties. I loved it dearly, particulary in the bold weight.

It was a revelation to me years later to see that this type of letter was common in the newspaper file copies of The Columbian. (This paper was begun in New Westminster just prior to the start of the American Civil War). The Clarendon-type faces were available it seems in quite a few variants of weight and width, in both metal and woodtype.

Egizio is was another inspiration, made in metal after WW2 by the Nebiolo of Italy, and was also available in medium and bold with accompanying italic.
