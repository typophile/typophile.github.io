What does everyone think of [[Dolly]] by [[Underware]]: http://www.myfonts.com/fonts/underware/dolly/ It looks like a very legible, general purpose font. It has a certain warmth and charm that are tugging at my pocketbook. It's a rather high-contrast face, which might take some getting used to, although that might do my readers a favour. For some reason this face doesn't get alot of attention. Or maybe I haven't been around the font scene long enough. Supposing I do buy this doggy in the window, can anyone suggest a good sans for this?
Check the crit thread/logo and i.d. and look for small gallery mark post. I'm going this direction with a small university gallery, largely because they need something good for wall text. Here's my short take on Dolly. It is very legible but also really versatile. The SC has a little different flavor than the Roman. The ital a little different than the roman. The bold, very sweet, but again the range of possible uses is very large. I've paired it with Formata, which has a very different x-height but fills in for tiny text etc. I'm using the combination to provide different looks for different shows, but all within this marriage of the two families. Working well so far. I have some super rough examples posted in the mentioned thread that will give you some idea. http://typophile.com/node/16675 Cheers

2005-12-22 21:02:37 • Palatine
Well,.... You've grasped the typographic meaning of contrast completely. Didot is high-contrast, Helvetica is extremely low-contrast. Even if you're using a yellow ink, Clifford is still darker than Centaur, because it was digitized and spaced that way. It doesn't refer to a literal gray value. You could easily compare 2 different weights of the same design in terms of their darkness. "The light weight is too light on the page, the Bold is too dark". The term would be qualified by what you are using it to describe. A dark tale could be printed on dark paper with a dark typeface in a dark ink, by a pressman in a dark mood, at a dark time in history. In this scenario dark refers to everything, but in different ways. None of these describe making type look thicker or darker on paper. Letterpress printing always carries the possibility of a darker or lighter impression (yet another variable), which can affect the look of the page, independently of the typeface or ink chosen. No need to avoid a term like dark, as long as it refers to something specific. For better or worse, typography and type design are full of arcane terminology, and some of it is not intuitive or logical at all. Continue at your own peril.... ; D
