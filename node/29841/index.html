Alphabets to Order: The Literature of
Nineteenth-Century Typefounders' Specimens
by Alastair Johnston Publisher: Oak Knoll Books; (October 2000) Paperback: 220 pages ISBN: 1584560096 Price: $39.25  Buy It! Reviewed by Poltroon: This book started out to show how Dadaism and concrete poetry were foreshadowed in the anonymous nonsense texts of type specimen books in the nineteenth century and grew into a much larger cultural study of the reading habits, politics and amusements of that era. The type specimens (many of which are illustrated) are a springboard to a wider discussion of many topics from hermits to the alcoholism of printers. Most of the fun occurs in the footnotes (there is even a fake footnote that can be deciphered as a reference to PALE FIRE by Nabokov). There are checklists of rare specimens in public & private collections in Britiain & the US which can be consulted & very useful family trees showing how the holdings of various typefounders passed from hand to hand. In addition new light is shed on the earliest American wood types cut for display in the 1830s as well as some interesting stuff on the graphic appeal of large type.

The Elements of Typographic Style
by Robert Bringhurst Publisher: Hartley & Marks; 2nd edition (January 1997) Paperback: 320 pages ISBN: 0881791326 Price: $20.97  Buy It! Reviewed by William Berkson: Few books deserve the name 'great', but this is one. The author brings a combination of eloquence, learning, and passion for excellence, and the result is a stunning achievement. This work seems to be at the top of everyone's lists of books in typography.       One of the book's greatest strengths is also a weakness, though. Bringhurst has the classical taste in art - a taste that treasures the rare combination of serenity and elation, and the works of art that give us this feeling.      Following this taste, he perceptively separates out the Romantic elements that we have come to accept as normal, such as bold fonts, grotesque style sans, wide pages, and the em dash. And he revives and highlights the classical views of proportion and design in typography.       All of this provides a powerful new standard of excellence for book typography - and books are really Bringhurst's focus.       The weakness of this book is that the Romantic taste does indeed have its place, and this taste is neglected. Not only advertising, but also any short material is often best served by typography that is not serene, but rather provocative. What would quickly become tiresome in a book can be delightful in smaller doses, and a different context.       I share Bringhurst's classical tastes, so to me the neglect of advertising and the Romantic side of typography is no big loss. The Romantic taste is to be found everywhere else, so this book is a rare treasure: an exploration of the classical taste in an art that affects everyone. Do you own this book? Add your review!

Paul Renner: The Art of Typography
by Christopher J. Burke Publisher: Princeton Architectural Press; (January 1999) Paperback: 220 pages ISBN: 1568981589 Price: $24.50  Buy It! Reviewed by Nick Shinn: What fascinates me most about typography is the sheer amount of meaning contained in a typeface. A quantum hologram, the more you look the more you find.      No one has looked harder than Chris Burke, here. This is a heavy-duty piece of scholarship written by a type designer. And what a story, putting type design at the centre of world events and contextualizing it with respect to everything: the designer's personal life, his design work and teaching, technology, politics, culture, business, and so on. All the details of Renner's life illuminate Futura, including those that happened many years after he designed it.      As a type designer, I found reading this to be very empowering, giving great relevance to a geeky, obscure pursuit.

A Short History of the Printed Word
by Warren Chappell, Robert Bringhurst (Contributor) Publisher: Hartley & Marks; 2nd edition (April 2000) Paperback: 288 pages ISBN: 0881791547 Price: $24.95  Buy It! Reviewed by Paul Stanley: The title is only partly right. Short this certainly is, relative to the ground it covers. But it is hardly really a history -- more a sort of chronology with comments. It does engage to some extent with technical developments, but it doesn't attempt any real cultural or economic analysis. Overall the tone is antiquarian rather than historical, and Bringhurst's sententious style grows, to my mind at least, tiresome here. Although it does cover territory other than books, it does not do so thoroughly; its focus is very much on high typographical culture. It can be read with some pleasure by those who are already interested in printing. But I am underwhelmed by it. There surely is a fascinating history of the "printed word" to be written; but I don't think this is it.

I owe a huge deal of my understanding of typography to this book.
