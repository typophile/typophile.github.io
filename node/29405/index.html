Orange is a very difficult color to match in process. Even if you did find a book which had full-chroma Orange I would make a guess that it is because of the separate CMY inks which were used. This is part of the reason why the hexachrome process was developed, AFAIR, to enable richer process colors with the additional hits of color. Reds and Pinks suffer the same, just as blue does. They are only using 2 of the colors (mostly) to make the color and so aren’t as rich on the page. The best thing you can do is to continue to use Pantone but design around the shortcomings of the process. Get into bed, so to speak, with your printer and explain your concerns and see if they aren’t willing to help you find a solution. Perhaps, once you’ve asked, they will tell you of the process they recommend.

2006-11-10 17:57:11 • elliot100

Pantone are a commercial interest selling a comprehensive 4-colour book is probably against their best interests. Printers used to produce 4-colour sample books that they gave away to good customers, obviously not cheap to produce and not exhaustive (normally gradated on 10% changes). Ask your friendly neighbourhood printer they might produce one, some digital printers do as well, I have one on my desk. However, like Pantone books, the colours fade so a really old one will only have the cyan left. Double hitting can also improve your coverage – a spot colour might prove cheaper in the end. I think Hexachrome was mainly introduced to correct the purple and orange problems of cmyk.
