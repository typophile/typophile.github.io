2010-06-26 12:31:01 • nina

Greta looks fine for the most part, and won’t give you any trouble in terms of weight, although the style is a bit more contemporary than Newzald. Among Slimbach fonts, the best match might be Minion Pro (possibly the caption cut, to get the weight and x-height closer to Newzald), although this is less contemporary in style than Newzald. GFS Elpis might be in the right vicinity too.

However, do you need the ancient numerals (like koppa and sampi)? If so, Elpis doesn’t have them. Minion Pro has them in lowercase, which is usually - but not always, depending on the kind of texts you’re setting - all you need. Greta has both uppercase and lowercase forms, but they’re not always well drawn in relation to the rest of the character set.

2010-06-28 17:36:20 • Nick Shinn

Hi Nina,

Nick is right on about the desirability of similar vertical proportions, but I also agree with the others who suggest you don’t try to “match.” I think there’s a middle ground between matching and jarring: A complementary non-match is better than an imperfect attempt at matching.

Though I don’t have my copy (copies) of Elements of Typographic Style handy, I think Robert Bringhurst suggests something like this when combining Latin and Greek (and Cyrillic) scripts. I seems to me that when you’re using a type doesn’t have Greek letters, it might be best to look for something different enough not to look like a bad match. It’s kind of like the style convention of using italic for occasional foreign words (using Latin script) within an English language text.

I like the idea of a sans like Fedra – or any Greek face that’s got workable proportions but less stroke contrast than Newzald. Let us know how your experiments with it go.

Most likely, the unsophisticated expectation of the reader is that all the letters in a text, regardless of the alphabet they belong to, should look as if they were part of the same set, i. e. typeface. If this is not possible because the font does not contain all the characters needed, it might be least distracting to use a noticeably different typeface for a well-defined group of letters, namely Greek ones, than to make a moribund attempt at creating the perfect match. To my mind, this is not erudite, it is just good sense.

2010-07-13 17:53:35 • ansibil

I don’t speak Greek, but I would guess from the context that the Greek here would not be Modern Greek. Does anyone know whether it would ever make sense to apply monotonic accents to anything other than Standard Modern Greek?

As far as I know, anything from Classical Greek to the adoption of monotonic orthography would best be written with polytonic orthography, and Archaic Greek or less well-known ancient dialects would dispense with diacritics altogether. But since marking Classical Greek texts with diacritics began in the Hellenistic Period, writing Classical Greek terms without diacritics would also be a valid approach. I just feel that adopting a pseudo-monotonic orthography would be an anachronism here.

2010-07-18 23:16:37 • Jongseong
Yes, monotonic ancient Greek would be an absurdity. Whether you dispense with diacritics or not for early inscriptions is mostly a matter of whether you use the original uppercase only or turn them into lowercase. Non-Attic dialects in lowercase are still normally marked with diacritics. The main exception is Mycenaean Greek, which (at least in English-language contexts) is normally written in the Latin alphabet without diacritics, if it isn’t in the original Linear B.
