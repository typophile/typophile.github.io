Actually that thread might mislead you, depending on what you want to do. If you're not looking to produce working a working [[font]], but rather just creating "digital lettering", there's no real need to spend $500 on Fontlab. Although its tools might be more adapted to typeface design, it seems to me your work stands between text and illustration, so Illustrator should do fine, in fact it gives more possibilities with color, etc. As to how to do it in Illustrator, you should type the text, convert it to outlines and then mess with it. If you don't already, learn how to draw using bezier curves. That's how I do it, and I figure most people use the same technique for the type battle stuff.

Peter, Given that you are doing hand work, and may or may not end up making type, consider this: Illustrator and the like have autotracing features. Try scanning your finished artwork at huge resolution and autotracing it. From that you can see how much accuracy the autotrace will give (adjust the preferences), and then from there make small adjustments to the outlines by tweaking. That way you will have real projects to learn the bezier tools with. If you want to move away from autotracing in the future, you can do that organically. There might be pieces you scan and then draw bezier outlines over by hand, like if the art is very regular or simple and sleek. Meanwhile you can get more of your lettering in digital format so you can color it, move it around, etc. etc. One way beziers might be useful to you is if you draw them with the pencil tool in illustrator. It makes the outline for you, which you can then touch up. Using the stroke feature with such outlines, then "outline storke" could take you places. Your work blends lettering, painting, decoration, and is very lively and energetic. if you learn to draw with beziers, I hope you will keep the energy and freshness your hand-lettering and sketches have. Nice work!

The nice thing is that beziers are useful for any kind of art, and they're also ideal for drawing letters.... Add the functions of a type drawing program and you've started to design type. If you've spent a lot of time drawing or writing letters then you have a head start. Lots of type designers work on shapes in Illustrator and then move them to FOG or FL. I tend to leave Illustrator for times when I just want a single piece of art. I just remembered a useful technique you might like: draw a little circle with the circle tool in illustrator, the exact width of your typical outline, stem width, or other repeated feature. Use it as a caliper or gauge for lines you want the same width. It works no matter what angle the lines are, since it's a circle. It's cloudy in San Francisco as well.
