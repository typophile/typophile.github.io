Corpus, Cicero, Body...

August 30, 2005 • Z.Y.L

Since I’m a layman in typography, I’m puzzled by a passage in “Letter Froms” by Stanley Morison.

“The present Corpus will make possible, for the first time, close comparison of body sizes and will establish the mathematical relation, for example, of the French, German, English and Dutch “Cicero”. It was an economic disadvantage to the printer when, as in the instance of such a frequently used body as “Cicero”, the German body was larger than the French.”

What dose the “Corpus” in the first line mean? Does Stanley Morison want to say that due to different difinition of Cicero, the German body was larger than the French in the same amount of Cicero?

Thanks for all the replys that helps.

According to Luc Devroye, the difference is:

French system, after Fournier: 1 Cicero = 0.1648 inch German system: 1 Cicero = 12 Didot-Punkte = 4.404 mm = 0.1733858 inch

Without knowing the context of the quote, I would suggest that “corpus” refers to the body (cast metal slugs) of some typeface, since corpus is the Latin word for body, from which we get corpus delecti, habeas corpus, corporal, corpulent and corpse.
