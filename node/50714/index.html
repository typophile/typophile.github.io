2008-10-20 23:44:02 • Justin_Ch

In my opinion, you’re approaching this design way too literally. The concept for the blog isn’t visually congruent and to try and create a visual image to go along with the blog is honestly, out of your skill range. Not to offend.

I think you’d have better luck creating a clean logotype and matching it with health related images (stock photography perhaps) in the banner of the site.

Also, to reiterate what Jay said earlier, avoid the “computer/arcade” type. It’s a poor concept to assume that if you’re creating a logotype for a website it has to look “techy or futuristic”. Personally, I’d use a friendly slab serif typeface like Soho, Archer, Zine Serif or Officina Serif. Any of these fonts would bring a confident look without seeming too cold or tiresome.

2008-10-22 03:50:07 • ikovic

A ballpark would START at around AU$2000 - that doesn’t take into account relative factors for legitimate NFPs or NGOs.

And continuing about relativity… - i.e, how much value is placed on the project, it’s an image and identity - a face - for the organisation. Then you need to factor in things like; research (there always should be if the project is of any value to the business - the designer needs to get up to speed with the company, service/s, structure, audience & visual language, and competition etc. to understand it.) then … project timing; specific requirements; approval processes; project management etc.

You’re paying for the creative’s/designer’s time as a creative/visual thinker and designer - if you think that time is worth $100 you’ll get a $100 result - because you will get A result. No doubt about it. Having said that, if you value a designers time at $100 … you won’t have any choice but to be happy with whatever the results are. Clipart with a font might work … who knows.

I’ll refer you to the triangle … you can have any two sides included in your project. Which two are most important to you?

You’re paying for the creative’s/designer’s TIME of finding an effective and appropriate solution to your requirements and their EXPERIENCE in finding that effective and appropriate solution.

It’d be great if it were a fixed price industry … but it’s not. There are too many variables involved.

At the end of the day, one thing never changes. You get what you pay for.

2008-10-24 00:37:31 • TGaskin

I believe the reason logo prices vary so dramatically is about process and measuring outcomes.

Some designers spend quite a bit of time doing visual research, sketching, research on the company, competitors, etc. to get a good idea of what the business is before trying to portray it visually. This is how major corporations do things.

Most often, a logo is not enough. You need business papers (letterhead, business card), and a style guide on how to implement the new look on other materials. Otherwise, you have a nice logo implemented badly, making the whole process moot. So, its hard to just quote for a logo.

Other designers just draw whatever they think might work on the computer, and it takes an hour. There are no restrictions to who calls themselves a designer, or what the process of designing a logo entails.

Another issue is very few designers try to measure the outcome of their success, so you can’t tell if it is successful.

Observatory to me is astronomy. Maybe there is a way to get the concept of “Health” in a simple (!) constellation. Or perhaps you use constellation / star chart in your supporting elements, and keep the logo typographic.

Logo Cost:

Consider two types of logo clients I’ve encountered: one will love whatever I do and sign off on the first concept, others want 20 versions and then want to build a logo blending them all. If you are client one and agree to pay hourly, you might be in for a cheap(er) logo. The other, not so much. For a fixed-price estimate I will split the difference and include an hour maximum to save me from client #2. For a small/new business the numbers thrown around (2-5k) seems reasonable.
