2004-03-30 23:59:02 • Si_Daniels
I'm still uncomfortable with the term so I alternate it with "character." I just can't get myself to refer to a drawing of a letter as a glyph.

Leslie, please do not alternate glyph and character, they are not synonymous terms in digital typography. The distinction of character (semantic encoded text entity) and glyph (conventional representation of character) is extremely important in making sense of the relationship between text processing (input, editing, storage, exchange) and text display (rendering, typographic layout, etc.). If you confuse these two terms, you leave yourself without a meaningful vocabulary for discussing this relationship. The fact that the relationship of character to glyph is not one-to-one makes it particularly important that the distinction is maintained.

You may find my article on OpenType Layout useful in understanding the distinction between and relationship of characters and glyphs.

2004-03-31 03:57:04 • lescab
To put it really simply (and not perfectly accurately), A glyph is a letter drawing, and a character is a specific encoding slot. In a font, a given character (slot) has a default glyph (outline) associated with it.

So, all three of those example uses are valid. However, to make the contrast more clear, one might say for #3, "I accidentally pasted the glyph for the character A into the window for the character B."

John's definition is the most correct. But if you want us to simplify (at the risk of losing some accuracy), I'd say: A character is the abstract idea of a letter, like the letter "A." A glyph is a particular version of a letter. So the character "A" in Warnock Pro Italic has a default glyph, but also has at least a couple of alternate glyphs, including a swash form and a small cap. So there's one character, but three glyphs.

Of course, characters actually go beyond letters. Things like numbers or even musical notes or certain symbols can be characters, as long as they have defined encoding slots, and are not simply alternate forms of something else.

It's also worth noting that the relationship between glyphs and characters is n-to-m, where n and m are integers zero or greater. In plain English, that suggests that several characters may combine to form one glyph (as in a fraction or ligature), and several glyphs may combine to form a single character (as with an accented letter, created on the fly from a letter glyph and an accent glyph).

It gets even more complicated with some other languages. English is the "easy" case. And there's a whole realm of debate on when to encode glyphs as their own characters, but I've spent too much time on that in email with John lately, so I think I won't go there tonight....

2004-03-31 06:20:53 • Thomas Phinney
Leslie, I think the most elegant simplification of "glyph" would be to say that it's "an instance of a character", meaning that it's a given rendering of a letter, numeral, or really any symbol, even stuff that's not encoded. The other terms, like "character", "letter", "symbol" are really abstract concepts, with no inherent physical presence, maybe not even a structural definition*. That's why really unorthodox glyphs can often be identified as being a certain letter with great certainty.

* I've come to realize for example that you can never really show the essence of a letter, you can only describe it.

2004-03-31 09:56:37 •
I'm not being facetious. Seems to me that "glyph" is commonly used both to describe a
letter drawing and a specific slot in a font. Is this right?

Yes, because a font is just a big collection of glyphs. Even within a font, and within FontLab, the relationship of a glyph cell to character(s) is not one-to-one: a single glyph can be mapped to multiple characters (this is very common in TrueType font, e.g. the /space/ glyph will be mapped to both the regular space character and the non-breaking space character).

I believe the easiest way to think about the distinction is to accept that 'character' is no longer a typographic term. Glyph is a typographic term that refers to the little images and to their storage in a font. Character is a text encoding term that refers to the abstract identity of, e.g. an uppercase A, that is stored as a number on a computer. The two concepts only come together during display of text, when the characters in the text are mapped to glyphs in a given font.

2004-04-01 07:21:03 • dotsara
I bought and am reading the book. I think it's delightful and useful. It is full of useful information, a significant amount of which is new to me, and it is done with charm and a light touch.

I particularly appreciate the creative and excellent typography.

You'd think that good typography would be a given in a book about type, but it seems that is often not so. For example, am I the only one who thinks that 'Type & typography' by Baines and Haslam is a typographic disaster?

A typographic comparison - appropriate as both are full of type and lettering illustrations, presenting a similar design challenge.

Both are on bright white coated paper, which of course helps with the illustrations, especially color. Baines & Haslam put all their type on the bright white pages, with a resulting dazzle that detracts from readability. Carbarga softly shades most pages, or uses colored type against white, squelching the dazzle, enhancing readability and adding a pleasant variety.

For text Bains and Haslam use a small size (9 or 10 point) of Swift in a long measure - I count 80 characters - and try to counteract the wide line with large leading - something like 10/14 point. Swift was designed for newspapers, which have narrow columns closely set, and hence is a condensed face with large x-height. Using it totally differently I think flops. The long line with the short extenders is just plain unpleasant to read, especially with the dazzle.

By contrast Cabarga uses the ridiculously easy-to-read Century Schoolbook (I think), whose wide letters match the sometimes wide measure, with an effortly readable result.

The captions and side bars in Bains and Haslam are in Meta, negatively tracked so tight that it is hard to read, for me at least.

The captions and side bars in Carbarga are in narrow columns in a compressed sans (what is it?) which is very readable. It adds fuel to my belief that only narrow sans work well in text. In any case the typeface and the measure and leading are beautifully matched.

Finally, Bains and Haslam have quotations on typography from various luminaries. They are set in a huge, 42 point Meta, with no leading, running across two-page spreads. The text runs continuously, but the two-page spreads are separated by 32 pages. This puts the spreads at the middle of signatures, so the text can align perfectly across the two pages. Clever, but reading the stuff is like putting together a jigsaw puzzle. Also the credits for the quotations jump back to the 10 point Swift, a totally different scale - a violation of Cabarga's rule 20 of the "Immutable laws of Lettering - and the myriad of exceptions thereto."

Overall, the effect in Bains and Haslem is of clever innovation whose result is off-puttingly difficult to read, and visually ungainly.

In Cabarga, I think more consistency from page spread to page spread would make the design stronger, as he uses many different principles to organize his page spreads. This threatens toward the visual chaos, but he manages to keep it undercontrol, perhaps by control of scale from page to page. The bottom line is that every page spread is inviting, readable, and effectively gets across what he wants to explain. To convey such a large amount of technical information in such an agreeable way - both visually and literarily - is an impressive achievement.
