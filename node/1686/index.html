I don't think that linguistics got much from Wittgenstein. Certainly not from the whole 'picture theory' bit. But the later stuff - the 'ordinary language' stuff - has it's impact. Even if every linguist isn't proclaiming the name of Wittgenstein.

I don't think much from the early 20th century has much impact on contemporary linguistics - not even the 'proper linguists' like Bloomfield and Sapir. Transformational synatx and generative semantics didn't really come about until the 1950s - and that's more or less the [general] model for what people are doing now.

The ideas of people like J L Austin are pretty well respected and, as far as I'm aware, they deal with similar things to later Wittgenstein (e.g., Philosophical Investigations). It's still the whole 'ordinary language' idea. And, like Austin's book says, they're both dealing with how language really works, and how we do things with it. But those ideas are all from around the 1950s - the birth of modern linguistics.

That's my understanding, for whatever it may be worth.

My direct experience is as an undergrad, grad and lecturer in humanities and social science depts in English universities (old & new).
