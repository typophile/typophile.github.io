Is there any real point to section, paragraph, mu, etc. in display and poster fonts?

December 1, 2010 • phrostbyte64

The fonts I create are display or poster fonts. I’ve worked mostly in the sign and screen print industry, so these are what I mostly use. My question, is there any reason to include document and math glyphs (such as mu, section, paragraph, etc.) in a font that no sane person would use on text applications? For signs, some of the math glyphs like the plus or minus glyph are necessary for acreages and legal descriptions. The other’s seem pointless. I usually use those places for alternate characters and symbols. Would this, in theory, reduce the marketability of a font.

James

I don’t think one can determine how one’s fonts will be used. Or misused. Typographers can be quite ingenious, if not downright perverse.

For instance, I have seen the Section symbol rotated through 90 degrees and used as a poster ornament.

The only area of discretion I would admit to typefounders in departing from standard encodings is to omit certain obsolete or obscure characters, such as Greenlandic k.

I dropped the extra math characters from Mac Roman or Win 1252 from my fonts because anyone setting that stuff should use a typeface meant for setting math. I do leave in section because that one gets use in legal advertising. I don’t waste effort on the generic currency symbol as it is only used by financial publications which do not use retail fonts. But I do draw pilcrows and daggers because I enjoy drawing them. A good baseline for these is H&FJ’s Latin-X encoding: if they can sell that many fonts without those characters then it’s pretty clear that nobody is complaining.
2010-12-01 23:57:48 • blank

I used to leave out the never-gonna-get-used-anyway-math symbols (NGGUAMS) in display fonts. But I’ve had so many requests to fill in sets to please corporate clients that a couple of years ago I started adding them as a rule.

Videogame companies very commonly require NGGUAMS. I don’t know what it’s like these days but when I used to make games, Sony would reject fonts that didn’t have NGGUAMS. It didn’t matter that they would never be used in-game, that was their policy. Sony will not listen to reason. My fonts tend to be used in videogames often so it’s NGGUAMS all around.
