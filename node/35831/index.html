2007-08-08 06:07:19 • Hiroshige

The problem likely begins with the fact that the bulk of Bringhurst’s poetry has been published by small Canadian presses, and, as noted above, many of those books are out of print (I’m talking strictly about his poetry here). There is some cross-over, of course: for example, his Ursa Major is a book of poetry (of sorts) that he also designed with Andrew Steeves at Gaspereau Press (and for which he received an Alcuin award [Canada’s national book design awards]), and it’s still available at a regular trade price. A few of his other books of poetry are still available on back-lists from various Canadian publishers, but, as above, the bulk of it is out of print. The argument above about his books being out of reach (due to price) really doesn’t apply to the bulk of his poetry, much of which was published long before he wrote ETS; the problem is simply that they’re out of print and hard to track down.

As for the expensive things, his most recent projects such as the Koch edition of Parmenedes or the elaborate New World Suite are really exceptions, and are designed and produced as fine-press projects that are not really meant for a strictly reader-audience. Then there are a few poetry collections that are also out reach due to cost and limited release (such as Barbarian Press' edition of The Blue Roofs of Japan, or Ninja Press' edition of The Book of Silences), but there are also plenty of basic trade editions of his work, many of which are listed in his Wikipedia listing.

2007-08-09 17:04:39 • jason

The market for poetry is minute; most writers are lucky to sell a few hundred copies – very lucky. In fact, it’s a bit like fonts.

Because words are particularly important in poetry, and you are meant to dwell on them, the type requires more attention than in other material, as everyone who publishes poetry knows. An astonishing amount of poetry is still published, expensively, today, on letterpress using metal Bembo, simply because there aren’t any digital alternatives that look as good in poetry.

Not to say that there haven’t been great poetry books published in other ways, for example offset from a typewriter – . But that wouldn’t be appropriate for Bringhurst – who probably, given his interest in digital type, wouldn’t go the metal route – I don’t know what his current thinking is on that particular issue, it would be interesting to find out.

Frankly, I’m amazed that anything Bringhurst does is finally open to criticism. I and some others were pilloried for doing that a few years ago!

By the way, what’s so bad about spending $100 for a book? If you think that’s a lot, maybe your priorities are wrong, because no matter how poor you are, $100 is not that very much money. It’s not like you’re being asked to choose between a book and a car. That said, I don’t myself spend a lot on books, though I would if I could!

The curious thing about the tight, impoverished poetry world is this: a few years ago, Poetry Magazine was given $90 million in someone’s will, a meteoric, absolutely unprecedented event in the entire history of poetry. I would have thought the result would, could, have been, a lot of great and beautiful subsidized poetry publishing: exquisite books that could sell for a very few dollars. Has any of this happened? What is the magazine doing with all its lucre?
