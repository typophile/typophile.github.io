My feeling is that a ball terminal (or any particular terminal convention) usually exists not merely as a style of "decoration" but also a functional way to add a particular kind of weight to that stroke. And I can imagine an /a/ and an /f/ in the same font that would have rather different needs for weight at their tops. In that case, identical terminals for consistency's sake wouldn't be particularly desirable. In my experience, a close look at successful fonts often reveals more inconsistency in terminal treatments than I expected to find.
2010-09-13 18:44:43 • eliason
How can it be a sans-serif typeface if it has balls? Ball terminals are a kind of serif. Look at Archer and Bodoni Egyptian. They are monoline faces with ball terminals, but also serifs, slab in fact. Imagine them without the straight serifs. Perhaps something like Sari manages the integration, mixing balls with rounded "plain" terminals, due to greater contrast of stroke thickness. But if you're following a hard-edged didone model, perhaps the answer is small balls. Experiment and see what works!
