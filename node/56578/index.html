I would have to agree with David here. If you trace a couple different styles of type, your hand will start to become used to the ‘movement’ of the type. From there, you can do what I do and draw yourself a baseline, x and cap height on a piece of paper then draw that same type freehand. Even if your new drawings aren’t perfect ‘tracings’ you’re still teaching your brain and arm how type flows. You’re essentially creating the Queneau Caslon or Queneau Bodoni or Queneau Fette Fraktur. Get it?

Hand lettering is not something you trace and draw. It is a function of the tool, either pen or brush, to make strokes which form the letters. It is technique, not mechanics. Drawing the letters is drawing, not lettering.

2009-04-04 16:28:36 • phrostbyte64

Hand lettering is not something you trace and draw.

It can be. There are many techniques of lettering, including the “built up” method, which is basically drawing the outline of the letters and then filling them in. You will see this covered in most how-to books about lettering. The page on the Letterheads site describes the typical method of showcard lettering, in which production speed and efficiency is most important, and usually only a brush and a minimum number of strokes is used for the finished lettering.

EDIT: to me the difference between calligraphy and lettering (as I practice it) is that the form that is laid down is not the “final word.” I futz with it for hours on end!

This is a tough topic to tackle… there are many facets to “hand” lettering. You are seeing the schisms as people post. Different people consider lettering to be different things (I noticed that you eschewed calligraphy BUT you have to remember that is is an integral part of understanding the tools). Tools can include the pencil and tracing paper, as well as ruling pens, chisel edged pens, brushes, round pens, square pens, pointed pens etc. ad infinitum. Then you can get into the division of commercial lettering artists, sign painters, sho card artists etc. (once again). What was lettering in the twenties is not the lettering of today. Engrossing is not the same as lettering of the ‘50’s and is not the same as today. What they did for movie posters is not the same as they did for titling on product… is not the same as they did for grocery store windows… so you can see that it is a DIVERSE field.

It is funny, as many of my lettering heroes reside in different camps. Some are calligraphers, others are sho card artists, sign painters, “lettering artists,” and the list continues.

As far as books… the chronologies are the best, but they do not show step by step. It is material that is meant to be absorbed, possibly copied until you get your wings, and or are inspirational in nature. The 21 editions of the speedball books is a good place to start, AS WELL AS some of the other stellar recommendations.

I know this is disjointed and disheveled but it is better than if I delineated every thought I had on the subject. Talk about War and Peace : )

Lettering Today The ABC books by Eric Lindegren Calligraphy Today by Heather Child (lots of lettering in there) Creative Stroke (1 & 2)

These are fairly contemporary… 60’s on. See the other similar thread that is going on for some more selections.

Oh my goodness the list is endless and the books cover my walls in multiple rooms. So decide what facet you want to explore and let me know and I will send you a list!

One of the exercises I run myself through (and it’s fun too) is to try to exaggerate the letterform in new and in interesting ways. While that aspect is fun the learning part comes in as you’re doing it - take notice of what letter attributes characterise a form as one letter, and not another; or the similarities and differences between uppers and lowercases.

It’s also a good exercise to loosen up the brain and get the gears spinning.

Believe it or not Queneau, Herr Zapf’s letters are more drawn than they are calligraphic. In the formation of a letter he employs many small strokes to achieve the form. It is, in essence, a building process. Most assume that letters so elegant would be done in a fluid motion but they are not.
