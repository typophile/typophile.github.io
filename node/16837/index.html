Best font for...

December 16, 2005 • Giordano

I am working on the layout for a magazine about the Montreal music scene, called The Montreal Chord. The magazine covers everything local, from new start-ups to established acts, and shows and festivals. It also has a liberal editorial line and some serious news features. The layout uses very tightly set Century Gothic for Section Names and headlines. The editorial pages have a serious black and white design, whereas the rest adds colours and vibrant pictures. The magazine really focusses on great pictures, and the clean, modern layout accentuate this.

I am now trying to decide on a good font for body text… I don’t know whether to use Century Gothic all the way through, or to use a different font. If I choose to go with a different one, I would be looking for something a little retro, reminiscent of the days of 8-tracks and the October Crisis. Any suggestions?

I definitely would NOT use century gothic for body copy, but I am a big fan of legibility, and I don’t consider body copy the appropiate place to get overly creative, or “designy.” There’s plenty of opportunity to get more expressive with the headlines, subheads, pull quotes, etc… There are a lot of good serif typefaces that could work with century gothic. There should be other threads on this site that would cover magazine body copy. Sounds like a great project, good luck with it…

Thanks Giordano. The two families I suggested were designed with history in mind.

Oz is an hommage to and reflection of the work of Oz Cooper; channeling Cooper Black (and the rest of the Cooper family) in a contemporary way, and “constructed” as we are able to do with digital technology.

Polaris is an unabashed capital-M Modernist family in the mould of Helvetica, Univers, and Akzidenz Grotesk which inspired those types.

Some of the lesser priests of Modernism descended upon Montreal from Switzerland and Germany in the run-up to the 1976 Olympics, meanwhile Oz Cooper’s work has always appealed to Montrealers, with our love-hate relationship with American corporations and popular communications. We embraced internationalism and the International Style, but couldn’t help ourselves from mixing it up with some sweaty funky expressionism.

For something that would be a 1970 authentic Canadian magazine text face, but not a type that is still widely used, I would recommend Bookman. It has a heft that would hold its own against the Century Gothic, and they are a good match history and style-wise. As a Linotype face, it has the period-but-modern feature of equal-width roman and italic. It also had some cachet as a display face at that time, especially the Italic with swash.

Of my own faces, I would recommend Worldwide, a Century revival, with the large x-height coming into vogue in the early ’70s.
