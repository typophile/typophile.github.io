Claims about what is most readable are being made all the time, most often with little or no solid research basis. It is prudent to check into such claims thoroughly before accepting them, because there are so many unreliable ones.

2006-12-01 03:35:34 • dezcom

They are wrong; all of them. If they claim just about any superior performance, preference, whatever; either the sample is ridiculous or the interpretation is skewed, or the testing conditions are completely divorced from real reading situations, or some combination of these and any number of other flaws in methodology and design. The only accurate answer is to say that everyone has a different opinion and there’s no proof of any opinion as yet.

The author might not be contradicting himself; as an example of the meaninglessness of the various studies, the findings he cites could indeed say that adult readers preferred Jenson but found Gill more legible. What that’s saying is that nobody learned anything valuable.

“I hope there is agreement in the basic questions already,”

Actually some typophiles are much more interested in the mechanism of reading in general, others more interested in learning about preference, or specifics about type design that influence readability. If you go outside our bubble, you find others whose interest starts from their work with the vision-impaired, or even from government-mandated standards that resulted from some of this dubious research. Since this dialog is heating up, and previously disconnected points of view are interacting, there’s an improving outlook for meaningful testing, so we are making slow progress.
