April 13, 2009 • west_46

Hi, I have a problem with (and now I try to explain it in my best Englisch) a single last paragraph word on a new line. I work in InDesign CS3 and I don't want that the last word of a paragraph starts on a knew line. I now the ways of correcting this manually, but I am looking for a way to prevent this. Any one a good idea /tip?
Hallo Hans, you can use GREP to solve this issue. In the search/replace window (in InDesign CS ≥3), there’s a tab called ‘GREP’. Search for \s\w+?\W+?$ \s is a placeholder for ‘any space character’. (That’s the last word space in your paragraph.) \w+? stands for ‘one or more word characters’. (Matches the last word.) \W+? is for ‘one or more non-word characters’: it matches the dot, but also the question mark, exclamation mark etc. Why ‘one or more’? Because your paragraph could end with a closing quotation mark and a punctuation mark. Maybe there’s even an additional superscript character, indicating a footnote. The final $ stands for: ‘look at paragraph endings only’.* Now you can easily add the ‘no break’ property (directly or via character style) to the hits. F *) Maybe there’s a more elegant way; I’m still getting used to GREP. Love to hear ideas.

I think most general rules or thoughts about this have all already been mentioned: When working with indents, the ending line better should be longer than the indent below. In justified setting, the most important aspect is even spacing. Applying a ‘no-break’ attribute to the last two words might counteract that goal. Certain circumstances (e.g. before subheads, as Charles mentioned, or your example with repeating words; ‘zijn’) are especially unfortunate and might demand a workaround. In ragged-right settings, things can be handled more permissively. If it’s the last paragraph of the column or page, I try to achieve an ending line with more than one word, ideally filling half the column width or more.

2009-04-16 05:48:57 • Nick Shinn
what do you do when the last line of a paragraph is flush left with “normal” word space widths, and just about every other line has much wider word spaces? Don’t you think that looks odd? Yes, you're right. And it does happen. The frequency depends on the text of course, but also on the layout program. For instance, I find that InDesign does this -- and other infelicities -- if (1) you use the paragraph composer, and (2) you don't get good values for min-ideal-"not honored" (aka max) spacing. The "best" values can change a bit with the size of the type and the measure. Not to pick on InDesign, TeX had a its oddities too. The penalty for increasing the word space was the same as for decreasing the word space, on a percentage basis. Well, using real numbers, if you set the minimum spaceband to 180 and the maximum (which TeX somewhat honors) to 400, using any value other than 290 as "ideal" (halfway in between) would skew the results, based on change penalties computed as a percentage. But 290 is too big; 240 is more like it. Since using TeX with these parameters "encouraged" adding space to justify, we wrote another routine adding a demerit for setting more lines that absolutely needed in a paragraph. The value you used for this demerit would control the spacing behavior. Too much information, I know. The point is, for setting extended text, you have to understand how your layout program works, & adapt it to your needs/desires.
