Take care of yourself

May 16, 2011 • Nick Shinn

A recent medical study compared subjects spending the same amount of time in front of a computer: one group did so with relatively few interruptions, the other splitting the time into smaller segments. Those who spent long periods seated at the computer had a worse record for heart conditions, etc.

And today another study in the news, comparing sitting with standing. Needless to say, you’re better off standing. (Winston Churchill, he of the cigars, brandy, and 90-year lifespan, is the poster boy for standing — he wrote all his many books standing, and pacing, at a lectern.)

So if you’re inclined to hunker down on a font (or whatever) for many hours at a time, beware.

I can also add my personal experience. I always thought I was pretty fit—worked out regularly, ate sensibly, good body/mass index. But last year I had a coronary problem (thankfully fixed with a stent), which I now attribute to all those dusk-to-dawn sessions at the computer.

So my advice, take lots of breaks. And get your 8 hours sleep.

Also, get a sonic toothbrush and brush your teeth everyday.

2011-05-17 15:02:22 • Chris Dean

Standing is better than sitting, but frankly I’m a bit dubious about that first study mentioned. Standing provides very minimal exercise, and it certainly isn’t the kind of aerobic exercise that benefits the heart. I wonder if maybe the study just found a correlation, rather than an actual cause-and-effect relationship.

If the two groups in the study were identical in every respect other than the frequency of standing, the only medical connections I can see offhand might be 1) a possible reduction in blood clots that can be caused by long periods of sitting (deep venous thrombosis), or 2) a reduction in pressure ulcers (often called “bed sores”) which are caused by remaining in the same position for long periods of time, and can sometimes lead to more serious health problems, especially with folks who already have health problems (elderly, diabetic, paralyzed, etc).

Don’t get me wrong, standing is a good idea. I’m just a bit dubious that standing, by itself, can be statistically linked to a reduction in cardiac disease.
