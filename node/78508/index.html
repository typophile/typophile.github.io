richard said:

E-pub, which, IMHO, is useless, stupid, and counter-productive.

well, what else would you expect from the corporate publishers? look at the members of i.d.p.f., which created the .epub format. (i remember their acronym as short for “i design poor formats.")

the e-book revolution is making their business-model extinct, as that model relies on managing the scarcity of p-books, so the .epub “standard” is one of their attempts to stifle_change.

it’s worth pointing out that .epub evolved from the o.e.b. format, also maintained by i.d.p.f., which became the “standard” back at the end of the 1990s, and the sabotage attempt did work then.

thanks to the kindle, however, which uses another format (.mobi), .epub’s shortcomings won’t be able to sideline e-books any more.

which is not to say that the corporate publishers have given up… to this very day, for instance, they’ve still neglected to ever create “reference implementations” for either an .epub authoring-tool or an .epub viewer-program. this means that other companies have had to start from scratch to build their viewer-programs, and – as anyone familiar with such a situation could readily tell you – the result is a bunch of apps badly inconsistent with the “standard” and inconsistent with each other in terms of their inconsistencies.

which means the e-book arena is now re-experiencing the awful nightmare of “browser inconsistencies” that plagued us in the past.

Browsers are ubiquitous - why not just go with HTML5?

well, first of all, browsers make very bad e-book viewer-programs.

aside from that, html5 would be ok, if you’re starting from scratch. but since i.d.p.f. doesn’t really want stuff to work, i’m guessing that they will choose instead to continue along their present path, and try to continue to support their legacy format which has served their (ulterior) motives so fine all along the line. or, to put it another way:

if you like that it’s broke, and even broke it on purpose, why fix it?

I have been dealing with this issue, too. We are developing a new platform for digital cookbooks and for us the ability to have well designed pages is critical. I’ve been talking with various foundries and I’ve been getting very different messages. So far, we have two foundries which are offering to provide us a package of 6000+ fonts for an annual license of a big over $12,000.

Our approach is to build the pages in HTML5 and use @fontface to integrate the fonts. When someone purchases a cookbook from us they would get the HTML5 files and fonts loaded from our server onto their iPad in a .zip file. So far only Hoefler & Frere-Jones has categorically refused to license for this type of deployment. We happen to have one author who uses their Knockout typeface. So, we need to find a suitable substitute. That isn’t great news. If we were to embed the font in the app itself, then they would allow that for an $800 annual fee.

We need a solution that allows authors/designers/publishers to create digital books using the typefaces that we use for print without any additional fees. For many books it would be prohibitive to have an additional $800 annually tacked on to the costs of the digital publication. I don’t think anyone would have a problem with some security around embedded fonts in HTML or ePub3 documents. But, the current solution won’t work long-term. Books are going digital and a dramatic increases in costs for typefaces can’t be supported financially in the long-term.

Of course, it’s obvious. Except to the folks who sell typefaces. When God wants to be unkind he makes you a part of the copyright industry and sends fairy-godmothers like Microsoft and Adobe to watch over you.

income for type designer and a reasonable protection against pirating

There is a simple solution to this. It flows from the fact that very, very few fonts made for graphics will also work well in browsers without adaptation for the purpose. (Epub rendering engines are browser engines. I don’t know of any exceptions or why anyone would want to deviate and re-invent the wheel. Amazon has flat-out announced that HTML5 will be it’s publishing language going forward.)

What foundries should be licensing for this work are their fonts post-processed and re-tasked for the web. Got OTFs with a million kerning pairs and advanced OT features guaranteed to send a tickle up your leg? Great, keep selling them. (Yeah, I know, licensing them.) But please, keep them off the web because they don’t belong there. Instead, toss the fracking kerning pairs out the window (‘cause browser support stinks and CSS can handle the kerning where it’s absolutely necessary, anyway) and license a font that’s -  • sub-setted with web publishing in mind  • converted to TTF - the format with the broadest support and, in Windows rendering envionments, a necessity  • tweaked with adequate TrueType hinting, normalized vertical metrics, scaling, whatever is necessary - for web publishing. (Epub being a subset of web publishing. Is iPad going to be the ONLY platform for you? I don’t think so.)

The idea that fonts created for graphic design work can be licensed for the web and/or epub AS IS and make a customer happy is delusional. As is the idea that there is a market for those very same fonts bundled in an epub wrapper and/or posted on a web server. Anyone who designs for the web, or it’s cousin e-pub, are finding this out now, bit by bit. It’s an astounding situation, really. Talk about elephants in the room, wow.

But once you realize that those fonts can fork into two distinct products - one font, the “original”, for print as is, and a post-processed version, the “web font”, for e-publishing there is no problem. What you’ve exposed to “piracy” is a low-cost spin-off. And a spin-off that works better for the customer’s purpose.
