TypeTool is fine. Consider it as Studio light. Its advantage is that it allows you to concentrate on designing. And it generates OT fonts. Should you really need to add OT features, use the free AFDKO. But do not try to do everything at once. One step after the other. It is perhaps a bit early for a 'time gain' question. You might consider getting ScanFont or Scanahand (Windows only), allowing you to start from your own hand-made input rather than playing with outlines alone.

If you design your f properly, you won't need ligatures at all. Ligatures are often just an affectation that impairs reading. Consider scaling back the scope of your project. You're lacking a decent kerning assistant or kerning classes. It's some thing you can overcome but to kern a family or 32 would be too much. Don't think you need to release a font all at once. You can start with 1-4 weights then expand the family later when your software/skills improve.

2010-08-15 16:02:42 • Frode Bo Helland
A font with 2 weights is a family. It's not a competition and the number of weights doesn't necessarily add up to more sales. Of my current best sellers, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 12 and 18 are single weights. Consider leaning toward display fonts while you're still learning. You'll have more creative freedom and you might need 1 or 2 weights, plus they're more fun to make. Above all: test print, make notes, fix, repeat. That's the part that most beginners miss and it's the most important thing. Why so many weights? Someday when you blend a black to an ultralight in Fontlab or whatever, you'll feel a little rush and you'll understand: it's an addiction.
