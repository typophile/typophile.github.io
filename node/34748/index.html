2007-06-26 18:53:59 • ebensorkin

is there a distinction?

The Chinese characters used for print have no circular strokes, and differ in this way from the Japanese Hiragana that Eben shows. They are straight, cornered, and curved with various terminals, but never circular. Chinese cursive writing or ‘grass script’ does have circular strokes but it is not normally printed as text. Incidentally, Kanji is the Japanese translation of Hanzi, the Chinese word for their characters.

2007-06-29 18:33:02 • hrant

“BTW I can’t quite support the idea of faux Chinese fonts being Chinoiserie.”

I mentioned Chinoiserie as a probably historical starting point for the emergence of faux Chinese roman letterforms. Sorry for not clarifying. Chinoiserie’s importance goes beyond its role as an artistic movement; in Enlightenment Europe, especially England and France, the Chinese imperial system was taken to be highly advanced politically. Rousseau thought that China’s system of government most closely represented rule by a philosopher king. This valuation of China then had its influence on artistic and then popular spheres. I wouldn’t completely equate Chinoiserie with orientalism, as the latter has a stronger ideological edge.

Fusaka, according to its own description, follows Chinese not with imitated brushstrokes, but by making each letter fit into a square. The extra lines and dots are based on portions of characters, as are many of the letters. The aesthetic of Chinese characters requires each character to be balanced in the context of a square. (Chinese fonts are of course monospaced.) And then in calligraphy, demands for overall page color will influence interaction between characters, especially in the highly cursive ‘grassy’ styles. In Fusaka the extra dots for example in the J and L make more sense in the context of a square.

From Sojin Kim & Somi Kim, “Typecast: Meaning, Culture, and Identity in the Alphabet Omelet,” Lift and Separate (New York: 1993), 32. (Found mentioned in a comment attached to the Design Observer article linked above.):

“In 1867 one of the earliest pseudo-Japanese fonts was patented in America. This alphabet, known as Mikita, consists of three-dimensional letterforms suggestive of wood or lacquer work. Rather than actual mimicry of other alphabets, the first fonts that drew on the Far East in their letter structure were more reflective of concurrent art/design movements (artistic printing, the aesthetic, arts and crafts, international symbolist, art nouveau). [fn: N. Gray, History of Lettering, 175] Informal writing styles and brush scripts, particularly Chinese and Japanese, also began to affect character shapes. Around 1885, a font called Japanese appeared in England, perhaps the first font to allude directly to Japanese or other East Asian culture through the design of letters that evoke the calligraphic brushstrokes of Asian writing. [fn: N. Gray, 19c Ornamented Typefaces, 101, 220] Despite the visual integrity and ingenuity of many of these alphabets, Western letterers' mimicry of calligraphic strokes used by other writing traditions inevitably fails to refer correctly to the ductus, or order and direction of strokes, of the different traditions.”

(And in the second footnote: “Mikita was produced by the type foundry D. & G. Bruce, New York, 1867, and offered as Novel by the English type foundries Austin Wood, c. 1868, and Figgins, c. 1883. A face called Chinese was offered in a supplement to the Bruce specimen book in 1879. Japanese was produced by the English type founder Sir Charles Reed, c. 1885.")
