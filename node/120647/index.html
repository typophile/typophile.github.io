October 19, 2014 • AlexanderKatt

This month I finally created a typeface that I feel happy about (maybe even too happy). This is just a compilation of different forms and ideas that I find appealing. I think I will also do a monospaced version. Some letters still have the a kinda “default” look, but I think that even in this early stage the personality of the typeface stands out?

Or does it?

Pretty cool design. I like its playfulness. I don’t quite know what kind of feedback to give on this though. I could tell you to condense n/m/u a bit more so the white space matches the counters of other letters, I could tell you to expand the D a bit, to decrease the top bowl of B, to slightly rotate S/s to the right, to raise the tittles in i/j, to fix the dark spot in 4 and to balance the t better, but honestly I find these irregularities rather charming. It’s not an all-purpose typeface but I can certainly see its application. What is this typeface intended for though? If it’s meant to be playful I wouldn’t change things drastically. If you’re attempting to design a well balanced typeface which is useful for much broader applications then I think you need to polish quite a bit. I wouldn’t do that though. I’m actually trying to develop a more impulsive approach to type design, where I’m less concerned with consistency and more with creative shapes, such as I see here. I’m fond of Eyal Holtzman’s work for this very reason.

I would however make the hyphen shorter and thicker. Right now it looks like an en-dash. On that note, did you also add the en-dash and em-dash? I’m asking because in the text you’re using a hyphen in the bottom left column where you should be using an em-dash.

Better, but there’s still a lot going on that needs to be fixed. H and S could be wider, I_r is too close together, the tittles on i/j should be higher (at around the height of where the terminal of /f ends), the /f should be wider, the crossbar of /t should be longer on the right while its terminal should be condensed a bit (and so should the tail of /j), the /v is too light, the diagonals in /k could extend a bit further and the /r is too heavy in the join.

There’s also something going on in the rendering. For example, in ‘abolish’ I see the /h with a thinner right vertical, the next /h looks good and the last /h in the text has a thinner stem. This makes it more difficult to check the typeface for inconsistencies. I just downloaded the PDF to see if it’s the same on my computer. It was. I then zoomed in on your typeface to look if I can identify where the rendering problem comes from. I’ve found it. For starters, your horizontals are thicker than your verticals. Note that horizontals always look optically thicker than verticals, so you need to compensate for that. But this is only the start of the issues with this typeface. If you look at the /n for example, you have a very thick shoulder, thinner stems and the weight of the typeface is all clumped together near the join. That’s exactly where you don’t want the weight to be. Make the horizontals thinner and move the weight at the top of the shoulder to the right and decrease the weight in the join. I can recommend reading this short tutorial I wrote on dark spots in type design: http://martinsilvertant.deviantart.com/journal/Dark-spots-in-type-design-322930112

Also have a look at the resources on this page: http://temple-of-typefaces.deviantart.com/journal/Type-resources-276795259 I think the Type Workshop might be particularly useful to you.

Also, here’s one of 3 tutorials I did on type design; this one is on designing a sans serif: http://martinsilvertant.deviantart.com/journal/How-to-design-a-typeface-Part-1-362811889
