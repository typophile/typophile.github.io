Portfolio Formatting

August 8, 2007 • Ehague

I’m not sure if this is something they cover in art/design school, but since I came to design rather obliquely, so to speak, I was never given a proper this-is-how-you-prepare-and-format-a-portfolio lesson.

Can anyone point me in the direction of some resources on how best to prepare a portfolio? I can’t seem to find much else beyond people selling the binders/cases themselves. Thanks!

Other info:

-This would be for book cover design. -At this stage I only have hard copies of some books and digital files for others (i.e., no cover flats).

In school you were taught to use 16 x 20" black matte boards with carefully mounted work and then placed in a huge $200 portfolio case that you then log around all over the place.

Everyone does this once, and then realizes it’s absurd and just takes actual sample pieces or 8 x 10 printouts.

I imagine there’s this portfolio-case industry cartel that secretly bribes art school professors while they’re off on their summer sabbaticals.

I agree with Aluminum, portfolio cases are a nightmare, and there’s nothing worse than watching eyes roll while you fumble around with boards. The best portfolios that I have seen are small spiral-bound books, these have the advantages of being easy to make and laying flat during an interview. Binders with sleeves are nice if you want to interview at a lot of different places and be able to swap pieces around, but they’re also outrageously overpriced, and the prices on the sleeves themselves are even worse.

11"x17" is a great way to go. They sell tons of accessories for it because it is a standard size used by architects, etc. There are many quality papers you can print to, including photo papers, so image quality can be whatever you want it to be.

Bruce Hale, an awesome lettering artist and graphic designer here in the Pacific NW, showed my class how he does things and he uses 11"x17" exlusively for presentations, proposals, portfolios, etc.

2007-08-10 15:12:13 • aluminum

Once my teachers saw the results, they started recommending every student to do their portfolios up in that way. It serves 3 purposes: You learn how to effectively present information, i.e., your portfolio; you learn how to prepare a press ready document with full bleeds, etc in a non-mission critical environment; and it looks professional when complete.

Imagine going to an interview and handing over a full-bleed, full-color book with glossy cover as your portfolio? Awesome.

I stole my current (yet outdated ;o) portfolio presentation method from the last design firm I worked at.

As Ricardo mentions, I first designed it all so it worked as a PDF for on-screen showing (in client presentations and the like).
