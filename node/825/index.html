The purchasers are rewarded, once again, for avoiding pay-per-use. Is it any wonder illustration as a way of life is nearly dead?

I'm truly curious, not being mean about this point: The editors get... what? An advance against royalties for putting it together? Royalties after a certain number of sales? Or only recognition? It's not something I want to do anymore. I'm only posting my position on giveaways as a bit of discouragement to any young designers who think lots of experienced ones are rushing to this opportunity. I'm not. I think reinforcing the idea of pay-per-use is the only way to turn the tide and get respect for the work we do as illustrators and type designers.

That said, I'm turning 40 and am WAY less concerned about all these issues. There is a whole range of work out there for every audience. My audience is the one with money to spend. To each his/her own. Publish away!

Thanks for your support, Anon.

Indie Fonts "does it right" because, as Stephen points out, it involves a "fair trade". Contributors get what they pay for. They prepare the artwork (some of it to template), and it gets printed. It is, in effect, a book of advertisements.

Another acceptable model for a type book that covers a variety of foundries is Allan Haley's "Cool Fonts...", published by Rockport. This is a proper book, because it has a writer who chose the content, and a designer who gave it a consistent look.

Too bad if you can't afford to be in Indie Fonts, and Allan didn't pick you for his book. (That's me on both counts.) But that's no reason to create spec work.

Booth Clibborn should know better than to exploit "young" designers for the "young" design market. That doesn't make it OK. And the design "names" involved should know better than to take advantage of this kind of set-up. The paradigm is reinforced -- put up with the crap when you're starting out, and reap the benefits later. But only a few do -- there has to be a better way.
2003-08-05 23:50:56 • Nick Shinn
When I read the discussion, I realize that so many of the 'moral' values have changed in the last decades since I have been a professional designer. I was shocked that the photocopy machine made it much easier than the reproduction camera to reproduce line art work and type. At that time you could easily copy any typeface from any type specimen book for a headline. No problem for the inventive designer. The introduction of the desktop computer made the darkroom obsolete. And not only the darkroom, the type setter as well. And designers thought it was great to put hard working people out of business.

Or what? Computers then made designers obsolete, because everybody can be a designer. And why not? So you're on you own and nobody will help you. It's do or die. Some will be lucky (who would have set up a reasonable business) and some are screwed (who would think they were artists). Some survived and some barely make it. The thing is: it's all around. In music, In art, in business, in science, in politics. in advertising. You're not alone!

If you think you're so great, well, get your act together, and eithter fight it all or sew everybody, be happy and retire. Or, be just ahead of it. ALL THE TIME. If you are such a champ you claim or think you are, you should know better, and deal with it accordingly, rather than whine.

This book will come and go and will mean nothing more than any other great book as a sign of the times when we look back to it. And, Typography Now made a lot of things happen.

To be honestly, under the (and my personal) current circumstances, I am happy with any exposure I can get. In Indie Fonts as well as the Fontmonster book, or an article in the Argentinean Tipografica magazine or the Brasilian Tupografia. In spite of my reputation or career. But I might have different expectations or even a different strategy then others of what I think I can get out of it.

There are no ethics, but there are... A changing world demands adjustments and flexibility. I think it's so great that younger designers then me, worry about stuff their generation made me realize that the evolution in this profession won't stop with yourself. All is relative.

A few thoughts:

If my work is so good big clients and publishers alike line up at my door,
why should I care about a book with free clip-art design?

If my work is so mediocre I feel threatened by the publication
of a free clip-art design book, shouldn't I worry about more important stuff?

What is the difference between clip-art and stock photography
or illustration? the price? or the decisions we, as designers, make in order to
choose the images that are right for the project?

If I need a high-quality, versatile, extended typeface family for a big corporate
identity project, I know where to look for it. If I need a fresh, insolent,
rebellious typeface for a guerrilla marketing project, I might not look for it
at the same place.

Given that all the details are in the open, aren't we all adults enough
to decide wether we want to participate or not in any project?

The fact that my work is offered copyright-free in this book, or copyrighted
in the latest AIGA annual, is not going to deter students, serious designers,
amateurs or pirates from copying it, draw inspiration from it, ripping it off,
referencing it, abusing it or, simply, laughing at it.
