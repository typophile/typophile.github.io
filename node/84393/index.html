2011-08-09 16:53:20 • PublishingMojo

This is an issue I’ve addressed with the Sense and Sensibility super family, which comprises “Modern” and “Oldstyle” sans styles with identical weights and vertical metrics, and almost identical copy fitting. Therefore, the tone of a layout may be changed by switching from one to the other without altering the layout—enabling a clear comparison of which option best suits the brand parameters.

This kind of design process may be pursued with any superfamily.

It can also be applied with types that have variants of “a” and “g”.

Admittedly, this is not painting in broad strokes, but it can be quite useful in determining whether a particular typographic execution meets strategy; it’s surprisingly clear when one compares two layouts with such a difference, which one better embodies typical keywords like “friendly” or “authoritative”.

However, although I priced the complete S&S package very attractively, most sales have been of either the Sense or the Sensibility sub family, so I must conclude that this design strategy is not the way that designers generally work — unless they are able to do it with quite different types, e.g. Futura and Helvetica, or even serif vs. sans.
