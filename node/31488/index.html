The range of language support can also be dependent on the specific version of a font you're talking about. You'll also have to be more specific about what groups or blocs of non-Westen languages you might be interested in. BTW, broadly speaking, if you're starting with western languages, there isn't much point going much beyond the Latin/Greek/Cyrillic range of writing systems within a single font style. Most other writing systems have so little in common with these that the idea of having the "same" design is no longer very relevant. Regards, T

I think you'll find Univers more a reference to what early- mid-2oth century people of a certain school thought letterforms should look like rather than an attempt to describe character compliment. What you are after could be called "Unicode ranges supported." I did a Google search & found http://www.alanwood.net/unicode/fontsbyrange.html but it looks a bit hard to use. I've seen a bar graph presentation somewhere, just can't remember where. I think Ariel has wide character support, as did the now withdrawn Bitstream Cyberbit. Bear in mind that "character support" is different than actually being able to write with the typeface.

Helvetica World (at MyFonts or Linotype) is an excellent multilingual typeface with non-Western diacritics and non-Latin alphabets completely redrawn a few years ago. It includes Western, CE, Cyrillic, Greek, Vietnamese, Hebrew and Arabic. Bitstream Zurich is an authorized renamed variant of Univers that has a multilingual version with Western, CE, Cyrillic and Greek characters. Microsoft Reader comes with a cuperbly hinted free multilingual version of Frutiger (Western, CE, Greek). ParaType offers FreeSet, a version of Frutiger with Western, CE, Cyrillic and Armenian. A.
