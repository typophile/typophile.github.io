2007-12-15 20:37:00 • ChuckGroth

Chuck I think we agree then. Fundamentally. Mostly.

Whatever a logo/identity is - it has to cohere & have an impact. Or perhaps better - have a result that is the desired one.

When you expand from logo into identity boundriess can be allowed to blur & squish with success I think. But again with the proviso that they cohere. And by cohere I don’t even mean that they share a style - nothing that formal - just that ultimately the impact you get is the one you want.

The messier/more complex things are the richer they can be. But richness can also create more & more opportunities for failure or simple mediocrity.

One of the reasons I love great typography is that it is one of the all time great tools for pulling an identity together & giving just the spin you want.

I also don’t think that a logo has to to resolve into something even as simple as The Unilever logo. But I think it is often advisable. And it’s even advisable to avoid the complexity altogether unless you are really on top of your game. And even then.

Anyway, this is a long way to go to try to suggest that Trifolium could have lots of flourishes and still cohere. So far it doesn’t.

Chuck’s advice is sound. Going simpler might help especially if they number of elements you have is getting hard to control.
