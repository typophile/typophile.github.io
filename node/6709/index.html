Ok, here's the deal. I'm trying to figure out how typographers determine the weight of a font. For example, Helvetica comes in several weights with different names, Ie Helvetica Neue 75 Bold. What does the 75 mean? Obviously the weight or stroke of Helvetica 25 is lighter than 45 or 75, but how do they come up with that number?? What measurement is that, and what point size are they basing that off of? For example, Helvetica 25 and 75 at 12 points doesn't appear to have any correlation. The weight of Helvetica 75 doesn't appear to be 3 times the weight of Helvetica 25, infact is seems to be more like 6 times the weight. Please any help is appreciated. The numbers don't have any correlation to metrics. They are an organization principle. This principle was first used by Adrian Frutiger for his Univers family. Here is an image which might help you understand his matrix:
http://www.typotheque.com/articles/images_articles/majoor/07-Univers-21-versions.gif
(a bit too small but this graphic is super famous, maybe you have ist somewhere in print)

or:
http://graphicdesign.sfcc.spokane.cc.wa.us/tutorials/process/type_basics/images/univer_grid.gif

The main, central weight of Univers is the Regular weight, Univers 55. 55 doesn't mean anything geometric; it is a combination of 5 and 5 (5 being the middle digit between 1 and 9).

The lighter you go, the lower the first number in the two digit combination drops (55 to 45 to 35 to 25 to 15). Higher is also bolder (so 65 is bold, 75 is bolder, 95 is totally fat…)

65 is Bold Regular, 35 is Thin Regular. The second number in the combination determines what happens to the basic weight other than heaviness/lightness. 56 is Regular Oblique, 57 is condensed. 46 is light italic, 67 is bold condensed.

The numbers are just an organizing principle!
Frutiger has used these numbers of his for almost all of his typefaces, but they never really caught on outside of his circle. Linotype tried them for a few non-Frutiger fonts, such as Helvetica Neue.

Does that explanation make sense?
