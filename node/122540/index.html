2015-01-17 14:46:05 • eliason
While I’m happy to help and discuss this topic, I disagree that geometric analysis addresses what is fundamental to typography. People have been trying to reduce typography to scientiﬁc analysis and management since Dürer, but it seems to me that most geometry in glyph shapes stems from the peculiarities of different technologies—both those used to design, punch or draw the type, and those used to render typography. Where the style is a script, the technology is a brush and paper, and the rendering is (hand-spaced) phototypositor, there is no geometry, and yet is this not type? Therefore, geometry is not fundamental. In the ﬁrst 25 years of digital type, prior to high-res Retina screens, the discipline of hinting, concerning alignment zones and stem widths, has encouraged neatness and regularity in type design—eminently measurable—but at the same time there has been a parallel track of grunge, deconstruction, distress and skeuomorphic imitation of rough, hand-rendered lettering of analog media. And this too is type!

The objection to using these forms entirely unchanged - ie without being redesigned typographically to adapt to the style weight, serif or sans etc., of the Arabic or Hindu or whatever font it is used in- still stands. One certainly could borrow the numerals from any number of Latin typefaces designed to simulate Arabic or Chinese, or even just from a calligraphic typeface. I can think, though, of a number of reasons this option was rejected. Since Arabic does have its own set of digits, it may have been thought that the foreign ones should stand out, so that people don't get too comfortble with them. And an attempt to stylistically Orientalize the Western digits could well have been perceived as patronizing and so on. Or, in a more pragmatic vein, since those weren't the forms people were taught from childhood to recognize, if they don't stand out as alien, they have the potential to cause confusion and slow reading. When English texts include words in Greek, Hebrew, Chinese, or some other language with a different script, ordinary typefaces for those scripts are used - often, the equivalent of Century Expanded for the Latin script: the plainest and most standard typeface one can find. In the hot metal era, that would simply be all that the printer would have on hand. (For Greek, there is a particular convention, known as Porson Greek, with its own history.) There is no temptation to handle those scripts with a hyper-Latinized typeface so that they will "fit in" visually with the surrounding text. If a paragraph suddenly contained, say, a word or phrase in heavily Latinized Gujarati... say (and the Gujarati script happens to lend itself to such treatment, with relatively simple forms and no Devanagari overbar - and I've seen it done too for display typography in that language)... unless context were to indicate the language likely to be used, the reader would likely be utterly perplexed as to what language these strange symbols might belong. Come to think of it, this is an indictment of the "unified" sans-serif typefaces currently being designed for mobile devices, which are the subject of another recent thread: they may be great for their intended purpose in user interfaces, but they are probably not recommended for (the kind of) scholarly publication (that includes interspersed quotes from different languages).

LePierre, it doesn’t matter that I and l are the same character shape, context will usually disambiguate them. They were different shapes prior to typography, but ironically, from your perspective, type design has brought them closer together, because of both the recognition of textual context for providing meaning, and typography’s ability to nuance the basic shapes with details that relate to how the bicameral (upper and lower case) system has evolved, namely stem thickness, relative height, and serif treatment. So the identicality of I and l is not a bug, but a feature that contributes to the overall readability of typography—although not of course individual glyph legibility. I would argue that, assessing the pros and cons of the simple vertical line as a glyph shape for two separate characters, the economy and simplicity of this shape is so powerful that the efficiency (both in execution and recognition) obtained by using it outweighs the drawbacks. This is what design is, a compromise (or transcendent raprochement) between competing criteria, in this instance legibility and readability. The same principal has allowed us to identicalize the character forms of O, o, zero, degree and ring, distinguishing them with subtle, typographically meaningful transformations of the basic round shape, without resorting to making any of them heart-shaped.

I think you have misinterpreted what I meant by “combine”; perhaps I should have said “are configured to form a coherent system”. I attempted to explain this with the example of the straight line (I, l and 1, and for that matter hyphen, dashes, macron and underscore) and circle/oval (O, o, zero, degree and ring). As individual symbols, the circles are identical, and easy to categorize and identify as such. That is legibility. But which character does this shape, once discerned, signify? For the type designer, the circularity is a given, and the important task, for disambiguation, is both to make sure they are not confused, and also to harmonize their treatment stylistically. In a typeface comprised entirely out of straight lines, these nominally circular or oval characters become quadrilateral, yet this conceit is taken in stride by the reader. Again, it is the type designer’s task to make sure that a particular shape is recognized as a specific character, and this is done by typographic means, in relation to the typeface as a whole.

Nobody created the alphabet saying that it was good, well founded, sensible: it's just a limited set of arbitrary shapes that typographers are forced to play with. Yes, but the alphabet very slowly and gradually developed over time. Some of the developments are hard to understand. The ancient Phoenician alphabet seems, to modern eyes accustomed to the Latin alphabet, to be quite legible - and it developed into the Greek alphabet and then the Latin one, legible as well by that standard. But other descendants include the Hebrew square script, which has several glyph pairs that are hard to distinguish, Arabic script, with multiple glyphs distinguished only by added dots, and the elaborate offspring of the Brahmi script, itself plain and legible. The explanation for the Arabic script, at least, is obvious - ease of writing, as with, say, Pitman shorthand. Some Indic scripts are also explicable, like one constrained by the need to write on palm leaves. But it's hard to justify what, for example, the Siamese have inflicted on themselves. How could such a script have developed, with each incremental step being "better" than the one before? Of course, part of the issue is that the question "better for what purpose" may be missed. If mass literacy is not the goal, but a dignified presentation of traditional religious texts, an elaborate script is not an issue.
