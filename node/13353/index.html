June 29, 2005 • oldnick

Civilized discussions require civilized rules. We call these little gatherings forums in part, I suppose, because they are patterned after the fora (the correct Latin plural, for what it’s worth) that existed at the heart of Roman towns. They began as marketplaces, like the Greek agora, but gradually evolved into each town’s civic center. The most famous of these was, of course, The Forum in Rome proper. Here, the great orators of the Republic and the early empire presented their cases directly to the people.

The great Roman orators developed a wide range of techniques in order to present their arguments forcefully, and these techniques form the basis of the art/science we know as rhetoric. When the goal was frank and open discussions of a topic, the techniques were straightforward and direct; when the goal was winning an argument, period, the techniques were often underhanded and well disguised. One of the orators' most forceful (and enduring) tricks was the argumentum ad hominem, in which an opponent’s arguments were sidestepped, and his character was attacked. Most if not all of us are familiar with this phenomenon: today, we call it “trash talking” or “politics as usual in the US of A.” One should note that, however effective this technique has proven in the political arena, within the confines of formal debate it is considered a logical fallacy, and therefore weakens–rather than strengthens–the argument of the person employing it. Calling someone else–or his or her ideas–stupid, explicitly or implicitly, doesn’t make you any smarter or your ideas any better…ever.

We are all, to one degree or another, ignorant. Ignorance is no shame. We are all born ignorant; ideally, we become less so as we grow and learn. In these forums, we can have a frank exchange of ideas; we can share–when we can–the knowledge we have gained; and we can learn from others who have gained other, different knowledge. Let’s keep it positive.

I think Nick’s notion to talk about rhetoric is a good one, but I think he makes a couple of misleading statements about the history and nature of rhetoric. The western rhetorical tradition begins with the Greeks, not the Romans, and most of the technical terminology of formal rhetoric is Greek not Latin. Interestingly, the argumentum ad hominem seems to be an exception, suggesting that this regretable addition to rhetoric might have been a Roman contribution, or at least more widely practised by the Romans than the Greeks. And it is regretable, but I’m not sure that I would classify it as a logical fallacy. A logical fallacy is a flaw in a logical argument that is independent of the truth of the argument, a kind of architectural flaw in the structure of the argument. The argumentum ad hominem, at least as recently practiced on Typophile, seems to me less of a flaw in the logic of an argument than an outright abandonment of an argument in favour of mere insult. The argumentum ad hominem as logical fallacy would be something along the lines of ‘X cannot equal Y, as you claim, because you’re an idiot’. Simply saying ‘You’re an idiot’ doesn’t even rise to the level of a logical fallacy: it is just an absence of charity.

What may be termed ‘political rhetoric’ – the form of rhetoric we encounter most often through our various media – tends to what would have been considered sophistry by the Greeks; that is, it seeks to persuade according to an agenda, which may or may not have any interest in truth; it may not even reflect what the sophist actually believes. The sophists were criticised and condemned by Sokrates, Plato, Aristotle, Isokrates and others, but their influence on political rhetoric is observable throughout subsequent history. The notion that one can persuade people of what is advantageous to oneself is compelling. The sophistic understanding of rhetoric is as techne, a technical method (to achieve an end).

Although sophism stands condemned, rhetoric itself does not. There is a hermeneutic tradition of rhetoric in which the tools of rhetorical persuasian are put at the service of a mutual search for truth. There is a funny exchange in A century of philosophy in which, over several pages, Riccardo Dottori asks the late Hans Georg Gadamer about various aspects of philosophy, particularly ethics, to all of which Gadamer replies ‘That too is rhetoric’. For Gadamer, seemingly all of philosphy comes down to this hermeneutic rhetoric through which two people go in search of the truth or, at least, of deeper understanding, through the process of conversation. To identify ethics with rhetoric is, obviously, to be talking about something other than the sophistic or political rhetoric of persuasian to advantage. Rhetoric, understood in this sense, is an ethical and social praxis, rather than the techne of the sophist. Rhetoric is practical, but understanding this requires a classical appreciation of the difference between practical and technical, and the association of the practical with phronesis, practical wisdom. Our modern notions of rhetoric, which are almost entirely sophist and negative (to the degree that we often accuse someone of employing rhetoric), erase the distinction between the practical and the technical, debasing the practical to the pragmatic.

The key to this kind of hermeneutic and practical rhetoric is charity, which means, among other things, that the argumentum ad hominem has no place in it.

2005-06-30 04:35:25 • John Hudson
By the way, I also have some disagreement with Nick’s opening assertion: ‘Civilized discussions require civilized rules.’ Civilised rules are what we require in the absence of civility; rules compensate for inadequate civility. And civility itself is often a poor substitute for what really benefits discourse, which is intellectual charity. Civility might mean nothing more than not telling the person you despise that you despise him, or not telling the person you consider an idiot that you think he is an idiot. The philosophical principle of charity, in which we suspend our prejudices in order to comprehend new ideas, is at its core a methodological principle – a means to understanding, from which more accurate criticism can be made –, but it also fosters charity toward the Other, and this is part of the social praxis of rhetoric. Yes, these things can be addressed through rules, but if there is a human commitment to charity or, if charity is beyond us, to civility, then the rules are redundant.

2005-06-30 17:12:19 • Glyn Adgie
I suppose it depends on what your definition of argument is. An argument can be “a fact or assertion offered as evidence that something is true.” Or it may be “a discussion in which reasons are advanced for and against some fact or assertion,” thus making it an argument about arguments. Since the second definition posits positions for and against, debate will naturally ensue. Debate can be sedate, calm, reasoned, orderly; or it can be lively, spirited, even heated. But the real bottom line is, what do you expect the debate to accomplish? If you’re after the truth (or at least a reasonable facsimile thereof), it doesn’t matter who wins or loses the debate, as long as you arrive somewhere in the near vicinity of your goal. If, on the other hand, the whole idea is to prove that you are right and the other guy is wrong, you might be a lawyer or a politician.

2005-07-01 01:14:25 • Glyn Adgie

the natural mechanisms that regulate behaviour tend to fail in this new environment.

Indeed (although I would quibble about that “natural”). There are three main reasons for this:

  1. Lack of gesture. In face-to-face communication a tiny smirk can do wonders to express one’s true stance. The absence of gestures reduces communication, and can allow [certain] people to assume greater animosity than there is.
  2. The big one: the internet removes the factor of geography. People get along better with people closer to their physical environment. The wider you cast the net, the more the tension. Now just imagine if the poor masses of this earth suddenly had internet access - they’d flame & shame us into the ground… :-/
  3. Immediacy: the two points above wouldn’t be so bad if the damn thing wasn’t so fast! Think of writing snailmail letters, which are much less liable to cause friction, simply because you have a lot more time to tone things down, to control. This immediacy is closer to our raw soul. In the big picture, it is a good thing. (So is #2.)

These three reasons combine to make one attribute more important than either civility or candor: tolerance. You need titanium skin and a cast-iron spine. Without tolerance, it’s all a sham.

On the tension between politeness and seeking the truth.

This is a genuine tension, as the anthropologists I cited above point out in their book. Seeking the truth requires clarity and frankness, but politeness requires not saying anything that might embarrass the other person. The tactics of politeness involves artful ambiguity and open questions–How are you?–that a person can take any way he or she wants.

Searching for the truth in a discussion often involves disagreement and debate, and it is easy to give and take offense when the other person’s heartfelt opinions are contradicted.

The standard that has evolved for scholarly discussions is relevance to the topic being discussed: contradiction and criticism are permitted if you stay on the issue being discussed. If you stay relevant, and disagreement over which solution to a problem or side of an issue is best, then the discussants are expected not to take offense, even if their cherished views are contradicted and criticised. What is out of bounds by this standard are personal attacks–attacks on the character of the other person.

Both the statement of Grice and my second century quote emphasize the importance of relevance, staying on topic, for discussion in search of the truth.

The problem with this standard for Typophile is that what we do here is a mixture of discussion of specific topics, and a conversation on whatever happens to interest us. Thus a thread that starts off with a discussion of one topic can hit on another topic, and change course completely, sometimes to the delight and sometimes to the distress of the participants.

If the threads are kept on topic, then the chances of people giving and taking offense is much reduced.

However, I for one enjoy the way our conversations enble us to discover new interests and ideas from one another, without staying to the strict rails of a discussion.

But this means that we must take great care, especially when the topics of politics and religion come up, not to engage in personal attacks.

I’d like to keep the freewheeling conversation of Typophile without the rancor that keeps popping up, but I have to admit that it hasn’t happened.

A ‘new topic, new thread’ rule would probably keep things more civil, but it would be less fun. My solution is for moderators to have a much heavier hand cutting out personal attacks, but I must admit this creates unpleasant work for someone else who is probably also here for their own amusement.

So the only solution seems to be self-restraint, which so far isn’t working that well.

Chris,

I will gladly accept your apology if you will accept mine, previously tendered. Despite any seeming evidence to the contrary, I am not a humorless old curmudgeon. I have been known to snicker, chuckle, chortle, guffaw and even howl at times when confronted with good-natured humor. In my high school, wit was a competitive sport and, although I never achieved the unanimous acclaim of my peers as the ultimate practitioner, I held my own during many an impromptu circle smirk. I also recognize and appreciate the many amusing points which have been advanced by visitors to this particular forum. However, you will notice that, when presenting humor, the person who posts it either credits the source, or says “I have heard it said…” or some other method of attribution. The clever person does not say “Here’s a witty story,” but rather “I am a witty person.” In the former instance, sharing something amusing with others is like making love; in the latter instance, stroking your own ego is like…well, you get the picture.

Misunderstandings happen: intent may not be what it appears to be. As has been mentioned previously, we suffer because the page is our stage, and relevant contextual indicators like tone of voice or a twinkle in the eye can’t be heard or seen in print, emoticons notwithstanding.
