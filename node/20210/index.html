By the way, I like the nav thing…I think it’s good for documents like these…keeps people thinking about the big picture. However, does it have to stand out so much? It’s the first thing I look at. Could it be incorporated more subtly somewhere into the top margin?

Justifying the copy and removing that second column would help simplify the layout, but I feel like you can also do something creative with the left alignment. You have a hierarchy started here (the title out to the left, the text in the middle, the definitions of phisiologic/psychologic further in), but I don’t think it’s pushed all the way (the headers are on the same level as the text, the list doesn’t conform). Besides, you’ve already imposed hierarchy by using bold and sans-serif, so is the whole title outdent thing really necessary? Aligning the title with the text would give you a strong lefthand alignment and would make the indent of the definitions even more powerful. Plus, without that outdent you could probably afford to move the text over a little to the left, giving the right column more room to breathe. But on the other hand, if you want to do the indent/oudent thing, push it.

2006-05-25 11:02:30 • poms

Alright, here I go again; I promise to not KILL this thread. A few remarks:

  1. If you are not already doing so, you NEED to be working on a typographic grid. From what I saw, you may well already be doing so, but, looking at only one page, I would have no idea. I hope that you are familiar with this concept, and if you are not, then you need to hurry out and purchase a copy of “Grid Systems in Graphic Design” by Joseph Müller Brockman.

  2. I typically prefer justified copy, but again, but this will mean that you are going to have to spend a lot of time with each page individually. Ensuring that you get decent spacing, and well proportioned text is going to be somewhat difficult, as this style is not something that just “falls into place”. As it stands now, your rag is horrible, and if you do go with a rag right, then you will yet again be forced to deal with balancing this out, so that your rag does not jump all over the place.

  3. I agree that HN is a poor choice. It has great legibility, but, it has become a less attractive choice due to its current overuse by too many designers. I would definitely suggest looking for alternatives, and don’t be afraid to use something that you may have not heard of. As long as its legible, then there really isn’t a problem with using it.

  4. Your body copy has rather poor contrast at the moment. I am not sure if this is due to the typeface, the color of the text, or a combination of the above. You might also try using a different face here. Depending on what kind of feel you are going for, I have a few favorites for body copy:

Utopia, Olympian, Sabon, Caslon, Bembo, Electra, Weiss, Garamond, Kepler, and the list goes on. Really, I try and make some sort of connection between the typeface I choose, and the subject of the content, but this is not always possible.

In conclusion, I am uploading a piece I am about to finish, as an example for you. Its a publication that is completely built around a typographic grid, and for this reason I have included all of the guides and, including the baseline grid. This obscures the aesthetic feel, but I felt it more important that you understand the structure of the piece, rather than anything else. Perhaps it may give you some ideas or inspiration. This is a piece that will print on a cream, antique white stock, just in case you think I’m crazy for using blue text with orange headlines. It does look rather crisp and contemporary imho.

I recommend avoiding justified text (and where possible hyphenation) for this kind of subject, my reasons are that many of the longer medical terms (the ones likely to need hyphenation) might not be familiar for the reader and will be easier to read in one word blocks and that a justified text, even with hyphenation, containing longer words is likely to produce rivers in the block. Ranged left, ragged right also needs careful attention to avoid widows, orphans and a loose rag but will serve the reader better. On another note I would run spellcheck over your copy and do a find and replace for double word spaces. If your learn more boxes are going to contain urls I would look at making them one line, maybe moving the box to the bottom of the page and taking it to the measure. Unless binding prevents it, the centre gutter seems huge, try mocking up a spread. The text printed as a tint is probably worth avoiding as well you could, if you feel it necessary, make the headings tinted but the text as it stands is too light. Hope some of this is helpful. Tim

First, you will not get the Canadian Ministry of Education to fund this, as there isn’t one. Education is provincial. (Health is federal, however).

Secondly, you won’t get anyone to fund it with the spelling errors that are currently in it. There are two on the first line alone.

For design, you are wise to be considering black and white only, as the majority of teacher guides are one color. But do design in color … ever now and then the government will splurge on things, for no apparent reason.

I also agree that the three column format is too tight for a 8.5 x 11 page. Keep in mind that this might get published in loose leaf format, with room needed for three ring binder holes. I would go to one major column and the side bar column, which is a bit better than two text columns, imho.

I would keep ragged right. I think the objections to it are due to the narrow columns. I would also look at reducing the amount of space between paragraphs to 1/3 or 1/2 line, rather than a full line space.
