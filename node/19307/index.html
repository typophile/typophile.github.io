Typeface to suggest infant punk

April 17, 2006 • tflavin

My client is a small semi-upscale boutique for designer infantwear. They describe themselves as punk, but everything looks too clean for me to endorse that fully. Rather, think D&G writ small, tiny rock-themed graphic Ts, asian patterns, etc.

They need a typeface (or two) to use across a range of publications. It should be suitable for body copy and headlines. I can use either a single face or a body/headline pairing. Their marketing strategy is entirely focused on the parents. The baby is framed as an accessory of sorts.

The concepts of ‘punkness’ and ‘infancy’ are almost completely antithetical. Thus far, every combination I have found comes across as a joke. Is there such a thing as clean, safe, adorable punk? They currently use all lowercase courier new, which is awful . . ..

2006-04-21 02:44:30 • jlg4104

Honestly, I think I would simply go with Courier, no alterations—the classic, not a modernized remake. It is just ugly enough to merit punk. If you notice the descender on the lowercase “p” you’ll see that it becomes quite muddled. That’s a good thing; it needs no alteration. And, the spacing of the letters are just discongruent enough that it won’t look perfect.

Part of the uniqueness of punk is that it doesn’t try too hard. Cut n' paste and typewriters are not punk because they’ve been sanctified by the Punk Rawk Academy, they are merely accessible and easy methods. Style was—at at least at one point—a byproduct of the tools, not the other way around.

As a person who grew up in a punk scene, I abhor grunge fonts. I feel that if one wants to dirty up a font, then that person should do it by hand. Could you suggest to your client that they take all their print material and photocopy it to give it that extra edge? I realize that’s a chore, but if you can sell them on the idea, I feel it would do your work (and most importantly their brand) justice.

Have fun.
