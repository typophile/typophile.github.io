The double, baseline apostrophe is a style of quotation marks (still used today in Germany). What is interesting in this Portuguese typography is that the quotation mark is repeated at the beginning of every line, not just at the beginning of every paragraph within the quotation, which would be the modern norm.

According to this article, they were even used that way in early English books:

Quotation marks began to appear in printed works in 17th century England. However, there was no standardization for the use of these marks. In early works readers would find quotation marks, distractingly, at the beginning of each line of a long quote. Eventually this typesetting style was abandoned and the quote marks were removed, but not the space they occupied.

2009-02-26 14:03:42 • faraqat

It’s not a J. But historically it was a slight upward diagonal, a long horizontal, and another slight upward diagonal. This was necessary because it was placed over multiple letters at once:

Overtime, it began to just be used to represent a deleted n or m: capitane –> capitãe (Sp. capitán). In Spanish at least, there was no real rhyme or reason, it was just a space saver. I’m not as current on Portuguese linguistics to know its history as marking nasality. Also in Spanish you encounter a lot the q with a tilde, which abbreviates “que” (hence aunq~ = aunque).

It’s because of this history that I prefer tildes that overall are flat or slightly rising. There are a lot of fonts that have it essentially dropping which I don’t like and I’d consider personally improperly made, but I don’t say that with any authority other than historical precedence :) Things are changing of course.
