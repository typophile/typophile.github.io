2015-01-17 02:23:12 • JamesM

I take it that the cliche you want to avoid when you say “western” is (what used to be called) French Clarendon, the usual style identified with the Gay ’90s - think Wendy’s.

But avoiding cliches and seeking true authenticity may go over the heads of a typographically unsophisticated public.

However, something authentic probably will be recognized as old because people will remember stuff from old movies, old books, and so on - thus one really shouldn’t be afraid of doing it right.

Also, the other important thing to consider is that the restaurant is intended to suggest a very high-class and expensive restaurant of that time period, not just an ordinary restaurant. That eliminates some of the styles of lettering used in that period - limiting one to more tasteful ones, less likely to be as obviously dated.

2015-01-18 04:41:41 • jvankriedt
The years around 1900 were marked by the the popularity of display fonts that imitated hand lettering. These would have been copied by sign painters. Perhaps one of the most successful was the style that became familiar with The Saturday Evening Post name plate. This was produced as Post Old Style. This was a “rugged” font – a style that today we see as distressed or grunge. Smoothed digital versions are available. Consider Saturday Morning Toast NF http://www.myfonts.com/fonts/nicksfonts/saturday-morning-toast/ and Lamp Post http://www.myfonts.com/fonts/jnlevine/lamp-post/ Don
2015-01-18 18:02:39 • donshottype
I agree that De Vinne was the height of fashion for display type circa 1900. There was a full range of variations from condensed to expanded. I believe sign painters modeled some of their lettered signs on De Vinne. For a digital version http://www.myfonts.com/fonts/woodentypefonts/de-vinne/ Note that for many years the name De Vinne has been appropriated for a font that bears no resemblance to the circa 1900 De Vinne. The 1900 era De Vinne is very similar to Hawthorn http://www.myfonts.com/fonts/linotype/hawthorn-lt/ Don
2015-01-18 18:05:09 • donshottype
BTW sign painters of the era avoided sans-serif like the plague, except for the most utilitarian or industrial usage. As an alternative to serifs with prominent serifs they might use tiny serifs and add some decoration such as a shadow line. This ethos is captured in a modern font, Horndon http://www.myfonts.com/fonts/linotype/horndon/ Don
