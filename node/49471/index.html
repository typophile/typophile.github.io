Researching by doing: capital proportions

September 15, 2008 • eliason

Found article on “the Geometry of Roman Lettering” (by Tom Perkins) that argues for geometry underpinning classical inscriptions. He goes through the alphabet, finding the rectangles that best fit the rubbings: A is based on a double Golden Rectangle, B is based on a root-five rectangle, C on a double root-five rectangle, etc.

I’m not convinced. (It seems that there’s a geometric rectangle of one kind or another passably close to every letter size, so I suspect geometry arises after the fact to account for the proportions rather than dictating them in advance.) But I think I’ll take Perkins' boxes as a starting point for the cap proportions for my font.

I’m thinking the the more cursive branching forms of the lowercase I’ve been toying with should be left out of the caps, though maybe could be designed as quasi-swash variants.

I think scribes don’t tend to write with proportian scales at the ready. My guess would be the eye was the guide with the possible aid of pen widths or stroke widths :-)

Actually you can find the grid in Rudolf Koch’s writing book. It is not geometric, as say some of the contemporary sans. It is based on the square but the widths fall outside of the geometric parameters. The M and W are 5/4, and the widths range fro 1/2 to 2/3 to 1/1 etc.

Calligraphers are indeed, if the teacher is worth their salt, taught these proportions. Romans are the most difficult and demanding of all the calligraphic forms.

L C Evetts wrote a book many decades ago about the “drawn” Roman, as have many others, It is a wonderful book but it has limitations. The Trajan was never meant to be viewed head on, but viewed from below. So there are problems with weight distribution, which can also be seen in contemporary adaptations like Trajan by Carol Twombly. Not that it is a bad font.
