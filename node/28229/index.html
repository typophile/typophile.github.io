2006-09-14 16:57:37 • pattyfab

The British historian and critic James Secord made this observation about literary classics

‘Those books that allow us to forget the most are accorded the status of a classic.'

And I think this is applicable to type design in many respects too. A classic typeface is one that dominates an idiom or genre, such that any other typefaces in that idiom are automatically compared to it. A classic typeface also becomes a kind of filter through which we view typographic history. So, for example, we have come to view Venetian typography through the filter of Bembo. And here is where Secord’s observation applies, because the filter makes us forgetful and we ignore large amounts of actual Venetian typography.

2006-09-14 18:20:14 • John Hudson

But who are the “We” who become forgetful?

There are media involved, most obviously when a classic has not been in continuous use. Then it is the cultural arbiters such as Updike, Meggs, Heller and Bringhurst, and the commercial influences such as Linotype, who have some say in the matter.

This is driven home by Charles Murray in his statistical analysis of “Human Accomplishment”, where he correlates achievement with mention in published reference works.

Also, if a particularly talented redesigner, or one who works for a dominant font distributor, goes to work on reviving a face, it stands a much better chance of becoming a classic.
