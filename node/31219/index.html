“Chuck” [[Charles Bigelow]] wrote one of the definitive articles on Galliard in Fine Print magazine, January 1979.

Of particular note, it was [[Mike Parker]], then director of typographic development for Mergenthaler, who made an extensive study of punches and matrices of [[Robert Granjon]]’s (1513–89) types found at the Plantin-Moretus Museum.

Together, Parker and [[Matthew Carter|Carter]] sought the “best” of Granjon’s faces suitable for contemporary typography, making Galliard more of an anthology than a revival of any particular type family.

This is working off the top of my head, so some of the early stuff could be in error. The later information is likely correct, as I was a bit involved.

Matthew Carter cut the original Galliard while still at Linotype. I imagine the font was licensed to ITC through Linotype. When he & Mike Parker started Bitstream, it was likely re-licensed back from ITC (Something similar happened to Sumner Stone with the “Stone clan”). Or maybe Carter held onto some rights.

There was a third incarnation when Carter started Carter & Cone. though the Carter & Cone fonts are not called “ITC.” At some point, a “Swedish” version was made, which had a slightly different “f” so as to fit with vowels with an umlaut better. A bit later, Richard Eckersley & I got with Carter to work out tabular old-style figures, and I believe a few other glyphs.

While too much a gentleman to say so, I believe Matthew is a bit tired of working on Galliard. It’s sparkle comes from the contrast of the letters; the relation of the fine strokes with the heavier ones. Galliard fared better when printed repro-negative-plate than direct-to-plate. To date, there has been no reissue of Galliard. I did make a slighter more robust version in OpenType –with some extra characters such as f-vowel-grave ligatures, and put the nut fractions into a stylistic set. This was for our own use, but I did send a copy of the font files to Matthew Carter. (We were on a panel at some meeting, & he tacitly agreed that direct-to-plate printing was a little hard on the font.) Never did hear back from him on this one though, so maybe he disapproved of the changes.

It may be that the Type 1 Carter & Cone Galliard is to be the final form. For the heavier weights, the Bitstream was the final form (to date).

Hope this helps,

Charles

2007-02-07 03:25:20 • charles ellertson

Just an addition of little know information about the development of Galliard–

The drawing and refinement of the Galliard family was no small task, but it is interesting to note that the [[Ikarus]] System was used to interpolate the Roman Bold weight, and the Ultra was extrapolated from the Roman and Black weights.

The italics, on the other hand, were drawn by hand and were based primarily on Granjon’s Ascendonica Cursive, cut for Plantin in 1570. (Reference– article by Chuck Bigelow on Galliard mentioned in my post above.)
