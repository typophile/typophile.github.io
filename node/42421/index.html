Most game designers are concerned with style, rather than legibility, as Eben said. They view type as a way to emphasize the setting or atmosphere of the game, and less as a data source. This is ironic, of course, as gamers depend on the HUD to give them instant-access information that is directly tied to their performance in the game world. Valve Software does a good job with Half-Life 2, using DIN throughout the HUDs of the three chapters out so far, as well as a legible screen font for small type (not sure which, offhand. Probably Verdana.) They have a keen eye for graphic design along with an incredible game design sense. Kind of like Pixar in many ways.

John’s also hit another issue on the nose, particularly with PC games (vs console). The advent of HD has also lead some developers to typeset info that is completely illegible on SD TVs. So lame.

If I had the free time, I’d love to write a paper on HUD/interface design in games.

2008-03-07 02:58:45 • Ikiimi

I’m impressed with the knowledge here about older (1990’s and older) video games and their role in typography (Oh Zork, how I loved you.). Usually in today’s day and age it seems to be overlooked by younger generations.

On to the topic, most electronic games tend to desire function over form when it comes to typography. As a few already have pointed out, letters on old or hand held systems were designed the way they were due to the pixel resolution’s limitations. Though, I agree, wading through sentence after sentence of an old final fantasy game with text larger than my character can get slow and choppy.

For a while there games were concentrated on either PC or consoles, consoles had their resolution limitations (640x480, yuck!) while PC’s could obtain much higher resolutions and provide better readability. Now a days game designers are focusing on the console crowd and higher resolutions as a second option and to focus on this is to work within limitations of screen and input options, resulting in poor typographic choices on a reading level (Oblivion and Deus Ex 2, I’m looking at you.). Thankfully this HD craze might help push game designers in a direction where all our eyes can be happy. Although I will argue that not being creative within constraints isn’t an excuse for bad type.

Part of the problem within game companies would be ignorance in typography. However, some execute this very well and as a system (as mentioned, Valve’s Half-Life 2) while others do what is right in terms of context (console style type, Ion Storm’s Deus Ex 1).

I also think context is a big issue in game typography that’s generally avoided (except in, maybe, the over used “future” and “outer-space” style typography). If you’re going to place a game in a certain time period, the designer should take account of the appropriateness of the type choices on many different reading levels.

I have to disagree with being used to the medium. I mean, I know exactly what you mean in terms of game play and how to use a game, but no one should get used to bad type.

In conclusion, I think this is a good point, perhaps there needs to be better graphic design practices in game design.

2008-10-14 09:21:17 • Kaliam

As a side note to the above thoughts, how Typography takes a backseat:

I have lost count of the amount of hours I’ve spent playing Final Fantasy games, and even now… in trying to visualize the type they utilized it just doesn’t come to mind. (Strangely though I can see the X in Final Fantasy 8 very well and it’s bitmappy as hell).

I think it goes without saying that, type takes a very, very, very far back seat. You have to remember that people will first be looking at the graphics. Something else important to remember is that type takes its most prevalent forms in two ways in video games:

Dialogue and Menus.

While you can make the argument that people will or will not notice the type in dialogues, Menu surfing, I’ve found, becomes systematic. If I’m pausing to restore a character’s heath, or to switch out a weapon I do it so quickly because I want to get back to the action! I think eventually, the menus become physical interface to our functional desires as opposed to these self-guiding mechanisms where we need to read the type. Every. Single. Time.

It’s just how it goes.
