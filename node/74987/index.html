Twenty years ago the congress of the United States declared in an action that the alphabet is in public domain such as the wheel. Since then the copyright laws have been extended to software. The altering of a letterform is not subject to copyright or patent unless the original name has been taken. I won a lawsuit over this matter. Dr. Type
2010-10-07 21:15:01 • Robert Trogman

The altering of a letterform is not subject to copyright

This may be misleading. You can’t take an existing digital revival and, eg, change the size a little, and legally resell it. The software copyright covers that, as I understand it, because of the ruling in Adobe vs SSI. This is in the U.S.

But as far as an old letter press typeface, I don’t know of any restriction on digitizing them. The name is a more complicated issue, and there may be restrictions.

Well, aside from the wild speculation I put in at the end, this post is pretty good for a summary: http://blogs.adobe.com/typblography/2006/01/legal_protectio.html

Note that copyright exists the moment you author something, while trademark and patent require paying money and submitting forms. However, you do get more benefits from copyright protection if you register the work.

Note also that almost all of this discussion is very US-centric. Broadly speaking, most European countries offer equivalent or greater protection.
