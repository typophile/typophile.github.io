Greetings.

I was wondering whether anyone here has got the font Monotype Ehrhardt. If so, may I ask where you got it, and whether your version has a full set of characters with diacritics and uncommon characters, especially characters no longer used for writing English (ash, thorn, eth, yogh)? I have a few books that are in Monotype Ehrhardt that do include such characters, but I have seen no downloadable versions of this font that do. Perhaps I have not looked hard or carefully enough, though.

I also wonder whether there is a Monotype Ehrhardt font for the Greek alphabet, complete with breathings, accents and other diacritics as well as older Greek characters used as numbers (the digamma, for instance). The Penguin Edition of John Milton’s Complete Poems uses Monotype Ehrhardt for Milton’s English, Italian and Latin poems, and uses what appears to be Monotype Ehrhardt Greek characters for Milton’s Greek poems. This is probably my favourite Greek font.

Any information would be much appreciated.

I have Monotype Ehrhardt, obtained as part of the Monotype Classics Library a few years ago. There is no Greek version of Ehrhardt included, or shown in their catalog. I’m afraid I don’t offhand know what the special characters are that you mentioned. I could look at character map, but I don’t know what I’m looking for.

Out of curiosity, how did you decide that your book used Ehrhardt Greek? The characters have no obvious relation to the Latin letters, so unless the typeface were identified as ‘Ehrhardt Greek’ what could you base that on?

Probably too late to be of any use to Percival, but here goes:

The Greek text in the Penguin Milton’s Complete Poems is something very like [[http://image.linotype.com/samples/hirespdf/9177.pdf|Linotype Baskerville Greek]], an upright, serifed Greek with a very roman feel to most of the letters (what is effectively an inverted ‘y’ standing in for lambda, for example). It has nothing - typographically - to do with the surrounding English and Latin text in Ehrhardt.

The forms of the Old and Middle English characters thorn, yogh, and eth, often found in company with Monotype Ehrhardt in Oxford University Press books (and particularly in editions published by them for the Early English Text Society), are, as far as I am aware, custom sorts from outside the main font (Ehrhardt), inserted into the text when called for. Some confirmation that this is the case can be had by glancing at a book like Michael Lapidge’s [[http://www.oup.com/uk/catalogue/?ci=9780198131830|The Cult of St Swithun]], (OUP, 2003), which, although set throughout in Monotype Bembo, uses versions of thorn and yogh in setting Middle English which are identical to those found alongside Ehrhardt elsewhere.

As far as I’m aware, the only easily obtainable (for a considerable price, though you do get a dictionary thrown in) typefaces to contain these ‘signature’ OUP forms of thorn, eth, yogh (and also wyn and the insular flat-topped ‘g’) are those [[http://www.oup.co.uk/ep/cdroms/ors/|versions of Plantin bundled with the electronic version of the OED]].

Another commercial font which contains yogh is Adobe [[http://store1.adobe.com/type/browser/pdfs/GRPP/GaramondPremrPro.pdf|Garamond Premier Pro]], which also contains thorn and eth (albeit these last two in their more rounded and familiar Icelandic forms). Free fonts containing these characters include SIL’s [[http://scripts.sil.org/cms/scripts/page.php?site_id=nrsi&item_id=Gentium|Gentium]] and [[http://junicode.sourceforge.net/|Peter Baker’s Junicode]]. This last is of particular interest, as it is intended entirely as a font for medievalists, and contains the distinctively insular versions of thorn, eth, yogh, wyn, &c, which are not far removed from those found in OUP books.

Finally, it should be noted that yogh should no longer be subject to any typographical confusion with ezh (although a flat-topped ezh is still often used in scholarly editions to represent yogh). Unicode now encodes yogh separately at codes U+021C and U+021D.
