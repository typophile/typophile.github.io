James A, Your definition of “substantial”, may be different from most, or at least misinformed.

Our method of self publishing the first 2 Indie fonts books involved a fee to be included in the book which was offset by being sent a number of books (100) that mathematics determined would be less than any wholesale rate would ever be. (therefore participating foundries would actually make money if they sold most of their books. The additional exposure has been reported to be helpful as well…not unlike advertising) This is how we paid the designer and for some of the printing. Our distribution was limited, but was somewhat effective: The first 2 sold quite well. In fact the indie fonts 1 book is all but sold out.

The books that James A is no doubt referring to is the Rockport publishers reprinting of the Indie Fonts books. We went with Rockport due to their tremendous distribution which we could not get anywhere near (in fact our only distributor that would take us at the time of the first book completely stiffed us and we never saw a cent from those). Our biggest disappointment with this arrangement was that Rockport decided to set the descriptive text a point size smaller than we had, in order to fit text in the event of a translated version of the book (which never happened). This reduction for our readable 5 point Faceplate to a miniscule 4 point made it unfortunately difficult to read. The other unfortunate change from our first edition was the Indie Fonts 2 being printed in halftone grey rather than the spot silver that makes the first edition quite appealing. The third volume was printed exclusively by Rockport with our concerns about the descriptive point size addressed, but late in the process we were told it would be a flexbound cover to match the reissues of 1 & 2 that Rockport was releasing. Our compromise is that we commissioned an edition of hardcover Indie Fonts 3 to self distribute.

To answer your “Why why why”, it is because you bought your indie fonts book from someone selling the Rockport reprints rather than directly from a foundry who was in the books. The small text is in the Rockport Edition. For full disclosure, we bought out some of the last stock of the indie fonts 1 book from Rockport and are selling them (at a special sale price, act now!) because we have no more of the first edition. http://www.p22.com/indiefonts/indie1.html

So to recap, the following volumes/edition of Indie Fonts exist

• Indie Fonts (P-Type publications) HC edition 5000 - distributed via foundries & limited bookshops (sold out*) • Indie Fonts 2 (P-Type publications) HC edition 5000 - distributed via foundries & limited bookshops (silver ink) • Indie Fonts 3 (P-Type/Rockport) HC edition 1000 - distributed via foundries (only hardcover edition)

• Indie Fonts (Rockport) HC edition - distributed via major chain & small bookshops (oop) • Indie Fonts 2 (Rockport) HC edition - distributed via major chain & small bookshops (oop)

• Indie Fonts (Rockport) flexbound edition - distributed via major chain & small bookshops • Indie Fonts 2 (Rockport) flexbound edition - distributed via major chain & small bookshops • Indie Fonts 3 (Rockport) flexbound edition - distributed via major chain & small bookshops

• Indie Fonts (Rockport/Japan) HC edition - Japanese edition- same content, Japanese foreword • Indie Fonts 2 (Rockport/Japan) HC edition - Japanese edition- same content, Japanese foreword

• Indie Fonts (Rockport/Russia) HC edition - Russian edition- same content, Russian foreword • Indie Fonts 2 (Rockport/Russia) HC edition - Russian edition- same content, Russian foreword

James M. is offering up the silver ink edition with proper size descriptive text.

*try Typeco or altered ego if you want to find a first edition copy.

A technical note for James Grieshaber and Richard Kegler—Indie Fonts could have been a much better designed book if you had made the page / book format rectangular in a landscape orientation. A rectangular format would have allowed twice as much width for the columns containing the descriptive text set in Faceplate sans condensed and left an adjacent square area for each font sample. You would have wound up with a rectangular-shaped book and lost the square appearance, but at least the area for the font samples would still be square, and the column for the descriptive text would have been wide enuff to use a normal width font or the same Faceplate condensed series at a larger point size.
