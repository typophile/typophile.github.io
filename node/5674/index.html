2004-07-09 03:08:15 • Giampa
Tom:

Right, I remember that problem now.

I have been giving it some thought, and I suspect it is a problem with Windows GDI using the ATM driver that is built into Windows (2000 and later), when dealing with synthetic obliques. My guess is that GDI isn't realizing that it has to allow for the fact that the fontmatrix has been slanted, changing the effective bounding box of the glyph, when it decides how much space to reserve for drawing the pixels onscreen.

So, by this theory, it would be a Windows bug.

Some readers may wonder what the heck a synthetic oblique and a fontmatrix are. If you want the full technical explanation of the gobbledygook, keep reading….

Type 1 fonts were originally developed to be built into output devices back in the early to mid 80s, when printer memory (ROM or even RAM) to store fonts was in short supply. So Adobe came up with the notion of having "synthetic" fonts that were just transformations of an initial font, and could refer to the original font and take up hardly any space. This was mostly used for oblique versions of some sans serifs, although it's also responsible for the horrid Helvetica Narrow, which was just regular Helvetica mathematically condensed by 18% (if memory serves). The "transformation matrix" is the bit of a Type 1 font that specifies any slanting or scaling of the outlines. It's in the header portion of the font, so it's plain text if you crack it open in a word processor or hex editor.

So, why does this matter for fonts on your computer instead of in a PostScript printer? Well, when making the computer-based version of the font, it was done the same way, except that the base font would be replicated, as well as having the transformation matrix applied. So the outlines for Univers Oblique in Type 1 are actually identical to the outlines for plain old Univers; the only difference is that transformation matrix specifying the slant.

Thus the theory that maybe Windows wasn't being quite smart enough to take that into account. It probably only affects cases where the transformation matrix is sufficient to stick something out past the end of the advance width of the glyph, mostly glyphs that are rendering right near the top right… like the cap W.

PostScript flavored OpenType fonts also support this same stuff. But when converting the Adobe Type Library to OpenType, besides the big stuff, we did a lot of minor improvements to how the fonts were built. One of them was to eliminate the synthetic font transformations using the fontmatrix, and instead change the actual font outlines.

So, if the Type 1 font has the problem, and the OpenType doesn't, well, given everything else, that sure seems like the probably culprit. It would be possible to fix the problem on the Windows side by fixing their bug, or by rebuilding the font to not use the synthetic oblique.

However, multiple masters or any axis-based solution are not the only way to achieve optical scaling, though they are certainly the most elegant. Note that just because the optically sized faces are packaged as separate fonts does not mean they have to be designed separately. Adobe uses MM technology in designing many of its typefaces today, including virtually all of those with optical size variants (although our upcoming Garamond revival has separately designed optical sizes, it's a special case).

Meanwhile, yes, you are wrong about the size of OpenType fonts. Is there some reason you can't read Adobe's OpenType User Guide, which I have pointed you at repeatedly? It's not that long, and written in plain English. Or my article on font formats, which is slightly more technical, but not too unreadable. Current version can be found here: http://www.adobeforums.com/Typography/1092

Anyway, with regard to OpenType font size, I have about 2500 fonts in my main fonts folder, including the entire Adobe Type Library in OpenType, which is most of that. The folder also has various supplemental files (PDFs, source files for some fonts, whatnot). The entire size of the folder is 550 MB.

OpenType fonts with PostScript outlines are generally considerably smaller than equivalent Type 1 fonts. They don't usually have the bitmaps at all, for starters (like Windows Type 1 fonts in this regard). CFF is simply more compact than Type 1, plus you can have subroutines to reduce the size even further. Compared to Windows Type 1 (which is smaller than Mac Type 1), one knocks off about 45% of the size.

Of course, depending on the size of the glyph set in the font and the extent of OpenType layout goodies involved, that can add a fair bit back.

A typical OpenType font from the Adobe library that was converted with no merged fonts and only minimal added extras is 28-45K, with 30-35K being most common.

A Pro font that adds CE language support but no extra typographic goodies might average 50-60K. A Pro font with CE support that also merges lots of typographic stuff averages around 100-150K. If it has really extensive language support as well (say Greek and Cyrillic, as well as Latin+CE), it might be in the 160-220K range. But it's not like your whole library will magically sprout CE, Greek and Cyrillic support. People still have to design this stuff.

Gerald & Gerald, was everything really Vne at your printing oWce a decade ago? Type 1 was an incredibly limited format even for English typography, let alone any of the world's more complex scripts. But everything is supposed to stand still because a couple of guys who would by happy to set all type by hand or on a Monotype caster thought messing around with expert sets to get their V, W, X, Y, and Z ligatures was Vne? A decade ago it didn't matter that the font format was limited to 8-bits, because so was most text processing and page layout software unless you happened to be Asian. But the idea that a font format limited to 8-bits has any future, or even much of a present, in 16- and 32-bit text environments it just laughable. You might also have noticed that a rather large percentage of the text you read every day is not beautifully printed on nice creamy paper: it's here on this screen. Something like OpenType happens to be necessary for the majority of people in the world to be able to read their own languages, particularly in electronic media in which, unlike print, the text remains live and the publisher has little or no control over eventual display. It happens that the companies that developed OpenType to meet this need also saw a use for it in making it easier to apply Vne typographic features, but typographers shouldn't be so parochial as to think this technology is all about them, or that just because they might have been satisVed with Type 1 – and I certainly wasn't satisiVed with it and wish it a speedy death – this has any bearing on the ability of billions of people to read their own languages in their own writing systems.

Gerald,
Surely you are joking? You must know what John means. 8, 16, 32 bits are the amount of information addressable by the computer. With each step up, the amount of information addressable increases by a huge amount. The first step is from 256 to 16,000,000. This means a font can contain not only enough information for the small Latin set of characters and so-called expert sets but for every known language including Kangi. It also can do a better job of kerning and smart replacement of glyphs giving type designers options they never had before. Surely you can see the value in this?
Every new technology comes in with bumps and bruises which take a while to repair. Remember when movable type came in? It pissed off a buch of caligraphers who had perfected the art of writing far better than any printing press at the time could come close to. Now measure how much your own company's metal types have improved upon typesetting since then. The hard part is for us old geezers. We have done the heavy lifting through earlier "new" technologies and remember the sigh of satisfaction of seeing improvement. To now see it all starting again and perhaps realizing that we don't have the energy of our youth and fear the task ahead may outlive us. Fear not, the young bit&byte mongers of this day will have their chance to finish the job and later lament the next wave of new technology to decend on them.
Gerald, keep making the beautiful type you are making and let the young tech jockeys of today ready it for the next "new" thing. It is like turning your drawings over to a punch cutter, you get to challenge them and speak your mind but they have to do the dirty work of cutting steel.
