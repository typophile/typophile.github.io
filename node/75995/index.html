2010-11-07 20:11:34 • frankrolf

Remember that ‘interpolation’ is just a ‘mathematical tool’ to quickly get a range of weights, stems and spacing worked out proportionally in a family of fonts and styles.

The greater the axis range, the more problematic the results.

“intrapolation” and “extrapolation”, going lighter than your lightest and heavier than your heaviest, usually give poor results or unexpected results.

Use it as a starting point, to establish the entire range of weights proportionally.

Then, tweak or edit the shapes in the center weight (usually regular), and re-interpolated the other in-between weights again.

The closer the weights are apart, the better the results will be. Also, very important to place the vector points in similar shape transition places along the letter shape. A little practice and you can get better results.

Remember, your eyes for critique process is the final stage to any successful range of weights and styles. Good luck.

Use the “Tools > Blend Fonts” function…

According to Luc(as) de Groot, where a is the thickness of the thin weight and c is the thickness of the fat weight, for b (the optical midpoint between a and c), b²=ac therefore b=√(ac)

so the magical percentage that will yield b (i.e. the correct stem thickness you require) is 100×(√(ac)-a)/(c-a).

Now there are a bunch of people round here who don’t like maths, so apologies to them.

I’m sure this has been discussed elsewhere but when I was designing my first font, I started with a regular, then drew a bold, then extrapolated at the thin end (which you’ve called intrapolation) and made a thin that I was reasonably happy with, then I extrapolated with the bold at the other end and got a heavy. After a few months of finessing I had a thin and a heavy and four intermediate weights that I was more or less happy with.

As for my current design, I started with a hairline as Kevin describes albeit with a slightly thicker stem and a heavy and I’m now starting to see some progress on the regular.
