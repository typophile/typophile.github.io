2009-09-11 23:58:10 • dezcom
This is a hard one; there are so many candidates. I’d probably give the nod to two: Jan Tschichold: typographer by Ruari McLean, and The Design of Books by Adrian Wilson. I never met either Tschichold or Wilson (I never met McLean either, but I spoke with him on the phone), but they had an enormous influence on my design aesthetic. And, through Adrian Wilson’s book, I was also strongly influenced by Jack Stauffacher, although I didn’t really know where the influence came from until I met Jack some years later. All of these typographers were concerned with readable text, and with effective design that aided the reader, not the ego of the designer. (Jack Stauffacher still is, I’m happy to say.) Trendy books are useless. Actually, they’re more than useless: they’re often pernicious. Though as Ben McElroy (bemerx25) points out, even those can serve as the spur to get us interested.

this old, small, tattered red-covered hardbound book called ‘painting signs and sho cards’ by e.c. matthews from 1920 that my dad had. it shared commercial art and composition philosophies as well as the intricacies of using type and lettering to set the mood and speaking to your audience or market. very little about set type but mostly about handpainting and fountain pen lettering. timeless design principles came from this little unassuming book that i still have to this day.
