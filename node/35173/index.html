2007-07-17 01:10:49 • sarafina

In general I’m down on the classics, but have to admit that I’ve used them all at one point or other – so I shouldn’t begrudge others having their fling. I even became quite cosy with Helvetica for a while in the mid ’80s.

In some ways it’s like the repertoire of classical music. There was one pianist who was 30ish, and “saving Beethoven for later” (maybe I got the instrument and the composer wrong, but you get the idea).

From a type designer’s perspective, many of us take a crack at the classics, doing “our” version of Jenson or whatever. Jenson, Bodoni, Garamond – those seem to be the “essentials” for reviving, and for using no doubt. So I would say, if you’re going to get involved with one of those hoary old faces, why not work with a few variations – including present day interpretations – to really get the feel of the thing.

In another thread today I said one could set just about any text (I’m speaking of body text as in a book) in a good cut of Baskerville. Interestingly, although I have a licence for Storm’s version, I’ve never actually used it, believing I could pick a typeface that exactly suited the purpose (Baskerville is comparatively wide, too, and economy is often an issue). Here’s what I’ve used over the years for setting books, and was happy with the results (in order of most to least used):

Filosofia Tyrnavia Minion Bembo Fournier Eplica Elysium Relato Caslon (Berthold and Adobe) Adobe Garamond Centaur Adobe Jenson Warnock Goudy Old Style

Myriad is the sans serif I use almost exclusively, mainly for labels on maps and diagrams and occasionally for photo captions (I’m not a big sans fan).
