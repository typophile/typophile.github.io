And if one of the most brilliant font experts uses the header “fraktur.htm”, you should probably go with it, for “Fraktur” is the generic term for ALL such fonts comprising ALL sub-categories including Schwabacher. Therefore books on ALL such fonts have the main title “Fraktur”, although they also deal with the sub-category of Schwabacher fonts. See e.g.

http://www.fraktur-mon-amour.com/

http://www.typografie.de/verlagsverzeichnis/typografie/260-0.html

By the way, the original spelling was “Schwobacher”:

http://www.sanskritweb.net/temporary/schwobacher.jpg (Zedlers Universal-Lexicon, 1732)

so that grumblers might also criticize the “wrong” spelling “Schwabacher”.

James, many, many German books use Fraktur as a generic term for all blackletters. It is just the way the language developed. For hundreds of years, the most commonly-used sort of blackletter in Germany were Fraktur types. I guess it became sort of a “brand,” you know, like Kleenex tissues or Xerox machines. Or Hoovers in England or whatever!

The point is, you are right, when using English. But in German, one can call a Schwabacher a “Fraktur,” even when it isn’t. Real typographers know the difference anyway, I guess. Sorry, but I think that Uli is correct here.
