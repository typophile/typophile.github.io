Serifed Caps With Notched Stems (Tuscan?) Of Varying Thickness Used In Powell & Pressburger's A Canterbury Tale

September 27, 2014 • darkandlong

This face is used in the closing credits, with a heavier weight used for most of them, but a lighter weight used here for ‘THE END’.

You can see the whole film on YouTube here: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rXQqzeiTYDo, with this still from 1:58:00. If you don’t know it, it is a very strange and rather wonderful film in parts and well worth watching.

This was the era when movie lettering was still hand lettered rather than transferred direct from a font. Looks like a version of the widely used Trajan column letters. Update The E is wider than usual for a classical Trajan. I agree that the letters have a Tuscan flavor with the bifurcated ends, but off hand I don’t recall a Tuscan that is an exact match. In these letters the bifurcated terminals are very restrained compared to the digital fonts that I have seen. Don
