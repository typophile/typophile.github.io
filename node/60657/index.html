I've always wondered about that first one. I don't think it's a real computer font from the when the film was made (1979). For one thing, it's proportional, not monospaced. It also seems like it has a lot of subtle curves and other details, which would mean it's probably not a bitmap font. My best guess is that it was set with conventional typesetting, animated on film or video and displayed on green-screen monitors. Still don't know what the typeface is. Could be custom. Whatever it is, it appears to be stretched horizontally. I tried condensing it, but it still doesn't look like any typeface I know. The second one appears to be a bitmap font based somewhat on Eurostile. (Square 721 is a clone, BTW.) It also appears that the smaller sizes were scaled down from the larger bitmap. You can see the usual distortion that happens when you do that.
2009-08-06 16:09:11 • Mark Simonson
I think the first one looks like a customized version of [[http://new.myfonts.com/fonts/berthold/city-be/|Berthold City]]. All you have to do is make the serifs full and it's very close. The numerals seem to match as is. Optically widening it would have been easy, if they were using photo-type. City was designed in 1930, so it would have been available. The other option is that it might actually have been a computer screen font, even though Mark didn't think so. Each computer system had its own built-in screen font in those days, I believe, and they were probably editable through software. - Mike Yanega

2009-08-07 02:47:50 • loungelizard
Mike, you pretty much nailed it. It's Berthold City Light, not modified. The light weight has the serifs that are missing on the bolder weights. The heaviness seen in the sample is no doubt from the glow of the video screen. The letters are scaled to exactly 200% of the normal width and tracked out about 120-160 em units.
