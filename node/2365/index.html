It's a complex book. Historically it's had "untouchable" status, but these days people have been pointing out weaknesses (which it does have). On the other hand, those -the few- who really bag on it have got it all backwards.

It's really a goldmine of type knowledge, and brings together many historic specimens, with usually expert analysis from an actual user of type - and a proficient one. However it's also a very personal book - not a "scholarly" one deep down. Like when Updike makes Dutch type sound like trash and Spanish type like the best thing since sliced bread. But if you take his particular opinions in stride and realize it has some historical gaps, then it's really uniquely useful - there's nothing like it. However, it's also a specialist's book. It's all about type, not much more. You really have to love type to enjoy it.

BTW, there have been three "historic" editions: 1922, 1937, 1967. The first one is supposed to have the best reproduction, the second one has new material, the third neither - which is I guess how I managed to buy a copy of that one (both volumes) for only $75. The Dover (?) reprints are not considered very good, but the textual content of course is there (as opposed to the fidelity of the specimens), and that's most of its value as far as I'm concerned.

Yes, there is no dot pattern with collotype but there is reticulation (zero to infinity on the grayscale) and often extreme exposure variation. I do know this as my partner and I spent six months researching how to do collotype and another six trying to do it. After all that time we actually got 146 pretty damn good copies (!!!) printing on an Albion iron handpress

But, that was just the results of a couple of enthusiastic learners. I'm quite sure, that as you say, the Oxford collotypes were quite on the mark. That was somewhat the peak of commercial collotype production.

Shortly afterward, the process began to disappear from the commercial scene. I have a very intriguing French literary magazine, published immediately after WWII, that has wonderful photos of the French Resistance Printers. And they were all in collotype, very, very rich in tonal quality. In the postwar years the process fell into the domain of fine printmakers. Very few folks are masters of it today.

2003-12-18 17:33:26 • Nick Shinn
Nick

Updike was a working printer with an intense interest in his heritage, and followed in DeVinne's path in this regard. Like DeVinne, because he was a printer, he brought a prospective to printing history that was refreshing clear compared with previous scholarly efforts.

This book is actually the first encompassing look at the history of type design and development. It is quite important despite any views we might choose to project from our contemporary perspective.

Updike had prejudices. He denigrated blackletter, had little use for the travesties of the nineteenth-century… He was a revivalist and sought to understand and explain that lineage, in doing so he discarded what would not fit.

He was no elitist (not in the way you choose to see him at least), just a proud working stiff, with, actually, very little use for work outside the commercial sphere. For instance, he had little respect for the private presses of his time period, oddly, as they contributed significantly to changes in the industry that Updike himself championed.

The typographic revival was not started by Updike, he was simply one of those who had a hand in legitimizing it.

Gerald, thank you for pointing out Updike's position as an innovative historian.

When you say he was a working printer, do you mean that he actually got his fingers dirty?

You are right, a little historical relativism is called for: what we would today call snobbery was, in the context of the class system of his time, merely "pride".

But by the same token, you shouldn't describe Updike as a "working stiff", with today's connotations of exploited labourer.
Updike's prejudices were those of his time. Book printers (such as he?) looked down their noses at jobbing printers who did commercial (ie advertising) work, did they not? So, in that sense, it is incorrect to describe him as a commercial printer.

Wasn't there a "class" system, with private presses at the top, the Updikes and De Vinnes in the middle, and jobbing printers at the bottom?

To revise my point: the idea we have now of the importance of popular culture didn't emerge until the 1960s. So Updike, in writing a serious history of his discipline, ignored popular (commercial) culture.

(Mind you, things haven't really changed, it's still only a small "arty" part of the graphics industry that gets the trade press coverage and the critical analysis in books.)

All the marvellous typographic inventions and virtuosity (you consider these "travesties"?) of the mainstream 19th century, Updike regarded as degradation. So, there are no showings in his book of fat faces, Clarendons, Sans Serifs, etc.

The founder's specimen books of the 19th century give a far broader idea of what was happening than Updike's narrowly focused exposition.

So, Updike: big ommissions, if you're expecting a broad coverage of type history. But at the time, innovative, and substantial in those areas he did cover. And very relevant to the graphic historicism of the early 20th century.

Gerald,

Updike was not alone in being unable to "see" the 19th century. To this day, the standard conception of modernity is that there were political and industrial revolutions c.1800, but that modern art didn't ramp up till impressionism, and take off till the 1900s, a century later. This viewpoint has been challenged recently by post-structuralists (eg "Techniques of the Observer, by Jonathan Crary) but there is still a huge amount of work to be done unearthing the modernity of the 19th century. Of course, type designers were right there early on, with the unicase bold sans serif!

As a counterforce to Revivalism, I would say that Cheltenham bridged the gap between 19th and 20th century modernism. It had a big, systematic family, printed well on coated and uncoated, in text and display, hot and cold. It's definitely not a Didone Modern, but in true Victorian fashion combined pseudo-historical forms with precisely engineered serifs. It was massive exactly concurrent with revivalism, until the 1930s. The Century typeface can also be seen as an evolution of the 19th Century modern. I have a 1921 edition of De Vinne's "Correct Composition", still set in – De Vinne.

For editorial text type, revivalism never supplanted the "numbered romans" (Victorian didone) in mass market magazines, books and newspapers. Even the Linotype "Old Style" is pretty much a Century (except for its italic).

Some considerable comment could be made about general quality of printed products during the nineteenth century.

Washboard type heights, multitudes of point standards unsuccessfully mixed into a singular type forme. Some reflection is more than deserved pondering master/slave working conditions non-conducive to fine typography. The results are generally best appreciated if you consider yourself viewing a typographical freak show.

Not to mention such things as cylinder presses that were built for one thing in mind, production. Quantity had clearly surplanted quality. The same could be applied to the inventions of both the Linotype, and Monotype machines. The mother of their inventions was Mrs. Get The Lead Out.

Later, Monotypes machines were tinkered with by the why can't you crowd such as the great Bruce Rogers. Cylinder presses were adapted for fine printing by John Henry Nash and Fount le Roy. The latter pioneered fine press work aboard semi automatic cylinder presses. A very novel idea at that time. I remember in my days this had been reversed and I was told that cylinder presses were superior to platens for fine printing. Both opinions are non sense if applied generally. Compare the problems of rule work on a cylinder press, vs. rule work on a platen press. Simplified, the purchase of a press was not a mechanical decision, it was a marketing decision.

Also the nineteen century was plagued by insidious optometry experiments regarding spacing techniques causing our great grand daddies to either become blind, or wishing they were blind. Pages with mutton spaces after a periods. (A frightful eyesore that lingered on even into my own day.)

This period of typography I hope does not inspire typographical nostalgia. Rightful it would be banned along with the past Chinese custom of binding young woman

If you were a myopic snob like Updike, you would now write a history of types that ended with the eclipse of metal, ignoring that which is immediately in front of you, phototype and digital type. Not to mention dry transfer type.

But from any meaningful historical perspective, surely the work that is most expressive of an era is done when designers come to grips with the new technology of that era. (Although, as you say, this takes no account of the social consequences.)

For instance, the ability of the phototypositor, and Letraset, to set type extremely tightly, overlapping and negative leaded even, produced a style of setting, starting in the early 60s, that later informed a generation of type designers such as Lubalin and Benguiat. Whatever one thinks of the quality of photofilm, At the time, it ruled.

Working with filmset ITC faces as an art director in the 70s and 80s, I always felt as if there was a wall between me and the ancient noble essence of typography that I sought to somehow incorporate in my work, yet at the same time, I have to say that the way the typesetting industry was set up put a lot of typographically skilled, knowledgable people into the production process workflow, and accorded a huge importance to typography – just look at its prominence in the print ads of the era, in the awards annuals.

And although Ed Benguiat thought Herb Lubalin was the only one who ever used Avant Garde's alternates with any taste, those alternates made typography a lof of fun for—the working stiff.
