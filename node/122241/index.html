If you have Photoshop or some other image editing software, create a clean black and white version of your image. Try to get the type as solid/black as possible, and the background as white as possible without blasting out any details of your type. Ideally, the letters should be at least 100 pixels tall. For script fonts, you also need to separate/create a gap between the letters so each glyph can be recognized by visual search software. Not really an issue with your sample, however. whatfontis.com does a decent job of font matching, especially for “freemium” fonts. You upload your image, identify the letters, and let it do its thing (you need to have at least two letters to search on for the free version; searches on one letter alone require registration). The higher resolution and clearer the image, the better luck you’ll have. Note that the best match may not be listed first. Find My Font, available for sale at Findmyfont.com, does an even better job of matching, and has a scissors tool that allows you to define individual letters in a line of script (no image editing required). It, too, works best with a clean black and white image at a decent resolution. The best matches are listed in descending order. WhattheFont on myfonts.com only works for commercial typefaces that Myfonts sells, and even if the typeface is offered for sale on the site I find its matching capabilities to be limited.
