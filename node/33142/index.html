Rounded Form = Organic?

April 18, 2007 • Will Miller

has anyone noticed a trend in organic this and that? nearly every instance i come in contact with on a day to day basis that utilizes type has some sort of rounded features softening the edges. i'm not saying the aesthetic is entirely off the mark, just seems weird that rounded types have come to mean organic. i guess childish/cute and soft means healthy and good for the enviornment...pure perhaps? has anyone else noticed this? does it bother anyone? _will
"childish/cute and soft means healthy and good for the enviornment" I don't draw this parallel. Sounds like there are several ideas/trends that overlap. Maybe it's worthwhile to identify the different trends. For instance, lots of polluting consumer items are styled to look soft and round (like cars). At the same time, lots of people concerned with sustainability and green materials don't follow the "soft and cuddly" esthetic. Form, in a lot of cases, is not following function, it's following trends. I think "organic" applied to type means there are human, natural elements, as opposed to mechanistic, geometric or modular. This could be anything from the proportions to the serif shapes to the curves to the edge quality. Not necessarily rounded or cute.

>childish/cute and soft That's only one kind of curve, not that I'm prejudiced against cute. The sinuous curves of art Nouveau are not 'cute' nor are the curves of Caslon's muscular italic. Curves can be dynamic, like the Concorde, or sexy, like--well choose your favorite lady or gentleman. Sharp corners and straight lines are mechanical, and can have their own power, but the world of curves has a much greater variety, both of form and mood.
