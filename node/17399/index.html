Axis and readability

January 19, 2006 • George Horton

Intuitively it seems to me that readability in moderately contrasting seriffed romans increases along the series vertical axis, oblique axis, variable axis, divergent interior and exterior axes. Would it be ridiculous to suggest that oblique axis spreads letter-differentiating information across a larger portion of the word, away from, as in vertical axis, the top of the vowel height; that variable axis positions differentiating information at different points on letters and so makes them more readily distinguishable, as well as spreading information across the word; and that divergence between the axes of partially or wholly enclosed counters and exterior outlines increases the perceived contrast between black and white themselves, allowing white portions of the word, as in Legato, to unite perceptually? Naturally, divergent interior and exterior axes are not incompatible with variation in either from letter to letter, and in fact in my fiddlings the impression of strength that comes from divergent interior and exterior axes is diminished if those axes and that divergence are too consistent across the alphabet.

I posted this idea to an old thread, then saw it was off-topic; I would love to hear what our readability experts have to say on the matter.

George,

I like your suggestion very much. It isn’t ridiculous because it is a long way from being proven or disproven, and also because it is intriguing. George, wonderfully provocative post. I might prefer to replace the term ‘readability’ here with ‘ease of visual wordform resolution.’ As I see it, visual wordform resolution is the pivotal perceptual processing event in reading, and depends on cross-letter binding. If one presumes that too much regularity makes the patternednes of a configuration salient and self-asserting, compromising–rather than solidifying–the self-assertion for perception of the configuration’s object-like integrity through effective binding, your sequence is just what one might expect.

This presumption about regularity strikes me as potentially testable.

Your series of contrast-types reads: modern / oldstyle / transitional / postmodern; or: expansion / translation / translation + rotation.

2006-01-20 04:00:29 • George Horton

Scientism is bunk. Reading is primarily a cultural phenomenon, not physiological. In America, reading became a national obsession starting c.1870. Public education and libraries were instituted, and an explosive growth of popular magazines, newspapers, and dime novels occurred from 1885 thru 1905. Almost without exception, the text types of the new mass media were moderns (didone), or “old styles” with vertical stress. If the modern style is so inimical to reading, why were these types preferred? The same could be said about sans serifs on the internet.

I would argue that the older forms are favored towards the end of an historical cycle, and divergency has nothing to do with it, other than being a marker of a media’s maturity.

And what, really, is scientism?

“The belief that the investigative methods of the physical sciences are applicable or justifiable in all fields of inquiry.”

Design is a piece of turf fought over by science and art. At the other end of the spectrum from science: “The only thing that matters in art is what cannot be explained” (Braque).

Braque was contrasting art with literal criticism, but I don’t see typography and design as part of this either/or scenario, because it’s not a question of whether something may or may not be explained; with design things are best demonstrated, which is a horse of a different colour.
