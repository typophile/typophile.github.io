2004-01-31 00:47:17 • steve_p
What a lot of people don't seem to realize is that a script like the one posted above is not a trivial thing to turn into a font. The complexity of just those two words is beyond what you would normally find in a font. For example,

* The two capital letters are not the same height.
* The trailing letters–the "e" and "n"–would not work in the middle of a word, and the internal letters would not necessarilly work at the end of a word.
* The way the "C" overhangs the "a" would not work next to a character with an ascender, like an "h" or "l".
* The connecting stroke between the "o" and "r" would probably have to be treated as a ligature. In any case, it is difficult to incorporate an "r" like that into a font.
* The connecting stroke between the "i" and "f" is wider than the others to make room for the crossbar on the "f".

This is not to say that some or all of these issues couldn't be resolved such as by adding alternate characters and ligatures. Unfortunately, users often don't know how to use such fonts.

For example, many of House Industry's script fonts have special characters and ligatures to overcome problematic situations. Yet, I often see these fonts used–even in design annuals–as if the designer had no idea these special characters existed.

You can also design a script font in such a way to avoid features that don't work with certain combinations, but at the sacrifice of many of the qualities that give hand-drawn lettering its appeal.

OpenType promises to make such complex scripts easy to use, but, so far, support is uneven. Yet, even when there is full support for complex fonts that can emulate custom lettering, it won't change the fact that if you want something unique, you won't get it by simply typing on your computer keyboard.

2004-01-31 17:56:19 • piccic
Doyald Young, on his site (www.delphipress.com) summed it up well.

If there are thousands of fonts, why handletter or modify a font to create a logotype? Because every company desires a distinct image for itself and its brands, which is unique and copyrightable, an important point. A custom logo is one of of a kind, precisely fashioned. One of the many design approaches, are logos that carry an identifying shape, or illustration. But the majority of commercial logos exist as name only and must rely on their letterforms to impart a distinctive image that the public will remember.

Commonly used fonts such as Times Roman, Garamond, Baskerville, Helvetica, and Univers are available to everyone, and because they are seen everywhere, their distinctive qualities are diminished by the boredom of repetition. These same fonts though, may be gently modified to create a more coherent and distinctive logo that is reproduceable in a range of sizes, positive and negative, and on a wide variety of surfaces.

A custom logo, handlettered or computer generated, must address only the letters that the name contains. Each letter can then be designed for maximum compatibility, a less daunting task than font design, where each letter of the font: caps, lowercase, figures, punctuation, and special characters, must be designed as a harmonious group.

Whether the logo is drawn by hand, or is completely designed on the computer, a thorough understanding of letterforms is vital for the successful resolution of a logo that is eminently usable, with a lifespan of 15 to 20 years.
