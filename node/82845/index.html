Hi everyone,

This is not so much a design question, but I’ve already spent quite a bit of time trying to decide on the fonts and formatting of my engineering PhD thesis (I should really be writting it, not making it pretty, so I thought I’d ask for some advice).

Some of my figures will be made using software that only allows for a choice between one serif or sans serif font. My thesis will be printed, but most of the versions will be read on screen, so I’d like fonts that are a good compromise between screen and print legibility.

Any help would be appreciated, as my sense of graphical design is twitching every time I look at my thesis in its current format.

2011-06-07 10:38:48 • sevag

Or, you could try the tandem Georgia (text, serif) & Verdana (figures, sans), or Arial (the options are quite limited) & Times New Roman. These four fonts should meet your typographical requirements and you most certainly have them installed.

Focus on the typography and not on the typefaces. Print samples. For example, try to see at what size/leading does Georgia (which is optimized for screen but should work in print also) invites you to read it, provided there are no more than twelve words per line. Check out Times too. It is neither ugly nor boring. Arial, having so many glyphs, is highly useful. You could do a nice piece of engineering of your thesis with some carefully planned typography using these fonts.

2011-06-10 17:41:12 • loveguga

I’m using .eps figures, so editing is likely to be tricky.

Inkscape edits .eps and .pdf figures. I usually save my Inkscape figures as .eps and then run epstopdf to get the corresponding pdf though Inkscape can also save pdf files (Inkscape’s “natural” mode is .svg). The resulting figures (.eps or .pdf) can easily be included in a LaTeX document as vector graphics (to be used with latex or pdflatex respectively). I use Inkscape on a mac, but it runs under X11. If your windows station has an XWindow terminal emulator, maybe you could run Inkscape from a unix server and get the display on your Windows station.

I looked at the Word thesis template provided by your university and even if they say that figure captions should use sans serif, their template uses the serif font. If the sans that is not Helvetica is used only in headers, the difference may not show that much.

Another option is to have the free font [[http://www.gust.org.pl/projects/e-foundry/tex-gyre/heros|Tex Gyre Heros]] installed on your PC. That font comes with the texlive 2010 installation; it is based on Nimbus Sans, that is installed on Linux stations in our grad lab (and available in Open Office…).

By the way, Informatics has a nice [[http://www.inf.ed.ac.uk/student-services/graduate-school/for-research-students/work-and-play/creating-informatics-theses-slides-and-posters|LaTeX template]] using Nimbus Sans for headers and captions. It might be worth a try.
