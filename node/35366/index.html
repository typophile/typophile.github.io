Typophiler David Hamuel has a lovely calligraphic font, [[http://www.myfonts.com/fonts/lighthouse/shirah-25/|Shirah 25]]. Matching Hebrew and Roman fonts is always difficult. It may be that a font with the 'crowns' on the letters will better match a roman script font, because of the high contrast. The best thing is probably to get a calligrapher with Hebrew expertise to do the invitation, and he or she will be able to match the Hebrew and English. I am talking about the calligrapher doing a model to photograph and print from, of course, not the addressing the envelopes etc. There are a number of fine calligraphers who do this kind of thing--Hebrew and English--in the New York area, where you are. They also do Ketubbot, etc.

2007-07-25 06:18:10 • John Hudson
Yes, I was referring to tagin. I think I remember 'stam' fonts being used on invitations, but they may have been Bar or Bat Mitzvah invitations. Because the Bar Mitzvah ceremony involves the first reading from the Torah for the child, it might be more appropriate there. I don't know of any prohibition on using this style, though. Overall, a copperplate latin script I think would be particularly hard to match, because Hebrew cursive script is not connected, and the normal square writing shapes are very different. I would think that a calligraphic roman, like Brioso or Diotima, would be easier to match with a calligraphic Hebrew. But overall, it is very hard because Hebrew has thin verticals and thick horizontals, and Roman the opposite. If the invitation is done by a calligrapher he or she can keep the pen at a similar angle for both, and make them work together. I put together David and Friz Quadrata on a project, and they worked as the low contrast made the differences in stress stand out less. But they wouldn't work on an invitation...
