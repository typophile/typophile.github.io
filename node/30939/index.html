2007-01-28 22:47:05 • Paul Cutler
You're off to a good start, but cut back to black-and-white for now. Also, be careful how you use the two circles; when they abut it can create some nasty visual problems. For example, in the second example the alignment of the little circle makes the area around it vibrate as it seems to warp the edges of the inner and outer circle. In the third example, because the circles are different colors, distracting little points are created where the circles abut. Last, don't use red and green together. They're complementary colors and buzz against each other, which tends to irritate people.

2007-01-30 23:09:55 • behren
The little circle is redundant and visually distracting. It's a symbol we read -- unlike a comma, we actually say "degree" when a circle follows a number. Therefore, your company name reads "nth degree degree". I say it needs to go. The rest is getting better. I'm bothered by the shift in weights between the "n" and "th" -- the font sizes are too close for these two elements to have different stem weights, it looks odd. Try scaling down the "th" or scaling up the "n". Perhaps the "n" can drift out of the box like the circle does? Just some thoughts. Overall, a great start for a non-designer design! Well done.

It might help to incorporate some "actual usage" examples into your design process. Try it small, on a business card, and large, on a lawn sign, and see how it looks in the hand, or driving by. That way you will be able to tell whether your combination of colors and type weights are working, and whether the design elements will scale nicely.
