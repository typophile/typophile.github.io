Line width and leading

May 19, 2006 • wspr

Over at Microsoft’s font blog 1 they discuss published reading speeds for various line widths and line spacings. The numbers are pretty clear: it’s best to read 10pt/12pt text with a width of 3.2 inches, closely followed by the same at 2.3 inches. In order to get a better feel for the numbers, I made a document with actual typeset examples of the layouts discussed (linked from some comments I had 2).

( Side note. 3.2 inches seems awfully narrow compared with that seen in academia! Some of the papers I read are hideous: documents with 11pt Times on A4 paper with one inch margins. On the other hand, most are two-column, which makes for annoying screen reading, but much easier reading overall. )

Anyway, how do these numbers correspond to what you use in practise? Do we mostly “do the right thing” without needing such quantitative evidence? Would anyone dispute the results given?

2006-05-19 11:47:08 • Grot Esqué
Bringhurst says that line length should not exceed 2.5 lower case alphabets of whatever face you are working with, or usually 60 to 75 characters. I have heard of some people who will accept a line length of 90 characters, but imho that is pushing it. I am not a fan of setting solid type, and will usually add sufficient leading to improve legibility, although there are times when equal point sizes for both can look pleasing. Its all relative to what goals you have with your design.
2006-05-19 11:51:04 • track and kern

In regards to line length, legibility can also be determined by the number of words in the line, with optimum legibility occuring at around 10-12 words per line (or 60-70 characters).

Herbert Spencer’s The visible word is a good source for legibility related research, may be hard to find though.
