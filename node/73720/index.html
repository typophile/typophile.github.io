2010-09-01 01:53:05 • quadibloc

Ulee, you are correct. When working with Indic languages, it’s important to establish which encoding to use before the translator keys the text. We do a lot of [[http://www.worldaccent.com/hindi/typesetting|Hindi typesetting]] and this comes up time and again.

It sounds as if there was not too much text, and the translator was able to re-type the text as Unicode. Hopefully that has fixed your problem.

If not, it may well be possible to convert, although this is often an involved process and in my experience can introduce some character errors. The other possibility is to set in the font the translator has used. All pre-Unicode Indic fonts were “proprietary” encodings but it is possible to use some of these in InDesign.

Although I don’t do a lot of foreign-language projects, when I do I try to work with translation services that I know to be computer-saavy. Generally I just turn over the English InDesign file to them and they do the translation and also revise the InDesign file. Because since I don’t speak the language, I wouldn’t have the vaguest idea whether the hyphenation is correct or not, or if the accents are correct in that font, and so forth, so I’d rather leave that to them.

One of my corporate clients has most of their pieces translated in foreign languages and the company they use is asisttranslations.com.
