2003-12-01 06:59:12 • John Hudson
Actually, the assertion that mac screens are 72dpi is also not quite correct either. The operating system renders type at a nominal resolution of 72dpi, but how its actually displayed on a particular monitor is up to the monitor's size and pixel resolution. Its an easy thing to prove too - just set your monitor's resolution to a different one than it is currently working at and the actual resolution of it in DPI will change, but the nominal resolution that the OS is drawing graphics at will remain at 72dpi on a mac or 96dpi (or whatever) on a Windows box. DPI is, as fellow hairyface Thomas said, irrelevant. 2003-12-02 04:36:38 • hrant
Here's one good reason: It makes it easier to do the math. 72 has a lot of numbers which can be divided into it evenly: 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 9, 12, 18, 24, and 36. Notice anything interesting about those numbers? Most of them are standard type sizes. It made it easy to figure out how many lines fit into an inch with standard type sizes. Even better, many of the standard sizes divide evenly into one another by a factor of 2 or 3. Some standard type sizes are odd (10, 11, 14, 30, 60) or only come out even every 2 inches (48), but not as many as there would be if a different number had been chosen. Try doing the same thing with 50, 75, or 100. With 50 you get 2, 5, 10, and 25. 75 gets you 3, 5, 15, and 25. 100 gets you 2, 5, 10, 20, 25, and 50. Since we use computers to do all our math now, convenient numbers like 72 are little more than historical habits, but it's not hard to see why they were chosen in the metal type days.

2003-12-09 19:48:38 • jcroft
From what I've seen, resizing the images doesn't affect it, but resampling it does. Aside from compression, the number of pixels involved (resampling) determines file size, not how many dots per inch (resizing). I saved the same image at 72dpi, 300dpi and 1000 dpi (resampling off in Photoshop to keep them at 100x100 pixels each). Each file was 884 bytes. Resampled to 200x200 pixels, the 72dpi image was 1,599 bytes. So I gather that pixels are information (bytes on disk), and dpi is how large the pixels are – in print.
