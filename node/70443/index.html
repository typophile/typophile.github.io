Transitional English type (from Updike)

May 15, 2010 • ncaleffi

This is from D. B. Updike’s “Printing Types”, vol. 2, pp. 186-187: it is a page from “a poem folio entitled ‘The Sovereign. Addressed to His Imperial Majesty Paul, Emperour of all the Russias’, by Charles Small Pybus, London, [Thomas?] Bensley, 1800”, described by Updike as “a superb showing of transitional Enghlish types just about to become modern face”. It seems to me a very interesting - and rarely seen - example of a way to approach a post-Baskerville typeface: an early Nineteenth century modern type which doesn’t look like nor Bodoni/Didot, neither the Modern/Scotch types. I don’t recall having seen a similar typeface in Nineteenth century specimens, other than here; and I wonder if someone can point to some other printing references for it.

Below is the full 300 dpi sample attached.

I went to Updike to check that, indeed, the typeface is not named in this instance.

Still, it does look oddly familiar: it reminds me of the faces of W. A. Dwiggins, such as Caledonia, which are classed as Modern and yet are clearly far more graceful than the usual Scotch Roman typefaces.
