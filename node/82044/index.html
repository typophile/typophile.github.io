Classic Vs. Modern

May 10, 2011 • Claire Bibio

so i have been hearing recently from people call fonts modern classic and i’m confused. isn’t the term classic for old things like Bodoni. also can something be modern and classic at the same time, could you explain please. when does a modern thing become classic and is it correct to call something created in the year 2011 a classic? thank you very much!
Hi Claire. ‘Classic’ does not necessarily have a very good definition anymore, within or without the type world. At its core, classic denotes timelessness, or being of a quality by which all subsequent works are measured. If something were produced today that fit those standards, it may then be considered a ‘modern classic,’ but this term itself is as you point out kind of silly because of its paradoxical nature and general overuse (and misuse). To further complicate the issue, pair that with typographic classification systems that use the same or similar words—classical describing the renaissance & post-renaissance Roman types, modern describing romantic faces such as Bodoni’s and Didot’s, modern describing a high-contrast Scotch Roman as seen in faces such as De Vinne, modern describing futurist types such as Futura, modern describing any types that coincided with the artistic movement called ‘modernism,’ and modern describing anything done recently.

There is a distinction between classic as noun and adjective. A new typeface can be classic, in the sense that it adopts a venerable style, but it can never be a classic until a suitable period of time has elapsed.

With music, classics are works that have been interpreted many times, over time. Perhaps the same meaning can be applied to typefaces.
