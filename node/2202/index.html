2003-11-25 15:27:45 • gerald_giampa
Caps at the beginning of sentences have the functionality of marking when once sentence ends, and another begins. In 'chatting' people generally do one or two sentences at a time, so the differentiation is already marked by the exchange, and caps don't help further. But in continuious text like this, they are very functional. You could do away with this use of caps by adding extra space between sentences, but this would not save space, as the Swedish official wanted to. In capitalizing proper names, you give the readers the signal that if the name is not important to them, they don't have to slow down to read it. So it is functional too. I don't know whether the noun caps in German are a benefit in German and what their impact on English would be. How to capitalize titles is also currently in flux, and I could see this changing, as it is not really an issue of functionality. I was interested to read in one of Giampa's links on the 'Bauhaus' thread that they were opposed to caps, as well as serifs. To a great extent their ideas have been tested over time. Sans serifs have succeeded in titles and display, but not in small solid text. And caps have not been abandoned. I personally think that this is not conservatism, but that the scribes who evolved these conventions - like the lower case letters themselves - were on to what is really functional. I think that those who don't use caps in continuous text either don't understand their functionality, or can't be bothered to hit the shift key to help their reader.

2003-11-25 19:07:39 • gerald_giampa
And since caps only take up only about 5% of text (although double to triple that in German)… Unlike lowercase letters, the frequency of caps depends very much on the nature of the text being sampled, e.g. something written in the first person is likely to have a high incidence of uppercase I, while something in the third person might have few or even none. Similarly, if a document makes reference to many proper nouns – people, places or organisations – or uses acronyms, it will have a higher frequency of uppercase letters. When I was sampling texts for average English letter frequency, I deliberately ignored uppercase letters, because including them distorted the frequency more than excluding them. I initially thought that it would be essential to sample uppercase letters in German frequency analysis, but I was surprised by just how low the frequency of uppercase letters compared to lowercase letters is even in German. Sampling the same text in English and German translation – a Bible text of 30,000+ words, including a lot of proper nouns that bumped up the average uppercase letter count for the English –, I found that in the German the uppercase letters only accounted for 3.7% of the total alphabetic characters. I think the frequency of uppercase letters in German is often overstated.
