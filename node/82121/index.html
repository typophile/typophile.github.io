Aldus: Greek vertical kerning?

May 12, 2011 • eliason

A student of mine has this query that I thought Typophiles might be able to help with:

For my current final paper, I have chosen to evaluate the life and printing/publishing legacy of Aldus Manutius, including his innovations in the way of printing Greek script during the early 1500s.

While researching this topic, I came across a claim from the work In Aedibvs Aldi: The Legacy of Aldus Manutius and His Press that it was a system of “vertical kerning,” among other typeface enhancements, which allowed Aldus to produce a successful Greek type. In addressing Aldus’s application for Privileges to the Venetian authorities, the book states,

“In justification for this application, Aldus mentions somewhat mysteriously ‘two new methods’ which he has invented for the printing of Greek. What these new methods were is not entirely understood…Barker…believes that the two methods mentioned were the vertical kerning of the letters and the system of using separate sorts of accents and letters.” (p. 34)

Earlier on, the source describes the physical attributes of the effect of this “vertical kerning” on printing Greek type with all breath and accent marks, stating:

“One important problem this first font had was that Aldus’s system of vertical kerning frequently produced ‘collisions’ between ascending and descending letters in adjoining lines. The first solution to this problem was to expand the amount of space between lines, but as Aldus continued to develop his presswork other improvements were made.” (pg. 31)

Although I have a mental picture of what this “vertical kerning” may have looked like, I haven’t been able to find any illustrations of the process, and I am therefore shaky in my description of it.

If there is anyone out there who can act as a reliable source, or has access to a reliable source which illustrates and explains vertical kerning in more detail, I would be very grateful for your help. Any illustrations of vertical kerning being used for greek type would be welcomed as well. I find this topic fascinating, and this is a piece of my research puzzle that I really need to solve to bring the topic together.

Here is the citation for the book I’ve been using:

Angerhofer, Paul J., Mary Ann Addy Maxwell, Robert L. Maxwell, and Pamela Barrios. In Aedibvs Aldi: The Legacy of Aldus Manutius and His Press. Provo: Brigham Young University, 1995.

I look forward to reading your responses!

Are there other scripts/writing systems with which vertical kerning has been tried (in metal types)?

As I quoted before, Angerhofer states, “The first solution to this problem (the collisions) was to expand the amount of space between lines, but as Aldus continued to develop his presswork other improvements were made” (pg. 31). This indicates as well to me that more leading was the obvious answer to this problem. Barker doesn’t address the collisions at all, but to answer the second question, he does state that this method had been used before:

“Neither of these was wholly new: vertical kerning had long been in use, if not for Greek type; separate accents had been used with conspicuous success in the Vicenza Chrysoloras. But if these were the two ‘methods’ that Aldus intended to protect. What counted, as far as the privilege was concerned, is that neither had been used before for the printing of Greek in Venice.” (Barker, pg. 93)

Barker, Nicolas. Aldus Manutius and the Development of Greek Script & Type in the Fifteenth Century. New York: Fordham University Press, 1992.

I haven’t read either book and don’t know much about Aldus' Greek type, but I think I can envision what’s being described. If the diacritics are on separate sorts from the letters, then the bodies that the vowels and rho were cast on must have been shortened above and in some cases below to leave space for the diacritic sorts to be inserted. Presumably it’s these spaces where the body is shorter than usual that are the “vertical kerning” referred to. In practice, for dealing with eta and some ligatures, you might need to have some diacritic space below/above adjoining letters as well.

So he would be casting at least some of the letters without ascenders on a body that extends up only to just above x-height, and casting at least some of the letters without descenders on a body that extends only just below baseline. But then for tightening up you would probably want small leads to fill in the gaps between x-height and ascender height (and between baseline and descent) for sequences without diacritics.

This doesn’t seem like the simplest way of casting Greek type, but I suppose it would keep the type case down to a reasonable size.

ETA: [[http://net.lib.byu.edu/aldine/ald15lf1.jpg|This example of Aldus' Greek]] uses an eta without descender, which solves the problem of placing iota subscripts without requiring any space under the preceding letter. And I don’t see any ligatures that would cause diacritics above the letter to be displaced horizontally either.

2011-05-13 15:14:42 • kentlew

Mark, If I were to do it, I would first run the 1st form as text without accents. Then I would tweezer out only the base glyphs to be accented and replace them with glyphs of accents only but a bit higher than type high–perhaps drop a spacer into the vacated slot to raise the accent above the rest. Next, I would add one 3pt (or whatever) slug from above the form which would shift the accented form down the needed amount. I would then reduce the packing to allow for the higher type position. Then run the second form. This would be “vertical kerning” because I would have shifted the accents closer to the base letters without worry of decenders above. This method is not much different than two color letterpress where the first form may be a table of figures or text in one color; then followed y a 2nd color form of rules set to the same leading and measure with a slug inserted to shift the form so the rules hit between lines of the first form. There would be no need to make any adjustments to type height or packing with this though.

This method would require the casting of all polytonic accents and breathing marks at the exact widths of each of the base letters which receive accents (they could even have been cast taller than type-high to eliminate the need for the spacers). This is a much smaller number than the entire Greek alphabet because many glyphs are never accented. Perispomeni have very few host glyphs, for example. The amount of pressure and inking required for such a sparse setting as the 2nd form would be less than a full block of text so doubling of the lower dropped base glyphs would be unlikely.

2011-05-13 18:59:15 • Mark Simonson

Part of Burnhill’s analysis of Adline norms relies on misprints in which the top or bottom edge of a sort makes a visible impression, e.g. because of grit on the type bed as Chris describes. Such accidents enabled Burnhill to accurately measure the body height of Aldine sorts and, among other things, confirm that no leading strips were used: interline spacing was determined directly by the body height with what we might now call ‘built-in leading’.

The method suggested by Nick, with decomposed accents fitted to mortised bodies, could have been done, but I’m not aware of any physical evidence for it, and I don’t think it would have been efficient. Burnhill suggests that accents were kerned off narrow sorts following the letter or, in some cases, on either side of a letter, which explains the inconsistent spacing between tone and breathing marks. This technique preserves the standard horizontal composition of sorts, without introducing a new class of mortised bases and vertically composed elements.

More controversially to my mind, Burnhill’s composition reconstructions suggest that at least some ligatures were composed of what we would think of a contextual variants. I’m willing to believe that it might have been the case, but I’m not sure whether the physical evidence supports it. Certainly, subsequent generations of renaissance Greek printers using Granjon’s types and those derived from them, did not do this, and the catalogue of Greek sorts from Enschedé and Oxford show precomposed ligature sorts. The trouble I have believing that the Aldine books were composed using contextual variants in this way stems from just how difficult this is for the compositor. It is something that we can do today because we have computers to perform the lookups for us, and they don’t get tired and they don’t get sloppy. The only handset type system I am aware of that definitely did use contextual variants— Mühendisoğlu’s Ottoman Arabic type—proved to be overwhelmingly difficult for compositors: even within a single book the quality of the typesetting degrades, with more and more mistakes being made.

[By the way, if anyone comes across examples of Greek capital letters with tone/breathing marks on their left, i.e. the ‘standard’ position, prior to the 19th Century, please let me know.]

“Aldus Manutilus and the Development of Greek Script & Type in the Fifteenth Century”, by Nicolas Barker, Fordham University Press, says on page 85…

that vertical kerning was not used with Type 2 (mostly used as a small supplementary type), Type 1 not being part of the Google Books preview,

and as for Type 3,

“Raised spaces again show that a third, even more revolutionary, but far simpler, solution was now introduced to deal with the problems of composing separate accents. This involved a reversion to kerning, in this way: the nominal gauge of the type, 4.2 mm, remained constant, but the letters were cast on a 3.15 mm body, kerning equally (up to about 1.6 mm) at head and foot; the accents were cast on a 1.05 mm body, occupying the appearing space between their proper line and the line above. The relative rarity of kerning sorts made collisions much less frequent than in the first version of Type 1:”

so Type 1 did have vertical kerning of some sort.
