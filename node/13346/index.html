Paperback

June 29, 2005 • Dan Weaver

http://www.creativepro.com/story/feature/23099.html

I like the concept of optical sizes for text usage but does anyone print paperbacks anymore?

A cursory search via Google suggests that paperbacks published every year still number in the tens of millions. And paperback books aren’t the only venue in which Paperback’s virtues would be appreciated: newspapers are still being published in hardcopy form, and newsprint isn’t a particularly deluxe printing surface.

Optical sizing certainly isn’t a new concept. Oldtime punchcutters kept it in mind when they put together their (real) fonts. A lot of old type specimens that I have encountered are quite different at different point sizes.

I recently did a lot of design work for a mass-market paperback publisher. But only book covers. And it’s the same for most publishers. They commit their creative budget to freelance design for the covers, and handle the interiors in-house, and these are not usually given the same degree of typographic attention. If they want to nuance the body copy, they use Bembo or Perpetua – which are too light for the taste of hardcore typographers..

But perhaps my experience is not typical. Verification, please?

So while I admire the goal and scope of the Paperback typeface, I think it may be an uphill battle to persuade trade publishers to get with the program.
