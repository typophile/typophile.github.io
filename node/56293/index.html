The skill is in looking and in understanding what I see. There is a difference of kind between the skill involved in making shapes on a computer and making shapes by hand, which has to do with the directness of the relationship between hand, tool and shape. What this means in terms of working with a pen or working with a computer is not that there is a switch between parallel but different skills -- since in the area of design the core skill, of seeing and understanding, remains the same regardless of the tool --, but whether a separate set of skills -- manual, fine motor skills, controlling a mark-making tool in direct contact with a medium -- is employed or bypassed. Your critique of derivative featural manipulation is good, but I think the problem goes deeper than chiro-derivative design; indeed, I would say that the discernable chirographic derivation is largely an historical accident rather than essential to the phenomenon of derivation. The fact is that the vast majority of type design can and largely has been built on derivation of past-practice: that past-practice has itself been derived from chirographic models, but it needn't have been so. Imagine that Gutenberg had not decided to emulate textura manuscript forms, and instead had invented an entirely new form of typographic letter and that this had been successful in terms of cultural and functional acceptance by readers. The subsequent history of typeface design could still have been largely derivative of past practice. Evidence for this can be found in the fact that a great many designers do not look at type as chirographically derived, instead stressing a distinctively typographic history independent of the pen (locally, Bill Berkson is usually the most vocal representative of this view), yet still rely heavily on past-practice as the guiding principle of typeface design. The problem with the incidental and ad hoc is that it can be contaminated with aesthetically preferential a priori. The proper concerns of type design are the optical-grammatical and the gestural-atmospheric. ...and the cultural-aesthetic. I don't think there are any grounds for reducing the concerns of type design to the optical-grammatical and gestural-atmospheric (not quite sure what you mean by atmospheric, though). I would agree if reading were such a difficult thing that all attention had to be paid to optimising shapes for legibility and readability. But reading isn't difficult, and as a result all writing systems -- in both typographic and chirographic form -- have tended toward cultural-aesthetic expression (not to be confused with the kind of individual expression against which Hrant rails), i.e. expression of shared ideas and ideals about attractive form married to the prerequisite function of text. We don't only want text to be readable, we also want it to be visually inviting and to reflect, in its form, broader ideas in our culture. There have certainly been times in history when the cultural-aesthetic concerns of type design have taken too great prominence and undermined the prerequisite functionality: the post-romantic Victorian types tended to this. But aesthetic decadence is a cultural phenomenon, and it is better to recognise it and work through it than to say that the cultural-aesthetic concern should be banished altogether. So the problem I see with incidental and ad hoc feature manipulation isn't ‘contamination with aesthetically preferential a priori’, but the tendency to rely on past practice rather than conscious understanding of what one is doing. Ironically, at least in Hrantian terms, it is Noordzij's stroke model analysis that first suggested to me a new kind of consciousness in type design: an understanding of letter shapes from the inside-out, rather than from what you might call historico-conventional derivation. And I think this consciousness -- what we can call para-chirographic consciousness -- is itself only recently bearing fruit in type design and, by virtue of its consciousness, invites post-chirographic response. So I see the para-chirographic and the post-chirographic as parallel directions, both characterised by a from-within consciousness in shape making.
