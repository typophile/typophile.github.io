2006-11-15 06:54:00 • gfmueden

I think the answer to missgigles' question ‘which is easily read’ has to be neither. Neither seriffed type nor sanserif type is easily read by dyslexic persons.

It is tempting to take results such as those about type-size cited in the O’Brien, Mansfield, Legge paper linked by Bill, and simply make them parts of a piece-meal (and blindly scientistic) typographic response to dyslexia. I think this approach would be unfortunate.

To make progress, though, I think typophiles might benefit from critical engaement of discussions of reading difficulties at the level the OM&L paper does.

The OM&L paper explores the print size effect in terms of magnocellullar deficits and their impact, and then cites a position coding deficit as a possible cause for the size effect, bringing into the discussion, spatial frequency and lateral interference issues along the way. The paper is the most thorough exploration of these issues that I’ve seen to date.

I wasn’t aware of the OM&L paper until I noticed William’s link to it., but I had been exploring the possibility that the magnocellullar deficit which is typical of some dyslexics impacts the perceptual learning necessary for adult-level skilled reading to emerge. I’ve previously talked about this on typophile in terms of ‘unitization’ or in terms of the formation of an alternative to ‘slot-processing’ by letter identities, or in terms of adult level processing being ‘bouma-based’. By contrast, what I’ve described as slot-processing relies on the ‘what / where’ letter identity + position coding referenced by OM&L as the crucial factor.

In my ‘typographical-craft-knowledge derived’ scheme, perceptual learning puts in place a coding of the whole pattern of blacks and whites inside the bounded map of the word at a level more primitive than that of whole letters. So for me the magnocellullar deficit impacts the consolidation of a supercessionary perceptual processing routine reliant on these whole-word maps of sub-letter features. In the abscence of this perceptual processing skill, the reader with a magnocellullar deficit is thus forced to rely on a more error prone and slower slot-processing, with it’s letter-identity + position encoding, and as OM&L suggest, this might indeed be more managable or less susceptible to lateral interference effects at larger sizes.

This might help us realize that, while larger print sizes might accommodate a perceptual processing deficit, they don’t solve the underlying problem.
