Histories of uppercase-lowecase pairings

December 20, 2014 • hrant

The evolution via writing facilitation of what we now consider lowercase forms out of monumental uppercase originals is well-documented. But I don't remember reading much about how eventually the two cases were combined to deliver text, and acquire the grammatical determinance we take for granted today. For example, how long did it take after the establishment of lowercase for this to happen? And did uppercase completely fall out of common use at any point? This applies not only to Latin but also Cyrillic, Greek and Armenian at least. Any elucidation would be most welcome. hhp

Well, it is known that for the Latin alphabet, dual case script was a deliberate invention - it came about when Charlemagne sought to have a standard scribal hand created, and so it combined the original Roman monumental script for capital letters with a lower-case based on uncial. According to this page, [[http://learnrussian.rt.com/alphabet/the-history-of-the-cyrillic-alphabet/]] lower-case letters were added to Cyrillic by Peter the Great. And the Wikipedia article cited in the post above gives information on the origin of Greek lower-case as well. So far, I've found nothing on Armenian lower-case. ...but now I remember that a few days ago, I came across something that mentioned that Bolorgir was originally the name for the Armenian minuscule hand, before it became applied to the traditional style of Armenian types.

Before minuscules were developed (Latin), there were uncial sorts used for body text in book production. To set captions, chapter initials, certain word or sentence beginnings and similar focal points inside the text against the common style, more “ancient-style” e.g. Roman capital shapes have been employed, more or less obvious/deliberate. I think there lies the origin of a dualistic behaviour which later became a bicameral canon. Still today the regular use of lower/uppercase is remarkably different in different languages. But no country adopted the tricameral system of the old days as mandatory, this is the sad side of the story ;)
2014-12-21 21:21:37 • Andreas Stötzner
Sumner Stone did several presentations about this stuff years ago. His general theme was the increasing complexity of the Latin writing system, which has had letters, case, punctuation, figures, and styles (Italic, then Bold) folded in over the centuries. As pointed out above, manuscripts in Carolingian Europe used Lombardic and Uncial caps for initials and lead-in text blocks of the Carolingian minuscule that provided the model for our lower case. The cpas moved from a role of initials and emphasis into one of more general case (still effectively initials) over time.
