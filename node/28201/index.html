2006-09-13 15:00:21 • franzheidl

You can throw marketing into the mix as well. Function can be defined more broadly than readability and reproducibility in a given environment. The type must function for the market it is used in as well. Function is quite fuzzy here since a publisher, designer, or art director will base function on their own experience. The question becomes, will this typeface and this layout (the 2 cannot be separated)sell the product or communicate the proper tone for this message better than some other combination? Here the form plays a role in the function. Does an elegant script do a better job of selling perfume than a more readable book face? Would a health report lose credibility set in Comic Sans? To me form and function are joined at the hip. You can see them as entities but you cannot remove them from each-other. While function may appear to be the more rational of the two, measuring it relies on the form to begin with. The rationale for the form may follow the rationale for the function but there is far more human interpretation involved than can be defined scientifically.

Why ‘in contemporary society’? Are you positing that in earlier society the form of type was more important than the function, or that form and function were equally important, and that there has been a recent shift?

If there has been a shift, I think you could make a good argument, from within contemporary graphic design culture, that the opposite is true: that the form of type is treated by many designers as more important than its function. One has only to look through the pages of so-called typography annuals to see evidence of this, of type used primarily as a graphic element not as a medium of language and textual content.

Norman Dexel (non vérifié) | 09/06/06 | emigre 65 “Helvetica again”:

Why do you use Helvetica ?

There are many reasons why we use Helvetica. Each is very different and sometimes seemingly contradictory, and they slowly but constantly change. Some of these reasons may be hard to follow, but we like to believe that it is exactly the complicated nature of our reasoning that, paradoxically, makes our designs so practical and clear. One of these reasons involves Helvetica’s neutrality. Of course, we fully realize that no typeface is neutral, and that Helvetica’s objectivity is a myth. But it is exactly this myth that turned Helvetica into one of the most widely used typefaces in the first place. So it is fair to speak of a myth that created its own reality. In that sense, Helvetica’s neutrality resembles a self-fulfilling prophecy. This neutrality, real or imagined, enables us and the user to fully focus on the design as a whole, neutralizing the typographic layer as a way to keep the concept as clear and pure as possible. There are cases, however, where for specific reasons, the concept demands a less neutral typographic layer. In those cases we never hesitate to use other typefaces. But those cases are rare

The difficulty is the definitions of the two. “Function” does not follow the same definition to many people. It’s definition may be a moving target. There are those who define it along the lines of readability alone. Some would add usability (does it have a selection of weights, small caps, OSF, etc.) There are others who take a far broader meaning from it and may even include human emotional reaction. There is nothing tidy about this interaction of terms. There is more argument about semantics than some people allow.
