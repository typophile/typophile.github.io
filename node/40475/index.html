Hello Weyert,

Only how can I cancel out this setting for the first paragraphs on the pages? Create another paragraph style (with properties based on your regular one, except the indent). There you can also specify which paragraph style is next. Then, you can select all of your text, right-click on your ‘first paragraph style’ and select ‘apply this paragraph style and then the next one(s)’ [sorry, I don’t know how they named it in English or Dutch].

That’s the CS2 way; I don’t know if there’s something new in 3 … F

2008-01-02 12:58:04 • wdeboer

If the break is mandatory, there isn’t much you can do about it, is there? I have never understood why a dash falling at the end or beginning of a line is considered so bad. There are a lot of “rules” that have come about because someone thought a particular situation would confuse a reader, or just looked bad. The usual argument against dashes at the end/beginning of a line, from an aesthetic point of view, is they weaken the text block. But so do ends of paragraphs, and we put up with that. Moreover, in the ragged setting you have shown, the text block has no clear right edge.

However, in your screen shot, the emdash appears to be spaced with a word space. If you are going to use such a long dash (em), space it with a hair or thin space. If you are going to use a word space around the dash, use an en-dash. Better still, a few fonts have a dash 3/4 of an em long, and that, thin-spaced, ties the line together rather better.

FWIW

The usual argument against dashes at the end/beginning of a line, from an aesthetic point of view, is they weaken the text block.

Additionally, some people claim a dash can be mistaken for a hyphen (when at the end of a line and being too short – not in your case) or a listing dash (when at the beginning of a line; like a bullet point, especially when there are several cases in succession).

I second what Charles said: your breaks take up lots of space. Emdashs can be quite elegant, but take care that they don’t tear holes in your text. The spaces before & after should be small and, moreover, even! (Look at the 2nd line: ‘Theorine —’ vs ‘Han   —’ F

2008-01-02 14:40:48 • Florian Hardwig

Yes, or alternatively an ndash with word spaces. I’ve been told former was the traditional Anglo-American way . . .

Actually, it was the Anglo way, not Anglo/American. Richard Eckersley (of British origin) use to have “discussions” with the editors at Nebraska Press, where he was senior designer, about quotes & dashes. Initially, he preferred the style common in the UK, where dashes were set as word-spaced en-dashes, and a single quote was used in places where those in the States use a double quote. We finally convinced him (honestly!) that a 3/4-em long dash centered in an em space (i.e., 125 unit l&r sidebearings) looked best of all. Never got him to change his mind about the quotes, though.

As you say, trust your eye.

2008-01-02 16:06:41 • charles ellertson

I’m talking book work here.

The beauty of the ‘Anglo’ way is that it produces a more even colour on the page. An em dash, irregardless of the spacing, leaves a bullet hole through the page, and if there are more than two or three per page, can impact the even colour that we try to achieve with small capitals, old style numerals, and titling fonts for headings.
