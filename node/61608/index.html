Variant on Courier for Programming

September 2, 2009 • beroe

I know the monospaced typeface topic has been done to death, but I think my question is slightly different.

We want to use Courier in our book to distinguish programming code, but were frustrated that you can’t distinguish some of the characters very easily. So I created a variation where you can tell apart 1/l, 0/O, and ~/-. All the other characters are the same. (I am surprised that nobody has done this before, but I couldn’t find anything.)

What are the rules for using/distributing this? I assume nobody will care if we use it in print, but what about putting it on the web for students to use?

Thanks, Steve

2009-09-03 00:34:00 • beroe

You could modify an open source version of Courier, such as the one included with Ghostscript. Then there is no question about releasing modifications (you just have to follow the license). The hinting on the Ghostscript font is not as refined (it has Postscript curves), but it should be fine for printing.

By the way, I like your idea of creating distinguishable characters for Courier. I understand your desire to have a monotype font that is immediately distinguishable from the surrounding text. Distinguishing Deja Vu Sans Mono from Deja Vu Sans for instance is almost impossible.

2009-09-03 01:03:52 • sgh

Raph Levien’s [[http://levien.com/type/myfonts/inconsolata.html|Inconsolata]] is a monospace font specifically designed for use in print, and is available under an Open Font License.

The [[http://dejavu-fonts.org/wiki/index.php?title=Main_Page|DejuVu]] fonts, which sgh mentioned, also use a free software license, and are modifications of [[http://www.gnome.org/fonts/|Bitstream Vera]], with better glyph coverage.
