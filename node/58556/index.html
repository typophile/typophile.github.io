This is the old display version of Helvetica, derived from the larger sizes of the original metal foundry type. Most digital Helveticas are descended from Linotype’s version, which was designed for 18-point and smaller (machine setting). URW’s Nimbus Sans Bold D (D=display) is about as close as you can get in a digital font.

http://www.urw.de/cgi-bin1/dalcgi/source/schnellsuche.htd?searchchar=nimbus+sans

I did say, “close as you can get in a digital font”. It’s quite possible that Letraset altered it somewhat when they did their “cut” of Helvetica for transfer lettering. However, they credit the source as Haas. Looking at an old Letraset catalog, the “e” in their Helvetica does have that slight tilt, whereas the “e” in the Helvetica shown in a metal foundry type catalog has it perfectly horizontal, or nearly so. While Nimbus Sans Bold D is an accurate digital version of the original Haas foundry cut, it looks like the Letraset “cut” of Helvetica may be a lost typeface.
