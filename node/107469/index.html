a perky cover-up - or: the many marry fonts of deutsche grammophon

November 30, 2013 • cartouche

greetings fine ladies and gentlemen of typophile! it seems quite a lot adults have a cherished steckenpferd. yours-a-many is typophilia - mine is the embellishment of my digital music library with original release covers. restoration via photoshop is the means by which i try to bring faded lp-covers back to a more mint life. deutsche grammophon recording seems to be the stylistically consistent-most amongst the recording companies when it comes to the design of their lp-era-covers. a fact which not least relates to their specific use of fonts in cover-art. i am hereby trying to verify which fonts exactly dgg used in their covers 1950s-60s

specimen 1 shows a detail of lpm 18 010 a 1951 recording with a 1st post-war style cover i’ve come to think that bauer bodoni roman osf fits best to the letters whereas the numbers are bauer bodoni lt.

specimen 2 shows slpm 138 072 - a 1959 recording in the 2nd post-war style (and 1st stereo style) both letters and numbers seem to be in weiss std. “amadeus-quartett” however appears set in weiss sb italic sw. main problem here is that i couldn’t find a font that provides me with the alternative style swashes (e.g. at top-right of a; extra wide bottomstroke of q) throughout all cover styles the record-id seems be set in a futura font.

specimen 3 finally is a detail of slpm 138 114. it is from a 4th style cover and stands at the transition in font-use and design at the time between 1959/60. identifying its fonts provides me with the most difficulties. if i set “johannes brahms” to garamond premier pro italic the differences to the actually used font don’t meet the eye in a way to brutish. here “amadeus-quartett” is set in regular italic as well although other covers show an inconsistent use of alternative swash letters for initials and finitials (is that a word?…) HOWEVER: i’ve spent a lot of time looking for the matching roman style font. to me it looks well like garamond. yet: neither garamond out there which i tried fits quite right. at this point i don’t want to go into further details but rather ask bluntly: any ideas?

Specimen 3, Roman text: Günter Gerhard Lange’s interpretation of Garamond is close, http://www.identifont.com/show?G8 but it was released by Berthold in 1972, which is after the date you state for the album. URW Garamond is also close, http://www.identifont.com/show?622, but again there is a date issue. Possibly a photo lettering era version of Garamond that has not been digitized? Don

thank you for a first input! please, everybody: feel encouraged to speak your minds on specimen 1 & 2 fonts as well!

on nr.3 : i take it that don too believes the roman text to be a garamond yet not one of the many (at least to my eyes: closely) garamondesque fonts. there are a few points i want to point out which might help coming closer to a solution for YOU the typophile experts. forgive my poor idiom - i hope you get what i mean to say

  1. the small letter a seems very distinctive - top leftward curve is rather fat, sitting on a slim downwards stroke,; the belly is not exactly drawing a curve but a little square top-left.
  2. the i’s corpus - i may be mistaken - gives the impression to narrow down a bit towards the bottom AND its dot is further up than any other garamond i
  3. the e’s horizontal stroke is way in the upper half of the corpus and looks more like a serif that goes “all the way” (consider the strange dark spot in the right corner)
  4. the t’s cross-beam is positioned further up than in any other garamond known to me; if one draws a triangle from both ends ot the cross-beam to the top of the vertical stroke - the top angle is much more obtuse than in all the other garamond small letter ts.
  5. the capital s might be leaning a bit further to the right than otherwhere and it might well be “kneeling” a bit more in the bottom curve.
  6. small letter p & q have an obviously shorter shorter vertical stroke than found elsewhere.
  7. capital q “tail” is much longer
  8. perhaps very interestingly number 7 does NOT have a serif!
