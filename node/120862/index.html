When I think of the history of French cinema, I think of auteurs who were formally innovative—e.g. Melies, Gance, Cocteau, Godard, and now Hazanavicius.

While it’s true that Cassandre’s Peignot was also formally innovative in its day, it has rather a traditional art deco look, even in the true unicase setting.

Why not go with something post-modern that suggests innovation today? Pierre di Sciullo: http://www.quiresiste.com/realisations.php?lang=en

Excoffon, 20th century master: https://www.myfonts.com/person/Roger_Excoffon/

A general specific french cinema style? Hardly to grasp. Let Google sort out images to “cinema français affiche” and you’ll see – everything. But looking for “cinema français affiche 1930” reveals a different scenery: type like [[http://www.myfonts.com/fonts/facetype/marlowe/|Marlowe]] or the stunning [[http://www.myfonts.com/fonts/marksimonson/mostra-nuova/|Mostra Nuova]] by – Mark Simonson. To him I’m especially grateful for his intricate review of type in the film The Artist. For a very long time I feel that lettering and type is awkwardly neglected by film makers, mostly. Mark’s article opened my eyes upon how crucial (!) script and lettering is for a silent movie in particular.
