The letters i and l in sans serif fonts, and readability.

October 29, 2014 • jaimes

I’m hoping someone can shed some light.

I notice that for a lot of (most?) sans-serif fonts, the letter i is as minimal as could be. Simply a stick and a dot. The L is similarly minimal, just a stick, or in the luckier cases, perhaps a kink at the top or a curve at the bottom.

I find that this hinders readability – or at least it does for me. Just now I was reading a page set in Verdana, and could not figure out if the word “ability” was spelled correctly. The i’s and l’s simply fused, and I could not tell whether there were 3 or 4 “sticks”. I had to get closer to the screen.

Do others have similar trouble with these letters? Some sans-serifs that make the i’s and l’s more recognizable: ITC Officina Sans, Monitor Pro, Fira Sans, Ubuntu, Trebuchet.

To me these look every bit as good as Clear Sans, Source Sans, Helvetica. Better, in fact, since I seem to parse them more easily.

Why are “skinny” l’s and i’s so popular? Purely historic/evolution considerations? Or perhaps to make lines of text fit more letters? And is there any word to distinguish fonts with skinny i’s from those with more serif-like i’s? Also, I’ve seen some mentions of the dots: square, round, diamond etc. Does that make a difference?

Strictly speaking this is a legibility issue, rather than readability, as these terms are generally now understood.

The problem you are describing concerns space, not shape. Quite simply, for the size at which the letters are being reproduced, the glyphs are too close to one another, and the tittle is too close to the stem.

I'm not. You don't need to be able to see the difference between 'ili' and 'illi' in words because you recognize whole words rather than that you read each letter individually. By making every letter more distinct you improve the legibility but in some areas it has consequences for the readability.

I would add a slab serif to i/j to some typefaces if I want to achieve a certain texture, but not by default. It improved legibility but it’s not consistent. It becomes more consistent by designing other letters to be more distinct as well, such as adding a tail to /l. If you do this, you might give a/d/h/m/n/u spurs as well, and with so many horizontals added you can see why it adds texture. This isn’t always wanted and in fact many sans typefaces are designed to be neutral and calm.

Also, geometric typefaces are generally meant to be minimalist and they take the proportions to extremes, where condensed letters like f/i/j/l/t are really condensed. Just have a look at Futura.

Here’s a quote from Reading Letters: The dots on the ‘i’ and ‘j’ are in danger of visually connecting with the stem and being perceived as ascending characters. This risk is more evident in sans serif than in serif typefaces. Although not approved of by some designers—who argue that serifs do not belong on a sans serif typeface—several newer sans serif typefaces have a slab serif on ‘i’ and ‘j’. The phenomenon is most common in typefaces designed for electronic media and signage, where optimal character recognition is thought to be essential, and where spacing is less subtle. The slab serif on ‘i’ and ‘j’ is often found in the sans serif faces by Erik Spiekermann, who further shows a liking for the sans serif ‘l’ with a tail to the right."

Also, some of these tricks don’t necessarily improve legibility either, as I also found out by typing that paragraph. I thought it said “who further shows a liking for the sans serif ’t' with a tail to the right.” The lighting is not great here and I looked at it for at least 5 seconds before I decided it has to be an ‘l’ because a ’t' has a tail almost by default. When the /t is wider it improves legibility, but when it’s too wide it can be easily confused with /c.

Look at the studies that have been done by Sanford (distance test & short exposure test, 1888), Dockeray (parafoveal vision test, 1910), Tinker (short exposure test, 1928), Bouma (distance test & short exposure test, 1971) and Geyer (short exposure test, 1977) to look at all the letters that are commonly misread given the use of specific typefaces.

Studies have also been done to get insight into which parts of the letters we look at for recognition, and apparently when it comes to /l we predominantly look at the upper part. So it seems the tail on /l is to make the letter more distinct by giving it wider side bearings and having the tail compensate for the extra space than it is about making the design of the letter itself more distinct.

“You don’t need to be able to see the difference between ‘ili’ and ‘illi’ in words because you recognize whole words rather than that you read each letter individually”

This is a popular misconception, but largely untrue. You recognize the letters individually, in parallel. There is some top-down effect of it being easier to recognize words, but it is not the shape of the word that causes this (the top-down effect, to the extent that it exists, exists for all-caps words as much as lowercase ones).

See: http://www.awmueller.com/psicoterapia-diversos/tec_word_recognition.pdf

That the process of reading is “far more complex than letterwise decipherment” is doubtless true, but since the act of reading is subjectively experienced as letterwise decipherment as one reads, that it plays a part, at least as a fall-back mechanism, is likely true as well.

In fact, I think that reading is mostly letterwise decipherment, but it runs more smoothly and more quickly because of assists from such factors as the redundancy of the language as well as word boumas. Redundancy, unlike bouma, is not a visual element, but it allows predictive decoding - reading is quicker when people are being largely presented with what they expect to see.

2014-11-20 16:08:22 • quadibloc

Indeed, letterwise decipherment is the lion’s share of reading… in the fovea, where acuity is sufficient. But about 2/3-rds of the visual field is beyond the fovea, where individual letters are far less decipherable. Saccades longer than the fovea’s coverage are but one clue that the exclusively letterwise models are wrong, and the parafovea –where clusters of letters are decipherable– plays a big role in immersive reading. The problem with many contemporary field research is that invasive methods that preclude true immersion give a false image of reading. Another problem is that some pundits would rather grasp at a nicely-shaped donut within easy reach rather than wait for something unknown but nutritious.
