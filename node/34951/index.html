Curse of the Typophile

July 7, 2007 • microspective

Every day we are exposed to typography of all levels - rare is the treasured occasion that we find truly moving type in public. Part of me loves this fact, as the discovery and appreciation of great type is a special treat. Another part of me gets impatient that not enough people (designers and general public alike) care about good typography, which, of course, means that there are poor design choices being made regularly, and published nonetheless!

Alas, my (our) obsession with type brings with it a curse: scrutiny of absolutely everything that uses letters. How many of us can read something without considering the appropriateness of the choices of typeface, proportion, kerning, leading, et cetera?

For better or worse, this is something we all must deal with on a day-to-day basis as typophiles. I’m curious to know how others handle this issue.

Thanks, Ray

When I encounter banal music (like when my wife drags me to Big-Lots, for instance), I like to counter it by whistling as though I am tone-deaf. This requires abusing interval relationships, as well as pitch.

I don’t know why this helps, but I get a tremendous kick out of it. It isn’t always easy for me, so it has the feel of an exercise. I sometimes wonder what someone in the next aisle might think if they overheard, but I figure that it should make them feel good about their own abilities.

Unfortunately I can think of no corollary application to your dilemma.

“How many of us can read something without considering the [type]”

A good question. I read almost everything without considering the type. It is only when I am being paid to do so, or, if there is “something wrong” in something I’m obliged or attracted to reading that I do consider it. It did, however, take me no short time and it was incomplete until I’d made a decidedly type-sparse environment. From the refrigerator to approximately 6 miles in all directions, superfluous type is rare.

This is not to say that there are not incredibly dense areas of type in the home/office, but they are kept at bay and hardly ever left in the right-reading position. As this is the point of view a type of a designer-with-an-outlet, it may be different from that of a non-type designing typophile — i.e. I can say to myself, “Well that looks bad”, when I trip over something bad from a print ad to an OS. “Poor thing.” But then I can put it to rest by making sure whatever I saw wrong “out there” is right in the font , fonts, or composition in progress.

Graphic designing typophiles must get similar satisfaction each time they don’t do something bad they remember seeing done, don’t they? If they don’t I think they should.

Eventually (hopefully) you will outgrow an obsessiveness to scrutinize type.

To mature as a designer, typographer, type designer, you need to be able to see with “fresh innocent eyes” at will. If one always approaches a piece with clinical scrutiny, then one is not able to experience the initial expression of what that piece has to offer.

The same holds true with many other skilled or artistic professions; culinary arts, music, film making, etc. It is the first impression which should remain unique and/or memorable, not the minutia.

It is the minutia that enhances the overall experience, but you can’t receive that experience by focusing on the minutia. Sort of like not seeing the forest because the trees are in your way.

Train yourself to see without typographic eyes at will, since there will always be time for nit-picking afterwards.
