In the 1920s, a "font" per se, would not have been used for the design of a sign. Excellent hand-lettering skills were the core of a signmaker's trade. The lettering would have been done by hand. The styles might have had some small influence from the trends in type, but just as often the influence went the other way. Unless your movie is set in London, the Gill Sans style is not going to be historically accurate. It was heavily influenced by the typeface designed for the London Underground by Gill's teacher, Edward Johnston, ca. 1916. Gill Sans, as a font, was not released until the very end of the 1920s and didn't have much impact in North America until a bit later. I don't know if any sign makers outside of London would have noticed for at least another decade. If you want some historically accurate suggestions, you should contact Mark Simonson, or hope he stops by this thread. For insight into sign making practices from the period, you could try contacting John Downer, an excellent sign painter in the old tradition and something of a historian of the trade. Better yet, get your production to hire one of them as a consultant. -- Kent.
