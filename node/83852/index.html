Teaching grids, styles, master pages: college students vs. adult education

July 17, 2011 • blank

I have noticed an interesting phenomenon when educating design students on the use of grids, styles, and master pages. Teaching this stuff to younger college kids (ages 18–22) is a slow, tedious task. I have to start with very simple exercises and constantly remind the students to use grids and styles. By the end of the semester some of them still cannot wrap their heads around their inability to produce a coherent multi-page document as having something to do with their constant refusal to just set up a gridded master page and create text styles. Even the lazy ones don’t seem to realize that doing this would allow them to keep being lazy and slap a quick modernist piece together.

But things are absolutely different with adult students. My older college students (age 24+) and adult education students seem to pick up on these concepts in about five minutes flat. I show them a few slides, do an Indesign demonstration, and hand out an example file packed with sample grids. The next week they show up with consistent hierarchies and page proportions.

Has anyone else experienced this? Do you have any insight to share on why adults who sometimes barely manage a web browser can handle grids and styles so easily while kids who live and breathe computers often find these concepts utterly incomprehensible?

I would suggest that the phenomenon can be explained for what passes for “knowledge” in the age of Google. It appears that many young people, growing up in a webcentric world, confuse instant access to information with knowledge. This is simply not so: knowledge is hard-won and it is cumulative, Furthermore, knowledge is dependent on–and, paradoxically, also increased by–pattern recognition. If the answers are simply handed to you on a platter, the path that leads to them is never seen.

Plus, since youngster of a certain age always know more than their elders, it’s difficult to convince them that “the old ways” have any value. Of course, that’s just my curmudgeonly opinion; some young whippersnapper will probably set me straight…

I’ve noticed this as well. Being one of the 24+ year-old students, I’ve seen classmates with the same confused and frustrated look with regards to grids. I think oldnick is right with his comment regarding the difference between “knowledge & information”. The younger students, from my experience, have a hard time understanding the idea of applying steps as a means to an end and not as the end result.

Maybe it’s because they think it is more work without any immediate benefit (it takes a little more time to set up a grid), time that could be spent throwing information onto the page and moving on to solving the next problem.

It seems that lately (I’ve discussed this with other professors and they seem to agree), younger students seem to lack problem-solving skills; almost as if the end goal of any design or academic problem could be solved through collecting and retaining information—not through critical thinking or experimentation.

2011-07-18 05:14:50 • JamesT

My experience has been that a surprising number of designers (of all ages) don’t use style sheets and sometimes not even master pages. At least not on shorter documents.

I’m not sure if it’s laziness, or lack of proper training, or feeling that these things impose too much rigidity to the design, or a feeling that setting these things up takes more time than they save.

I’m not sure why your younger students are having more problems, but you might want to set up some assignments in a way that using style sheets actually saves them time. For example, a multi-page project in which their assignment calls for them to to set it 2 different ways, and they get a graphic demonstration that using style sheets makes that kind of change a snap.

Thanks but I was actually referring to style sheets as a timesaver, not grids.

Grids are certainly a good thing, and I guess they can save time in some situations, but I think of style sheets and master pages as practical tools which 1) insure consistency and 2) make it faster and easier to make changes. And I think that many people regard #2 as their main motivation for using them.

If you create a long document and then decide to make changes to the margins, fonts, type color, point sizes, or whatever, it might take quite a while to go through the document and change each instance manually, but if you’ve used style sheets and master pages you can make those global changes in seconds.

2011-07-20 03:42:51 • JamesM

Could be a couple of things.

• People’s brains function differently at different ages. They process and organize information differently.

• Life stages: when you’re 13-22, all you have is time. Us adults have busy lives that need more efficiency. 24+, you’ve probably have held a real job and needed to become more productive. Before that, who cares if you have to recreate a template 99 times? Necessity is the mother of invention.

Try creating a need for them to use master pages, by giving them an overwhelming volume of work. Then you’ll see who works smart over who works hard.

To that last point, I think 95% of people are fed the line that hard work is everything. Spending a prodigious amount of hours at something means you’ve done good at it. Which isn’t really the truth. Ultimately, you must produce something worth while.
