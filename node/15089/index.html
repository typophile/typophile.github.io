When 45 point isn't 45 point.

September 12, 2005 • Stephen Lording

Hi there,

Can someone please explain to me why two typefaces at the same point size will not necessarily have the same x-height?

I’ve always undestood point size to be a standard unit of measurement that measures the x-height of a typeface. And yet, changing the font on some text set at, say, 45 point, yields many different x-heights. What’s point size actually meant to measure?

Thanks.

The point size stands for the body of the typeface (a heritage of the metal printing era when type was casted on a body of a determined point size).

The relative dimensions of type are not related directly to the body size and so there are typefaces with greater x height than anothers, longer or shorter ascenders and descenders, etc…

2005-09-12 01:47:25 • Stephen Lording
Yes, anything can happen within the point size. Different x-heights will be more appropriate for different situations - and I hope I’m right here - typically larger x-heights are more common for text that is on screen - because on screen, at smaller sizes, it becomes less legible. Compare verdana, a font designed for screen, and garamond, a print font. At smaller point sizes, verdana is easier to read because it has a larger x-height. Think of it as a ratio - x-height:point size

You will prpbably all recall that in the seventies up to the mid-nineties ITC in working with URW produced all of their fonts with a common set of parameters: the Cap height, x-height and descent was identical. trade typesetter in matching up to a previously set piece of work had a clear plastic scale showing the cap height for each size feom 5 point to 48 point. By laying the scale on a cap they could tell very accurately what the point size was.

A god many designers felt that something was lost in giving Bembo the same proportions as Baskerville, and I had to agree. Nonetheless their fonts were simply top work.
