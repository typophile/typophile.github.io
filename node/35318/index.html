I personally don’t like ordinals (1st, etc.) written with a combination of number and letters – I’d use “first” etc. and would also spell out the day of the week and month. It’s not pedantic: if anything, the abbreviations are lazy, and there are too many styles that want different abbreviations in different ways to stay consistent.

And for times, well, that’s another kettle of fish. I like the 24-hour clock with all four digits written, but it’s not common here in North America, except in Quebec, and if one must use a.m. and p.m., use periods. Brett’s second example might be something I’d write on a calendar for a party to remind my husband, but never in a publication.

2007-07-21 14:37:19 • Linda Cunningham

It is a British English convention to put the date before the month in this type of usage. I personally prefer this form, but here in the States, the more typical convention would be

on Saturday, July 21, 2007.

You may find more Americans using the European date-month-year form than you used to, but I believe it is still more common to see month-day-year.

The ordinal form is not generally used, even though it might be read that way out loud. (And btw, it would be 21st, not 21th.)

– K.
