A few inexpert smartass comments:

You usually buy the licence and get the font for free with it. Oh, they say you’re buying a font, but you’re not. Read it (the licence) really carefully (this is a good time for deerstalker and magnifier) for it details–or at least should detail–what you may and mayn’t do with the font.

The logo designer will need a licence for the fonts (s)he uses. The client? If your client is using the font to produce new text, then yes. If the client is just rubberstamping with a pre-prepared logo, (s)he will probably not need hir own licence.

2011-08-02 20:28:30 • andi aw masry

To add simple answers to your questions: Whoever INSTALLS the fonts needs a license in the name of the user or company where the fonts are INSTALLED. It usually doesn’t matter what the fonts are used for. If your clients just get PDFs, logos converted to vector graphics and so on, they don’t need a license.

And in addition to Andi’s comment: It doesn’t matter how the money flows. You might charge your client for purchasing the font license, but that doesn’t mean they own it then or could use the font. Nor could you grant them permission to do so. The only thing that matters is, which user (licensee) is specified in the font license.

2011-08-02 20:50:46 • Typography.Guru

“It usually doesn’t matter what the fonts are used for”

It shouldn’t, but there are common (though not universal) exceptions. Typically, you can’t use the type created from the font as part of a commercial product (like kid’s blocks using the letters as the design). And, often, you’re not allowed to embed the font without a separate license (in a PDF, as part of software, etc.)

Some foundries also require separate licenses for identity work (for instance, I believe House requires a separate license if you want to use their fonts in a logo).

2011-08-16 14:25:51 • kentlew
I am not sure if all the questions have been answered, but if it were me, I would only use the logo as outlines, and never as text. This of course avoids any issues with font embedding, and allows you some level of control over people modifying your logo (consistency is key in branding). You probably want to manually adjust kerning and spacing to fit your project too, and it is a bit risky to assume those attributes will carry over if you bring your logo into a program as text. Anyway, my point is that you should convert your final design to outlines to make your logo design master, usually *.eps format.
