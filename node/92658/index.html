To me the “secret” of translating a typeface’s idiosyncrasies (better: characteristics) across scripts is to avoid the facile migration of formal shapes (which very rarely works, and often results in cultural damage) and focus instead on figuring out what those shapes evoke in the source culture and migrate those to shapes that evoke similar things in the target culture.

Deadlines and confused clients can certainly cause one to design something not to be proud of… Barring those things, as a rule it’s possible to translate any “feeling” since humans anywhere all feel similar things.

2014-10-27 21:31:44 • riccard0

Nobody should expect them to live in poverty. That’s exactly why we should fight cultural impoverishment.

It’s true that cultural impoverishment is a loss, which should be avoided. My point is, though, that in conditions of material poverty, escape from it becomes the first priority, and this leads to cultural damage when an alien culture holds the promise of escape from material poverty.

Making use of things from the outside, rather than assimilating them first, is the quickest and cheapest route to having them available. And telling desperate people to “go slow” is likely not to have the desired result.

Of course, often we’re not dealing with something as excusable as dire poverty - humans are naturally imitative as well, and so they will gratuitously imitate foreign models with some sort of prestige as well. But the less desperation is involved, the more a foreign model is likely to be just one alternative, which can end up enriching rather than overwhelming the borrower.

The real Myriad Arabic indeed shows that a sans-serif unstressed Arabic can be far more authentic and natural than the example you gave earlier as showing what not to do. Never mind cultural values - it’s likely to be far more legible to a native reader of Arabic as well.

Still, the techniques used in the negative example are not always wrong. Had the Latin typeface in question been Clearview instead of Myriad, then the expedient could be defended as necessary to allow the Arabic language to benefit from the legibility research that went into the highway typeface without doing it over again.

Since legibility is what people are used to, though, the process shouldn’t end with that first step - which is what happened in the typeface you rightly deem worthy of criticism.

If you look at someones handwriting in the native script you can find similarities between the typefaces and the way hand writes letter shapes. Naturally when you write a sentence in latin and in whatever style you can imagine you will get a horizontal line that could imply the x-height. If you take greek as an example of successful latinized script when entered type systems it’s because it shares many similarities like x-height and separated letters as latin but arabic is cursive and fluid. I don’t think that attitudes in the West changing or not will matter when it comes to people in other societies choosing to imitate the West - because their reasons for such imitation have no real connection to any shortcomings the West perceives in their societies.

Instead, it has to do with how they perceive the West, not how the West perceives them.

So, to take a less controversial example, look at movie posters in Korean that make their script look like heavy metal Fraktur or something else which has no resemblance to a Chinese brush stroke. (And the fact that their script is partly indigenous and partly derivative from another culture to begin with puts another face on the issue - why not abandon the foreign Chinese style for Korean for another foreign style, as of course the Korean alphabet could be done up to look like Eurostyle…)

America is the land of Hollywood and Playboy, Coca-Cola and Apple. It impresses foreigners for certain reasons, just as, say, France impresses foreigners for certain other reasons.

(Although this is directly a response to Martin Silvertant’s post, clearly its premise implies disagreement with Hrant Papazian’s preceding post as well - imitation of the West for its glamor has little to do with the Arab Spring and its - from a Western perspective, as valid as I may feel that perspective to be - failure.)
