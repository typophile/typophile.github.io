2007-01-04 10:38:57 • dan_reynolds

The Theory & History and the Information Design programmes are quite different in approach . The MA InfoD is structured around the practical work the students do: a series of projects and assignments which culminate in a show of work in early summer; they later submit a dissertation of around 12,000 words. The MA T&H shares some sessions with the MA InfoD and the MA Printing & Book Design History, and does not engage explicitly with any practical assignments, but instead increases the focus on Research Methods and culminates in a substantial dissertation of around 20,000.

In day-to-day terms, the nature of the practice-oriented MAs is fairly directed, stemming from the responses to practical assignments, whereas the MA T&H is much more flexible in its direction: students can have have a greater contribution to the direction of their studies, depending on their particular interests. So, for example, one student may opt for an approach that will lead them to engage with the methodology of subject experimentation and results analysis, while another may direct their focus on, say, the practicalities of printing processes, and engage with different devices for rendering.

Now, with regard to your questions:

  • Are these quality courses?
  • Any particular positives or negatives?
  • What kind of influence is present in the teachings

Defining “quality in HE” is a pretty wide topic worthy of several threads in itself, but here’s an off-the-cuff shortlist of criteria for you to think about:

  • engagement with the discourse in the field of study
  • resources and original material available to work with
  • opportunity for contact with a range of staff members and visitors
  • support in the development of a range of skills
  • challenge and relevance represented by the assignments
  • quality of final student work
  • graduate destinations immediately following a programme
  • graduate careers in the mid- / long-term

Unfortunately, the work produced especially by the MA InfoD students is not easy to show and explain online. More importantly, it is difficult to reflect the development process in the final deliverables without recourse to a workfile, so it is not at the moment possible to just point you to a link and say “here’s sample work from the MA InfoD”.

However, if you’re seriously interested you should plan to visit the Department on its next Postgraduate Open Day, on Thursday 8 March. Contact Carolyn Davidson at c.d.davidson at reading.ac.uk for more information.

    Dear Roy,

    I cannot answer you question as well as some others might be able to, but I have travelled abroad for post-undergraduate study once before. In 2003, two years after I finished my BFA in the US, I moved to Germany to study design further. In retrospect, this may not have been the best idea, school-wise, but it certainly helped my career path, and it made me happy. I’m still here, too.

    Anyway, to try to answer your first question on a very basic level: no one has to go to graduate school. While some companies may tend to favor job candidates with MA degrees, as designers, we more often place weight on personal knowledge, experience, portfolios, etc. Some people I know are even adverse to candidates with advanced degrees, for fear that they would command higher salaries.

    While the knowledge you might acquire at any graduate school would probably be acquirable by yourself elsewhere (through personal study, dedication, etc.), further study is a luxurious opportunity. Away from the daily grind of a job, you have the opportunity to focus your time on the subject at hand. Furthermore, you will be surrounded by like-minded people, both students and faculty, which will probably cause you to worker harder and discover more more quickly than you might otherwise.

    As for your last point, that this isn’t the best time for an American to try to finance a UK-based education, you certainly bring up a good topic. However, on the flip side, one cannot predict the short term performance of global currencies. Who knows, it might never be a good time ever again to study in the UK; the dollar could deflate even further. In the year before I left the US for Germany, I watched the dollar loose 20% of its value against the Euro. This meant that the money I had been saving up for my education had lost 20% of its value, too. Since 2003, the dollar has dropped even further against the Euro, but there isn’t anything that I can do about that. Many of the top design-related programs in the US at private universities are still more expensive than the costs related with moving to the UK for a year. And, with apologies to MasterCard, there are some opportunities that really are priceless. These decisions are best left up to the buyer; I can only offer you my own opinion!

    Contact Carolyn Davidson at c.d.davidson at reading.ac.uk for more information oops – I got the email wrong: it is c.davidson at…

    Now on to Roy’s questions: Do you agree that designers and/or typographers with a BFA from a good design program do not need a graduate-level education unless they want to teach? Firstly, the teaching bit. Indeed it is increasingly a prerequisite to have an MA or MFA to teach, and I will not be surprised if in twenty years' time this requirement has crept up to degrees that are explicitly research-based. The issue is closely linked with what each region assumes each level of higher education represents, both in terms of provision by the institution and teaching staff, and in terms of the students' experience and outcomes. The range is wider that you would think considering the [im]maturity of the sector, but this I think is endemic in the humanities and applied arts. In my experience many designers get fed up at some point with the daily grind of a client-based career, and welcome the diversion that teaching may offer, taking advantage of the fact that design is very well suited to part-time or occasional teaching engagements. Your problem would be that it is more likely that you would feel the need for a career change sometime in your mid- or late thirties, when it is more difficult to take a year out to obtain the required qualification (I know, because we have students in this situation every year). So, you could say that, at minimum, getting another qualification may come in handy in the future in ways that you cannot predict now. There is another angle to this, however, unrelated to the formal qualifications for teaching. A good postgraduate programme will give you skills in self-directed learning and research, writing, preparing and presenting your work, and an altogether deeper understanding of your discipline that BAs and BFAs cannot accommodate. This is more so as the population of students in undergraduate programmes increases. There seems to be a gradual shift towards a model of BA/BFA for general grounding and professional competence, followed by an MA/MFA that can target learning better, and offers also a better framework for funding support of applicants. So, my second point is that postgraduate programmes are becoming the accepted culmination of one’s studies (outside the domain of pure research); I would argue that this is the case for some time now in many other fields, but some areas of the humanities and the applied arts in general lag behind, largely due to the degree to which the respective knowledge fields have been defined (or, have not, as the case may be). Finally, with regard to the impact on careers: in the context of typographic design (i.e. a relatively narrow field with a good network for recognising reputation and professional contribution) my experience is that a good MA degree can be transformative for a person’s prospects and career. Whichever way you look at it, there is absolutely no way you could ever put yourself through the intensive intellectual environment and sheer workload that a proper MA programme represents. All this, remember, is just my opinion, therefore worthless as guidance for others; what seems reasonable to me may mean nothing to the next person. But because you are considering something that has the potential to direct your life in ways you cannot anticipate (see Dan’s comments, above) you must research all your options carefully, and then try to get some gut reaction to the environment and the people you may place yourself amongst.

    and: What career paths do graduates of the MA InfoD program take? A couple or three InfoD graduates work as teachers, but most are employed by larger companies that have an information design perspective on their work. A lot depends on where you intend to live after the programme: the UK, Australia, Canada, and to a lesser degree the US do have a functioning market for information design. In other countries the picture is patchier, and information design projects are farmed out to graphic design companies. The big opportunity (and, still, the big unknown) is interface design: a recognised need for expertise, but which so far is mostly covered by experiential practitioners. (See all the “big names” in web design rediscovering principles of typographic design applying to design for the web that typographic designers have “owned” all along, but were poor at adapting in language and process for the new media.) So, I think that InfoD grads will slowly migrate from the more conventionally typographic design to occupy a position between the coders and the editors – if not out of preference for the material, out of necessity for the volume of work available.

    If you’re an American applying to Reading, it would be good to get going on the official paperwork, especially if you’re considering taking out any loans to finance your degree. Reading is one of the schools you can specify when you fill out your Free Application for Federal Student Aid (http://www.fafsa.ed.gov/), but you will probably need to keep after the university to make sure they know what to do with any info that comes from the U.S. Everyone’s extremely friendly and helpful, but they don’t deal with American loans often enough to realize when they’re supposed to take the ball and run with it. Once you’re approved for any Stafford loans, or if you get any private loans, you may need to shop around a little to find a lender who will let you take out a loan for an overseas school.

    It’s actually not a hard process at all, just one that you need to nudge along now and then. When you apply for a student visa, though, you need to verify that you can cover a year’s fees and expenses, so you need to have a statement of your savings or a loan guarantee to go along with your visa application.

    It’s really worth it. It’s pretty fantastic over here. There are so many good people and good resources at our disposal here that sometimes the biggest challenge seems to be deciding what to focus on.

    2007-01-16 04:49:35 • Thomas Phinney

    I’d like to strike a cautionary note. Not that I don’t have the highest opinion of Reading, having once even applied for a job there, but it can’t be all things to all people.

    I’m also not against the professionalization of graphic design, being an RGD member.

    However, the academicization of art and design within the education system is, in my view, problematic. These are practice-based disciplines, after all.

    When I was at art school, graduating in 1974, the qualification was a Dip.AD (Diploma in Art and Design). Now it’s a BA. More cred there, apparently, without the taint of marginalization. So this degree granting status, and the access to government and foundation money it provides, is something that many colleges go after. Gotta have their own postgraduate course. Gotta get some research fellows, with their grant money. To prosper, they’re compelled to keep up with the academic credentials game. There’s a term denoting a principle that keeps the education machine ticking, “measurable outcome". But art and design education doesn’t have to be quite so subservient to the performance principle, to use Freud’s distinction, play is a good idea too.

    The inflation of required qualifications makes it harder for designers without MAs to move to teaching after a professional career, which is a loss to everyone and a widening of the divide between the commercial world and the academic world. Commercial designers are not all self-taught “experiental practitioners”, to use Gerry’s term, many are older designers who would make valuable educators. They got their qualification years ago, but with degree inflation, they are less favoured by the system than career academics.

    So it’s true, as Gerry says, that to maximize your options for later on, you should get a higher degree now. But does careerism belong in Academia? Or anywhere, for that matter.

    Planning one’s career while at college may not be the best idea. If there’s one thing that’s constant in the graphic arts industry these past 150 years, it’s changing technologies creating massive roadkill, and who can predict what will come along next? An open-ended education will provide a more flexible means of having a long-term career to cope with these changes, than tailoring one’s education to a specific job or job sector. Careers have a habit of careering all over the place.

    There are good reasons for a conservative, academically-driven education, if you’re conservative, or if you want to know the enemy (which was Neville Brody’s reason for attending the London College of Printing). But not every rebel has such resolve.

    Petitepetite, what alternatives have you considered for pursuing your passion for the theory and practice of typography and information design? It may well be that Reading is your best bet. But I would be interested to know what the options are.
