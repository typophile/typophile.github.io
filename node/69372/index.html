Can't find Exact version of font. Advice?

April 13, 2010 • 123eyes

Hi there,

I’m a freelance graphic designer. I was hoping if I could get a little advice on font licensing and such. I’m keen to keep everything legit, but find it a little confusing.

Over the few years that I’ve been working in the industry, I’ve accumulated a lot of fonts - from other designers, clients, print houses etc. I understand that I can’t legally use fonts that I do not own the license for, and that’s absolutely fine. The problem is, I’ve sorta lost track of which fonts I can/can’t legally use.

Some time back, I was working on a client project that used a lot of Din Condensed. At the time, I had a copy of the font installed on my system, but had not yet purchased the license - I have no idea where I picked up the copy along the way. My intention was to keep working on the job using the (unlicensed) font and then buy a license before final delivery to the client.

The thing is I couldn’t seem to find the font with the EXACT same name - ‘DinCond’. In the end, I opted to buy the nearest thing I could find, which was the ‘Din OT Condensed’ family, and removed the original unlicensed version from my system.

But I’m doubting myself a little now - for example: inDesign can’t find the font I originally used because the name is slightly different, even though the set I bought looks identical.

My questions are:

  • Does the licensed copy that I purchased cover me, even though the name itself is slightly different?

  • Generally speaking, what can happen if it turns out I’ve used a font in the past that I didn’t realise was unlicensed?

Any advice would be much appreciated.

Dee

To answer your first question. It depends. There are more than one DIN out there, and they come from various resellers. Unless it was the exact same font — in this case if it were an OT update of the old font — you don’t own a license to the first font.

Btw, the dialog Indesign shows when you don’t have the correct font installed allows you to redfine all your styles using the old font in just a few clicks.

“… what can happen if it turns out I’ve used a font in the past that I didn’t realise was unlicensed?” There have been some cases of big corporations recieving lawsuits, but in most cases I’d guess the type designer would just ask you to license the font. I can’t really answer for others, and I’m not a professional anyway. I don’t question your motives asking this question, but in general I think respecting the hard work of a fellow designer by not stealing it is just plain good for you, for the client, for the design industry and for the type designer.
