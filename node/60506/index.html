2009-08-04 00:25:43 • Chris Dean
I will use the term "Italics" as used by editors to describe the typographic form things like book titles should be set in. Italics have a different need than bold although both are attempts to separate some parts of text from the normal roman book face. Bold text set inline with the book face stands out indeed with weight being the primary driving force for contrast. Italics have a subtler use. They are not meant to leap out at you and make a jump in color like bolds do, they just make enough of a distinction for a person to see them as "different than roman" but not be emphasized above roman. You might call this a non-hierarchal difference or separation without dominance. By removing weight change as an option, slant is one of the few remaining players that cuts across all type families. There are other things possible such as the degree of cursive form historically used. The earliest use of italic type mixed inline with upright roman texts were not from the same type family and a pairing was made based on how closely the x-heights matched when set as neighbors. Shortly after that, foundries took the opportunity to design a so-called matching italics to be sold as a companion to the upright. We can debate at this point, hundreds of years after the fact, how closely matched the early italics really were. Today, italics are IMO better matched as family than the older ones were. We have trained ourselves to accept the variance over the past centuries. Whatever we come up with as a substitute for the current use of slant with a strong cursive influence and use of entrance and exit strokes, we will also require several years before the reading public begins to "get it". I would be the last person on earth to say, "don't even try it" and I welcome the opportunity to see your attempts as you proceed. I would only say do not throw out the tools people are accustomed to--or at least do not curse them as evil. It is a good thing to boldly go about and create an alternative to current italics as long as you do not then make it a crusade to rid the world of other useful tools. If you build it, they will come. ChrisL

Chris, no crusade in sight on this end, just a desire to see what italics are and what they do, and if they need to be what they usually are in order to do that job. Like you say, italics were usually separate types and only later were culturally "adopted" to be a subordinate "style" to complement a given upright font. And my view is, just because it was culturally "learned" to do it this way doesn't necessarily make it the Only True Way. Sometimes today secondary fonts are used inline for a different way of adding gentle emphasis or denoting other layers of meaning, for example the sans variant of the text face or maybe a different typeface altogether – much like the old guys used cursive fonts as secondary "italic" ones. It's a great effect if done well, and shows IMO that the point is not that such an emphasis-mark needs to be cursive and/or slanted, but that it simply needs to be different enough while still belonging to the regular cut. The following is based on posts in another thread, starting from: http://typophile.com/node/88224#comment-487778 -- > I would have thought you might be critical of how upright the Ernestine italic is. Critical of it? Here's a clue: the slant is exactly the same as in Patria... :-) But I know what you're getting at: I've said -and maintain- that an Italic doesn't need to be cursive, and should not have a very different voice than its Roman. That might imply I that believe in strong slant, but actually I like seeing it only as strong as it needs to be based on the other characteristic; in fact characteristics such as width, weight and even x-height are typically sufficient in decent repro conditions even at smaller text sizes, so all that remains is that the slant be visible. When I made Nour&Patria I arrived at 1/16* (~3.5 degrees) as a good place to be, but to be honest this did take into account that the slant of the Latin-Italic was to match the slant of the "non-Italic" Armenian design, which I didn't want to be too slanted (for reasons of readability, and to leave room for an "Italic" in the future). Now, Ernestine has an Armenian too** so can follow that logic; a font that is unlikely to end up with an Armenian (like, most of them :-) does not need to be so stingy in terms of slant. * It only makes sense to use integer slopes. ** It's interesting that although Ernestine's Italic is in fact influenced by another writing system I don't think it's possible to glean that, and certainly not as having had a negative effect. Probably due to the wonderful "speculative" design that Latin type has enjoyed, it feels quite acceptable. As for using Italic structures (like for the "a" and "g") I can think of three reasons: one that Nina already mentioned which is that some Roman structures just don't look too happy when slanted; the other is to pull the Italic just a little but further away from the Roman (so nothing to do with cursiveness per se*); and the third could be as a ruse, to make people accept it (although this smells of peer acceptance). * You will notice that Nina and I avoid the hokey descending "f". BTW, I'm happy you like Ernestine. As to John's and Nick's valid questioning of calling Ernestine's Italic a "slanted- Roman", I would say (as I did at TypeCon-LA) that -at least until the style becomes a valid, mainstream way of making Italics (as I think it was before WWI)- it's most useful to see it as a loose spectrum, unifying the various ways people can go with its spirit, in contra to the traditional Italic philosophy. So sure, Ernestine's Italic is not a mechanical (and not even a subsequently-tweaked) oblique, but it has the spirit of the slanted-Roman. To me even something like Adelle's does. http://www.fontshop.com/fonts/singles/typetogether/adelle_italic_ot/ I guess the "Roman" in "slanted-Roman" is a two-edged sword: it reinforces the Italic's [typically] subservient role, but on the other hand it might create too strong an association in terms of how it was designed. What I was getting at above is that once enough slanted-Romans have been made we can tighten/expand the terminology. I would hate to see a fragmentation of effort that terminological infighting can lead to. Would I have made Ernestine's Italic exactly like that? No. For one thing I would have made the diagonals straight. But maybe for that reason it would have ended up less successful on the market... BTW Nick, this discussion naturally pertains less to sans fonts, although I certainly do appreciate what you've done with S&S. hhp

Nina, in response to your points in the other thread: John, I'm glad you like it. And surprised about your assessment of the Italic! When you speak of «conventional Italic» letter shapes I assume you must be looking at things like the a and g? ... But if you look at the whole thing in terms of construction, they're interrupted (? not continuous) strokes, the curves are quite rigid, and rather than pen-reminiscent instrokes and outstrokes this one has flat serifs (ok, half ones) in the placement typical of a Roman; all characteristics that to me make it more of a slanted-Roman than a classic cursive-infused Italic. Not just the a and g, but also the related bowl shapes in the b d p q, and the uninterrupted construction of the e. Note that I was responding to the top line of this image, which perhaps biases things by including only one n and no m or h. These letters are closer to what I think of as slanted-roman forms. I entirely agree with you that this is something other than ‘cursive’, but it probably deserves some better term than slanted-roman. I'd be inclined -- following my analysis that all literary cultures produce both formal (slow, interrupted, formata) and informal (fast, cursive, corsiva) styles -- to call this a ‘formal italic’.
