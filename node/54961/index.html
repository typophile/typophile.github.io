2009-02-18 20:45:49 • Scott Leyes
It kind of depends on just which fractions you need. Recent Adobe fonts have "arbitrary" fractions, so if you're using a layout program that can use OpenType features, you can set about anything, including such oddities as 127/653. There is long debate over just what to call these critters. "Case fractions" or "piece fractions" are two of the most common terms. To me, "arbitrary fractions" most closely resembles the historical "piece fraction," but you could argue that, too. From Adobe, Arno comes to mind. Makes me hungry for more . . .

2009-02-18 21:08:15 • Plaidjaney
My progress on this: Scotch Modern and Figgins Sans have "nut" fractions, if you like that sort of thing (as well as "slash" fractions). They also have the OpenType "fraction" feature coded so that "number-space-number-slash-number" sets as "number-hairspace-fraction", so that all you have to do is apply "fraction" to the style sheet globally, and the original text manuscript sets every number and fraction correctly. I've put that feature in all my recent releases, Duffy, Beaufort Pro, Pratt Pro, and the upcoming FF Oneleigh Pro.

2009-02-18 22:46:34 • Plaidjaney
> The brilliant “space to space.thin” idea is taken from Tal I'll take some credit for that. I went to Tal with that issue, because of my work with cookbooks and building books. The promise of OpenType and Unicode was that the underlying semantic information would be retained when crossing presentational thresholds, and yet integer-fraction combinations would break down moving from Editorial (where there was a space to distinguish 1 1/2 from 11/2) to Design where space between integer and proper stacked fractions is routinely removed. Backing text out of Design files for other editions or re-purposing would require careful proofing to find these integer-fraction cases. I didn't realize that Karsten had come up with an algorithm to remove dates from being caught up by the substitution. That was one of the only situations where I foresaw a breakdown in this global approach. (That and specialized conventions like roof pitches, where 9/12 is not a fraction.) But Tal and I figured that manually unstyling a date exception would be less onerous than all the fraction styling in many situations. I remember some threads about fraction codes, but I don't often keep tabs on the Build forum, so I must have missed some. Glad to see you're implementing this approach. I've convinced FB to put it into Whitman OT (when we get around to building that out), and I think they're going to adopt it as a standard implementation. -- K.
2009-02-18 23:05:05 • kentlew
Kent, It looks OK except for those of us who have a history of doing fractions a certain way. For example, we "preprocess," or "precode" text files, changing them to "InDesign Tagged Text" files before bringing them into InDesign. The instruction to use the fraction "feature" is coded there. And any space following the full numeral removed; there is a class kern between any number and any numerator. Not saying this is better, since most people don't preprocess their text files (but thereby lose a lot of possibilities). BTW, we use to have a lot of such features with the old PostScript fonts, using TeX & macros. We always made up a "superiors" font, alpha/numerics, with everything on the baseline. We wrote a "nut fraction" macro; as you can imagine, we had, essentially, "arbitrary" nut fractions. A slight modification gave us musical time (stacked numbers w/o the bar). Arbitrary slashed fractions were a piece of cake. I suppose most of this could be implemented in OpenType, except how to draw the variable length bar with arbitrary nut fractions? BTW2, Matthew Carter frequently has nut fractions available for his fonts, but sometimes you have to get up with Cherie Cone & buy them. Always Type 1, to date. I believe I have them for Miller, and as mentioned above, Monticello.

2009-02-19 15:49:43 • kentlew
John Downer's Paperback Text series includes some nut fractions. There is a full set of single digits -- 0/0, 0/1, . . . 9/9. These are even substituted with an {afrc} alternate fraction feature, but I don't think that is supported in any software yet. It would not be able to easily handle a double digit fraction like Charles's example of 11/16, except by some careful manual manipulation, like setting 1/1 next to 1/6 and kerning them until the fraction bar touches. Not elegant. And it wouldn't be of any use in a single-over-double situation. IIRC Roy Preston was attempting to come up with an elegant solution for arbitrary stacked fractions in his Preferent fonts. This was before OpenType, and I don't know what became of the effort. -- K.
