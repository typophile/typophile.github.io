Antique sign typeface

May 31, 2009 • nationalbroccoli

I’m trying to run down the provenance of a particular typeface used in a series of steel sign letters. It’s a distinctive face, but I’m not sure exactly which one it is; I suspect that it hails from the late 1800s to the early 1900s, but have no more information than that.

The sign letters are “SANTA FE,” and have the following features:

  • The letters are serifs, with half-serifs used in the upper-left of the N, on the crossbar of the T, the arms of the F and E, and the upper and lower ends of the S;

  • The crossbars of the F, R, and A have angles marking their ends;

  • The crossbar of the A extends beyond the body of the letter to the left;

  • The S has an asymmetric spine, with the bottom of the S being considerably larger than the top;

  • The lower edge of the end of the diagonal stroke on the N is halfway on the x-height, so that the entire diagonal stroke is very high on the letter;

  • The letters are very narrow, with the width of the letter about 1/3 that of the x-height.

I don’t have any images yet to post of the typeface, but I’ll try to get one up. I’m neither a typographer nor a graphic designer, so I’m well out of my depth here!

I think we are going to need the picture or a sketch. Verbal descriptions are very hard to convert (for me) into a mental image of the typeface.
