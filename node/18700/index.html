From the title of this thread I thought this was about Chinese, and I’d started working up a reply in my head! :-)

There are two reasons to think that slant harms readability:

  1. People aren’t used to it (in long stretches).
  2. It distorts the boumas readers expect, for example by confusingly offsetting the ascenders and descenders in bouma silhouettes. Our Peter E might be able to throw one of his image-processing algorithms at this and come up with interesting insights (although he’s actually not a silhouette fan for one thing).

Fonts with less slant presumably suffer less, although they can have another problem: especially at small sizes and/or bad output conditions, the difference between Roman and Italic can fade. BTW, this is why the so-called “upright Italic” style can’t work as an Italic (which is why I prefer calling it “cursive Roman”).

Some notable slightly-slanted Romans: Syntax (1/2 degree) Rialto (1 degree) Seria (variable, ~1 degree) Flora (~2 degrees?)

In Nour&Patria, the slant (in Nour’s Armenian and subordinate Latin, and its Latin italic) is 1/16*, which is about 3.6 degrees. This seems to be the least slant one can safely have (in a text face). I think it’s safe enough that Nour’s subordinate Latin (codenamed Harrier)** can be used for setting a (short) book.

  • In digital fonts I think it makes more sense to use integer slopes instead of degrees. The latter is a pretty arbitrary measure, while the former allows easy control over slope segments.

Jay, try to think of bouma shape in whole-word pattern terms, not in envelope-structure or silhoutte terms. In other words, try to think of the whole word as a pattern of whites (counters, inter-letter spaces) and blacks (stems, bowls, diagonals) whose visual integrity as a unit is more overpowering than the identity of the component parts.

This makes visual wordform resolution a special case of direct pattern recognition (perceptual, white + black based), not a case of letter-recognition mediated word-identity computation. (In the later scenario the letter recognition part is perceptual, and black-specific, the computation part post-perceptual. This route is less direct.)

Yes, people make regressive fixations to previously fixated words.

I quote from Keith Rayner’s excellent paper “20 Years of Eye Movements”. From the section titled What Causes Regressions?:

Readers are much more likely to regress to a word on the current line than to words on previous lines. However, when they do regress further back in tect, although there are sometimes backtracking movements readers often have fairly good spatial memories for where they went wrong in comprehension and make fairly accurate saccades to that region of text.
