It has been my experience that this is very little agreement among various programs as to where points actually fall.

I generally import my outlines into Fontographer first, then do clean-up and basic metrics set ups. For very complex outlines, I generate a Postscript font, then run it through DTL Contourmaster, which has the most sophisticated and comprehensive clean-up options of ANY program I have yet encountered. I do my final build – fine-tuning metrics, kerning and generating fonts in FontLab. Yes, its complicated and involved, but it works for me. Except…

NONE of the programs ALWAYS agree where points should fall. With a delicate curve, a point shift of one em unit can affect the overall look (and, if you ask FontLab to fix a problem, it inevidently chooses the WRONG point to shift to).

Bottom line, unless you want to build your characters ENTIRELY in FontLab, you’re going to have to do some fiddling with them.

2005-10-27 00:22:53 • oldnick

When copying and pasting from Illustrator to FontLab, the following things are true (making sure “Do not rescale EPS files on import and export” is checked in FontLab Preferences and “AICB” is checked in Illustrator Preferences under “File Handling & Clipboard”):

1 point in Illustrator = 1 em unit in FontLab.

The zero point in Illustrator corresponds to the zero point in FontLab.

Armed with these two facts,

If you scale your glyph art in Illustrator so that 1 point corresponds to 1 em unit, it will be exactly the right size when you paste into FontLab.

If you set the zero point in Illustrator to line up with the baseline and the left sidebearing of your glyph artwork, it will be pasted in exactly the correct spot in FontLab. The right sidebearing must still be set manually.

It’s not quite as slick as the old auto-scale trick in Fontographer, but it is consistent and reliable.

(Disclaimer: I have been doing all my drawing in FontLab since switching from Fontographer a couple years ago.)
