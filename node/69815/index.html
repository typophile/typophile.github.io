>What resolution would an ebook device require, when it shall display body text in 8.5 points? Assuming some hints & just average anti-aliasing (aa), and to be safe, one would want a font where all the main stems have become 2-pixels-worth of the em. For most text faces that falls somewhere around 22-24 pixels per em. For 23 pixels to be 8.5 point you'd need 195 dpi. Assuming no hints but scary-good aa, one can obviously get it to work at 135 or so, with user distance/size of screen — key. Cheers!

2010-04-29 09:48:16 • Arno Enslin
The sub-pixel R, G and B are on the same position and they are not smaller than a pixel Yes, physically, they are exactly that. Physically, in a TFT, each pixel is made up of three distinct R/G/B elements (usually in that order), each of which is a third of a pixel wide. This is what subpixel rendering uses: Of course you can only edit full pixels, but when you make a pixel blue, then technically only the rightmost third of it is glowing. When you make it red, only the leftmost third. That's why subpixel rendering works. Obviously when you zoom in, your hardware stays the same, so you only see colors, not the active and inactive subpixels… There's a bunch of material on it out there. You might start here: http://www.smashingmagazine.com/2009/11/02/the-ails-of-typographic-anti-aliasing/

The illusion does work better, if the physical resolution of the TFT is higher.But just make a screenshot, if sub-pixel rendering is active, and zoom in. If the screen shot is viewed at the original resolution on a TFT (or any other kind of LCD), then turning on/off a sub-pixel "works" and produce something reasonably convincing to most viewers. Of course, if one views it at any other resolution, then of course that breaks the effect, because instead of turning on/off a single sub-pixel, the same sub-pixel is affected on several contiguous pixels, affecting one out of three sub-pixels repeatedly, creating what is most definitely a single color. The whole thing is only slightly more of an illusion than the LCD screen in the first place. We are already creating an "illusory" white by turning on three adjacent subpixels that are red, green and blue. A horizontal white line is simply a row of alternating red, green and blue subpixels. If we end that line a third of a pixel sooner, by not turning on that last blue subpixel, we still have a line ending in one subpixel of each color, so it looks white. Depending on the details of how the subpixel rendering is done, the amount of space between x-direction features, the individual's sensitivity to it, and the resolution of the screen, one can sometimes see "color fringing" with sub-pixel rendering. But that doesn't mean that sub-pixel rendering is somehow illusory. At text sizes on screen, for most people viewing it, it is immensely beneficial the overwhelming majority of the time. Cheers, T

2010-05-01 21:45:26 • Arno Enslin
Dots and pixels as noted are not the same in offset printing. A minimum 150 line screen can have positive and negative elements at the outer reaches of the tonal spectrum. Imagine how much pixel resolution it would take to make a consistently defined halftone dot at 3% of its fullest possible size within the 150 lpi grid. That is why they use imagesetters in the area of 3,000 dpi to resolve a 3% dot at 150 lpi. Solid type has no halftone dots so there is much less resolution needed to get reasonable text print at book sizes. A 1200 lpi Linotronic will do. When smaller sizes are needed, imagesetters at 2538 or higher are used. Since photographs are most often printed with fine quality book text, the 2538 and above imagesetters are used anyway. The main thing to realize is that dot printed on even a 2400 dpi laser is no where near as crisp as an imagesetter at the same setting. Inkjet is a whole other beast and they are not reliable for long runs.
