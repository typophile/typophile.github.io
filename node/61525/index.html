“Almost all of the preliminary sketches you ever see for a typeface are bogus, created after the fact because a reviewer asked to see them’. So if you ever wondered how these sketches were always so neat and perfect, that’s why.”

I can’t leave this uncommented. How about other type designers here? It’s frankly not true at all in my case. I don’t start a new design with anything but ideas and pencil/paper. I’m always sketching. Once an idea starts taking shape, there can be many, many re-drawings of key characters before any digitization takes place. It’s the way I’m most comfortable working because I have done a lot of drawing and lettering. And most of those pages don’t look either neat or perfect. Is that what people really expect, or want, to see?

If publications want to see early stage material of a typeface, it is likely that the designer is reluctant to show them, because often the early drawings, and even several iterations of proofs, can look unflatteringly rough, half-baked and even unrecognizable. Carter has said that he doesn’t feel comfortable with the drawing stage and does very little drawing. It’s just one way to work, and clearly it’s a viable one. But I don’t think a generalization can be made from that.

Back to James’s original topic, there does seem to be something of a vogue for annotated proofs (distinct from preparatory sketches) as marketing. FontFont seems to have a penchant for this, especially Spiekermann collaborations. Maybe that’s an unfair impression.

2009-09-02 15:06:07 • paragraph

I can’t imagine promoting an author’s latest novel by showing pages of the original manuscript with the copyeditor’s markup all over it.

No, because novels are already perceived as crafted, laborious and creative works of artists. Fonts, on the other hand, either ‘have always been around’, or are ‘spit out by some computer program, if you just hit the right button’.

The sketch is an icon, a piece of evidence for the tedious and sophisticated process of type design – and the human mind (even better: the human hand) that has been involved in it.

While novels might not be promoted with showings of edited manuscripts, their authors are very often depicted in the act of writing, or with their tool – be it a pen or a typewriter. I’d rather see some more (informative) correction sheets than those pictures of the type designer sitting in front of a screen, working on the kerning. F
