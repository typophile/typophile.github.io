2010-07-28 14:10:59 • riccard0

Joe, not all English native speakers will pronounce Futura like you do, and your analogy with ‘future’ doesn’t necessarily hold.

In ‘future’, the historical cluster /tj/ (’t' followed by the ‘y’ sound) is changed to /tʃ/ (the ‘ch’ sound) because it occurs in an unstressed syllable. This much is common to most dialects of English.

However, when a historical /tj/ comes in a stressed syllable as it does in Futura, it is treated differently depending on dialect. In many American pronunciations, in fact, it is likely to turn into a plain /t/ (i.e. ‘tune’ is pronounced identically to ‘toon’), a phenomenon called yod-dropping. Others preserve the /tj/, and still others will turn it into /tʃ/ (yod-coalescence).

The last alternative is not something really associated with North American English. Do you also pronounce ‘Turing’ and ‘enduring’ as ‘CHOOR-ing’ and ‘in-JURE-ing’? If not, your pronunciation can only be explained as a back-formation from ‘future’. If Futura followed the normal course of English phonological development, we would expect it to be pronounced ‘few-TOOR-uh’ by those who have yod-dropping after ’t'. I would expect ‘few-CHOOR-uh’ from someone who orders CHOO-na sandwiches, but it sounds jarring to me from a North American speaker.

Nick’s suggestion of ‘few-TURE-uh’ is the best since it works for any speaker, whether they preserve, drop, or coalesce the yod.

Joe, you’ve misunderstood my whole point. I treated Futura like a normal English word from Latin. I wasn’t suggesting that we apply a Classical Latin pronunciation, though others in this thread have.

English has lots of words from Latin, and there’s a pattern to how they are pronounced. There are complex alternations to the vowels according to which syllables receive stress, but native speakers have mostly internalized these rules. So we can read a word like ‘sesquicentennial’ which we have never heard pronounced, but can reasonably guess that it would be pronounced sesk-wee-sen-TEN-ee-uhl, while ‘sesquicentenary’ would be sesk-wee-SENT-uh-nare-ee.
