Design and lettering by Alex Steinweiss, 1948
From "For the record"

The incomparable Steinweiss used many pens, did plenty of embellishments in many genres, but never classical-style flourished ligatures. (So far as I can tell from my record collection and the examples in the book.) Here, he could have done the "st" lig in Kostelanetz.

The style of lettering identified with him, now available as a font named "Hairspray" for some strange reason, and (shame!) unattributed (and I bet he doesn't get royalties either), is a casual, cursive, light monoline script. It was made into a font in 1939, "Steinweiss Scrawl", and available from Photolettering Inc. in the 1950s.

Conclusion: the flourished joining ligatures are a product of writing with a broad-nib pen. They would be easy to do with a round-nibbed "Speedball" pen (presumably what Steinweiss used), but they don't belong ... unless the calligrapher is in a Post-modern groove.

Or Pseudo-proto-modern: Storm added the ct and st ligs to Sebastian, his 18th century sans serif.

There are plenty of other reasons to design new text typefaces and to design them well, but to pretend that we're chasing some elusive readability is, as I've said before, to willfully ignore the daily reading experience of many hundreds of miliions of people reading a great diversity of writing systems under incredibly diverse and often adverse conditions. Human beings are better at reading than any improvement at the type design level can significantly affect. ... random words on text make it less readable

Random words in text make it less comprehensible, but not necessarily less readable in the sense that 'readble' is commonly used on Typophile. The line between the cognitive acts of text recognition and of understanding is a porous one, and 'reading' understood in toto involves both, but the impact of random words on reading speed has to do with our ability to make sense of the text, not our ability to recognise words. In most of the discussions of readability among typographers and type designers, the comprehensive aspect is ignored, since the professional obsession is with the cognitive act of text recognition. Introducing the comprehensive aspect of reading further diminishes the relative impact of type design, since it is obvious that good writing is more important than a good typeface.

2004-06-09 17:15:45 • Nick Shinn
> the distinction between legibility
> and readability is not clear enough

Yes, it's confusing.
"Legibility" has a (relatively) focused meaning, concerning the decipherability of individual glyphs; but readability is over-loaded*, with at least three different meanings: to most poeple it refers to the textual content, as in text that's written so as to be easily understood; to most typographers it refers to the page layout, paragraph attributes like good leading, linebreaks, etc.; and the third -most esoteric and controversial, but I feel most impotant to the context of type design- meaning concerns the font itself: the way in which a font promotes decipherment of boumas - different than the decipherment of individual letters. So for example a sans is more legible but less readable than a serif font; and tight letterspacing helps readability but reduces legibility.

* Such over-loading is certainly inconvenient, but it's common, and generally context -which is always there- focuses the meaning quite well.
