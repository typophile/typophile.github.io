How small will it need to get, and where will it be seen? This is really relative, so as much as possible we test logos in their final environments and worst case scenarios. For a film production company we tested movement at small reversed sizes at screen res (ie as seen at the end of film credits), for an electrical engineer we tested etched in metal at around 10mm across.

Unfortunately, Nicholas, I don’t have very much experience to speak of. My gut feeling is that if you’re thinking this much about clarity, it shouldn’t be much of a problem in the end. The things I know you need to watch out for are little details that could get blurry or filled up when scaled down. If your logo has a lot of fine details, then it might be a good idea to make a simplified version for use in these difficult circumstances. 2007-02-01 05:34:45 • blank

Good rule of thumb on the pen – but I think you have to consider resolution/quality/colour issues as well.

Business cards are generally the smallest full colour litho application, but very high print resolution so rarely any problems.

Pens the smallest print application, generally screen printed in 1 or 2 solid colours, potential for ink spread.

You should check how it prints at likely sizes on low resolution devices - colour and b/w laser prints. Fax, although dying out, should be at least considered.

Then there are web graphics and perhaps application icons. I remember having to make a logo work on an early WAP phone - tiny screen, no greyscale let alone colour.
